Chapter Two

The Loyal Soldier

The courier rode through the night.

He left Yerushalayim at dusk and rode east through the hills of Yehuda, then north up the King's Highway across the Yarden, then east again into the rolling plains of Ammon. He carried a sealed message from the king to the commander-in-chief of the army of Israel. The message was short. It contained no explanation. Send Uriah the Chitite to me. The courier did not know what was in the message and did not ask. He had been carrying messages for the king for fifteen years and he had learned long ago that the contents of sealed letters were none of his business.

He reached the camp of Yoav at the second hour after dawn on the third day.

Yoav read the message. He frowned. Then he set the parchment down on the table in his command tent and looked up at the courier.

"Tell the king the message has been received. Tell him Uriah will be in Yerushalayim by tomorrow night."

"Yes, my lord."

The courier left.

Yoav sat alone in the tent for a few minutes. He read the message again. Then he stood up and walked to the entrance of the tent and called for his adjutant.

"Find Uriah the Chitite. He is in the third division — Eliam's command. Tell him the king has summoned him personally and that he is to leave for Yerushalayim within the hour. Give him a fresh horse and a war report to carry. Tell him to ride hard."

"Yes, sir. Anything else?"

Yoav was quiet for a moment. He was a man who had spent his life reading other men. He knew the king better than anyone alive. He had seen the king's face when Avner had been killed. He had seen the king's face when Ish-Boshet had been beheaded. He had seen the king's face on the day the Aron came home and on the day Michal had despised him. And he had a very strong feeling, looking at the sealed message that had just arrived from Yerushalayim, that something was wrong.

"No," he said finally. "Just send him."

* * *

Uriah arrived in Yerushalayim the next evening, dusty and saddle-sore, carrying a leather satchel full of dispatches from the front. He went directly to the palace. He was admitted without delay.

David was waiting for him in the audience chamber.

"Uriah."

"My lord."

David rose from the throne and walked down to greet him personally. He clasped Uriah's forearm. He looked at the man's face and saw what he expected to see — a hard, lean, weather-beaten captain, his beard caked with the dust of three days' riding, his eyes alert and respectful and a little tired.

Uriah was a Chitite by birth — a convert to Israelite faith who had cast his lot with David's army many years earlier and had risen through the ranks by sheer competence and loyalty. He had a Hebrew name, Uriah, which means "Hashem is my light" — a name he had taken at his conversion. He had taken on the full discipline of an Israelite warrior. He had married a daughter of Eliam ben Achitofel, one of the most prestigious families in the kingdom. He had fought in every campaign since the war with the Pelishtim at the valley of Refa'im. He was a man of perfect discipline and perfect loyalty, and David had personally inducted him into the Thirty — the elite unit of David's most trusted warriors.

David looked at him and felt something cold settle in his stomach.

"You have ridden hard."

"Yes, my lord. The orders said to ride hard."

"How is Yoav?"

"Well, my lord. The siege is going well. The city walls are mostly breached on the eastern side. Yoav expects the city to fall within two months."

"And the men? How is morale?"

"High, my lord. The men are confident. We have lost very few in the past month. The Ammonite resistance has weakened since we took the outer fortifications."

"Good. Good."

David paused. He searched for the next thing to say. He had been rehearsing this conversation in his mind for two days, and now that the man was actually standing in front of him, the rehearsed words felt thin and unconvincing.

"Uriah, you have ridden a long way and you must be tired. I will not keep you here. Take the rest of the day and the night. Go down to your house. Wash your feet. Eat a good meal. See your wife. You have earned a night of rest. Tomorrow morning come back here and I will give you my reply for Yoav and you can ride back."

The Hebrew phrase the king used was uchatz raglecha — "wash your feet." It was a euphemism. Every Israelite man understood the euphemism. The Tanach uses similar language elsewhere — most famously in the story of Yael and Sisera, where the literal washing of feet has overtones of intimacy and the marital bed. Go down to your house and wash your feet meant go home and be with your wife. There was no other meaning. Both men in the room knew it.

Uriah said nothing.

He bowed.

"Thank you, my lord."

He turned and walked out of the chamber.

* * *

David had a small gift sent after him.

The verse says it explicitly: "And there went out after him a portion from the king." The Hebrew word is mas'at — a special gift of food and wine sent from the royal kitchen, an extraordinary honor that signaled royal favor. It was the kind of gift a king would send to a soldier he wanted to thank — a gift that should have made Uriah feel cherished and appreciated and welcomed home.

David sent it because he was hoping it would persuade Uriah to do what the king's instructions had not been able to persuade him to do. Go home. Eat. Be with your wife. Sleep in your own bed.

The gift arrived at Uriah's house an hour after Uriah himself should have arrived there.

Uriah was not at home.

The servant carrying the gift looked around in confusion. The house was locked. Batsheva had been informed (through her own channels) that her husband was returning home that night, and she had prepared the house — fresh linens on the bed, food on the table, oil for his bath. She was waiting in the inner room. But Uriah had never come.

The servant carried the gift back to the palace and reported the strange news to the chamberlain.

David received the report at the second hour of the night. He was already in bed. He sat up in the dark.

"Where did Uriah go, then?"

"My lord — he is sleeping at the gate of the palace."

"At the gate of the palace?"

"Yes, my lord. With the king's guards. He took a blanket and lay down on the stones outside the gate. He has been there for three hours."

David lay back down in the dark. He stared at the ceiling. The cold thing in his stomach had now spread to his chest.

He understood, in that moment, that Uriah was not going to cooperate. He did not yet know whether Uriah suspected the truth or whether Uriah was simply being the disciplined soldier he had always been. But he knew that Uriah was not going to walk into the trap that had been so carefully prepared for him.

* * *

The next morning, David summoned Uriah back to the audience chamber.

"Uriah."

"My lord."

"You have not come home from a journey. Why did you not go down to your house?"

Uriah's face did not change. He stood at attention. He gave his answer with the calm, steady voice of a man who had thought through what he was going to say and was prepared to say it.

"The Aron, and Israel, and Yehuda, are dwelling in booths. And my lord Yoav and the servants of my lord are camped on the open field. And I, then, should go to my house, to eat and drink and lie with my wife? By your life, and by the life of your soul — I will not do this thing."

The Tanach preserves the speech almost word for word. It is one of the most famous lines spoken by a minor character in all of Tanach — and Uriah, despite his short appearance in the narrative, is one of the most morally serious figures in the Davidic court. He could have gone home. The king had personally given him permission. The royal gift had been sent. His wife was waiting for him. Every consideration of personal pleasure and personal advantage and personal exhaustion was telling him to go home.

He refused.

The Aron of Hashem was sleeping in a tent in the field outside Rabbah, because the Levi'im had taken a portable Aron with the army for purposes of inquiring of Hashem during the campaign. The men of the army were sleeping in tents in the field. Yoav, the commander-in-chief, was sleeping in a command tent. Eliam, Uriah's father-in-law, was sleeping in a captain's tent. Every other man in Uriah's life — including the most sacred object in his religion — was sleeping outside, on the ground, in the camp, in the dust. And Uriah was being told to go home. To eat a hot meal. To sleep on a feather mattress. To be with his wife.

He could not do it. He would not do it. The very idea was an offense to everything he had become when he had converted to the God of Israel and put on the uniform of the army of David.

* * *

The Etzion Yeshivat commentary raises a difficult question about this scene. The question is: Did Uriah know?

There are two ways to read his answer. The first way is straightforward: Uriah was a deeply principled soldier who refused to enjoy domestic comforts while his comrades were suffering at the front. His refusal was a pure moral statement, completely unrelated to anything else. He did not know about David and Batsheva. He simply could not bring himself to enjoy what his fellow soldiers could not enjoy. This is the simple, surface reading.

The second way is more complicated. Uriah arrived in Yerushalayim and was sent home. Instead of going home, he slept at the gate of the palace, with the king's guards. The king's guards were the very men most likely to know what had happened in the palace several weeks earlier. Word travels fast in any royal court, and word travels especially fast among guards who have nothing to do at night but stand around and gossip in low voices. Is it possible that one of the guards, in the course of those long hours together by the gate, told Uriah something? Not the whole truth, perhaps. A hint. A rumor. We saw a woman come into the palace one night last month. We thought you should know.

If Uriah had heard something, his speech to David takes on a different meaning. The phrase "and lie with my wife" — u-leshkav im ishti — would be a deliberate, pointed reference to exactly the thing that David had been hoping he would do. Uriah would be saying, in effect: I know what you want me to do, my lord. I know why you summoned me from the front. I will not do it.

Read that way, his words "by your life, and by the life of your soul, I will not do this thing" become almost a confrontation — a soldier looking his king in the eye and using the standard oath formula of the army to refuse a direct command, and daring the king to do anything about it.

We will never know for certain which reading is correct. The Tanach is silent about what Uriah knew or suspected. But the Etzion commentary points out that Uriah's behavior is hard to explain on the simple reading alone. A loyal soldier who is given a night off by his king does not normally sleep on stones outside the palace gate. He goes home, has a quick meal, sees his wife briefly, and then perhaps says he has to return to camp because the war is in a critical phase. He does not refuse his king's hospitality with such finality, and he does not make a speech about it. The speech only makes sense if Uriah is saying something more than the literal words convey.

Whatever Uriah knew, David understood that Uriah was not going to cooperate. He understood it the moment Uriah finished speaking.

But David did not give up.

* * *

"Stay here today," David said. "Stay one more day. I have a war report I want to compose for Yoav, and I need you in the city to carry it. Stay tonight as well. Tomorrow morning you can leave."

Uriah bowed.

"As my lord commands."

He left the chamber.

David called for his steward.

"Tonight Uriah eats with me. Set out the best wine in the cellar. The aged wine from the Pelishti merchant — the one I bought when we took Gat. Bring out the silver cups. I want a full feast for two. I want him to drink more wine tonight than he has ever drunk in his life."

The steward bowed and went to make the arrangements.

David sat alone in the chamber for a long time after that. His face was hard. He was making a calculation that he had been resisting for two days, and he was beginning to recognize that the calculation had only one acceptable outcome.

If Uriah went home tonight — if the wine could persuade him where the personal command had not — then the problem would solve itself. Batsheva's pregnancy would be timed in a way that allowed Uriah to plausibly claim it as his own, and the matter would be quietly forgotten. David would do penance privately. He would offer sacrifices. He would say nothing more about what had happened in the palace. The kingdom would never know. Batsheva would go on with her life. Uriah would raise a child who was not his, never knowing it. Achitofel would go on serving as David's counselor, never knowing it. The story would die in the dark room where it had been born.

That was the good outcome.

The other outcome — the outcome that David could not yet bring himself to think about clearly — was the outcome where the wine did not work either. And David sat in the chamber and tried not to think about what he would do if the wine did not work.

* * *

The feast was set in the smaller of the two royal dining halls. There were two places at the table. The lamps were lit. The wine was poured. Uriah came in at the appointed hour, washed his hands with the silver pitcher, and sat down across from his king.

David ate sparingly. Uriah ate well — he had been on hard rations for months, and the food in front of him was the finest in the kingdom. The lamb was rich. The bread was fresh. The figs were sweet. The wine was deep red and old and very strong.

David kept refilling Uriah's cup.

"You served well at Refa'im five years ago. Do you remember the second day, when the Pelishtim broke through our left flank?"

"I remember, my lord."

"You held the gap with eleven men. You held it for three hours."

"It was my duty, my lord."

"Drink. To duty."

Uriah drank.

David refilled the cup.

"And the night raid on Tzova. You were one of the first up the wall."

"I was, my lord."

"Drink. To Tzova."

Uriah drank.

The cups were refilled and emptied and refilled and emptied. David offered toasts to every battle Uriah had fought in. He offered toasts to Yoav, to Avishai, to Benayahu, to Eliam — and Uriah, hearing his father-in-law's name and feeling the wine begin to soften the edges of his discipline, drank to his father-in-law's health. He offered toasts to the Aron, to the kingdom, to the city of Yerushalayim. He offered toasts that an ordinary soldier would have declined for being too sentimental, but that a man who has been sleeping in a war camp for six months and is now eating roasted lamb in the king's dining hall cannot easily decline.

By the second hour of the feast, Uriah's eyes were glassy.

By the third hour, his speech was slurred.

By the fourth hour, he was visibly drunk — drunk in the way that only a soldier who has been deprived of wine for half a year can become drunk on the king's strong vintage.

David watched him with cold attention.

"Uriah."

"My lord."

"It is late. Go home. Sleep in your own bed tonight."

Uriah stood up. He swayed. He caught himself on the back of the chair.

"Yes, my lord. Thank you, my lord."

He staggered out of the dining hall.

David sat at the table and waited. He drank a single cup of water. He stared at the door.

* * *

A servant came in two hours later.

"My lord."

"Yes."

"Uriah the Chitite did not return home."

David closed his eyes.

"Where did he go?"

"He is at the gate of the palace again, my lord. With the guards. He fell asleep on the stones outside the gate. Drunk. He is sleeping there now."

David said nothing for a long time.

The servant waited.

Finally, David opened his eyes. They were dry. The look on his face was not the look of a man who was about to cry. It was the look of a man who had just made a decision he would carry for the rest of his life.

"Bring me a parchment. And ink. And a fresh seal."

"Yes, my lord."

"Send the courier. The same courier who brought Uriah from the camp. Wake him up. He rides at dawn."

"Yes, my lord."

The servant left.

David sat alone at the table. He pulled the parchment in front of him. He picked up the quill. He dipped it in the ink. He wrote, in his own hand, in the kind of careful Hebrew script that no scribe would have been allowed to copy:

To Yoav, commander of the army of Israel.

Place Uriah at the front of the heaviest fighting. When the battle is at its most fierce, withdraw the men from behind him. Let him be struck down and die.

David, King of Israel.

He let the ink dry. He folded the parchment. He sealed it with hot wax and pressed his ring into the wax. He wrote Uriah's name on the outside.

Then he got up and walked to the door of the chamber.

"Wake the courier. Tell him this letter is to be delivered to Yoav directly, into his own hand, no other. Tell him to give it to Uriah to carry."

The servant blinked.

"To Uriah, my lord?"

"To Uriah."

The servant did not understand. He took the letter. He went out into the night.

David walked back across the dining hall. He stood for a moment looking at the table — the empty plates, the wine cup with the last few drops still in it, the chair where Uriah had been sitting an hour earlier.

Then he turned out the lamps and went to bed.

* * *

The Talmud later describes this moment with quiet horror. David sent a man to his death by giving the man his own death warrant to carry. Uriah, riding back to Rabbah at dawn with the sealed letter in his saddle pouch, had no idea that the parchment under his hand was the order for his execution. He did not break the seal. He did not read what it said. He carried it to Yoav, the way a loyal soldier carries any letter from any king.

The Midrash adds a detail. As Uriah rode east through the hills of Yehuda, the dawn rose over his right shoulder. He was riding back into the war. He was thinking about his men. He was thinking about the next phase of the siege. He was perhaps thinking — if his head was clear enough after the wine of the previous night — about the strange days he had just spent in Yerushalayim. The king had been so insistent that he go home. The king had pressed wine on him with unusual eagerness. The king had ridden a strange and quiet mood the whole time.

Uriah may have suspected something, in those hours on the road. But he could not have suspected this. No man has ever ridden quite so unknowingly toward his own death. The letter in his pouch was the simplest thing in the world — a sealed message from a king to his general, the kind of message couriers carry every day across every kingdom in every century. And the contents of the message were the cruelest thing a king of Israel had ever written.

The Tanach, with its characteristic restraint, does not editorialize. It simply records the next phase of the story:

"And when Uriah had come, David asked of the welfare of Yoav, and the welfare of the people, and the welfare of the war. And David said to Uriah: Go down to your house, and wash your feet… But Uriah slept at the door of the king's house with all the servants of his lord, and went not down to his house."

And then, after the failed feast:

"And it came to pass in the morning, that David wrote a letter to Yoav, and sent it by the hand of Uriah."

By the hand of Uriah.

By the hand of the man whose death the letter ordered.

That is how Chapter 11 of Shmuel Bet records the worst single sentence ever written by a king of Israel. It records it without comment. It does not need to comment. The reader is left alone with the sentence and with the silence that follows it.
