Chapter One

The Roof in the Spring

## Volume III — The Sin ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

It began with a king who did not go to war.

The verse that opens Shmuel Bet 11 is one of the most quietly devastating sentences in all of Tanach: "And it came to pass at the return of the year, at the time when kings go forth to battle, that David sent Yoav and his servants with him and all of Israel, and they destroyed the children of Ammon and besieged Rabbah. But David remained in Yerushalayim."

Read it again. At the time when kings go forth to battle. That is the technical Hebrew phrase for the spring military campaigning season — the period after the winter rains had ended and the roads had dried out, when armies could move and supply lines could function. Kings go forth to battle. Plural. Every king in the region was at war. Yoav was at the front. Avishai was at the front. Benayahu was at the front. The entire fighting force of Israel was at the front. And David — David remained in Yerushalayim.

The Malbim notes the significance of those four words. Vedavid yoshev biRushalayim. And David sat in Yerushalayim. The verb yoshev means more than "remained." It means "settled," "rested," "took his ease." The Malbim reads it as the first crack in the story. David should have been with his army. He was the king. His soldiers were sleeping in tents in the field outside Rabbah, eating cold rations, dying on enemy spears. And David was sleeping in a cedar-paneled chamber in the palace, eating roasted lamb at his own table, walking on his own roof in the cool of the evening.

The sin of David and Batsheva did not begin with what David did on the roof. It began with the fact that David was on the roof. A king who is in his army's camp does not have the leisure to wander around his palace looking out at his city. The leisure itself was the first temptation.

And David, who had spent the entire first decade of his reign in the field — who had subdued the Pelishtim and the Moavim and the Aramim and the Edomim, who had bled in every campaign and slept in every kind of weather — David had finally stopped going.

He was getting older. He was almost fifty. His joints ached in the cold mornings. His captains were younger than he was, and they were perfectly competent. There was no good military reason for him to go to Rabbah personally. Yoav could besiege a city without David's help. Yoav could besiege a city better without David's help, in fact, because Yoav did not need anyone looking over his shoulder.

So David stayed home.

And in the spring — late afternoon, on a day when the air was warm and the sky was gold and the city of Yerushalayim was spread out below him in the long shadows of the western light — David walked up onto the roof of his palace.

* * *

He had not slept well the night before.

The Midrash preserves a tradition that David's troubles in this chapter began with a request he himself had made of Hashem. He had been studying the Avot — Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov — and he had noticed that each of them was tested by Hashem at a moment of their greatest spiritual elevation. Avraham at the Akedah. Yitzchak at the well of Rechovot. Yaakov at the wrestling match by the Yabok. And David had asked: Why do You not test me, Hashem? Why do my prayers begin "the God of Avraham, the God of Yitzchak, the God of Yaakov" — and not "the God of David"? It is because You have not tested me. Test me. I am ready.

Hashem, according to this midrashic tradition, had answered him: Are you sure?

David had been sure.

And Hashem had said: I will test you in the matter of a married woman.

David had not understood what that meant. He thought he was being told that he would be tested in something abstract — a question of halacha, perhaps, or a test of resolve. He had not understood that the test would be physical, immediate, in the body, in the eye, in the late afternoon sun on a flat roof in the spring.

This is one tradition. There are other traditions. Some sources say that David's sin was not preceded by any request at all — that it was simply a moment of human weakness in a man who had reached the heights of power and forgotten, for an hour, that he was not God. Other sources say that the entire incident was orchestrated by Hashem to teach later generations the laws of teshuvah, and that without David's sin and David's repentance, the doctrine of repentance itself might never have been so clearly articulated in Tanach. The Gemara in Avodah Zarah 4b–5a preserves this idea: "David was not the kind of man fit to commit such a sin, nor was Israel the kind of nation fit to commit such a sin as the Golden Calf — but Hashem allowed it to happen so that future generations would know that the gates of teshuvah are always open."

Whichever tradition you prefer, the fact on the ground was the same. David walked up onto the roof in the late afternoon. He had not slept well. He was restless. He was alone. He was almost fifty years old, and he was not at war with his army, and he was on the roof of his palace.

And he looked down.

* * *

The Midrash adds a small detail that the Tanach itself does not include. Just before David came up to the roof, a small bird had fluttered onto the parapet near him. David, who was in the habit of feeding birds and who had a particular fondness for sparrows, took a stone and threw it gently to scare the bird away — perhaps to play, perhaps in distraction. The bird flew off the parapet and disappeared into the courtyard of one of the houses below.

David's eyes followed the bird.

That was when he saw her.

* * *

She was bathing.

The Tanach says it in the briefest possible way: "And from the roof he saw a woman bathing, and the woman was very beautiful to look upon."

The mefarshim are careful with this verse. The Radak notes that Batsheva was not bathing in a public place. She was in a courtyard inside her own home — a small enclosed space, with high walls on three sides and a privacy screen on the fourth. She had every reason to expect that she could not be seen. The only way anyone could have seen her was from a much higher elevation than the surrounding houses — from, in fact, the roof of the king's palace, which towered above the city. Nobody should have been able to see into her courtyard. Nobody except the king himself.

She was performing her ritual immersion at the end of her menstrual period, the way every observant Jewish woman did. She was a married woman. Her husband was Uriah the Chitite, a member of David's elite guard, currently at the front in the siege of Rabbah. Her father was Eliam ben Achitofel — and Achitofel, her grandfather, was the most respected sage and royal counselor in all of Israel. She was, in other words, not just a beautiful woman. She was a woman with deep roots in the Davidic court, the daughter of a captain, the wife of a captain, the granddaughter of the king's most trusted advisor. She was the kind of woman who should have been especially protected from the king's gaze — by every consideration of loyalty, by every bond of friendship and service.

And David saw her.

He stopped walking.

He stared.

The seconds stretched. The Tanach does not tell us how long he stared. The Tanach does not need to tell us. The next verse moves directly to the inquiry.

* * *

He turned away from the parapet at last. He walked back across the roof. He went down the stairs into the palace. His face must have been strange, because the servant at the door of his chamber looked at him oddly when he came in.

"Bring me Eliyahu the Chamberlain. Quietly. I want him in this room within five minutes."

The servant ran.

Eliyahu the Chamberlain was a man of David's inner circle — a fixer, a discreet handler of personal matters that needed to be handled without official record. The Tanach does not name him, but the Yalkut Shimoni and other midrashic sources fill in the figure of the messenger David sent. He was a man whose loyalty was unquestioned and whose discretion was absolute. He had served David since the wilderness years.

He came into the chamber. He saw the king's face.

"My lord."

David did not waste time.

"There is a woman bathing in a courtyard near the eastern wall of the metzudah. The third house from the square. I just saw her from the roof."

"Yes, my lord."

"Find out who she is."

Eliyahu blinked.

"That is all, my lord?"

"That is all. Find out who she is. Find out who her father is. Find out if she is married. Find out everything you can in the next hour and bring it back to me. Quietly. Use no names. Do not write anything down."

"Yes, my lord."

"Go."

Eliyahu went.

* * *

He came back forty minutes later. His face was tight.

"My lord."

"Tell me."

"The woman you saw is Batsheva bat Eliam. Her father is Eliam ben Achitofel — the captain of the third division, currently at the front. Her grandfather is Achitofel of Gilo, your senior counselor."

David's stomach tightened.

"And her husband?"

"Uriah the Chitite, my lord. Of the Thirty. Currently at Rabbah."

David closed his eyes.

He had been hoping — though he had not allowed himself to articulate the hope even in the privacy of his own thoughts — that the woman would turn out to be unmarried. A widow, perhaps. A divorced woman. Someone he could legally take as a wife without violating any commandment, without crossing any line. He had told himself, in the seconds while Eliyahu was gone, that he would investigate first and decide later. Find out who she is. Then I will decide what to do.

The answer that Eliyahu had brought back was the worst possible answer.

She was the wife of one of his Thirty. She was the granddaughter of his most trusted counselor. She was the daughter of a captain currently fighting his war. She was, in every imaginable way, off-limits.

A righteous king would have ended the inquiry there. A righteous king would have said: I am sorry I asked. I should not have looked. Forget I sent you. Do not speak of this to anyone, ever. And the matter would have been closed. The story of the Aron at Peretz Uzza had ended that way — David had recognized his mistake and stopped before things got worse. He could have done the same thing in this room, in this hour.

He did not.

Instead, he opened his eyes.

"Eliyahu."

"My lord."

"Send messengers to her house. Tell her the king wishes to see her. Tonight. After dark. Bring her to the side entrance of the palace through the servants' corridor. Quietly. Do you understand me?"

Eliyahu hesitated.

"My lord — "

"Do you understand me?"

"I understand, my lord."

"Then go."

* * *

The messengers of the king came to Batsheva's door at the sixth hour of the night.

There were two of them. They knocked quietly. Batsheva's only servant — an older woman named Rachel who had served the household since before Batsheva's marriage to Uriah — opened the door.

"What is it?"

"We are from the king. We are here to bring the lady of the house to the palace."

Rachel stared at them.

"At this hour?"

"At this hour. Those are our orders."

"Whose orders?"

"The king's."

Rachel did not move from the doorway.

"My mistress is the wife of Uriah the Chitite. He is at the front. She is alone. The king cannot summon a married woman alone to the palace at night. There must be some mistake."

The first messenger looked at her without expression.

"There is no mistake. Please bring the lady."

Rachel turned and went into the inner room. Batsheva was sitting on the edge of her bed in the lamplight, her hands folded in her lap. She had heard the knocking. She had heard the conversation at the door. She knew what was coming.

"My lady — "

"I heard them, Rachel."

"You do not have to go."

"I do, Rachel. I do have to go. He is the king."

"Your husband — "

"My husband is at Rabbah. He will not know." Batsheva's voice was very calm, very flat. "Help me with my robe."

Rachel wept while she dressed her mistress. Batsheva did not weep. She let the older woman fasten the brooches and braid the hair and arrange the veil, and she stood up and walked out of the inner room into the front room where the messengers were waiting.

"I am ready."

The first messenger bowed.

"This way, my lady."

She walked out into the dark street. Rachel stood in the doorway holding a lamp. She watched her mistress disappear into the night with the two royal guards on either side of her, and she put her face into her hands.

* * *

The Tanach records the next moment with unflinching brevity:

"And David sent messengers, and took her, and she came to him, and he lay with her — for she was sanctified from her impurity — and she returned to her house."

Every clause of that verse is doing work.

And David sent messengers. David did not go to her. He sent for her. He used his royal authority to summon another man's wife to his palace at night. The verse uses the same verb — vayishlach — that has been used throughout David's reign for the official business of the king. He had sent messengers to Avner. He had sent messengers to the Yevusi. He had sent messengers to Chiram of Tzor. Now he was sending messengers to a woman bathing on the other side of the city, summoning her to his bed.

And he took her. The verb vayikacha is the verb of acquisition. It is the same verb used in the Torah for taking a wife in marriage, and the same verb used elsewhere for taking by force. The mefarshim note the deliberate ambiguity. The Tanach does not say David seduced Batsheva. It does not say she came willingly. It says he took her, with all the force of a verb that can mean either consent or compulsion, and lets the reader sit with the discomfort of not knowing.

And she came to him. The verse adds this clause to clarify that she did, in fact, walk into the palace under her own power. But she walked in because the king had sent for her, and a woman whose husband was at the king's war and whose father was a captain in the king's army and whose grandfather was the king's most trusted advisor — that woman did not have the practical option of refusing.

And he lay with her. The Tanach does not soften this. It does not call it love. It does not call it passion. It calls it what it was: a king lying with another man's wife.

For she was sanctified from her impurity. This is the parenthetical clause that the Talmud in Shabbat 56a will eventually use as part of the case for David's defense. The Hebrew is vehi mitkadeshet mitumatah. It tells us that Batsheva had completed her menstrual purification — that she had gone to the mikveh — that the bathing David had seen on the roof was the ritual immersion of a woman who was now in a state of purity. The detail is included because it matters halachically. If she had been in her impurity, the act would have been an even greater violation than it already was. The parenthetical is technical, and it is also haunting. She had just been to the mikveh. She had been preparing, in her purity, for the eventual return of her husband from the war. And on the very day of her immersion — perhaps within the hour — the king had taken her.

And she returned to her house.

She walked back through the dark streets of Yerushalayim to her empty house. Her husband was at Rabbah. Her father was at Rabbah. Her grandfather was in Gilo, asleep. Her household was a few servants and herself. She walked back, and she let herself in, and she went to bed alone, and she did not know — no one yet knew — that what had just happened in the king's chamber was going to change the history of the world.

* * *

Several weeks passed.

The Tanach jumps over the weeks with the brutal economy that has characterized this story from the beginning. "And the woman conceived, and she sent and told David, and said: I am pregnant."

Five words in Hebrew. Hara anochi. I am pregnant.

The Midrash imagines the moment Batsheva realized. She had been counting the days since her last menstrual period, the way every observant woman counts. The days had passed. She had expected the next period. It had not come. She had waited a week. It had not come. She had counted again. She had checked her body for the small signs that women learn to recognize. She had felt the changes. She had known.

And she had been terrified.

If she said nothing, the pregnancy would become visible eventually. When her husband returned from the war, he would do the simple arithmetic that any soldier does when he comes home from a long absence. I have been gone for X months. The child cannot be mine. And the consequences for her — under the laws of the time, under the laws of every time — would be unambiguous and final.

If she said something, she might at least save her own life. The king who had summoned her to his chamber had the power to manage the situation. He could not undo what had happened, but he could perhaps arrange things so that her life and her family's honor were preserved.

She wrote a message. She sent it through one of her own servants, not through the palace. The message contained two words.

I am pregnant.

She did not say we. She did not name David. She did not mention what had happened in the palace. She simply stated the fact. She trusted — or perhaps she desperately hoped — that David would understand what she was telling him and what she was asking him to do.

The messenger arrived at the palace late in the morning. David read the message standing at his window. He read it twice. He sat down. He read it a third time.

Then he stood up and walked across the chamber and called for Eliyahu the Chamberlain.

"Send a message to Yoav at Rabbah. Tonight. By the fastest courier."

"What is the message, my lord?"

David turned. His face was pale.

"Send Uriah the Chitite to me."
