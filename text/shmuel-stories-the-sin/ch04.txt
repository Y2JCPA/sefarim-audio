Chapter Four

Two Men in One City

Five weeks passed.

The thirty days of mourning for Uriah ended quietly. Batsheva went through the rituals — the torn garment, the seven days of sitting on a low stool, the visitors who came and brought food and spoke in low voices, the prayers, the silence. She did not weep extravagantly. She did not need to. The grief was real, but it was buried under something heavier than grief — under fear, and under the knowledge that her own body was changing in a way that nobody else in the room could yet see.

When the thirty days were complete, the king's chamberlain came to her house.

"My lady. The king has sent for you. He wishes to take you as his wife."

Batsheva looked at him with eyes that were dry and tired.

"When?"

"Today. The carriage is waiting."

She did not protest. She had nowhere to protest from. Her husband was dead, killed in a war for the king who was now summoning her. Her father was still at the front. Her grandfather Achitofel was in Gilo, bewildered by the death of his son-in-law and not yet aware of the rumors that were beginning to spread through the inner circles of the court.

She gathered her belongings into a single chest. The chamberlain's men loaded the chest onto a cart. Batsheva climbed into the carriage. The wheels rolled. She was driven through the streets of Yerushalayim, past the marketplace where she had bought her bread, past the public well where she had drawn water with the other women of her neighborhood, past the synagogue courtyard where Uriah had once stood to pray. She did not look out the window. She kept her eyes on her hands in her lap.

The carriage reached the palace.

She was brought into the royal household. She was given a chamber. She was given new clothes. The marriage was performed with the simplest possible ceremony — there was no public celebration, no feast, no proclamation. Five weeks after the death of her first husband, Batsheva became the wife of the king who had killed him.

A few months later, she gave birth to a son.

The Tanach records the birth in a single sentence and then closes Chapter 11: "And the wife of Uriah heard that Uriah her husband was dead, and she made lamentation for her husband. And when the mourning was past, David sent and brought her to his house, and she became his wife, and bore him a son. But the thing that David had done was evil in the eyes of Hashem."

That sentence is the last sentence of Chapter 11.

The first sentence of Chapter 12 is: "And Hashem sent Natan unto David."

* * *

The two sentences are placed back to back in the Tanach with no transition. The narrator does not pause to explain what happened in the months between. The narrator does not describe how the gossip spread through the court, or how Achitofel slowly pieced together what had happened to his granddaughter, or how David tried to compose psalms during this period and found that the words would not come. The narrator simply moves from the thing was evil in the eyes of Hashem to Hashem sent Natan.

The juxtaposition is the entire commentary.

David had been hoping, for those months, that the matter was over. He had Batsheva. He had a son. The kingdom was at peace. The siege of Rabbah was finally drawing to a close. Yoav was about to send word that the city had fallen and that the king should come east to receive the formal surrender. Everything that David had done was, on the surface, working. The cover-up had succeeded. The marriage was legal. The child was thriving. The court had quieted. Even Achitofel — even Achitofel — had said nothing.

But the Tanach says: Hashem sent Natan.

The verb is vayishlach. Hashem sent. It is the same verb that David himself had used at the beginning of the previous chapter — and David sent messengers, and took her. Now the same verb is used in the opposite direction. And Hashem sent Natan. David had sent for a woman; Hashem was now sending a prophet. The two sendings balance each other. The two sendings are part of the same divine economy. What David had set in motion with his summons, Hashem was now answering with His own summons.

Natan walked into the throne room.

He was alone.

He had no entourage. He had no scroll. He had no formal request for an audience. He had walked past the guards at the gate — the guards knew him; they let him through without question — and he had walked through the corridors of the palace until he came to the audience chamber, and he had walked into the chamber as if he were paying a casual visit.

David looked up from his desk.

"Natan."

"My lord."

"Come in. Sit down. I have not seen you in weeks."

Natan came in. He did not sit down. He remained standing.

"I have a story I want to tell you."

David smiled. He had always enjoyed Natan's stories. The prophet was, in addition to being a man of God, a gifted storyteller — he had a talent for parables and aggadot, and he had often used stories to illustrate points of law or theology that he wanted David to consider. The king set down his quill and leaned back in his chair.

"Tell me."

* * *

Natan began.

"There were two men in one city. The one was rich, and the other was poor."

David nodded. He was listening with the attention he gave to all of Natan's stories — half official-attention, half genuine interest. The opening was a familiar form. Two men in one city. The setup of a parable. He wondered where it would go.

"The rich man had exceedingly many flocks and herds. But the poor man had nothing — nothing — except one little ewe lamb, which he had bought and reared, and it grew up together with him and with his children. It ate of his own bread, and drank of his own cup, and lay in his bosom, and was unto him as a daughter."

David's eyebrows lifted slightly. The image of the ewe lamb was tender — almost sentimental. Lay in his bosom. Was unto him as a daughter. These were not the words of a parable about livestock. They were the words of a parable about love. Natan was clearly building toward something.

"Natan — what city is this?"

"Does it matter, my lord?"

"I am asking because the case sounds like a real case. If a poor man has been wronged by a rich man somewhere in the kingdom, I want to know which town the case came from, so I can send a magistrate."

"It is a case in your kingdom, my lord. That is all I am at liberty to say at the moment. May I continue?"

"Continue."

"And there came a traveler unto the rich man."

"A traveler. A guest."

"Yes, my lord. A guest."

David nodded. Hospitality cases were among the more difficult cases under the Torah. A guest had certain rights. A host had certain obligations. The arrival of a traveler usually triggered a feast — and the feast required meat.

"And the rich man was unwilling to take from his own flock and from his own herd to dress for the traveler who had come to him. And so he took the ewe lamb of the poor man, and dressed it for the man who had come to him."

The story stopped.

Natan stood silently in the chamber.

David's face went red.

The shepherd in him — the man who had once spent years on the hills above Beit Lechem with sheep that had names and personalities, who had killed a lion and a bear to protect a single lamb from his father's flock — that shepherd rose up in him and roared. He pushed back his chair. He stood up. The Tanach uses a particular verb for what happened next: vayichar af David ba-ish me'od. "And David's anger burned against the man, very greatly."

The verb vayichar. The same verb that had been used in Chapter 6, when David's face had darkened over the death of Uzza. Vayichar le-David. The same root, the same physical reaction — David's anger flaring up out of the deep places of his moral conscience, the way it always did when David encountered an injustice.

He spoke. The Tanach preserves his exact words.

"As Hashem lives, the man who has done this thing deserves to die!"

He took another breath. He was trembling. The injustice was so clean and so simple that he could see it without any effort at all. A rich man with everything had taken the one beloved possession of a poor man with nothing. A guest had been entertained on stolen meat. A child-like animal had been killed for a stranger's dinner. The arithmetic of moral horror was instant.

"And he shall restore the lamb fourfold," David said, "because he did this thing, and because he had no pity!"

Fourfold.

The Torah's law on the theft of a sheep, in Shemot 21:37, requires fourfold restitution: a thief who steals a sheep and slaughters it must pay four sheep for one. David was applying the law correctly. He was applying it from memory, the way a king who knew the Torah as well as David knew it would apply it without thinking. He was rendering an instant verdict in a court case that had not yet been formally presented to him, on the strength of a single one-sided narration. He was certain. He was angry. He was absolutely sure.

He did not yet understand that the court case was about him.

* * *

Natan looked at David.

The prophet's face was very calm. He had been waiting for this moment for years. He had been waiting since the night the second word of Hashem had come to him about the Davidic covenant — the night when he had been told that David's house would stand forever but that "when he commits iniquity, I will chasten him with the rod of men." He had not known, on that earlier night, what the iniquity would be. Now he knew. And now Hashem had sent him to chasten the king.

He spoke six words.

"Atah ha-ish."

"You are the man."

* * *

Three syllables in Hebrew. A-tah ha-ish. Two words. They are perhaps the most famous two words spoken by a prophet to a king in all of Tanach. They are used today in every language, in every country, whenever a moment of moral confrontation needs a phrase that captures the precise instant in which a person realizes that the story being told about an abstract villain is actually a story about themselves.

David's face changed.

The fury drained out of his cheeks. The redness faded. His mouth opened slightly. His eyes went unfocused for a moment, then refocused on Natan with a sudden, terrible clarity.

He understood.

In the space of two seconds — from the moment Natan said the words to the moment David fully grasped their meaning — David traveled a distance that most men spend years failing to travel. He went from righteous anger at an abstract villain to recognition of himself in the role of the villain. He went from I am the king who renders justice to I am the man who has stolen the lamb. He went from confidence to despair. The journey is preserved in the Tanach in the silence between the two halves of the verse.

Natan kept speaking. He was not finished. The parable had been the setup. Now came the actual rebuke.

"Thus says Hashem, the God of Israel: I anointed you king over Israel, and I delivered you out of the hand of Shaul. And I gave you the house of your master, and the wives of your master into your bosom, and I gave you the house of Israel and Yehuda. And if that were too little, I would have added to you many more such things. Why have you despised the word of Hashem, to do that which is evil in His sight? Uriah the Chitite you have struck down with the sword, and his wife you have taken to be your wife, and you have killed him with the sword of the children of Ammon."

The speech was a long indictment. It opened with everything Hashem had given David. I anointed you king. I delivered you from Shaul. I gave you everything that belonged to your master. I gave you the entire house of Israel. And if that had not been enough, I would have given you more. The list was meant to make the contrast unbearable. You had everything. There was nothing you needed that was not yours for the asking. And yet you took the one thing that was not yours. You took the wife of one of your own captains.

The phrase "the sword of the children of Ammon" — Hashem's specific naming of the instrument of Uriah's death — was the most damning phrase in the speech. David had not killed Uriah with his own sword. He had used the swords of the enemies of Israel as his weapon. He had outsourced the murder to the very enemies he was at war with. The legal niceties of his cover-up — the conditional get, the technical permissibility, the timing of the marriage — all collapsed in front of that one phrase. Hashem named the instrument. Hashem identified the killer. The killer was David. The instrument was the army of Ammon. There was no escape from the simplicity of the indictment.

Natan continued. He delivered the four punishments.

"Now therefore the sword shall not depart from your house forever, because you have despised Me, and have taken the wife of Uriah the Chitite to be your wife."

That was the first punishment. The sword would not depart from his house. David would lose sons to violence — not one son, not two sons, but a series of sons, generation after generation, in a chain of bloodshed that would haunt his family for the rest of his life and beyond.

"Thus says Hashem: Behold, I will raise up evil against you out of your own house, and I will take your wives before your eyes, and give them to your neighbor, and he shall lie with your wives in the sight of this sun."

That was the second punishment. Evil would rise up out of his own house. His own children would turn against him. The phrase "out of your own house" was specific — the rebellion would come from inside the family. David's wives would be taken from him publicly, in the sight of the sun. (This would be fulfilled in a few years, when Avshalom would publicly take David's concubines on the roof of the palace as an act of rebellion — the same roof from which David had first seen Batsheva.)

"For you did it in secret, but I will do this thing before all Israel, and before the sun."

That was the third punishment, or rather the explanation of how the second punishment fit the crime. You did it in secret. You hid your sin. You arranged a quiet murder, a quiet marriage, a quiet cover-up. I will do this thing before all Israel, and before the sun. The exposure of David's sin would be public, total, undeniable. The story would be told. The book of Shmuel Bet would be written, and these chapters would be read aloud in synagogues for three thousand years. There would be no hiding place.

The third punishment — the death of the first child of David and Batsheva — was about to come, but Natan was not finished yet.

David stood in the chamber and listened to the prophet recite the punishments. His head was bent. His hands were at his sides. He was not weeping yet, but he was past anger and past denial and past all the defenses he had been building for the last several months. He was simply standing there receiving the indictment.

When Natan finished, David spoke five words.

* * *

"Chatati la-Hashem."

"I have sinned against Hashem."

That was all he said. Five Hebrew words. Two and a half syllables for each man involved in the crime — for David, for Uriah, for Batsheva, for the unborn child whose fate hung on his confession. He did not make excuses. He did not say but I had reasons. He did not say but the conditional get. He did not say but Uriah was rebellious or but the woman bathed in a place I should not have been able to see or but Hashem tested me and the test was too hard. He did not say any of the things that a defensive man says when he is finally caught.

He said: I have sinned against Hashem.

Five words. The Tanach does not record any other words from David in this scene. The prophet had spoken at length. The king answered in five words.

The Yeshivat Har Etzion commentary, comparing this moment to Shaul's behavior when he was rebuked by Shmuel after the war with Amalek, observes that David's response is the gold standard for what teshuvah looks like. Shaul had hesitated. Shaul had argued. Shaul had said but the people made me do it and but I did obey, I just didn't kill the king and the best of the animals and a hundred other excuses, until eventually he confessed — but his confession came after the excuses had run out, and it was therefore a confession of exhaustion rather than a confession of conviction. David's confession came in the first second after the indictment was complete. He did not wait for Hashem to add another argument. He did not wait for Natan to push further. The instant the prophet stopped speaking, David said I have sinned. That is what teshuvah looks like when it is real.

* * *

Natan was watching him.

The prophet had been carrying, in addition to the indictment and the punishments, one more piece of news. Hashem had given him one more sentence to deliver, and the sentence was an answer to David's confession.

"Hashem also has put away your sin. You shall not die."

David looked up.

"What did you say?"

"I said: Hashem has put away your sin. You shall not die."

"Say it again."

"My lord — "

"Say it again, Natan. I need to hear it again."

"Hashem has put away your sin. You shall not die."

David put his hand over his mouth.

"He forgives me."

"He forgives you."

"Just like that. I confess and He forgives."

"Just like that, my lord. That is how it works. That is how it has always worked. The gates of teshuvah are never closed to a man who comes through them in truth."

David's eyes filled. The first tears of the encounter — and the first tears David had shed in many months — began to run down his face.

"I did not deserve this."

"No," Natan said quietly. "You did not. That is what mercy means."

The Talmud reads this exchange with great care. The technical punishment for taking a married woman is death. The technical punishment for orchestrating the death of an innocent man is also death. David had committed both offenses, and the standard halachic outcome would have been a death sentence. Natan's words were a stay of execution, granted by Hashem in direct response to David's confession. The instant David said chatati la-Hashem, Hashem said you shall not die. The mercy was instantaneous. There was no waiting period. There was no deliberation. The teshuvah and the forgiveness were almost simultaneous.

But Natan had one more thing to say.

"However — because by this deed you have given great cause for the enemies of Hashem to blaspheme — the child also that is born to you shall surely die."

David's face went pale.

The child. The first son of David and Batsheva. The boy whose birth had been the proof that the cover-up had succeeded. The boy David and Batsheva had named together, and watched grow over the past several months, and held in the cradle, and prayed over.

That child was going to die.

It was not a punishment that David could bargain with. It was a consequence. The chapter of life that had begun on a roof in the spring had to be closed somehow, and Hashem was going to close it through the child. The blasphemy that the sin had caused — the chillul Hashem — the desecration of God's name in the eyes of those who saw a king of Israel taking another man's wife and arranging the man's death — could only be answered with a public, painful, undeniable consequence. The child would be the consequence.

David did not protest.

He bowed his head.

Natan stood for a moment longer in the chamber. He looked at the king. He had completed the task Hashem had sent him to complete. He had spoken the parable. He had said you are the man. He had recited the punishments. He had delivered the verdict. And he had also delivered the mercy — the you shall not die that came in the same breath as the indictment.

He turned and walked out of the audience chamber.

He did not say goodbye. He did not bow. He did not perform any of the formal courtesies that a court prophet performs at the end of an audience with a king. He simply walked out. He had been a messenger. The message had been delivered. He went home.

* * *

David sat alone in the chamber for a long time after Natan was gone.

He did not weep, at first. The weeping would come later. For now, he was simply absorbing the verdict. He understood that the child was going to die. He understood that he himself was going to live, and that he would have to live with the death of the child for the rest of his life. He understood that the sword would not depart from his house, and that this meant his other sons would also die before him — perhaps Amnon, perhaps Avshalom, perhaps Adoniyahu, perhaps all of them. He understood that whatever joy he was going to have in the rest of his life would be balanced by an equal weight of grief.

He understood, also, that he had been forgiven.

The forgiveness was the strangest part. The death sentence had been lifted. He was not going to die for what he had done. He was going to continue to be the king of Israel. He was going to continue to write psalms. He was going to continue, in some way, to be the David that Hashem had chosen on the hillside above Beit Lechem when he was a boy.

But he was no longer going to be the David he had been before that afternoon on the roof. That David was dead. That David had been replaced by a David who knew, in the deepest parts of his soul, that he was a man capable of great evil. That David could no longer ever say I would not be capable of that. He had been capable of it. He had done it. He would carry it forever.

The first composition that came out of him in the days that followed was Psalm 51 — the great penitential psalm of David, which is read in synagogues on every fast day and on Yom Kippur and at the most solemn moments of the Jewish year. The heading of the psalm is preserved in Tehillim: "For the leader. A psalm of David, when Natan the prophet came to him after he had gone in to Batsheva." The opening lines of the psalm are some of the most well-known lines of liturgical Hebrew in the world:

"Be gracious to me, O God, according to Your kindness; according to the abundance of Your mercies, blot out my transgressions. Wash me thoroughly from my iniquity, and cleanse me from my sin. For I know my transgressions, and my sin is ever before me."

My sin is ever before me. That is the line that gives away the truth of David's repentance. The sin would always be in front of him. He could not forget it. He could not move past it. He could not pretend it had not happened. Le-negdi tamid — always before me. Every day for the rest of his life, when David closed his eyes, the sin would be there. That was the price of being forgiven. The forgiveness was real. The grief was also real. The two would coexist for the rest of his life.

* * *

Natan's last sentence — the child that is born to you shall surely die — would begin to be fulfilled within days.

The next chapter opens with the beginning of the child's illness.

But that is the next chapter.

This chapter ends in the audience hall of the palace, with David alone after Natan has left, sitting in the chair where he had been working when the prophet first walked in, looking at the wall, his hands flat on the desk, his face wet for the first time in many months.

He was forgiven.

He was also broken.

Both things were true.

Both things would always be true.
