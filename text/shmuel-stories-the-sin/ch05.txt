Chapter Five

The Living Son

The child fell ill that night.

The first symptoms appeared in the early hours of the morning after Natan's visit. The child began to cry — a high, thin, fretful cry that the nursemaids recognized at once as something more than ordinary discomfort. By the second hour after dawn, the child was running a fever. By the fourth hour, the fever was high enough that the chief physician of the royal household had been summoned. By midday, the physician was no longer hopeful.

David came into the nursery in the late afternoon.

He had not slept. He had spent the morning sitting in his audience chamber with the door closed, staring at the wall, trying to put together in his head what had happened in his life over the previous several weeks. When the message came to his chamber that the child was ill, he stood up at once and walked across the palace and entered the nursery without ceremony.

Batsheva was already there.

She was sitting on a low stool beside the cradle. The child was in the cradle, very small and very flushed. Batsheva had not left the room since the morning. Her hair was uncombed. Her face was empty. She did not look up when David came in.

David walked across the room and stood beside the cradle and looked down at the child.

The boy was eleven months old. He was small for his age, but had been healthy until that morning. He had David's dark eyes. He had Batsheva's quiet temperament. He had been the center of his mother's world during the months of David's distance, and he had been the center of David's world during the months of guilt that preceded Natan's visit. He was the proof of the cover-up. He was also, separately and without contradiction, a beloved son. Both things had been true. Both things would continue to be true for the next seven days.

David put his hand on the child's forehead.

The forehead was hot. Far too hot.

He turned to the physician, who was standing in the corner of the room with his bag of herbs and his small mortar and his face full of the careful neutrality that physicians put on when they have nothing useful to say.

"What can be done?"

"My lord — we have applied cool cloths. We have given the child willow bark tea. We are doing everything we know how to do."

"What else?"

"There is nothing else, my lord. The illness is in Hashem's hands."

David nodded slowly. He had known the answer before he asked the question. Natan had told him the day before. He had simply needed to hear it from the physician — needed to confirm, with his own ears, that the consequence was beginning.

He turned to Batsheva.

"I am going to fast and pray. I will not eat. I will not sleep on a bed. I will sleep on the floor of my chamber until — until Hashem decides one way or the other. Will you be all right here?"

Batsheva looked at him for the first time since he had come in. Her eyes were red but her face was dry. She had not been crying. She was past crying.

"Yes."

"I will come back tonight. And tomorrow morning. I will come every day."

"Yes."

He left the nursery and walked to his own chamber and closed the door behind him.

* * *

For seven days, David fasted.

The Tanach records the seven days in a few sentences. "And David besought God for the child. And David fasted, and went in, and lay all night upon the ground." The verb vayishkav artzah — and he lay on the earth — is the verb of mourning, of supplication, of a man who has lowered himself as far as a man can lower himself without dying. David did not sleep on his bed. He did not sleep on a mattress. He did not sleep on a cushion. He lay on the bare stone floor of his chamber, sometimes with his face against the cold flagstones, for the entire night, every night, for seven nights.

The elders of the royal household came to him on the third day. They brought a tray of food. They tried to coax him to eat.

"My lord."

David did not look up.

"My lord. We have brought you bread. And wine. And a little broth from the kitchen."

David's voice came up from the floor, low and hoarse.

"Take it away."

"My lord — please. You have not eaten in three days. The chief physician says — "

"Take it away."

The chief steward knelt down beside the king.

"My lord. May I speak openly?"

"Speak."

"The child is in Hashem's hands. You cannot help him by destroying yourself. The kingdom needs its king. Yoav is at the front. The siege of Rabbah is in its final phase. Messengers are arriving every day with questions that only you can answer. Please. A little bread. A little wine. For the kingdom, if not for yourself."

David turned his face toward the steward for the first time.

"The kingdom can wait. Hashem cannot."

"My lord — "

"Out."

The steward bowed his head and gathered the tray and stood up. He looked once more at the king lying on the bare stone floor with his face streaked with dust and his hair tangled. Then he carried the tray out of the chamber and closed the door behind him.

The elders left. They came back on the fifth day. They tried again.

"My lord. It has been five days. Please."

"No."

"Just the broth, my lord. The physician says — "

"No."

They left.

By the seventh day, the elders had stopped coming. They were beginning to be afraid. They were afraid that the king was going to die in his chamber from grief and starvation. They were afraid that they would have to send for Yoav. They did not know what to do.

David spent the seven days in the chamber, alone, on the floor, fasting and weeping and praying. The weeping had finally come on the second day, and once it had come it did not stop. He prayed in the words of the older psalms he had written in the wilderness — psalms about Hashem's mercy, about the willingness of the Holy One, blessed be He, to forgive a man who turned. He prayed in the words of the new psalm he was composing in his head, the one that would eventually become Tehillim 51. He prayed in silence. He prayed in screams. He prayed in the wordless rocking of a man kneeling on a stone floor with his arms wrapped around himself.

He was not asking for the child to be spared because he doubted Hashem's verdict. He had accepted the verdict. He was asking — because the only thing a parent can do is ask — for the verdict to be reversed. He was asking the way Avraham had once asked Hashem to spare Sodom. He was asking knowing that the answer might be no, but asking anyway, because to not ask would be to abandon the child before the child was even gone.

On the seventh day, the child died.

* * *

The servants did not know how to tell the king.

They gathered in the corridor outside David's chamber and whispered to each other. The child had died around the second hour of the day. The body was being prepared for burial in the room of the wet-nurse, where Batsheva had been sitting all night. The chief steward had to tell David. But the chief steward was afraid. He had watched the king weeping and starving and lying on the floor for seven days, and he was afraid that the news of the death would push the king into a despair from which he would not recover.

"What did he say when the child first fell ill?" one of the servants whispered.

"He said he would fast and pray."

"And now that the child is dead — what will he do?"

"I do not know. I am afraid he will do something terrible. I am afraid he will harm himself."

They stood in the corridor for almost an hour, debating what to tell the king and how to tell him, and David — inside the chamber, lying on the floor — heard the whispering. He could not make out the words. But he could hear the tone. He could hear the fearful, hesitant pitch of servants who were trying to figure out how to deliver bad news to a powerful man.

He pieced it together himself.

He sat up.

He called out through the door.

"Is the child dead?"

The whispering in the corridor stopped.

There was a long pause.

The chief steward opened the door cautiously. He stepped into the chamber. He looked at the king sitting on the floor with his hair uncombed and his eyes red and his hands streaked with the dust of seven days of mourning. He bowed his head.

"Yes, my lord. The child is dead."

David nodded once.

He stood up.

The Tanach records what he did next in a sequence of verbs that astonished the elders of the household and that has astonished readers ever since. "And David rose from the earth, and washed, and anointed himself, and changed his clothes, and came into the house of Hashem, and worshiped. Then he came to his own house, and he asked, and they set bread before him, and he ate."

Five verbs. Rose. Washed. Anointed. Changed. Worshiped. And then a sixth verb, the most surprising of all: Ate.

The chief steward could not contain himself. He was a man who had been with David for twenty years, and he had never seen anything like this in a parent's response to the death of a child. He spoke without permission.

"My lord — what is this thing that you are doing? While the child was still alive, you fasted and wept. Now that the child is dead, you rise and eat? I do not understand."

David turned to him.

He spoke quietly, with the tiredness of a man who had spent every emotional reserve he had over the past seven days and who now had nothing left except a clear understanding of what was true.

"While the child was still alive, I fasted and wept. For I said: who knows? Perhaps Hashem will be gracious to me, and the child will live. But now that he is dead — why should I fast? Can I bring him back again? I shall go to him. He shall not return to me."

That last sentence is one of the most extraordinary lines in the Tanach. Ani holech elav. Ve-hu lo yashuv elai. "I shall go to him. He shall not return to me."

It is the first clear statement in the Hebrew Bible of the belief that the dead do not come back to the living, but that the living will, in time, go to where the dead have gone. It is a statement of faith in something that comes after death — that the dead child is somewhere, that David will eventually be in that same place, and that the parting is therefore temporary in the largest possible sense even though it is permanent in the only sense that matters in this life.

It is not a statement of resignation. It is a statement of theological clarity.

David had been weeping for seven days because he was hoping the verdict could be reversed. The verdict had not been reversed. The verdict was final. The child was dead. There was nothing more to be done — for the child, for the situation, for the past. The only thing left was to live forward into the next day, into the next month, into whatever Hashem had next prepared for him. To continue to fast and weep would be to refuse the verdict. And David had never been a man who refused Hashem's verdicts. He had argued with them. He had wept over them. But he had never refused them.

So he washed. He changed his clothes. He went to the tent where the Aron of Hashem was kept, and he prostrated himself in front of it, and he worshiped. And then he came home and he ate.

The elders of the household watched all of this with their mouths slightly open.

* * *

David walked from his chamber to the room where Batsheva was sitting with the body of the child.

She was alone now. The wet-nurse had stepped outside. The body of the child was lying on a cloth on the bed, very small, very still. Batsheva was sitting on the edge of the bed with her hand on the child's chest. She was not weeping. She had wept for the seven days while the child was sick, but the moment of the death had quieted her.

David came in and stood in the doorway.

"Batsheva."

She looked up.

"He is gone."

"Yes."

David walked across the room. He sat down beside her on the edge of the bed. He did not say anything for a long time. He looked at the small body of his son. The child's face was peaceful — peaceful in the way that the faces of dead children sometimes are, as if the illness had finally let go and what remained was simply the face the child would have grown into if he had been allowed to grow. David put his hand on the child's foot. The foot was cold.

"I am so sorry."

Batsheva did not look at him.

"It was my sin," David said. "Not yours. He died because of what I did."

"I know."

"I want you to understand that. I want you to understand that nothing you did caused this."

"I know," she said again.

She was quiet for a moment. Then she added something that David had not been expecting.

"But he was also my son. And I loved him. And he is gone."

David nodded.

"Yes."

He put his arm around her.

For the first time since the night on the roof many months earlier, the two of them were not playing a role in front of each other. They were not the king and his stolen woman. They were not the murderer and the wife of the murdered man. They were simply two parents who had just lost their first child, sitting together on the edge of a bed with the small body of their son in front of them, and the grief of the moment was the same grief that any two parents in any time would have felt in the same room.

The Tanach records the Tanach's most surprising verse next. After David had comforted Batsheva — after the days of mourning for the child had passed, after the household had performed the appropriate rituals — the next verse is this:

"And David comforted Batsheva his wife, and went in to her, and lay with her. And she bore a son, and he called his name Shlomo. And Hashem loved him."

* * *

That is one of the most strange and beautiful verses in all of Tanach.

The story up to this point has been a story of unrelieved tragedy. The sin. The murder. The cover-up. The prophet. The verdict. The death of the child. Every step of the story has been a downward step. Every chapter has felt heavier than the chapter before it. The reader who is reading the chapter for the first time, in any age, in any language, has been waiting for the story to bottom out, has been wondering when the descent will stop, has been bracing for the next blow.

And in the middle of that descent, the next verse says: And David comforted Batsheva his wife, and went in to her, and lay with her. And she bore a son.

The verse contains a number of small but significant details. David comforted Batsheva. That is the first detail. The Hebrew is vayinachem David et Batsheva. The verb yenachem is the verb of nichum aveilim — the consolation of mourners. It is a technical, almost halachic word. David did not just feel sad with her. He performed the full ritual of consoling a mourner — visiting her, sitting with her, speaking the standard words of comfort, sharing in her grief. He treated her as a wife in mourning. He treated her as a person whose grief was real and whose loss deserved the same dignity as any other woman's loss.

His wife. That is the second detail. The Tanach calls her ishto — his wife. It is the first time in the entire story that Batsheva is called by this title in a positive sense. Up to this point, she has been called eishet Uriah — the wife of Uriah. Even after the legal marriage, the Tanach has continued to call her the wife of Uriah, as a kind of accusing reminder of the original status. Now, for the first time, after the death of the child and the ritual of consolation, she is called David's wife. The marriage has been recognized — not legally, because it was always legally valid under the rabbinic reading, but emotionally, theologically, narratively. She is now genuinely his wife. The child is going to be the firstborn of a real marriage rather than the child of an arrangement.

And lay with her, and she bore a son. That is the third detail. The new conception happened almost immediately after the death of the first child. The text moves quickly from one to the other, as if Hashem is in a hurry to begin the redemption of the situation as soon as the punishment has been completed.

And he called his name Shlomo. That is the fourth detail. The name Shlomo comes from the Hebrew root shalom — peace. The child whose father had brought a sword into his house was to be called Peace. The contrast is the entire point. David, naming the child, was both confessing the absence of peace in his own house and praying for peace in the next generation. The name was a hope. The hope would be fulfilled. Shlomo would be the king under whom Israel would have its longest period of peace and prosperity, and he would be the king who would build the Beit HaMikdash that David himself had been forbidden to build. The Davidic covenant from Chapter 7 would be fulfilled through this child.

And Hashem loved him. That is the fifth and most extraordinary detail. The Tanach almost never says, of a specific human being, Hashem loved him. It is one of the rarest phrases in the entire Hebrew Bible. It is said of Avraham. It is said of the people of Israel. It is said of Shlomo. The phrase signals divine favor of the deepest kind — a favor that is not earned but freely given, a favor that marks a person as set apart for a particular destiny. The child of David and Batsheva — the child whose parents' marriage had begun in a sin so grave that the prophet had come to rebuke it — was loved by Hashem. The love was not a reward for the sin. It was a sign that the sin had been forgiven and that the consequences had been paid and that the relationship between Hashem and the house of David had been restored.

The next verse adds one more detail:

"And He sent by the hand of Natan the prophet, and he called his name Yedidyah, for the sake of Hashem."

Natan came to the palace one more time. He came with another message from Hashem. The message was a single name. Yedidyah. Beloved of Yah. The same prophet who had come to rebuke David in the previous chapter now came to give the new child a divine name — a name that meant the one whom Hashem loves. The same Natan. The same king. The same chamber. But this time, the message was a blessing instead of an indictment.

David received the blessing in silence.

* * *

The Yoma 22b passage that we have referenced throughout this volume teaches that David paid for his sin "fourfold," in the deaths of four of his children. The first of those four deaths has just occurred — the death of the unnamed child of the original sin. The other three will occur over the coming years. Tamar (in the spiritual destruction Amnon will visit upon her). Amnon (whom Avshalom will kill in revenge for Tamar). Avshalom (whom Yoav will kill on the battlefield in violation of David's orders). Each death will be a piece of the fourfold judgment that David himself unwittingly pronounced when he heard the parable of the poor man's lamb. The sword that Natan said would not depart from David's house will work its way through the next several years, taking sons one at a time, leaving David alive at the center of the bloodshed to grieve them all.

But that is the next arc.

This arc ends with the death of one child and the birth of another, in the same chamber, in the same family, within a few months of each other. It ends with David and Batsheva sitting together as parents. It ends with Natan walking back into the throne room not to deliver an indictment but to deliver a blessing. It ends with the name Shlomo — peace — being whispered over a cradle in the heart of Yerushalayim.

The sin had been real.

The repentance had been real.

The forgiveness had been real.

The price had been real.

And the new beginning was also real.

Both things would always be true.

The next king of Israel would be born from the sin. Hashem had not erased the sin. Hashem had made the sin a part of the redemption. The line that would eventually produce Mashiach ben David would run through the bedroom that David had defiled and through the woman David had taken and through the child David had named Peace. There is no way to make this fact comfortable. There is no way to make it fit into a simple morality tale where the bad people are punished and the good people are rewarded. The Tanach has no interest in simple morality tales. The Tanach is interested in what really happened, and what really happened is that the worst sin of the greatest king in Israel was somehow folded into the greatest blessing of the greatest dynasty in Israel, and the child who would build the Holy Temple was born from the union that had begun on a roof in the spring.

Some Jewish traditions have been horrified by this. Others have found in it the deepest possible illustration of teshuvah. Both responses are appropriate. Both responses are part of how Jews have read this chapter for three thousand years.

David, in any case, lived through the rest of his life carrying both halves of the truth at once. The sin was always before him. The mercy was also always before him. He had been broken, and he had been forgiven, and he was now the father of the child whom Hashem loved, and the price he was paying for what he had done would not be finished for many years yet.

The arc ends here.

The next arc — the longest and most painful arc in the entire book of Shmuel Bet — will tell the story of how the sword Natan promised began to walk through David's house.

It begins with the rape of Tamar.

But that is for another volume.
