Chapter Three

The Sword and the Letter

The courier reached Rabbah on the morning of the fourth day.

Uriah had ridden ahead of him by two hours, but a cavalry courier on a fresh mount can outrun a tired soldier carrying war dispatches, and the courier had been instructed to deliver his letter before Uriah arrived. He came galloping into the camp at dawn, threw the reins to a stable boy, and ran directly to the command tent.

"For the general. From the king. By his own hand."

Yoav took the parchment. He looked at the seal. He looked at the writing on the outside — "To Yoav. Personal." He recognized David's hand.

He broke the wax. He unfolded the letter. He read it.

He read it again.

The adjutant standing across the tent from him saw Yoav's face change. It was a small change — a tightening of the jaw, a brief narrowing of the eyes — and it lasted less than two seconds. Then Yoav's face was its usual unreadable mask again. But the adjutant had been with Yoav for a decade, and the adjutant knew what the small change had meant.

The general had just received bad news.

Yoav folded the letter carefully and put it inside his tunic, against his chest, where no one would see it.

"Send Uriah to me when he arrives," he said. "And bring me the maps of the eastern wall."

* * *

Uriah arrived an hour later, exhausted from the ride.

He came into the command tent and saluted.

"My lord."

"Uriah. Welcome back. Sit down. Eat something."

Uriah sat.

Yoav set a plate of bread and dried meat in front of him and a cup of wine, and watched him eat. Uriah was a man Yoav had known for years. They had fought together in three campaigns. Yoav had personally recommended Uriah for promotion to the Thirty. Yoav had stood at the wedding of Uriah and Batsheva five years earlier, and he had drunk wine at the wedding feast and danced with the rest of the men under the bridal canopy in the courtyard of Eliam's house in Yerushalayim.

Now Yoav had a letter inside his tunic, against his chest, that ordered him to kill this man.

"How was Yerushalayim?" Yoav asked.

"Quiet, my lord. The city is at peace."

"And the king? How did he look?"

Uriah hesitated for a fraction of a second. Yoav noticed the hesitation.

"He looked well, my lord. He inquired after you. He inquired after the war. He treated me with great honor — beyond what I deserved. He had me to his table. He gave me good wine."

"Did he."

"Yes, my lord."

"And what did you do, in Yerushalayim?"

"I delivered the dispatches. I waited for the king's reply. I slept by the palace gate."

"You did not go down to your house?"

"No, my lord. I did not feel it was right."

Yoav was quiet for a moment. He looked at Uriah's face — the dust still on the cheeks, the wine still in the eyes from the previous night, the tired determination of a man who had refused to accept a comfort he had felt was not his to take.

He understood, in that moment, why the letter was inside his tunic.

He understood the whole thing. He had not yet pieced together every detail, but he understood the shape of it. The king had needed Uriah to go home, and Uriah had refused. The king had then needed Uriah to die. Yoav was the only person in the kingdom who could be trusted to arrange the death without leaving a trail, and so the letter had come.

A different general might have refused. A different general might have sent the letter back to the king with a sharp note saying I will not do this thing. A different general might have torn the letter up and ridden to Yerushalayim himself to confront David in person.

Yoav was not a different general.

Yoav was the man who had killed Avner in the gateway of Chevron, and who had killed Avshalom many years later in the forest of Ephrayim, and who had killed Amasa in the fields of Givon by stabbing him in the belly while pretending to embrace him. Yoav was the most skilled killer in the army of Israel, and he had spent his entire career doing the things that David needed done and could not do himself. He had killed Avner without an order. He had killed Avshalom in defiance of an order. He had killed many men in many ways. He was not a man who refused to kill when killing was required.

But this letter was different. This letter was a king ordering the death of one of his own most loyal soldiers, for no military reason that Yoav could identify, in the middle of an active campaign. There was nothing legitimate about it. It was murder.

Yoav had a calculation to make.

If he obeyed the letter exactly — if he placed Uriah at the front of the heaviest fighting and then ordered the men around Uriah to withdraw — every soldier in the army would understand what had happened. The general murdered the captain. The whisper would travel. The discipline of the army would erode. Yoav's own men would stop trusting him. The kingdom would suffer. Yoav was not going to do that.

But if he disobeyed the letter — if he refused to kill Uriah at all — the king would know, and the king would never forget, and the king's anger would eventually fall on Yoav himself. Yoav had survived in the Davidic court for two decades by being indispensable, and he was not going to make himself dispensable now by refusing the king's most personal request.

There was a third option. He could obey the letter partially. He could send Uriah to a section of the wall where the fighting was hard and the casualties were heavy, but he would send other men with him — men who would also die. He would not pull back from behind Uriah. Instead, Uriah would die along with a number of other soldiers in a normal battlefield disaster. The death would look like the chaos of war. Nobody would suspect a hidden order.

The cost would be the lives of the other men.

Yoav decided he could live with that cost.

* * *

Uriah finished his bread and his wine. He looked up at the general.

"My lord. What is the letter the king sent you?"

"The letter is for me, captain. Not for you."

"As you say, my lord."

Yoav stood up.

"You will rejoin your men this morning. Eliam needs you back at the eastern wall — the third division has been advancing on the gate tower since you left. There has been some progress and some loss. We are going to need a hard push tomorrow. I am putting you with Eliam's company at the front line."

"Yes, my lord."

"Get some sleep. I will send for you when the orders are issued."

Uriah saluted. He turned and walked out of the tent.

Yoav watched him go.

When Uriah was out of sight, Yoav sat back down at the table and pulled the letter out of his tunic and read it one more time. Then he took a small earthenware cup and held the parchment over the flame of an oil lamp until it caught fire. He held the letter until the flame had eaten almost all of it. He dropped the last burning corner into the cup and watched it turn to ash.

There would be no record of this letter. No one would ever read it again.

The only place it would survive — and Yoav did not know this yet — was in the book of Shmuel Bet, in the second half of Chapter 11, where the prophet who eventually wrote down the story would recover what had happened in this tent and would write it into the eternal record. But Yoav did not know about the eventual prophet. Yoav was thinking about the immediate problem, and the immediate problem was now solved.

He stood up and called for his adjutant.

"Issue orders for the morning. Eliam's third division will lead the assault on the eastern gate tower. I want them in position before dawn."

* * *

The assault began at first light.

The eastern gate tower of Rabbah was the strongest section of the city's wall. The Ammonites had concentrated their best archers on the tower, knowing that any breach would have to come through there. The tower was high. The walls were thick. The defenders had stones and boiling oil and arrows in great supply.

Yoav had been planning the assault for weeks. He had not intended to launch it for another month, when more siege equipment would have been brought up from the Yarden. But the letter from the king had changed his timeline. He launched the assault that morning with the equipment he had on hand, knowing that the casualties would be heavier than they needed to be, knowing that men would die who would not have died if he had waited.

Eliam's third division advanced on the tower at dawn. Uriah was in the front rank, alongside the other captains and their best men. They carried scaling ladders. They carried short swords for the close fighting they expected at the top of the wall.

The Ammonite archers opened fire as soon as the Israelites were in range. The arrows came in waves. The first wave killed three men in the front rank — not Uriah, but men beside him. The second wave killed five more. The Israelites raised their shields and pressed forward. They reached the base of the wall. They began to raise the ladders.

That was when the Ammonite garrison did the thing that Yoav had counted on. They did not stay behind their walls. They opened the gates and charged out — a counter-assault, a sortie, a desperate gamble to drive the Israelites back from the wall before the ladders could be planted.

The two forces met at the base of the tower in a chaos of swords and shields and screams.

Uriah fought.

He had been a soldier for a long time and he had fought in this kind of close combat many times before. He killed two men in the first minute. He took a cut on his shoulder and kept fighting. He was pushing forward toward the gate when the second wave of Ammonites came out of the city — fresh men, larger men, men with longer spears.

One of the spears found him.

It went in through the gap between the breastplate and the shoulder strap of his armor. It went deep. Uriah felt the shock more than he felt the pain. He took two more steps. Then his legs gave out and he went down on the rocky ground at the base of the eastern gate tower of Rabbah, in the dust of a city he had been besieging for six months, in the company of men who would also die that morning, and his last thought — according to a tradition the Midrash preserves — was a prayer for his wife and a question about why he had not gone home when the king had told him to.

He died at the base of the wall.

Several other captains died with him. The third division was driven back from the tower with heavy losses. Yoav, watching from a slight rise behind the lines, saw the retreat and gave the order to withdraw. The assault was over within an hour.

The day was a tactical defeat for the army of Israel.

It was, in the privacy of Yoav's mind, a tactical success of a different kind.

* * *

Yoav called for a courier.

"Ride to the king. Tell him about the assault. Tell him about the casualties. Tell him about the Ammonite sortie. Tell him about the men who died at the base of the wall."

The courier nodded. He had a good memory and he was used to delivering verbal reports. He prepared to leave.

Yoav stopped him.

"One more thing."

"Yes, my lord."

"If the king is angry — if he asks you why we attacked the wall in this way, why so many of our best captains were killed, why we lost the tactical advantage by attacking too soon — if he is angry — then at the very end, after he has finished his anger, you will say one more sentence. Memorize it now. Repeat it back to me."

"Yes, my lord."

"You will say: Your servant Uriah the Chitite is also dead."

The courier blinked.

"You will say it exactly that way. Your servant Uriah the Chitite is also dead. Not at the beginning of your report. At the end. Only after the king has expressed his anger about the failed assault. Do you understand?"

"I understand, my lord."

"Repeat the sentence."

"Your servant Uriah the Chitite is also dead."

"Good. Go."

The courier rode west.

Yoav stood alone outside his command tent for a long time after the courier had disappeared over the ridge. He looked east, toward the smoke rising over the still-besieged city of Rabbah. He looked at the dead being carried back from the wall on stretchers. He looked at the bodies, including the body of a man he had personally inducted into the Thirty.

He thought about the king who had sent him the letter that morning.

He did not feel anger toward the king. Yoav was not the kind of man who felt anger toward kings. He felt something more like understanding. He understood what David had done, and he understood why David had done it, and he understood that from this day forward he, Yoav, was the only living human being in the world who knew what David had done. The letter was ash. The courier knew nothing. Uriah was dead. Only Yoav remained.

The understanding gave Yoav a strange kind of power over his king. He did not yet know what he would do with the power. He did not even know that he would use it. But he understood, standing outside his command tent that morning, that his relationship with David had changed. From this day forward, David would always know that Yoav knew. And Yoav would always know that David knew that Yoav knew.

That kind of knowledge between two men is heavier than any chain.

* * *

The courier arrived in Yerushalayim the next afternoon.

He went directly to the palace. He was admitted to the audience chamber. David was on the throne.

"Speak."

The courier began his report. He described the assault on the eastern gate tower. He described the formation, the timing, the resistance. He described the Ammonite sortie out of the gates. He described the moment when the third division was caught between the wall and the counter-attack. He described the captains who had fallen.

He was a good courier, and he did his job well. He gave the report the way a courier is supposed to give a report — facts, names, numbers, no editorial comment. He waited for the king to react.

David's face went red.

"Why did you go so close to the wall?"

"My lord — "

"Did you not know that they would shoot from the wall? Did you not know what happened to Avimelech ben Yerubeshet at the wall of Tevetz, when a woman threw a millstone from the wall onto his head and killed him? Did you not know that you should not approach the wall? Why did Yoav permit this attack? Why did he attack with this formation? Why were our best men in the front rank? Who plans an assault this way?"

The Tanach preserves David's outburst almost word for word. It is the angriest David is shown in the entire chapter, and it reads like the outburst of a man who has been waiting for news and has just received the wrong news. David was furious that the assault had been a tactical disaster. He was furious that Yoav had spent so many lives.

He was furious — and the courier saw it, and the courier was waiting — because the report had not yet contained the one piece of information that David needed to hear.

When David finally paused for breath, the courier delivered his line.

"Your servant Uriah the Chitite is also dead."

The chamber went silent.

David's face changed.

The fury drained out of it in a single moment. The redness in his cheeks faded. The clenched line of his jaw relaxed. He sat back on the throne. He took a slow, careful breath.

He was relieved.

That was the worst thing about the moment, and the Tanach captures it perfectly. The courier had just told David that one of his best captains was dead, and David was relieved. He could not show the relief on his face. He had to hide it. He had to put on the face of a king who had just received bad news. But internally, the cold thing in his stomach that had been there for a week — the thing he had been carrying since the night Uriah refused to go home — unwound. The problem was solved. Batsheva would be his. The pregnancy would be explained. Achitofel would never know. The kingdom would never know. The sin would never become a public matter.

He composed himself.

"Tell Yoav this," he said to the courier, in a voice that was carefully neutral. "Tell him: Let not this thing displease you, for the sword devours one as well as another. Strengthen your battle against the city, and overthrow it. And tell him to encourage the men. The death of a captain is a loss, but the war must continue."

The Hebrew of David's reply is one of the most chilling sentences in Tanach. Al yera be-einecha et ha-davar ha-zeh. "Do not let this thing be evil in your eyes." David is telling Yoav not to feel bad about the deaths. Ki ka-zo veka-zo tochel ha-charev. "For thus and thus the sword devours" — the sword eats one man like another, it makes no distinction, do not blame yourself.

A king who has just been told that several of his best men have died in a failed assault should not be telling his general not to feel bad about it. A king who has just been told that one of his Thirty has fallen in battle should be tearing his robe and weeping. He should be ordering a day of mourning. He should be sending a personal letter of condolence to the family.

David did none of those things.

He sent a message of consolation to his general. Don't worry about the deaths, Yoav. War is war. The sword does not discriminate. Press on with the siege.

The Talmud, reading this exchange centuries later, will note the linguistic structure of David's reply. David says al yera be-einecha — let it not be evil in your eyes. The verb yera is from the same root as the word ra (evil) that appears in the very next chapter, when Natan tells David that the thing that David had done was evil in the eyes of Hashem. David tells Yoav not to see evil. The narrator tells us, as the chapter closes, that Hashem sees the evil. Two pairs of eyes. One human, one divine. The human eyes are being told to look away. The divine eyes are not.

* * *

The courier left.

David sat alone on the throne for a few minutes after the courier was gone. Then he stood up and walked out of the audience chamber and went to find Eliyahu the Chamberlain.

"Send a messenger to the house of Batsheva bat Eliam. Tell her servants that her husband has fallen in battle at Rabbah. Tell them with the appropriate gravity. Make sure she is treated kindly. The king grieves with her."

"Yes, my lord."

"And after the days of mourning are complete — the seven days of shiva, and then the thirty days of shloshim — bring her here. To the palace. As my wife."

Eliyahu looked at the king.

"My lord — the period of mourning for a woman whose husband has died — "

"Is thirty days. Yes. After the thirty days, she will come here."

Eliyahu bowed and left.

David was alone in the corridor. He stood for a moment looking at the wall. He was a man who had just arranged a murder and was now arranging a marriage. The two arrangements had taken him twelve days to complete, from the moment he had first walked up to the roof in the late afternoon. Twelve days. A short time, by the standards of historical events. A very short time, by the standards of the consequences that would follow.

The Tanach closes the chapter with a sentence that contains no editorial commentary, but that has more weight than a thousand sentences of commentary could have:

"And the thing that David had done was evil in the eyes of Hashem."

That is the verse. Nothing more. Vayera ha-davar asher asah David be-einei Hashem. And the thing that David had done was evil in the eyes of Hashem.

The chapter ends.

The next chapter will open with a prophet walking into the throne room, carrying a story about a poor man and a lamb.

But that is the next chapter.

This chapter ends with David walking back into his private quarters, a free man as far as the laws of his kingdom were concerned, with no human witness to his crime except a general at Rabbah who would never speak of it.

He was a free man for about five weeks.

Then Natan came.
