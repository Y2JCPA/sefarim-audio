Chapter Four

The Wise Woman of Avel Beit Ma’akhah

"Are you Yoav?"

The voice came from the top of the wall.

Yoav looked up. The sun was behind the parapet, and he had to shade his eyes to see the figure who had spoken. She was standing at the edge of the wall, leaning slightly over the stone, looking down at him. She was an old woman — very old, in fact, older than any woman Yoav had ever seen in his life. Her hair was white. Her face was lined with the accumulated weather of more decades than he could easily count. Her voice, though, was clear and strong — the voice of a woman who had been speaking with authority for a very long time and was accustomed to being heard.

"I am Yoav."

"Come closer. I wish to speak with you."

"Speak from where you are. I can hear you."

"I have a matter to discuss with you that requires discretion. You do not want the men of your army to hear it. And I do not want the men of my city to hear it. Come closer."

Yoav considered.

He was a professional soldier, and professional soldiers do not approach the base of a city wall during a siege without good reason. The defenders on the wall could drop stones on his head. They could shoot arrows at him. They could pour boiling oil — though boiling oil was not yet the weapon it would become in later centuries, the principle was the same. Approaching the wall of a besieged city was, as a general rule, suicide.

But the voice from the wall was not the voice of a soldier preparing an ambush. It was the voice of an old woman who wanted to talk. And the request for privacy — I do not want the men of my city to hear — was a request that struck Yoav as reasonable and, frankly, intriguing. A woman inside a besieged city who wanted to talk to the general of the besieging army in private was a woman who might have something useful to say.

He walked forward.

He stopped about ten paces from the base of the wall, close enough that their conversation could be conducted in normal voices without carrying to the men of the army behind him or to the defenders on the wall above her.

"I am here. Who are you?"

"My name does not matter. What matters is what I am going to tell you. But before I tell you anything, I want to ask you a question. Are you listening to me carefully?"

"I am listening."

"Hear the words of your handmaid."

"Speak, and I will hear."

* * *

She spoke slowly. Every word was measured. She had clearly been planning what she was going to say for some time — perhaps since the moment she had first heard that the army of David was marching on her city, perhaps for days, perhaps her whole life. Her tone was not the tone of a frightened woman begging for mercy. It was the tone of a woman who had seen many sieges and many kings and many wars, and who was now explaining something important to a man who she suspected did not yet understand what was happening.

"They used to have a saying, in the old days. If you want a true answer, ask in Avel. The saying is preserved in the mouth of the elders of Israel. Avel was known, throughout the generations since the time of Yehoshua, as a place of wisdom. Difficult questions were brought here. Disputes between tribes were brought here. Men who needed counsel on the hardest matters of law and life and war came to Avel for an answer, and Avel gave them an answer, and the answer was trusted throughout Israel. That was our reputation for four hundred years."

"I have heard of Avel's reputation."

"Then you understand what I am about to say. I am one of the peaceable and faithful of Israel. You are seeking to destroy a city and a mother in Israel. Why should you swallow up the inheritance of Hashem?"

Yoav blinked.

He had not been expecting this speech. He had been expecting a plea for mercy — the standard speech of a city about to be destroyed, asking for terms, offering tribute, begging for the lives of the women and children. He had heard that speech many times over the course of his career. He was prepared to respond to it with the standard response: surrender the rebels, open the gates, and we will spare the city.

But the old woman was not making that speech. She was making a different speech. She was making a theological speech. She was framing the siege as a crime against Hashem. She was calling Avel the inheritance of Hashem and asking why Yoav — the general of the king of Israel, the supposed protector of the inheritance of Hashem — was about to destroy what he was supposed to protect.

The speech was working.

Yoav was not a religious man in the way the woman was religious. But he was not stupid. He understood, immediately, that the framing she was using was not his framing. He had been approaching the siege as a technical military problem: how do I get the rebel out of the city? She was reframing the siege as a moral problem: why are you attacking the inheritance of Hashem at all? The reframing caught him off guard. It required a different kind of answer.

"I am not trying to destroy the city. I am not trying to destroy anything. Far be it from me. I am here for one man. Only one man. A Benjaminite named Sheva ben Bichri has lifted up his hand against the king, against David. Deliver him alone, and I will withdraw from the city."

"Deliver only him?"

"Only him."

"And if we deliver him, you will lift the siege? You will take your ram away from the wall? You will march your army back to Yerushalayim?"

"Yes."

"Swear it."

"I swear it. By the life of the king I serve. Deliver Sheva ben Bichri and the city will be untouched."

The old woman nodded.

"His head will be thrown to you over the wall. Wait."

She turned away from the edge and disappeared behind the parapet.

* * *

The mefarshim have a great deal to say about this exchange.

The first thing they say is that the wise woman was Serach bat Asher.

The identification comes from a midrash preserved in Bereishit Rabbah and cited by Rashi in his commentary on Shmuel Bet 20:19. The midrash notices that the woman introduces herself as "one of the peaceable and faithful of Israel" — anokhi shelumei emunei Yisrael — and reads the phrase as a cryptic self-identification. The woman is saying: I am the one who fulfills the count of the faithful of Israel. The fulfillment of a count, in the language of the midrash, is an echo of the moment when Yaakov and his family descended into Egypt in Bereishit 46, and the Torah listed seventy souls. The listed names add up to only sixty-nine. The seventieth is Serach bat Asher — the granddaughter of Yaakov by his son Asher, whose name appears in the list of those who descended. She is the one who completes the count. She is the seventieth soul. She is the one who fills the quota of the faithful of Israel.

This wordplay — shelumei, from the root meaning "to fulfill" or "to complete" — is the midrash's key. The old woman on the wall of Avel was not introducing herself with a generic description. She was identifying herself, to anyone who knew the old traditions, as the one who had filled the count of Israel. She was Serach bat Asher. She had survived from the generation of the descent into Egypt, through the generations of slavery, through the Exodus, through the forty years in the wilderness, through the entry into the Land under Yehoshua, through the four hundred years of the Shoftim, through the reigns of Shaul and now David — and she was still alive, still wise, still in Avel, still taking counsel on the wall of her city in the final generation of her extraordinary life.

The tradition about Serach entering Gan Eden alive adds a further dimension to her story. Because Serach was the one who had informed Yaakov that Yosef was still alive — breaking the news gently so that the old man would not be shocked into death by the sudden revelation after twenty years of mourning — Yaakov blessed her with a life that would extend far beyond any natural human lifespan. A later tradition, preserved in the Sefer HaYashar, fills in the tender detail of how she delivered the news: she approached her grandfather while he was in the middle of prayer, and she sang to him softly, "Od Yosef chai" — "Joseph still lives" — on a stringed instrument, so that the melody would prepare his heart for the words. According to the broader tradition of Derech Eretz Zuta and related sources, Serach was one of the few individuals in human history who never tasted death but was taken directly into Gan Eden. The wall of Avel, in this reading, was one of her last stations on earth before her final ascent.

It is worth sitting for a moment with the weight of what the midrash is telling us.

The same woman who had once brought the news of Yosef's survival to the elderly Yaakov in the tents of Be'er Sheva — who had been the gentle messenger between the lost son and the grieving father — was now standing on the wall of a northern Israelite city, speaking to a professional assassin, negotiating the death of a rebel to save her city from destruction. The arc of her life had carried her from the end of the age of the Avot to the middle of the age of the Kings. She had seen everything. She had seen the famine in Canaan and the descent into Egypt and the bondage under Pharaoh and the miracles at the Sea and the giving of the Torah at Sinai and the forty years in the desert and the conquest under Yehoshua and the long centuries of the Shoftim and the anointing of Shaul and the rise of David and the rebellion of Avshalom and now the rebellion of Sheva ben Bichri.

She was the living memory of the nation.

And she was about to use that memory — the accumulated authority of the woman who had once broken the news of Yosef to Yaakov — to save a city.

* * *

She walked down from the wall and into the main square of Avel.

The men of the city were waiting for her. They had seen her go up to speak to Yoav. They had seen her come back down. They understood that she was now the effective leader of the city's response to the siege — not by any formal appointment, but by the force of her age and her reputation and the ancient saying that "if you want a true answer, ask in Avel." She was the answer. She had been the answer for longer than any of them had been alive.

She stood in the square and raised her voice.

"Men of Avel. Hear me."

The men fell silent.

"Yoav ben Tzeruyah is not here to destroy our city. He is here for one man only. A man who is currently inside our walls. A man named Sheva ben Bichri. A Benjaminite. A man who has lifted his hand against the king, against David, the anointed of Hashem. Yoav has sworn that if we deliver Sheva alone, he will withdraw the army and the city will be safe."

One of the men of the city stepped forward. He was a middle-aged man with the dress of a village elder.

"My mother — we cannot do that. We cannot hand over a Jew to a besieging army. The law forbids it. It is written in the teaching of our rabbis that a group of Jews must all be killed rather than hand over one of their own."

The old woman looked at him.

This was the objection she had been expecting. This was the halachic problem at the heart of the sugya, the problem that the Tosefta and the Yerushalmi would argue about a thousand years later, the problem that would become one of the foundational questions of Jewish law regarding the limits of self-preservation.

"You are right about the general rule. The rule is that a group of Jews may not hand over one of their own to save the rest. Even if the enemy says give us one of you and we will spare the others, the group is forbidden to choose a victim. All must let themselves be killed rather than designate a single person for death."

"Then how can we — "

"Let me finish. The general rule has an exception. The exception is that if the enemy specifies a particular individual by name — if the enemy says give us this man, the one named so-and-so, and not just give us any one of you — then the rule changes. Because in that case, the enemy has already marked the man for death. The man is no longer a random Jew among many. He is a specifically-identified target. The group is not being asked to choose a victim. The group is being asked to confirm a target that has already been chosen by the enemy."

She paused.

"And Yoav has specified Sheva ben Bichri by name. He is not asking for a random hostage. He is asking for one particular man, whom he has named. The rule that forbids handing over a Jew does not apply in this case. The rule that applies is the other rule — the rule that a named rebel against the anointed king of Israel is chayav mitah, liable to death, and that anyone who shelters him becomes his partner in the rebellion."

The elder stared at her.

"My mother — are you certain of this law?"

"I am certain. I have been thinking about it all morning. I have been checking it against everything I know. The law is clear. We are permitted to hand him over. More than permitted — we are required to hand him over. If we do not, we become rebels ourselves, and the city will be destroyed because we were protecting a man whose protection the Torah does not require of us."

A second elder stepped forward. He was younger, with a sharper face.

"My mother — with respect. The man has taken refuge in our city. The laws of hospitality — "

"The laws of hospitality do not apply to a mored b'malchus. A rebel against the king does not receive the protection of the city gates. When you welcome a rebel, you become a rebel. The law is clear."

"But we did not know he was a rebel when we welcomed him. We thought he was a traveler. We thought — "

"And now you know. Now you know what he is. The question is not what you thought yesterday. The question is what you do today, now that you know. And what you do today, under the Torah, is hand him over."

A third elder.

"My mother — my wife is from the tribe of Binyamin. She has cousins in the clan of Bichri. If we deliver Sheva, my wife will weep for her cousins. Her family will never speak to me again. I will be an outcast in my own house."

The old woman looked at him with a mixture of pity and impatience.

"Your wife will weep. I do not doubt it. Family ties are strong and they are sacred. But your wife would rather weep for a rebel cousin than for her husband and her children and her neighbors and her whole city. Ask her. I know what she will say. She will say: deliver him. Because the alternative is that all of us — you, her, your children, every infant in the cradle, every old man in his bed, every animal in the street — all of us die tomorrow when the wall comes down. That is the alternative. One rebel, or all of Avel. Ask your wife. She will not even hesitate."

The third elder was silent.

A fourth stepped forward — an older man, perhaps a contemporary of the woman herself in age, but clearly her subordinate in authority.

"My mother. You are the oldest among us. You have seen things none of us have seen. If you say the law is clear, we will accept the ruling. But — forgive me — I have to ask. Are you asking us to do this because the law requires it? Or because you are afraid for the city?"

The old woman looked at him for a long moment.

"Both. The law requires it. I am afraid for the city. The two are not in conflict. The law was given to Israel so that Israel would not destroy itself through misplaced loyalties. The law is for the city. Following the law is how the city is saved. Do not pretend there is a gap between the two."

The fourth elder bowed.

"Then we will do what you say."

The other elders nodded, slowly, one after another.

The old woman turned and began to walk out of the square. She stopped at the edge.

"Send men to find him. Do not make a spectacle of it. Do it quickly and quietly. And when it is done, bring the head to me at the wall. I will throw it over myself."

The Tanach does not preserve the names of the elders or the exact words of the internal debate in Avel. The Tanach preserves only the result. But the mefarshim — particularly the Tosefta in Terumot 7:23 and the Yerushalmi in Terumot 8:4 — treat the wise woman's speech in the square as one of the most important halachic discourses in all of Tanach, preserving the underlying principles even though the exact words of the speech itself are not in the text.

* * *

The halachic discussion that later generations would draw out of the scene is worth pausing for.

The Tosefta in Terumot 7:23 formulates the core principle: if a group of Jews is approached by Gentiles who say "give us one of you so that we may kill him, otherwise we will kill all of you," they must all let themselves be killed and not hand over one single soul of Israel. But if the Gentiles specify a particular individual — as in the case of Sheva ben Bichri — he may be handed over so that the others may not be killed.

The Yerushalmi in Terumot 8:4 adds a further layer to the discussion. Two Amoraim debate whether the rule applies even to an innocent individual or only to a guilty one like Sheva. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the rule applies universally: if the enemy specifies a particular person by name, that person can be handed over even if he is completely innocent, because the designation by name is itself the legal trigger. Reish Lakish disagrees: the rule applies only if the specified person is himself deserving of death under Torah law. Under Reish Lakish's view, Sheva ben Bichri was a valid subject for the rule because he was a rebel against the king and therefore already liable to the death penalty — but an innocent person named by an enemy could not be handed over even if refusing meant the destruction of the whole community.

The Rambam in Hilchot Yesodei HaTorah 5:5 rules according to Reish Lakish. The specified person must be independently liable for death. And the Rambam adds a further caveat: ein morin ken — "we do not instruct this." Even in cases where the law technically permits the handing over, a rabbi should not issue the ruling openly, because the ruling is too easily misused. A rabbi should let the community work it out on its own conscience, or refuse to answer the question, rather than give an explicit ruling that would establish a precedent that could be abused by people who wanted a cover for betraying innocent Jews to enemies.

All of this halachic weight is hiding behind the simple narrative verse: "They cut off the head of Sheva ben Bichri and threw it to Yoav." Every word of that verse became the foundation of centuries of rabbinic discussion about when — and under what conditions — a Jewish community is permitted to sacrifice one of its members to save the rest. The wise woman's speech in the square of Avel was the practical precedent on which all later rulings would rest.

* * *

The elders of Avel accepted the ruling.

The story, from here, moves quickly. The Tanach does not describe the details of what happened inside the city. It says only that "they cut off the head of Sheva ben Bichri and threw it to Yoav." The mefarshim fill in the blanks. Some of them assume that the citizens of Avel confronted Sheva directly, told him that they were not going to sacrifice their city for him, and executed him on the spot. Others assume that Sheva tried to fight or flee when he understood what was happening, and that there was a brief struggle in the streets of the city before the citizens caught him. All agree that the execution was swift and public.

The head was brought up to the wall.

It was thrown over the parapet. It landed at the foot of the siege ramp, perhaps thirty feet from where Yoav was standing.

Yoav looked down at the head. He identified it. Sheva ben Bichri — the ish Belial from Binyamin, the man who had blown the shofar at Gilgal, the man who had announced we have no portion in David, the man who had rallied the northern tribes and fled to the walls of Avel — was now a head in the dust at Yoav's feet. The second rebellion was over.

Yoav raised the shofar that hung from his belt.

He blew it once — the long, clear note of a general recalling his army from a completed mission. The men of the camp around him heard the blast and understood. The siege was over. The ramp would not be completed. The walls would not be breached. The city of Avel would not be destroyed. The campaign was done.

Yoav turned to his officers.

"Break camp. We march back to Yerushalayim tomorrow at dawn. Return the siege equipment to the supply train. Pay off the local laborers who were helping with the ramp. Leave the city in peace."

"Yes, my lord."

He looked up at the wall one more time.

The old woman was back at the parapet, watching him.

He raised his hand in a kind of salute — not quite a military salute, because she was not a soldier, but a gesture of acknowledgment. The kind of gesture one leader makes to another after a negotiation has been successfully concluded. He was acknowledging, without quite saying so, that she had saved her city, that she had saved his mission, and that she had saved both of them from a bloody assault that neither of them had wanted.

She nodded back.

She did not wave. She did not smile. She simply nodded — the quiet nod of an old woman who had been saving Israel, in one way or another, for longer than any one of them could quite understand.

Then she turned and walked back down from the wall, and the Tanach does not mention her again.

* * *

The army broke camp the next morning and began the march back to Yerushalayim.

It was a long march. Several hundred miles, across the full length of the northern tribes, back down through the hill country of Binyamin, and home to the city. Yoav rode at the head of the column for the entire march. Avishai rode beside him. The Kreti and the Pelti marched in formation behind them. The men of Yehuda who had been absorbed into the army after Amasa's death marched behind the Kreti and the Pelti, slightly subdued, not quite comfortable with what had happened at the great stone at Giv'on but no longer openly resisting the new chain of command.

Yoav was restored, in practice if not in formal announcement, to his old position as the captain of the host.

David would never formally rescind the dismissal letter. The letter would simply become, over the following weeks, a piece of paper that no one referred to anymore. Amasa was dead. There was no one else to take the job. Yoav was the general of the army because there was no other general of the army and because the king, whatever his private feelings, could not afford to leave the position unfilled during a time when the kingdom was still fragile. When the column reached Yerushalayim, Yoav rode into the city at the head of the army and reported to the palace and gave the king his formal after-action report, and the king accepted the report without comment, and the restoration was complete.

But it was not really complete.

The ledger was still there. In David's mind, the killing of Amasa had been added to the list — the list that included the killing of Avner at Chevron, the killing of Avshalom in the forest of Efrayim, and now the killing of Amasa in the road at Giv'on. Three senior men, three deliberate killings by Yoav, three moments when Yoav had acted on his own judgment in defiance of what David wanted. David would carry the list with him for the rest of his life. He would not act on it while he needed Yoav. But when the time came, on his deathbed, he would whisper the list into the ear of his son Shlomo, and Shlomo would finish the reckoning.

That is all for a later volume.

* * *

The Tanach closes the chapter of Sheva ben Bichri with a list.

It is the list of David's officials — the men who held positions of authority in the kingdom at the moment when the kingdom was finally at peace after the two rebellions. The list is short but dense with meaning:

Yoav was over all the host of Israel.

Benayahu ben Yehoyada was over the Kreti and the Pelti.

Adoram was over the forced labor.

Yehoshafat ben Achilud was the recorder.

Sheva was the scribe.

Tzadok and Evyatar were the priests.

And Ira the Yairi was also a priest to David.

The list is the closing frame of Shmuel Bet chapter 20, and it functions as a kind of restoration of the ordered administration of the kingdom after the chaos of the two rebellions. Every position has a name. Every man is in his proper place. The machine of the kingdom has been reassembled. The king is back in his palace. The army is back under its old general. The Kreti and the Pelti are back under Benayahu. The recorder is recording. The scribe is scribing. The priests are serving in their places. The forced labor — the corvée that would become a source of grievance in the next generation under Shlomo and an explosive political issue in the generation after that — is being supervised by a man named Adoram, who will reappear in Melakhim Aleph 12 at the moment when the kingdom finally splits, and who will be stoned to death by the rebelling northern tribes as the first victim of the secession.

But that, again, is for a later volume.

For now, the kingdom is at peace.

David is in his palace. Yoav is in the field. The ten concubines are in the house of guarding. Shimei ben Gera is in his house in Bachurim with the royal oath of pardon hanging over him like a temporary stay of execution. Mefivoshet is in his fields outside Yerushalayim with his half of the inheritance of Shaul. Barzillai the Gil'adi is back in Roglim waiting to die in his own village. Serach bat Asher is somewhere in the northern tribes, in the last stations of her extraordinary life. Sheva ben Bichri is a head in a dust pile at the foot of a siege ramp that will be abandoned to the wind and rain of Avel Beit Ma'akhah.

And the arc of the Restoration is complete.

* * *

The next volume will turn to a different kind of story.

Up to this point, the book of Shmuel Bet has been a linear narrative — the rise of David, the establishment of Yerushalayim, the sin of Batsheva, the Avshalom arc, the Sheva arc. The narrative has moved forward in time, chapter by chapter, following the events of David's reign in more or less chronological order. Starting in the next volume, the Tanach does something different. It turns back. It begins to fill in the gaps — to tell the stories of David's warriors, the men who had fought beside him from the cave of Adullam through the years in the wilderness through the battles of the kingdom. It tells the stories of the Three and the Thirty. It tells the story of Benayahu ben Yehoyada, the commander of the Kreti and the Pelti, and the lion he killed in a pit on a snowy day, and the Egyptian giant he disarmed with the giant's own spear. It tells the story of the water of Beit Lechem and the three warriors who broke through the Pelishti lines to bring David a drink from the well by the gate of his own childhood village. It tells the story of the sons of Tzeruyah and the deep roots of Yoav's relationship with David. And it circles back to Uriah the Chittite — not as a victim this time, but as a warrior, one of the Thirty, a man whose name is inscribed in the Tanach's register of David's greatest soldiers alongside the names of the men who betrayed him.

These are the stories of the Gibborim.

They are the foundation of everything that happened in the linear narrative. They are the reason David had a kingdom to lose and regain. They are the men who were with him from the beginning, and whose loyalty made the kingdom of Israel possible in the first place.

We turn to them in Volume VI.
