Chapter Two

Sheva ben Bichri

The shofar sounded at Gilgal.

It was the morning after David had crossed the Yarden. The royal procession had camped overnight in the fields outside the old town, in the same strip of ground where Yehoshua had camped four hundred years earlier after the miracle at the Yarden. David was in his tent. The Yehudite delegation was in its own encampment, some distance from the Israelite delegation, the two groups still not speaking to each other after the quarrel of the previous afternoon. The royal guard was posted around the king's tent. The morning was cool. Mist was rising off the river.

Then — from somewhere in the Israelite encampment — a shofar sounded.

It was not a friendly shofar. It was not the shofar of a celebration or a religious ceremony. It was the long, sharp blast that every Israelite ear recognized instantly as a call to arms. The same blast Avshalom had sent through the kingdom six weeks earlier, when his coded signal had run from Chevron to Dan within twenty-four hours. The same blast that had been used to rally Israel to war against the Ammonites under Shaul, against the Pelishtim under Yonatan, against the army of Sisera under Barak. The long war-horn. The teru'ah.

David heard it in his tent.

He came out immediately, half-dressed. Yoav was at the door of the tent before David could raise the question. Yoav had heard it too. Yoav had been sleeping — if a man like Yoav ever slept — with his sword at his hand and one ear on the camp, and he had been on his feet within three seconds of the first blast.

"My lord."

"What is happening?"

"Someone in the Israelite camp has blown the war-horn."

"Against whom?"

"I do not know yet. I am going to find out."

"Go."

Yoav went. David stood at the flap of his tent, watching his general stride across the dew-wet grass toward the Israelite encampment. Around him, the camp was waking up in confusion. Men of the royal guard were running to their weapons. Officers were shouting for information. The Yehudite delegation, in its separate camp, was hearing the shofar with the same alarm as David.

Yoav reached the edge of the Israelite camp. He stopped. The entire Israelite delegation was in motion — not in the ordered motion of a disciplined army but in the chaotic motion of a crowd that had just heard something it was not expecting. Men were running in different directions. Some were gathering weapons. Some were running away from the source of the sound, back toward their villages.

Yoav grabbed the nearest man by the shoulder.

"Who blew the horn?"

"Sheva ben Bichri."

"Who?"

"A man of Binyamin. From the village of Bichri. I do not know him well. I saw him yesterday in the delegation — he was standing at the back during the argument with the Yehudites. He disappeared overnight. He came back at dawn with the shofar."

"What did he say?"

The man hesitated.

"He climbed on a rock. He blew the horn. And then he shouted something to the crowd."

"What did he shout?"

The man looked away. The words were the kind of words a loyal Israelite did not want to repeat in the hearing of the king's general.

"Repeat it, man."

The man swallowed.

"He said: Ein lanu chelek b'David, ve-lo nachalah lanu be-ven Yishai. Ish le-ohalav, Yisrael. We have no portion in David, and no inheritance in the son of Yishai. Every man to his tent, O Israel."

Yoav stared at him.

"Say that again."

"We have no portion in David. No inheritance in the son of Yishai. Every man to his tent, O Israel."

Yoav's face went gray.

* * *

He walked back to David's tent at a fast clip. He did not run — Yoav never ran in public, because a running general signaled panic to the camp — but he walked with the kind of speed that told every soldier who saw him that something serious was happening. By the time he reached David's tent, David was fully dressed, with his outer robe thrown on and his sword at his hip.

"Well?"

"It is not good, my lord."

"Tell me."

"A man of Binyamin has blown the war-horn in the Israelite camp. He is named Sheva ben Bichri. He is from the house of Shaul — or at least from a Benjaminite clan with old grievances against your house. He climbed on a rock in the middle of the camp at first light this morning, blew the horn, and shouted: We have no portion in David, and no inheritance in the son of Yishai. Every man to his tent, O Israel."

David closed his eyes.

He recognized the words.

He recognized them because they were a deliberate echo of the language the ten tribes had used against the house of David in the moment of David's greatest weakness. No portion. No inheritance. To your tents. It was a formal repudiation of his kingship — not an accusation of a particular policy, not a complaint about a particular decision, but a flat rejection of the principle that the house of David had any claim on the loyalty of the northern tribes at all. It was a political statement. And it was a statement that had been waiting to be made for years, perhaps decades, and had now been made at the exact moment when the fracture in the kingdom was at its rawest.

"How many men went with him?"

"I do not know yet. Most of the Israelite camp, I think. The men who had been planning to come the rest of the way to Yerushalayim with us are now breaking camp and going home. Some of them are going back to their villages. Some of them appear to be following Sheva — I saw perhaps two hundred men gathering around him at the rock. More are joining them as they walk."

"Two hundred?"

"Perhaps more. The numbers are growing as the word spreads through the camp."

"And the Yehudite delegation?"

"Still with us, my lord. The Yehudites did not join the walk-out. They are still in their own camp, watching the Israelites leave with very uncomfortable expressions."

David opened his eyes.

"Yoav. Listen to me carefully. Sheva ben Bichri is going to become Avshalom within a week if we do not stop him now. This is not an ordinary grievance. This is a new rebellion. It is being led by a Binyaminite who represents the house of Shaul, and it is going to draw in every man in the northern tribes who has been nursing a grudge against me since I took the throne. If we let him reach a fortified city and raise the full army of the north, we will be fighting a second civil war within the month."

"My lord — I understand."

"I want him stopped. I do not want him captured and brought to me for a trial. I do not want negotiations. I want him dead. Today, if possible. Tomorrow at the latest. This cannot become another Avshalom."

Yoav nodded.

"My lord. I am your general. Give me the order and I will ride out this morning."

David looked at him for a long moment.

The look was painful. Yoav was the right man for the job. Yoav had just defeated Avshalom's army in the forest of Efrayim. Yoav knew how to chase a fugitive across the tribal territories of the north. Yoav would have Sheva's head within a week. There was no soldier in the kingdom better suited for this particular mission.

But David had publicly promised the job to Amasa.

The letter to Yerushalayim had been sent before the shofar blast at Gilgal. The letter had announced, in front of the elders of Yehuda and the High Priests, that Amasa ben Yeter was now the captain of the army in the place of Yoav. The dismissal had been public. The replacement had been public. David could not reverse the decision twenty-four hours later without humiliating himself in front of the kingdom.

And there was another reason. David could not stand to look at Yoav. He could not stand to send the man who had killed Avshalom on another mission that might result in the killing of another troublesome young man. David's grief for Avshalom was still too fresh. He needed distance from Yoav. He needed the new general — the one he had appointed as a gesture of national reconciliation — to prove himself.

David's face tightened.

"No, Yoav. Not you. I am going to send Amasa."

Yoav did not say anything at first. His face did not change. He had been a soldier for too long to let his face show what he was feeling.

"Amasa has not yet arrived, my lord."

"I am aware. He is still in Yerushalayim gathering the men of Yehuda. I will send him a message today. I will tell him to muster the army of Yehuda within three days and pursue Sheva ben Bichri into the northern tribes before Sheva can fortify a city."

"Three days is a long time, my lord."

"Three days is what I am giving him."

"My lord — with respect — by the time Amasa has gathered the army of Yehuda, Sheva will have reached the territory of his own tribe and will be gathering men to him. By the time Amasa marches, Sheva will have a thousand men. By the time Amasa reaches Binyamin, Sheva will have three thousand. The delay favors the rebel."

"I know, Yoav."

"Then let me go now. With the men I have with me. I can ride out of this camp within the hour. I can catch him before he reaches his own territory."

"No."

"My lord — "

"No, Yoav. I have appointed Amasa. Amasa will do this."

Yoav was silent for a long moment.

Then he bowed.

"As my lord commands."

He turned and walked out of the tent.

* * *

He walked very slowly back to his own tent.

He was not angry in the way most men get angry. He was not shouting. He was not throwing things. He was not even muttering under his breath. Yoav's anger, when he was really angry, was a cold and quiet thing that lived in the space behind his eyes and waited for its moment. He had been angry at David for a long time, in a quiet way, about the dismissal letter. He was angrier now. He was angry because the king's new general had not arrived yet, because the king was going to give the rebel a three-day head start, because Sheva ben Bichri was going to become a real threat while Amasa ben Yeter was still fumbling around Yerushalayim trying to recruit men from the tribe that had just thrown its support behind David.

Yoav knew, with the kind of professional certainty that only a lifetime soldier knows, that Amasa was going to fail.

He went into his tent. He sat down on a low stool. He called for his brother Avishai.

Avishai came in. He was Yoav's younger brother, the second of the three sons of Tzeruyah (the third was Asa'el, who had been killed by Avner in the forest of Giv'on a generation earlier — his death was what had started the long blood-feud between Yoav and Avner that had ended in the gateway of Chevron with Avner's own death). Avishai was a shorter, quieter, more religious version of his brother — a man who had once asked David for permission to kill Shaul in the cave at Ein Gedi, a man who had wanted to kill Shimei at the Yarden during the flight, a man whose instinct in any difficult situation was to draw a sword and end the problem.

"Yoav. What is happening?"

"Sheva ben Bichri has blown the war-horn. A second rebellion has started."

"Who is he?"

"A man of Binyamin. An ish Belial. No one of particular importance on his own, but he has chosen the right moment to strike. He is going to become a real threat within a week."

"Are we going after him?"

"We are not. The king has given the mission to Amasa."

Avishai's face changed.

"Amasa? Avshalom's general? The man we just defeated in the forest of Efrayim?"

"That is the man the king wants."

"Yoav — that is insane. Amasa cannot command the army of Yehuda. The army of Yehuda was on the other side of the field from him six weeks ago. They will not follow him. They will wait for three days out of obedience to the king's appointment, and then they will go home. Amasa will stand in the courtyard of the palace with five old men and no real fighting force. Meanwhile Sheva will be raising the tribes."

"I know."

"The king has to be told."

"The king has been told. I have just told him. He is aware. He does not care. He has dismissed me and he has appointed Amasa and he is going to watch it play out the way it plays out."

"Yoav. The king loves you. He has loved you for thirty years. Why is he doing this?"

Yoav looked at his brother.

"Because I killed Avshalom."

Avishai nodded slowly.

"The king is punishing you for that."

"The king is punishing me for saving his throne. Yes."

There was a long silence.

"So what do we do?"

Yoav stood up. He walked to the flap of the tent and looked out at the camp. The Israelite delegation was gone now — the tents were down, the ground was bare, the footprints in the dew led east and north toward the hill country. Sheva ben Bichri was out there somewhere, walking toward his own village with his shofar and his two hundred men, gathering more as he went.

"We wait," Yoav said. "We march to Yerushalayim with the king. We let Amasa have his three days. And when Amasa fails — and he will fail — we will be ready."

"Ready for what?"

Yoav turned around.

"Ready to finish the job."

* * *

The royal procession left Gilgal at mid-morning.

The mood of the march was different from what it had been the day before. The day before had been the ceremonial homecoming of a king — the crowds, the psalms, the welcome from the elders of Yehuda. This morning was the anxious march of a king whose kingdom had just begun to split again before his eyes. The Yehudite delegation walked in silence. The royal guard walked with weapons drawn. David walked in the middle of the procession, saying almost nothing, his face set in the expression of a man who was mentally calculating how much time he had before a second rebellion became unmanageable.

They reached Yerushalayim three days later.

David walked through the Gihon gate of his own city for the first time in almost two months. The crowds were smaller than they should have been for a returning king — many of the people who would have come out to welcome him had already gone home after Gilgal, and the rumor of the new rebellion had spread to the city before the procession arrived, dampening the celebration. David walked through the streets of Yerushalayim without smiling, without waving, without stopping to greet the people who had come out to see him. He walked directly to the palace.

He went into the palace.

He went into the inner chambers.

He found the ten concubines who had been left behind during the flight — the ten women whom Avshalom had taken on the roof in the public fulfillment of Natan's prophecy from many years earlier. We have already told this story in the previous volume. The Tanach recounts the scene with terrible compression: "And the king took the ten women, the concubines whom he had left to keep the house, and put them in a house of guarding, and provided for their needs, but he did not go in to them. So they were shut up unto the day of their death, living in widowhood." They had been violated on the roof. They could not return to the king's bed. So David provided for them — fed them, housed them, kept them safe — but never went in to them again. They lived in the palace as widows even though their husband was the king and the king was alive.

The Tanach inserts this detail here — not at the moment of Avshalom's act of violation, but at the moment of David's return — because the Tanach wants the reader to feel the weight of what David was walking into when he re-entered his own palace. He was not walking back into a happy household. He was walking back into a house that had been desecrated in his absence, that now contained ten women whose lives had been ruined by the political calculations of his dead son, and that would be marked by their quiet, unbreakable grief for the rest of David's reign.

He settled them into the house of guarding. He gave orders that they were to be provided for. And then he turned his mind to the problem of Sheva ben Bichri.

* * *

He summoned Amasa.

The summons was formal. Amasa was in Yerushalayim already — he had arrived a few days before David, and had been waiting in the officers' quarters for his formal installation as the captain of the army. He came into David's audience chamber in a plain military tunic, without ceremony, bowed before the king, and waited.

David wasted no time.

"Amasa."

"My lord."

"You know about Sheva ben Bichri?"

"I know, my lord. I heard the report on the road."

"Good. Then I will not need to explain the situation. Here is what I want from you. I want you to muster the army of Yehuda within three days. Every fighting man of military age in every town from Chevron to Be'er Sheva. I want them assembled here in Yerushalayim three days from now. I want you at their head. And then I want you to march north through the hill country of Binyamin and find Sheva ben Bichri and end the rebellion before it grows."

Amasa did not answer immediately.

"My lord — three days is very short."

"I know it is short."

"The roads to the southern towns are long. Some of the villages are two days' ride from Yerushalayim. A man who rides out today with the summons cannot reach the furthest towns before tomorrow night. The men of those towns cannot be assembled here before the day after that. Three days from this morning is not — "

"Three days from this morning is the order, Amasa."

Amasa stopped talking.

"My lord. I hear the king's command."

"Good. I want you to go. Now. Take as many riders as you need. Send them to every town. Make it clear that the king himself is commanding this muster. Make it clear that any man who does not answer the summons will be considered a rebel against the throne. Make it clear that the matter is urgent. I do not want any delays. I do not want any excuses. I do not want anyone dragging his feet. Do you understand?"

"I understand, my lord."

"Go, then. Go now."

Amasa bowed.

He left the audience chamber. He went immediately to the stables. He assembled a team of riders. He gave each of them a sealed message bearing the royal seal. He dispatched them to the towns of Yehuda. Within the hour, there were thirty men on horses riding south from the gates of Yerushalayim with the king's summons.

Amasa rode south himself.

* * *

Three days passed.

On the morning of the third day, David sat in the audience chamber of the palace, waiting. The hour of the muster came. He waited. The hour of the muster passed. He waited longer. By midday, he was growing uneasy. By mid-afternoon, he was sending runners out of the gates of Yerushalayim to look for the returning army. By evening, he understood that the army was not coming.

Amasa was late.

The reason for Amasa's lateness is one of the most discussed questions in the entire sugya of Shmuel Bet 20. The Tanach itself says only "and he tarried longer than the time appointed to him." The Talmud in Sanhedrin 49a and the Me'iri to that sugya preserve a tradition that has become the standard rabbinic reading of the delay.

According to the Me'iri and the Sanhedrin tradition, Amasa rode to the towns of Yehuda to muster the fighting men — and when he arrived at the first major town, he found the men of the town engaged in the public study of Torah. The study was not a small private gathering. It was the full public beit midrash of the town, with the senior scholars leading and the younger men listening, and the subject of the study was one of the weightier halachic questions of the day. The study was in full swing. The men of the town were absorbed in the arguments. The sound of Torah was rising from the courtyard of the study house into the midday air.

Amasa stopped outside the courtyard.

He stood there, listening.

He was carrying the royal summons. He had been commanded by the king to muster the army within three days. The men in the courtyard were exactly the men he had been sent to muster. All he had to do was walk into the courtyard, interrupt the study, read the summons aloud, and tell the men that the king required their immediate mobilization.

He did not interrupt.

He stood outside the courtyard for a long time. He listened to the arguments. He waited for a natural pause in the study. When the pause came, he still did not interrupt. He waited for the next pause. He waited for the next. The Torah study went on.

A senior officer traveling with Amasa came up to him.

"My lord — we should go in. The king's command — "

"I know the king's command."

"Then — "

"I will not interrupt the study of Torah."

The officer stared at him.

"My lord. The king has ordered — "

"The king has ordered me to muster the army. I will muster the army. But I will not muster it by walking into a public beit midrash and tearing men away from their Torah. I will wait until they finish the session. If the session continues past the hour of the muster, I will wait until the next break. If they continue all day, I will wait all day."

"But my lord — "

"Did you hear me? I will wait."

The officer bowed and stepped back.

Amasa stood outside the courtyard of the study house for the rest of the afternoon. When the session finally ended and the men began to disperse, he walked in and delivered the summons. Most of the men responded with enthusiasm. Some of them went immediately to gather their weapons. But the process took time. It was almost evening by the time Amasa had the first town mustered and ready to march. And he still had to ride to the next town, and the next, and the next.

He was going to be late.

He knew he was going to be late. He also knew, with the kind of moral clarity that some men in some moments simply have, that the Torah study was worth the lateness. The men who were learning Torah in that courtyard were doing something that was more important, in the final accounting of the kingdom, than any three-day military muster. The Torah was what made the kingdom of Israel the kingdom of Israel. Without the Torah there would be no reason for the kingdom to exist. A king who would order an army to be mustered by interrupting a public study session was a king who had forgotten what the kingdom was for. Amasa would not be the instrument of that kind of forgetting. He would deliver the army. But he would deliver it a day late.

The Me'iri treats this decision as one of the most admirable moments in Amasa's short career. The man had failed as a general in the forest of Efrayim. The man had chosen the wrong side in the rebellion. The man had been an enemy of David for the previous year. But in this moment — outside the courtyard of the beit midrash, with the king's summons in his hand and the sound of Torah in his ears — Amasa made the one decision that a righteous man should make and that a lesser man would not have made. He honored the Torah over the king.

It was the decision that would cost him his life.

* * *

Because Yoav was not going to see it that way.

Yoav was a soldier. Yoav had spent his entire adult life in the service of kings. Yoav understood military orders the way a blacksmith understood iron — as a physical reality that had to be shaped in a certain way on a certain timeline and that did not bend for any reason whatsoever. A man who failed to muster an army on the appointed day was, in Yoav's operational vocabulary, a man who had failed his king. A man who failed his king in the middle of a live rebellion was, in Yoav's operational vocabulary, a man who was helping the rebellion. A man who was helping the rebellion was, in Yoav's operational vocabulary, a rebel himself.

Yoav was going to conclude, when he heard about the delay, that Amasa was a mored b'malchus — a rebel against the kingship. The term is a technical one in halachic literature. It refers to a person who openly defies a royal command in a time of war. A mored b'malchus can be killed on the spot by any authorized officer of the king, without trial, without witnesses, without warning. The rule exists because in wartime a king cannot afford to run a full trial for every soldier who refuses an order — by the time the trial is over, the war has been lost.

Yoav was going to invoke this rule against Amasa.

He was going to invoke it without consulting the king. He was going to invoke it without consulting any rabbinic court. He was going to invoke it on his own authority as a general of the army, and he was going to execute the judgment himself, and he was going to do it in a way that was so morally ambiguous that even the rabbis of the Talmud, reading the text a thousand years later, would not be able to decide whether Yoav had been justified.

And the rabbis would have one more thing to say about it. They would say that Yoav was wrong — not because Amasa did not technically qualify as a mored b'malchus under the narrowest reading of the rule, but because the rule was never meant to cover a man who had been delayed by the study of Torah. The study of Torah was itself a service to the king, because the king of Israel existed to protect the Torah. A man who interrupted the king's command because he was honoring the Torah was not a rebel against the king. He was the king's deepest kind of loyal servant. Yoav, in killing Amasa, had misread the situation. He had applied a technical rule without seeing the deeper reality the rule was meant to protect.

This is the reading of the Me'iri. It is not the only reading. Other mefarshim are kinder to Yoav. Some of them argue that Yoav genuinely believed Amasa was a rebel and was acting in good faith according to the best information he had. Some argue that Yoav was motivated primarily by the personal injury of having been dismissed by David and was using the mored b'malchus rule as a cover for what was really a revenge killing. Some argue that both motivations were in play at once — that Yoav was genuinely convinced Amasa was a rebel and was happy to have a justification for killing the man who had taken his job.

All of these readings will matter. They will all matter especially later, when Shlomo puts Yoav on trial at the end of David's life, and the question of the killing of Amasa becomes one of the three charges against him.

But we are getting ahead of ourselves.

On the morning of the third day, in Yerushalayim, David was sitting in the audience chamber, waiting for an army that was not coming. And when the army did not come, he made the decision that set the rest of the story in motion.

* * *

He summoned Avishai.

"Avishai. Amasa is late. I cannot wait any longer. Sheva ben Bichri has had three days to raise his men. Every hour we sit here is another hour he is closer to becoming a second Avshalom. I am not going to let another son of Israel die in a civil war because I was too proud to change my orders."

"My lord."

"I want you to take your brother's men — Yoav's personal guard, the Kreti and the Pelti, and every mighty man who is currently in Yerushalayim — and pursue Sheva ben Bichri immediately. Today. This hour. Do not wait for Amasa. Do not wait for the army of Yehuda. Do not wait for anything. Ride out of the city at once and do not stop until you have him."

"My lord — you are sending me? Not Yoav?"

David's face was tight.

"I am sending you."

The Tanach records the order explicitly. "And David said to Avishai: Sheva ben Bichri will do us more harm than Avshalom did. Take your lord's servants and pursue him, lest he get himself fortified cities and escape out of our sight."

The phrase your lord's servants is ambiguous. It could mean the servants of David (your lord = David). It could also mean the servants of Yoav (your lord = Avishai's brother, who was still technically Avishai's superior in the military hierarchy even after the dismissal letter). The Radak reads it the second way. The order was essentially: Take Yoav's men and go. David was bypassing Yoav personally by giving the order to Avishai, but he was still relying on Yoav's men to carry out the mission. It was a strange, triangulated command structure — David was refusing to send Yoav but was also refusing to send anyone except Yoav's own soldiers.

The mefarshim read the strangeness as evidence of David's continuing inability to resolve his feelings about Yoav. He could not bring himself to speak to Yoav directly. He could not bring himself to formally restore Yoav's command. But he also could not pretend that the mission could be accomplished by anyone other than Yoav's own forces. So he gave the order to Avishai, and Avishai — who understood the politics perfectly — went back to his brother's tent within the hour and told Yoav what had happened.

"The king has told me to pursue Sheva ben Bichri. He has told me to take your men."

"My men."

"Your men. The Kreti, the Pelti, the personal guard. He has told me to ride out today."

"And Amasa?"

"Amasa is missing. No one knows where he is. The muster failed."

"Does the king still want Amasa as his general?"

"I do not think the king knows what he wants at this moment. I think the king wants the rebellion over, and he is willing to temporarily use whoever can get the rebellion ended, and once it is ended he will go back to worrying about which general has which title."

Yoav nodded.

"Then I am coming with you."

Avishai blinked.

"The king did not order you to come."

"The king did not order me not to come. The king ordered you to take my men. I am the commander of those men. I am not letting my men ride out of the gates of Yerushalayim into a live military operation without me at the front. If the king objects, he can have me killed when I return. But I am going with you."

"Yoav — "

"I am going, Avishai. Prepare the men. We ride within the hour."

* * *

The men rode out of the gates of Yerushalayim in the early afternoon.

They rode hard. They rode in full armor, with their swords and spears and shields, in the disciplined formation of a professional military unit. Yoav rode at the head — not Avishai, whatever the king's formal order had been. Every man in the column knew who the real commander was. Every man had served under Yoav for years. No one was going to pretend otherwise.

They were riding toward the great stone at Giv'on.

Giv'on was the town where Yoav had fought Avner forty years earlier in the first civil war of the book, the town where Avner had killed Asa'el with the back of his spear at the fifth rib, the town that was the ancient heart of the long blood-feud between the house of Yoav and the house of Shaul. It was fitting, in a way that only the Tanach's architectural sense of place can make fitting, that the next chapter of the story should take place on the same ground.

And it was fitting, in a way that made the mefarshim weep when they read the chapter, that the man Yoav was about to meet at the great stone at Giv'on was going to be Amasa ben Yeter — who had finally mustered the men of Yehuda, who was marching north to join the pursuit, who did not yet know that Yoav had already been released to ride out with the king's forces, and who was walking into the worst ambush of his life without having the slightest idea that an ambush was waiting for him.

Because the ambush was not going to be laid by Sheva ben Bichri.

The ambush was going to be laid by Yoav ben Tzeruyah, in the open road, in front of dozens of witnesses, with a sword that slipped from its sheath at exactly the wrong moment.

* * *
