Chapter Three

The Great Stone at Giv’on

Amasa was marching north.

He had finally mustered the men of Yehuda — a day later than the king had ordered, but with a full and willing force. The men had been ready to fight. The delay at the beit midrash had cost him a day, but once the Torah session had ended and he had delivered the royal summons, the response had been immediate. The men of Yehuda had gone home, gathered their weapons, kissed their wives, and reported to the muster point within twelve hours. By the morning of the fourth day, Amasa was at the head of an army of perhaps two thousand men, marching north from Yerushalayim on the road that ran through the hills of Binyamin toward the northern tribes.

He did not know that Yoav was already on the road.

He did not know that Avishai had been given the king's order the previous day, that Yoav had insisted on riding out with his men, that Yoav had left Yerushalayim by a different road, that Yoav was now somewhere in the same hill country with the Kreti and the Pelti and the mighty men of David's personal guard — perhaps three hundred elite professional soldiers — and that these two columns of David's army were approaching each other from different directions.

He also did not know that his appointment as commander of the army, the appointment David had announced by letter three weeks earlier, was now in a state of political limbo. David had not formally rescinded it. David had also not formally confirmed it. The dismissal letter about Yoav had been sent. The appointment letter about Amasa had been received. But the actual chain of command had been short-circuited by the king's impatient decision to send Avishai with Yoav's men, and Amasa — marching north with the Yehudite army, confident that he was the supreme commander of David's forces in the field — was about to meet his technical subordinate, who had never accepted the dismissal, and who was now approaching from the east with his own column and his own plans.

They met at the great stone at Giv'on.

* * *

The great stone was a well-known landmark on the northern road out of Yerushalayim. It was a large outcropping of pale limestone that stood beside the road about half a mile north of the old town of Giv'on, where David had years earlier fought one of the battles of his rise and where Shlomo would later have his famous dream. The stone was too large to move and too old for anyone to remember who had first placed it there — if anyone had placed it at all; it may have been a natural formation that had become a landmark over generations of use. Travelers used it as a meeting point. Shepherds used it as a resting place. Armies used it as an assembly area.

On the morning in question, Yoav's column had halted at the great stone for a brief rest. The men were drinking water from the spring that ran near the foot of the stone. The horses were being watered. Yoav himself was standing in the shadow of the stone with Avishai beside him, conferring in low voices about the route they would take into the hills of Binyamin.

Then the lookout called from the higher ground.

"My lord. A column approaching from the south."

Yoav turned.

"How many?"

"A large force, my lord. At least a thousand men. Perhaps two thousand. Marching in formation under the banner of the tribe of Yehuda."

"Whose banner?"

The lookout squinted into the distance.

"I cannot yet see the commander's banner clearly. It is — it is the banner of the captain of the host, my lord. There is a man at the head of the column. A tall man in the armor of a general."

Yoav's face did not change.

"Amasa."

Avishai looked at him.

"Amasa is coming up the same road?"

"Apparently."

"Then — then the muster succeeded. He is bringing the army of Yehuda north. He is here to join the pursuit of Sheva ben Bichri."

"Apparently."

"Yoav — "

Yoav turned and began walking toward his own horse, which was tethered near the base of the stone. His pace was unhurried but purposeful. He stopped beside the horse and began to check his armor — tightening the straps of his breastplate, adjusting the position of his helmet, running his hand down the length of his sword belt.

The sword belt was the thing he was most carefully adjusting.

His sword was the long, heavy, straight sword of an Israelite officer of the period — about three feet of sharpened iron in a hard leather sheath. The sheath was strapped to his belt on the left side, the way every right-handed soldier carried his sword. But Yoav was not wearing the belt in the usual way this morning. He had put the belt on in a particular way — with the sheath lying against the front of his body rather than hanging down at his hip, the buckle slightly off-center, the angle of the sheath tilted slightly downward. It was a way of wearing a sword belt that a casual observer would not have noticed but that a trained soldier might have registered as odd.

Yoav checked the buckle. He loosened it by one notch. He tightened it by half a notch. He stepped back and shook his hips gently to see how the sheath would swing against his body when he walked. The sheath swung freely. The sword inside it — because the sheath had been oiled that morning, and because the sword was smooth and polished, and because the angle of the sheath meant that gravity was working with the sword's desire to slip out rather than against it — moved inside the sheath more freely than it should have moved.

If Yoav were to walk a certain way, the sword would slip out of the sheath by itself.

If Yoav were to bend forward at the waist, even slightly, the sword would come all the way out and fall to the ground.

If Yoav were to be standing near another man when this happened, that other man might reasonably assume that the falling sword was an accident, and that Yoav would have both hands occupied with picking it up — one to bend down and lift the sword from the dust, and the other presumably balancing his body.

That other man might relax. He might lower his own guard. He might let his own sword-hand rest at his side. He might, for half a second — half a second is all it would take — forget that the general in front of him was the most dangerous professional killer in the kingdom of Israel.

Avishai watched his brother adjust the belt.

He did not say anything. He had been his brother's shadow for forty years. He had seen Yoav rig a belt in this particular way exactly once before, many years earlier, on the day Yoav had killed Avner in the gateway of Chevron. Avishai did not need the rigging explained to him. He understood, without a word being spoken, what was about to happen at the great stone at Giv'on.

He turned away.

He walked toward the edge of the encampment and made himself busy with something else.

* * *

The Yehudite column reached the great stone.

Amasa was at the head, riding a pale mare, wearing the full armor of a commander of the host. His face was sunburned from three days on the road. He had been riding hard, and he had been thinking the whole way about how to present himself when he arrived in Yerushalayim. He had been practicing, in his head, the formal report he would give to David — explaining the delay, explaining the Torah study, explaining that the army had been successfully mustered despite the delay, explaining that he was ready to lead them north immediately. He had been expecting to arrive at the palace, deliver the report, receive the king's blessing, and ride out of the city with the full authority of the captain of the host.

Instead, half a mile north of Giv'on, at the great stone, he saw a column of three hundred men in the distance — a column that was clearly also part of David's army, a column that was clearly already on the way to intercept Sheva ben Bichri.

He did not understand at first.

He pulled up his mare. He raised his hand to halt the Yehudite column behind him. He stared at the men at the great stone, trying to identify them. They were wearing the uniforms of the Kreti and the Pelti, David's personal guard. Their banner was the banner of the mighty men. And at the front of their column, in the shadow of the stone, was a figure in the armor of a general — a man whose posture and height and carriage Amasa recognized instantly, even from this distance, as unmistakable.

"Yoav."

The name came out of Amasa's mouth as a single quiet syllable. He had been expecting to meet many people in Yerushalayim. He had not been expecting to meet Yoav ben Tzeruyah in the middle of the northern road with three hundred elite soldiers and no orders from the king to be there.

He turned to his second in command.

"Wait here. Keep the column in formation. I am going to ride forward and find out what Yoav is doing in the field."

"My lord — should not I come with you? With a guard?"

"No. Wait here."

"My lord — Yoav was dismissed by the king. His presence here with three hundred men is not — it is not explained. Something is wrong. Let me ride with you."

"I said no. Keep the column in formation. If I am gone longer than a quarter of an hour, send a rider after me. Otherwise, wait."

"Yes, my lord."

He urged his mare forward.

He rode slowly toward the great stone, alone, leaving his own column behind. He could see Yoav more clearly as he approached — Yoav standing in the shadow of the stone, watching him come, making no attempt to ride out to meet him. Yoav's posture was casual. Yoav's hands were loose at his sides. Yoav's sword was in its sheath at his hip, where a sword was supposed to be. Nothing about Yoav's appearance suggested any kind of threat.

Amasa reached the base of the great stone. He dismounted. He walked the last ten paces on foot, leading his mare by the reins.

Yoav walked toward him.

The two men met about five paces from the stone, in full view of their respective columns and of the dozens of soldiers who were now watching the encounter with the mild curiosity of men who had been expecting to see their generals greet each other.

Yoav opened his arms slightly — the gesture of an older man greeting a younger man, or of a senior officer welcoming a junior one who had come up in the ranks. His face was set in a small, friendly, professional smile. He was the picture of a veteran soldier meeting a colleague in the field.

"Ha-shalom atah achi?"

"Is it well with you, my brother?"

It was the standard greeting of Israelite officers in the field. It was the greeting Amasa would have expected from a senior commander. It was the greeting of peace.

Amasa smiled back.

"Shalom, Yoav. I did not know you would be on the road."

"The king sent us ahead."

"Sent you? Under whose command?"

"Avishai has been given command of the Kreti and the Pelti. I came along — I still have men under my care, and I did not want to let them ride out without me. The king understood."

"I see."

"The muster in Yehuda — it went well?"

"It went. I was delayed. I will explain to the king when we return."

"Of course. Of course. A delay in the muster — what caused it? I would like to know, Amasa, for the after-action report I will be writing. What held up the Yehudite army?"

"A — a matter of timing. The men of some of the towns were occupied with a matter I did not wish to interrupt. It took an extra day. The army is here now. We are ready to march."

"Torah study?"

Amasa looked at him.

"How did you know?"

"I guessed. The Yehudite towns are full of men who put Torah before almost anything else. It is the only kind of delay that would hold up a man like you. You are a fair man, Amasa. You would not interrupt the beit midrash even if the king himself were waiting at the gate."

"It was — yes. It was Torah study. I waited until the session ended."

"Of course you did."

Yoav took another step closer. He was now close enough to reach out and touch Amasa. His right hand was still loose at his side. His left hand was slightly extended, as if he were about to take Amasa's arm in the standard military embrace — the embrace of two officers who had known each other for years and were meeting in the field.

Amasa, reading the gesture the way any Israelite officer would read it, stepped forward to accept the embrace.

He did not guard himself.

He did not look at Yoav's sword.

He was too relaxed. He had been keyed up all morning expecting a difficult conversation in the palace at Yerushalayim, and now, instead of the difficult conversation, he was having a casual field reunion with a colleague, and his body was relaxing into the reunion the way any tired soldier's body would relax in the moment of receiving a friendly greeting from a fellow professional.

And that was when Yoav's sword slipped.

* * *

The Tanach records the moment in a single sentence. "And Amasa took no heed of the sword that was in Yoav's hand."

The sword had slipped out of its sheath at exactly the calibrated moment — as Yoav took the step toward Amasa, the angle of Yoav's hip changed, the oil on the sheath did its work, and the polished iron slid free. It fell toward the ground. Yoav, who had been waiting for exactly this moment, bent forward at the waist in a single fluid motion — not so quickly that it would look unnatural, but quickly enough that his hand could intercept the sword before it hit the dirt.

The sword landed in Yoav's right hand.

He did not pause. He did not straighten up. He continued the motion of his body — the bend at the waist turning into a half-step forward, the right hand coming up from below with the sword now gripped in the proper position, the left hand reaching out to grasp the beard of the man standing in front of him.

His left hand caught Amasa's beard.

His right hand — with the sword now gripped blade-forward — came up under Amasa's breastplate, finding the soft unarmored gap at the base of the sternum where the breastplate met the leather skirt.

The sword went in.

It went in hard. It went in to the hilt. Yoav had put his full body weight behind the thrust. The blade passed through Amasa's stomach and out the other side and continued some inches into the air behind him.

Amasa's face changed. His eyes opened wide. His mouth opened but no sound came out. His hands, which had been rising to return the embrace, froze in the air in front of him. He tried to speak. He tried to say Yoav's name, or to ask the question that was forming in his mind, or simply to make the sound that a man makes when he realizes that the man he has just greeted as a brother has killed him.

Nothing came out.

Yoav pulled the sword back out of the wound. It came out wet. Amasa's intestines followed it. They spilled out of the opening in the armor in a dark, glistening mass that fell to the ground at the feet of both men.

The Tanach says that Yoav did not need to strike a second time. "And he did not smite him again, and he died." The single thrust had been perfect. The blade had found the place ba-makom she-marah ve-chaved teluyin bo — "the place where the gallbladder and the liver are suspended" — the exact vulnerable spot that an experienced soldier knew was the quickest way to kill a man in full armor. Yoav had killed Avner in the same place, in the gateway of Chevron, forty years earlier. He had been aiming for the same spot. He had hit the same spot. He had used the same kind of quiet, brutal, professional efficiency.

Amasa fell to his knees.

He did not quite fall over at first. He knelt in the road, with his hands pressed against the opening in his armor where his intestines had just come out, looking up at Yoav with an expression that was not anger and not fear but a kind of slow, uncomprehending disbelief.

"Achi — "

The word came out of his mouth as a wet whisper. My brother. The same word Yoav had used to greet him thirty seconds earlier. The word of Israelite officers in the field.

Yoav was already walking away.

He did not look back at Amasa. He walked past him — past the man still kneeling in the road, past the dark mass spilling onto the dust, past the pale mare that was standing a few feet away watching its rider die — and toward his own horse. He wiped the sword on a piece of cloth he kept folded in his belt for exactly this purpose. He returned the sword to its sheath. He put his foot in the stirrup. He mounted his horse. He turned the horse back up the northern road.

His voice, when he finally spoke, was loud enough to carry to the men of both columns at once.

"Whoever is for Yoav and whoever is for David — follow Yoav after Sheva ben Bichri!"

It was a single, calculated sentence. It was addressed to the men of the Yehudite column — the men who had just lost their commander. It was giving them a choice. Either they could stand in the road and mourn Amasa ben Yeter, in which case the rebellion of Sheva ben Bichri would grow and the kingdom would be in danger. Or they could follow Yoav — the general they all knew, the general who had been leading David's armies for thirty years — and join the pursuit.

Yoav was not giving them much time to choose.

He kicked his horse into a trot. The Kreti and the Pelti fell into formation behind him. Avishai, who had been waiting at the edge of the encampment with a look of deliberate non-involvement on his face, mounted his own horse and joined the column. Within two minutes, the full force of David's personal guard was moving up the road at a steady pace, with Yoav at the head.

The Yehudite column stood in the road, uncertain.

* * *

Amasa was still alive.

He had fallen onto his side. His hands were pressed against the wound. His intestines were spilling out onto the dust of the road. His eyes were open. He was making small, wordless sounds. He was not quite dead, but he was no longer able to stand, and he was not going to survive the next hour.

The Yehudite soldiers gathered around him.

They did not know what to do. Their commander was dying at their feet, in the middle of the northern road, killed by the general of the very army they had been marching to join. They had no orders. They had no replacement commander. The man who would have given them orders was the man on the ground in front of them, and he was no longer able to speak.

They stood in a circle around him and watched him die.

One of Yoav's young men — a junior officer, probably about twenty years old, who had been left at the back of Yoav's column with instructions from Yoav himself — now walked forward through the stunned Yehudite soldiers and stood over Amasa's body. He raised his voice.

"Whoever desires Yoav, and whoever is for David — after Yoav!"

The young man was repeating Yoav's own words, louder, more clearly, directly into the faces of the Yehudite soldiers. He was not asking them a question. He was not giving them a choice. He was telling them, in the name of the most senior officer in the kingdom, that the time for mourning was over and the time for marching was now. The war was not going to wait for a funeral.

The Yehudite soldiers did not move.

They kept looking at Amasa.

He was still, in that moment, still alive. His chest was still rising and falling. His eyes were still open. He was still the commander they had been following for the past four days, still the man they had been willing to fight for, still the man who had been the king's appointed captain of the host. They could not simply step over his body and march away.

The young officer saw that the crowd was not responding. He understood, with the quick operational judgment that Yoav had specifically chosen him for, that the problem was the body. As long as Amasa was lying in the middle of the road in full view of every passing soldier, the column would not move. Men would stop. Men would gather. Men would ask questions. The march would stall.

He bent down and grabbed Amasa under the arms.

"What are you doing?" one of the Yehudite soldiers shouted.

The young officer did not answer. He dragged Amasa backwards across the road, toward the verge, and into the field beside it. Amasa was still making small sounds. His legs were dragging in the dust. His intestines, which had been spilling onto the road, now trailed behind him in a grotesque dark line as he was pulled across the ground.

The young officer dragged him perhaps twenty paces off the road, into the low grass of the field. He set him down. He pulled off his own outer garment — a long military cloak of plain brown wool — and spread it over Amasa's body. The cloak covered the face, the torso, the still-moving hands. Only the feet were still visible, sticking out from under the cloth.

Then the young officer stood up and walked back to the road.

He raised his voice again.

"He is out of the road. Whoever is for Yoav and for David — after Yoav!"

This time the Yehudite soldiers moved.

The body being out of sight made all the difference. The men had been unable to march past their commander. Now their commander was not in the road anymore. He was in a field. A cloak was over him. He had been moved out of the way. The reason for standing still had been removed.

The Yehudite column began to march.

Slowly at first. Then more quickly. They fell into formation behind the Kreti and the Pelti. They moved up the northern road in pursuit of Yoav. Within five minutes, the great stone at Giv'on was empty except for a pale mare grazing in the grass, a dark stain on the road, and a body in a field with a cloak over it.

The Tanach records this moment with one of its most devastating details. "When he was removed out of the road, all the people passed on after Yoav, to pursue Sheva the son of Bichri." The verb is vayaavor — "they passed on." They passed over Amasa. They moved on from Amasa. They left Amasa. The man who had been appointed commander of the army of Israel four days earlier, the man who had been promised the command "in the place of Yoav" in a letter bearing the royal seal, the man who had ridden north with two thousand soldiers of Yehuda expecting to lead the pursuit of Sheva ben Bichri — was now a corpse under a cloak in a field beside the northern road, with the army he had just mustered marching past him in obedience to the very man he had been sent to replace.

* * *

The mefarshim have spent a lot of ink on this scene.

The first question is why Yoav did it. The second question is whether he was justified. The third question is what Amasa had actually done wrong, and whether his delay at the beit midrash really constituted a capital offense. The fourth question is whether David, back in Yerushalayim, was going to hold Yoav accountable when the news reached him. And the fifth question is whether the entire killing was a calculated political maneuver by Yoav to reclaim the command he had been dismissed from.

Let us take them in order.

The first question — why — has an obvious surface answer and a less obvious deeper answer. The surface answer is that Yoav wanted his job back. Amasa had been appointed as the new commander of the army. As long as Amasa was alive and functioning in the role, Yoav was a retired general with no command and no future. With Amasa dead, the command would fall, by default, to the next most senior officer in the field — which was Yoav himself, or his brother Avishai, or (most likely) Yoav through Avishai's nominal authority. Killing Amasa was the simplest possible way for Yoav to recover the position he had been dismissed from.

The deeper answer is more complicated. Yoav was not only motivated by career concerns. He was also motivated by what he genuinely believed was a military necessity. In Yoav's reading of the situation, the kingdom could not afford to have an incompetent general in command of the army while a live rebellion was spreading in the north. Amasa had already failed by being late to the muster. Every hour Amasa continued in command was an hour the rebellion grew. Yoav was convinced, with the kind of operational certainty that his entire career had reinforced, that the kingdom needed him in command of the army, and that the only way to get himself back into command was to remove Amasa from the picture. The killing was, in his own mind, a service to the king and to the kingdom — even if the king had explicitly not ordered it.

The second question — whether he was justified — has no consensus answer. Some mefarshim, including the Me'iri, say flatly that Yoav was wrong. The Me'iri's argument is that Amasa was not a mored b'malchus because his delay was caused by the honoring of Torah study, which is itself a service to the king. Under this reading, Yoav was a murderer who killed an innocent man and used a legal technicality as a cover. Other mefarshim are more sympathetic to Yoav. They argue that Yoav did not know about the Torah study, that Yoav was acting in good faith on the information available to him, and that the delay itself — whatever its cause — genuinely did threaten the kingdom. Under this reading, Yoav made a tragic mistake but was not morally culpable in the full sense. And a third group of mefarshim argue that Yoav was partially right and partially wrong — that he had a genuine military justification but also a personal motive, and that the mixture of motives makes it impossible to judge the act cleanly.

The third question — what Amasa had actually done wrong — is the one the Talmud in Sanhedrin 49a wrestles with most directly. The Gemara asks whether a general who delays the mustering of an army can be killed as a rebel. The answer depends on exactly what kind of delay. A delay caused by laziness or cowardice would qualify. A delay caused by a mechanical problem with the logistics of the muster would not qualify. A delay caused by honoring a higher value — such as the study of Torah — is a contested case. The Gemara does not resolve the contested case cleanly. Rabbinic tradition leans toward the view that Amasa was innocent and Yoav was wrong, but the leaning is not unanimous.

The fourth question — whether David would hold Yoav accountable — has a complicated answer that reaches forward several years into the story. In the immediate aftermath of the killing, David did nothing. He could not afford to discipline Yoav in the middle of a live rebellion. He needed Yoav to finish the job against Sheva ben Bichri. He swallowed the news of Amasa's death and continued working with Yoav as if nothing had happened. But the killing was not forgotten. It was added to the ledger in David's mind — the ledger that also included Yoav's killing of Avner and Yoav's killing of Avshalom in defiance of orders — and at the very end of David's life, on his deathbed, David would whisper to Shlomo: "You know what Yoav ben Tzeruyah did to me, what he did to the two captains of the hosts of Israel, to Avner the son of Ner and to Amasa the son of Yeter, whom he slew, shedding the blood of war in peace. Do therefore according to your wisdom, and let not his hoary head go down to the grave in peace." Shlomo would later execute Yoav in the Tabernacle itself, at the horns of the altar where Yoav had taken refuge, precisely on the charges of these two killings. The reckoning would come. It would just not come in the middle of the pursuit of Sheva ben Bichri.

The fifth question — whether the whole killing was a pre-planned political maneuver by Yoav — is the question that the sugya in Sanhedrin 49a ultimately focuses on. The Gemara traces Yoav's preparation of the sword-belt with forensic care. The sword fell because the belt was rigged. The belt was rigged because Yoav had planned in advance to kill Amasa. The plan was laid before the meeting at the great stone. The greeting — Is it well with you, my brother? — was part of the trap. The gesture of the embrace was part of the trap. The whole encounter was choreographed by Yoav to produce exactly the outcome it produced. This was not a moment of rage or a crime of passion. This was a calculated assassination, executed by a professional soldier, using a technique that Yoav had used once before (against Avner) and that he knew would work because it had worked before. The Gemara, in its legal analysis, treats this calculation as evidence that Yoav was fully aware of what he was doing and could be charged with premeditated murder if the court could ever find witnesses willing to testify against him.

Witnesses, as it turned out, were not hard to find. The killing had taken place in full view of hundreds of soldiers. But no witnesses were willing to come forward while Yoav was alive and in command of the army. The killing was allowed to stand, for the next forty years, without any formal legal consequence — until Shlomo inherited the kingdom and decided that the time for consequences had finally come.

* * *

The pursuit of Sheva ben Bichri continued.

Yoav led the combined force of the Kreti and the Pelti, the mighty men of David's personal guard, and the newly absorbed army of Yehuda — perhaps two thousand five hundred men in total — up the northern road through the hill country of Binyamin. They moved quickly. Yoav was a professional soldier and he did not waste time. The news of Amasa's death spread through the army as they marched, and the men who might have been inclined to slow down or ask questions were silenced by the presence of Yoav himself at the head of the column, riding with the calm, unreadable expression of a man who had done exactly what he had intended to do and would not be answering any questions about it.

They passed through the hills of Binyamin.

They passed through the territory of Efrayim.

They passed through the edges of Menasheh and came out into the open country north of the Kinneret.

And they came, at last, to the city of Avel Beit Ma'akhah in the far north of the land.

The city was fortified. It was one of the oldest cities in the northern tribes — the Tanach describes it in Shoftim and Melakhim with a kind of affectionate respect, as a place that had always been a center of wisdom and counsel in Israel, a place where difficult questions had been brought for decision since the time of Yehoshua. The walls were high. The gates were strong. And inside the walls, according to the intelligence Yoav had gathered on the march north, was Sheva ben Bichri — with perhaps five hundred men who had joined him on his flight north, holed up in the city and counting on the fortifications to protect him.

Yoav ordered the siege to begin.

"Avishai. I want the ramp started on the northeast corner. The wall is lowest there. The ground slopes up toward us, which will save us a third of the earthwork. Have the men begin cutting timber from the grove to the east. I want the first layer of earth in place by sunset."

"Yes, my lord."

"And send a man to scout the other three sides. I want to know if there are any sally ports or secondary gates where they might try to break out at night. Seal any we find."

"Yes, my lord."

"And the messengers?"

"Two of them made it out of the city about an hour before we arrived, my lord. They rode north. I have dispatched six men to track them and bring them back."

"Good. If they reach another city and raise it against us, we will have a second siege on our hands. Catch them before they talk."

"Yes, my lord."

The men of the combined army set up camp outside the walls. They began to pile up earth against the walls — building a solelah, a siege ramp, that would eventually allow them to bring rams and siege ladders up to the level of the parapets. They began to batter the walls with whatever battering rams they could construct from the trees of the surrounding country. They were preparing to take the city by force.

Yoav was standing at the base of the main siege ramp, directing the work, when he heard a woman's voice calling down to him from the wall.

"Are you Yoav?"

He looked up.
