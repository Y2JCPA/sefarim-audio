Chapter One

The Ten Parts

## Volume V — The Restoration ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

The kingdom did not come back easily.

It would have been satisfying, at the end of the previous volume, if the death of Avshalom in the forest of Efrayim had been the end of the story. It was not the end of the story. The death of Avshalom had ended the rebellion as a military matter, but it had not ended the rebellion as a political or emotional or spiritual matter. The wound in the kingdom was still open. The men who had declared for Avshalom in the towns of Yehuda and the hills of Efrayim and the villages of Binyamin and the eastern slopes of Menasheh were still alive. They were now trying to figure out whether they had a place in the kingdom they had tried to overthrow, or whether David would come back with a sword and a ledger and exact the vengeance that a lesser king would have exacted.

David understood this perfectly.

He had been watching the kingdom from his upper chamber at Machanayim, and he had been receiving reports from his spies and his couriers and the returning officers of his army, and he had been piecing together the political geography of what had just happened. He had understood that the death of Avshalom was only the end of the first phase. The second phase — the phase of coming home — was going to be harder than the first phase in some ways, because it required not the defeat of an enemy but the re-assimilation of a population that had briefly and almost catastrophically ceased to be loyal.

So David was patient.

He did not march on Yerushalayim the day after the battle. He did not march on Yerushalayim the day after that either. He stayed in Machanayim for a week. And then another week. He waited. He was waiting for an invitation. He had concluded, sitting in the small palace of the eastern town, that the homecoming had to be requested — that the men who had rebelled against him had to come to him and ask him to come back, not the other way around. If he marched home uninvited, he would look like a conqueror reclaiming lost territory. If he marched home invited, he would look like a king being restored by his grateful people. The difference was not cosmetic. The difference was the difference between stability and another rebellion within a year.

The invitation was slow in coming.

* * *

The tribe of Yehuda — David's own tribe, the tribe that should have been the first to welcome him back — was the slowest of all the tribes to extend the invitation.

This was strange. The Tanach is careful to preserve the strangeness. Yehuda should have been the tribe where the invitation originated. David was a Yehudite. He had been crowned in Chevron, the capital of Yehuda, at the very beginning of his reign. The elders of Yehuda had been his closest political base for forty years. Yet the elders of Yehuda were silent in the weeks after Avshalom's death. They did not send messengers to Machanayim. They did not organize a delegation. They did not even propose a public debate about the matter in the town squares of Chevron or Beit Lechem or Zif.

Why not?

The mefarshim suggest several reasons. The first is shame. The rebellion had started in Chevron, when Avshalom had sent runners to every tribe with his coded shofar signal. The men of Yehuda had been the first to answer the call. Many of them had joined Avshalom's army. Many of them had carried spears in the forest of Efrayim on the losing side. Now they were afraid. They did not know whether David would remember who had joined Avshalom and who had not. They did not know whether returning to him meant forgiveness or a quiet execution list. They were paralyzed by the possibility that the invitation would be the first step in their own destruction.

The second reason was the presence of Amasa.

Amasa ben Yeter was Avshalom's former commander — the man Avshalom had appointed over his army in the place of Yoav. He was a Yehudite by birth. He had led the rebel forces in the forest of Efrayim. He had survived the battle. He had fled back to Yehuda after the defeat. And now he was sitting in the towns of Yehuda with a kind of quiet political authority — a man who had been a general, even if a defeated general, and whose presence in the tribe was a reminder that Yehuda had chosen the wrong side. The elders of Yehuda could not send an invitation to David without Amasa's approval, and Amasa had no reason to approve an invitation to the king whose army had just defeated his.

David understood all of this from Machanayim. He sent a letter.

He dictated the letter in the small stone chamber that served as his audience room in the upper palace of Machanayim. His scribe was sitting on a low stool across from him with a wax tablet and a stylus.

"Write this. Address it to Tzadok the Kohen and to Evyatar the Kohen. 'To my trusted priests in the city of Yerushalayim. The peace of Hashem be upon you. I have been waiting, in this town across the Yarden, for an invitation from my own tribe to return to my throne. The invitation has not come. I have heard the word of all Israel that has reached me in my chamber — the northern tribes have been speaking openly about restoring the king to his house. And yet the tribe of Yehuda — my own tribe, my own bone and flesh — remains silent. Are they waiting for me to march on them? Are they afraid of my vengeance? Or are they merely slow?'"

The scribe wrote.

"Continue. 'I want you to go to the elders of Yehuda. I want you to speak with them in person. I want you to ask them, from my own mouth: Why should you be the last to bring back the king to his house? You are my brothers. You are my bone. You are my flesh. Why should you be the last to bring back the king? Deliver this to them in a public hearing, where every elder of every clan can hear it. I want the question asked in the open.'"

"Yes, my lord."

"Continue. 'And there is a second matter. I want you to deliver a personal message to Amasa ben Yeter, my kinsman, who served my enemy Avshalom as the captain of his host. Tell him this, exactly: Are you not my bone and my flesh? May God do so to me and more also, if you are not captain of the army before me continually in the place of Yoav. Tell him the king has spoken. Tell him his old loyalty is forgiven. Tell him his new position is confirmed.'"

The scribe's stylus hesitated above the wax.

"My lord — did you say in the place of Yoav?"

"I said it."

"You wish me to write this? The dismissal of your general — by a letter delivered to a priest to deliver to a former enemy?"

"I wish you to write it."

"My lord — may I — "

"No, you may not. Write it. Seal it. Send it by the fastest messenger to Yerushalayim. I want it in Tzadok's hands within three days. Go."

The scribe wrote. He sealed the letter with the royal seal. He carried it out of the chamber and handed it to the courier who was waiting at the gate.

Within the hour, a rider was carrying the letter west toward the Yarden.

* * *

The Tanach preserves this third element of the letter as a single sentence buried inside the second message. "If you are not captain of the army before me continually in the place of Yoav." The phrase in the place of Yoav is the crucial phrase. David was announcing, in a message that would be read aloud to the elders of Yehuda, that he was firing Yoav — the general who had served him for decades, who had killed Avner in the gateway of Chevron, who had received the sealed letter about Uriah and asked no questions, who had just defeated Avshalom's army in the forest of Efrayim, and who had (in defiance of David's order) killed Avshalom himself to save the kingdom.

David was firing the man who had just saved his throne.

Why?

The mefarshim give several reasons. The surface reason is that Yoav had killed Avshalom in defiance of David's explicit order. David was furious about this. He had told the three commanders, publicly, "deal gently for my sake with the young man." Yoav had heard the order, had nodded politely, and had then thrown three javelins into Avshalom's heart. David's grief for Avshalom had curdled, in the weeks after the battle, into a quiet, implacable anger at the man who had killed him. The anger was not reasonable. Yoav had done the right thing for the kingdom. David knew this intellectually. But emotionally, David could not bring himself to continue working with the man who had executed his son. He needed a new general. He was going to take the opportunity of the homecoming to replace Yoav with a man who had no blood of Avshalom on his hands.

The second reason was political. By appointing Amasa — Avshalom's former general — as his own new commander, David was sending a message to every Yehudite who had joined the rebellion: you can come home. There will be no purge. The man who led Avshalom's army is now leading mine. If I can forgive him, I can forgive you. The appointment of Amasa was a calculated gesture of national reconciliation. It was the kind of gesture that only a king of David's political skill would have thought to make. And it was the kind of gesture that could only work if David could stomach the personal cost — dismissing Yoav, rewarding a man who had fought against him, trusting a former enemy with the army of Israel.

The Etzion commentary reads this decision as one of the great gambles of David's reign. On the one hand, it was masterful statecraft. On the other hand, it was a betrayal of the most loyal soldier in the king's service. Yoav had been the king's right hand for thirty years. He had never wavered. He had done every hard thing David needed done, often without being asked. And now, at the moment of the king's restoration, the king was firing him.

Yoav, when he heard about the letter, said nothing publicly.

But he remembered.

* * *

The three messages in David's letter arrived in Yerushalayim within a day of each other. The elders of Yehuda heard their message through Tzadok and Evyatar. They understood that the king was inviting them to invite him home. They understood that the king was not going to punish the rebels. They understood that the king was even going to appoint a rebel general as his own commander.

The calculation changed overnight.

The elders of Yehuda sent a delegation to Machanayim within the week. The delegation was led by the oldest of the Yehudite elders, a man named Kilav ben Yishai of Chevron. He came into David's small audience chamber at Machanayim and prostrated himself.

"My lord."

"Kilav. You have come."

"My lord — we have come to bring you home."

"All of you?"

"All of us. The elders of Yehuda, from Chevron to Be'er Sheva. Every town. Every clan. We have been slow. We have been afraid. We were wrong. We should have come the first week. We are here now. If you will permit it, we will escort you across the Yarden and back to Yerushalayim."

David looked at him for a long moment.

He was sixty-five years old. He had been away from his own palace for several weeks now. He was tired. He had just lost his favorite son. He had been living in a small provincial town on rations donated by the eastern tribes. Every instinct in him wanted to say yes, take me home, let us go now.

But David was also a political operator, and he knew that his response to the delegation would be read by everyone in the kingdom. He could not appear too eager. He could not look like a man who was desperate to be back on his throne. He had to respond with the dignity of a king who was accepting a request rather than receiving a rescue.

"Rise, Kilav. Your delegation is welcome. Tell your men to make camp outside the city. We will leave at dawn the day after tomorrow. The road back to Yerushalayim will take us south through the eastern hills, across the Yarden at the ford near Gilgal, and then west up the road to the city."

"Yes, my lord."

"And Kilav — "

"Yes, my lord?"

"Tell your men that there will be no punishment for those who supported Avshalom. The matter is closed. Today is the day I become the king of all Israel again, not just the king of the men who fought for me in the forest. I do not intend to make distinctions. Do you understand?"

Kilav's face was wet.

"I understand, my lord. And the tribe of Yehuda thanks you."

He bowed and left the audience chamber.

* * *

Two days later, the procession left Machanayim.

It was the opposite of the procession that had left Yerushalayim a few weeks earlier. The earlier procession had been a flight — a desperate retreat of a king and his household, walking out of the city with torn robes and ashes and weeping, escorted by a handful of loyal soldiers and a Pelishti mercenary captain named Itai of Gat. This procession was a homecoming — the king walking back across the same territory he had fled across, escorted by a delegation of elders, met at every village by crowds that came out to watch him pass, moving slowly and deliberately toward the river that was the border of the heartland of the kingdom.

The route went south through the eastern hill country. The procession passed through Gil'ad. The old hills where Yiftach had once judged Israel, where Shaul had once fought against Nachash the Ammonite, where David himself had hidden from Avshalom's forces a few weeks earlier. The people of Gil'ad came out to watch him pass. Some of them wept. Some of them shouted blessings. Most of them simply stood in silence, reckoning with the strangeness of seeing their king walk home under his own power after the storm that had almost destroyed everything.

The procession reached the Yarden at the ford near Gilgal.

Gilgal was the town where Yehoshua had first camped after crossing the Yarden nearly four hundred years earlier, the town where the twelve stones from the riverbed had been set up as a memorial, the town where the people of Israel had been circumcised before entering the Land, and the town where Shmuel had crowned Shaul as king in an earlier, more hopeful time. It was a place of beginnings. It was a fitting place for David's restoration to begin.

The procession reached the eastern bank of the Yarden in the late morning. The men of Yehuda had assembled a ferry — a large flat-bottomed boat that could transport the royal household across the river — and the Yehudites who had come out from Yerushalayim with Kilav were waiting on the western bank to receive them.

David crossed first.

He stepped off the ferry onto the western bank of the Yarden and set his feet on the soil of the land he had ruled for thirty-three years. The crowd on the western bank — the delegation of Yehuda, a handful of officials from Yerushalayim, a few dozen townspeople from Gilgal and Yericho — erupted in shouting and weeping. Men ran forward to kiss his hand. Women threw branches on the ground in front of him. A small choir of Levi'im had been assembled and began to sing a psalm of welcome. The noise was overwhelming.

David walked through the crowd slowly, accepting the welcome, nodding to the elders who came forward to greet him.

Shimei ben Gera was in the crowd.

We have already told the story of Shimei's encounter with David at the Yarden in the previous volume — the man who had cursed David from the hillside during the flight, who now threw himself on David's mercy and was forgiven. Mefivoshet ben Yonatan and Barzillai the Gil'adi were also there. Each of the three encounters took place in the long hour after David's feet touched the western bank. Each of them has already been described.

What had not been described yet — what belongs to this volume, because it is the seed from which the next rebellion will grow — is what happened after those three encounters, when the immediate ceremony of the river-crossing was done and the men of Yehuda were preparing to escort David the rest of the way back to Yerushalayim.

* * *

The men of Israel arrived.

They had been coming, through the night and the early morning, from the northern and eastern tribes — the tribes of Efrayim and Menasheh and Yissachar and Zevulun and Naftali and Asher, the tribes that had been most heavily involved in Avshalom's rebellion, and the tribes that had heard (with a week's delay) that David was crossing the Yarden at Gilgal. They had ridden and walked as fast as they could to be present at the homecoming. They had wanted to be part of the ceremony. They had wanted to be seen welcoming their king home.

They arrived about three hours after the ceremony had ended.

They found David already on the western bank, already surrounded by the Yehudites, already being prepared for the next leg of the journey — the march from Gilgal back up into the hill country toward Yerushalayim. The men of Yehuda were forming up in the road. The royal household was being arranged. The march was about to begin.

The Israelite delegation — perhaps two hundred men, elders and officials from the northern tribes — walked up to the assembly area and stopped.

They looked around.

The elder at the front of the Israelite group turned to the man beside him.

"We are too late."

"No — the king is still here. The march has not begun."

"We are too late for the ceremony. Look. The welcome has already happened. The choir is dispersing. The elders of Yehuda are already in the honor positions at the front of the column. The prayers have already been said. The ferry is already beached on the bank. We were supposed to be in the choir. We were supposed to be in the honor positions. The Yehudites did the whole welcome without us."

"Maybe they did not know we were coming."

"They knew. How could they not know? We sent riders ahead yesterday. They knew we were on the way. They went ahead anyway. They did it deliberately."

"What do we do now?"

"We go to the king. We demand an explanation."

"An explanation? From David? He did not organize the ferries. The Yehudites organized them. The king is blameless in this."

"The king is not blameless. The king is the king. He could have waited for us. He could have sent word to the Yehudites to hold the ceremony until the northern tribes arrived. He did not. He let the Yehudites run the welcome the way they wanted to run it. That makes him complicit."

"So — "

"So we go to him. And we make our grievance heard. Now. Before the column starts to move."

The elder walked forward through the crowd, with the rest of the Israelite delegation following him. They were furious.

* * *

The spokesman of the Israelite delegation walked forward to where David was standing with the Yehudite elders. He was a tall man from the tribe of Efrayim, perhaps fifty years old, with the weathered face of a man who had walked many miles to be here. He bowed formally to David.

"My lord the king."

"Speak."

He turned — not to David, but to the Yehudite elders standing next to David. The question he asked was directed at them, not at the king.

"Why have our brothers, the men of Yehuda, stolen you? Why have you brought the king and his household over the Yarden, and all David's men with him, without us?"

The word stolen is the key word. The Hebrew is genavucha — they have stolen you. The same root as the verb the Tanach used earlier in this story — when Avshalom had stolen the hearts of the men of Israel at the gate of Yerushalayim. The Israelite spokesman was deliberately using the same loaded verb to accuse the Yehudites of the same crime. You stole the king from us. You stole the homecoming from us. You stole the political advantage from us. We were supposed to be part of this.

The elders of Yehuda heard the accusation. They did not like it.

The chief of the Yehudite delegation — Kilav ben Yishai of Chevron, the same man who had led the delegation to Machanayim — stepped forward. He did not look at the Israelite spokesman. He spoke to the space between them.

"We did not steal him. The king is our close relative. His bone is our bone. His flesh is our flesh. Why are you angry? Have we eaten any of the king's food? Have we taken any gifts from him that you have not? Have we received any advantage from him that you have been denied?"

The Yehudite answer is technically correct, but the tone is defensive. We are his tribe. We have the right. Stop complaining. It was not the answer that would calm the situation. It was the answer that would inflame it.

The Israelite spokesman took a step closer.

"Close relative? Close relative? Is that the entire basis of your argument? That he happens to share your blood? The king of Israel does not belong to one tribe. The king of Israel belongs to all of Israel. If David is the king of Yehuda only, then tell us now, and we will go home, and we will find ourselves another king."

"Watch your words, man. That is very close to rebellion."

"It is not close to rebellion. It is close to justice. We have ten parts in the king. And we have more in David than you. Why then have you treated us with contempt? Were we not the first to speak of bringing back our king?"

Aseh yadot li ba-melekh. "I have ten parts in the king." The phrase is one of the most politically loaded lines in all of Shmuel Bet. The Israelite spokesman was invoking the arithmetic of the tribes. Israel had ten tribes. Yehuda had one tribe (two, counting Binyamin, but Binyamin was counted differently in different contexts, and in this case the spokesman was counting Yehuda alone against the ten northern tribes). Ten is more than one. We have ten times as much claim on the king as you do. We are ten times as important. We should have been ten times as central to the ceremony.

And then the Israelite added the second line: "Were we not the first to speak of bringing back our king?" This was a reference to the internal debate in the northern tribes during the weeks after Avshalom's death, when some of the Israelites had suggested that David should be invited home. The suggestion had been real. It had not been acted upon, because the Israelites were divided and afraid. But they had spoken of it first. They had proposed the homecoming before the Yehudites had. And now the Yehudites had organized the actual ceremony without them. The Israelites felt cheated twice — once by the initial delay, and once by the final exclusion.

Kilav's face went red.

"You spoke of it first? You spoke of it first? You did not do it first. You did not send a delegation. You did not ride to Machanayim. You did not escort the king to the ford. You sat in your villages and argued about whether to send the invitation, and while you were arguing, we got on our horses and went. That is the difference between a tribe that loves its king and a tribe that likes to argue about its king in the abstract."

"Our words were the beginning of the restoration. Your actions were only the ending of it. Without our words, the king would still be in Machanayim."

"Without our actions, the king would still be in Machanayim."

"Our words — "

"Your words. Is that all you have? Words? The tribe of Yehuda has given this kingdom a king. The tribe of Yehuda has given this kingdom its capital. The tribe of Yehuda has bled for David in every battle from the cave of Adullam to the forest of Efrayim. And you want to stand here and lecture us about words?"

The Tanach records what happened next in one of the most devastating sentences in the whole chapter. "And the words of the men of Yehuda were fiercer than the words of the men of Israel."

Vayikash devar ish Yehuda me-devar ish Yisrael. "And the word of the man of Yehuda was fiercer than the word of the man of Israel."

The Yehudites did not merely respond. They responded more fiercely than they had been addressed. The Israelite spokesman had raised his voice. The Yehudite responded by raising his voice even higher. The exchange escalated. Accusations flew. Old grudges — grudges that went back to the civil war between David and Ish-Boshet forty years earlier, grudges that went back even further to the time of the Shoftim — resurfaced. The crowd of Israelites began to mutter. The crowd of Yehudites began to shout back. The two delegations, which had come to welcome the same king, were suddenly on the edge of physical violence.

David stood between them.

He was an old man. He was exhausted from the journey. He had just made peace with three of his most complicated personal enemies — Shimei, Mefivoshet, Tziva. He had crossed a river and stepped onto his own soil for the first time in weeks. And now, before he had even begun the march back to his own capital, his kingdom was fracturing in front of him over a matter of protocol and tribal pride.

He raised his hands.

"Stop."

The shouting did not stop at once. He had to raise his voice a second time.

"I said stop. All of you. This is not the day for this quarrel. This is the day of my return. We will not stain it with an argument about ferries."

The shouting subsided. Slowly.

But the underlying grievance did not go away. The Israelite delegation muttered among themselves. The Yehudite delegation stood stiffly. The march resumed, the two groups walking side by side on the same road but with several paces of hostile space between them. The ceremony of the homecoming that had been meant to unite the kingdom had, in fact, revealed exactly how fractured the kingdom still was.

* * *

The Tanach is telling the reader something important with this scene, and it is telling it subtly.

The argument at Gilgal was not a small argument. It was a preview. It was the first visible crack in the kingdom that had been created by Shaul and consolidated by David. The ten tribes of the north and the one tribe of the south — Yehuda — had been held together, for two generations, by the sheer personal authority of David himself. Under Shaul, the fracture between north and south had already been visible (the first civil war of the book, between David in Chevron and Ish-Boshet at Machanayim, was essentially a north-south fracture). Under David, the fracture had been covered over by the king's political skill and by the emotional loyalty he inspired in men of both regions.

But the fracture was still there.

It would widen, over time. It would widen catastrophically at the end of Shlomo's reign, a generation from now, when Rechavam would refuse to lighten the tax burden of the northern tribes and they would secede to form the northern kingdom of Israel under Yerovam ben Nevat. The civil war that followed would split the kingdom permanently. The northern kingdom would last for two hundred years before it was destroyed by Assyria. The southern kingdom of Yehuda would last for four hundred years before it was destroyed by Bavel. The two kingdoms would never again be united in history.

And the first visible sign of the fracture — the first time the phrase "we have ten parts in the king" appears in any political speech in Tanach — is in the argument at Gilgal during David's homecoming.

The mefarshim treat the argument as the seed of everything that comes later. Rashi on Shmuel Bet 19:43 notes that the Israelite spokesman is already using the accounting language that would later become the slogan of Yerovam's secession. The Ralbag adds that the fiercer response of Yehuda is the first sign that the tribal pride of Yehuda — the pride that would later carry the Davidic dynasty through centuries of exile and persecution — had already begun to harden into the kind of haughty self-regard that makes political compromise impossible. And the Malbim reads the phrase we have ten parts in the king as a direct theological statement: the king belongs to all of Israel, not just to his own tribe. Yehuda had begun to treat David as their king. Israel was reminding them that he was everyone's king. The reminder was correct. But the tone was wrong. And the tone would eventually produce a secession.

* * *

The argument at Gilgal did not end peacefully.

The march to Yerushalayim resumed, but the two delegations walked separately, and by the time the procession reached the ascent into the hill country, the Israelite delegation had begun to thin out. Men were peeling off. Going back to their villages. Going back with their grievance intact. Going back to the corners of the northern kingdom where they would nurse the memory of having been excluded from the ceremony at Gilgal.

And somewhere in the back of that departing Israelite crowd, listening to the muttering and watching the king disappear up the road with his Yehudite escort, was a man from the tribe of Binyamin.

His name was Sheva ben Bichri.

He was a descendant of the house of Shaul. He had been loyal to the old king's memory his whole life. He had watched David rule for thirty-three years and he had never quite made peace with the fact that the kingship had been taken from his own tribe. He had joined Avshalom's rebellion as soon as the shofar had sounded in Chevron. He had survived the battle in the forest of Efrayim. He had ridden back to Binyamin and had been hiding in his ancestral village for the past several weeks.

He had come to Gilgal with the Israelite delegation in the hope of being part of the welcome.

He had watched the argument.

He had listened to the Israelite spokesman invoke the ten parts in the king.

He had listened to the Yehudite respond with even fiercer words.

He had watched the delegations leave with their grievance unhealed.

And he had decided, walking back up into the hills of Binyamin that afternoon, that he was going to do something about it.

* * *

He was an ish Belial — a worthless man, a man of Belial, the Hebrew term the Tanach reserves for the lowest and most contemptible characters in its narrative. The phrase ish Belial is used in Shmuel Aleph for the sons of Eli who corrupted the priesthood. It is used for the worthless men of Givat who provoked the war with Binyamin at the end of Shoftim. It is used now, in the next verse of the Tanach, for Sheva ben Bichri.

But he had something that worthless men sometimes have: he had a moment.

The moment was the argument at Gilgal. The moment was the muttering of the Israelite delegation. The moment was the departing crowd of men from the northern tribes, who had come to welcome their king and had been made to feel like uninvited guests.

Sheva ben Bichri decided that he was going to exploit the moment.

He walked back to his village in Binyamin. He picked up a shofar. And he began to plan a second rebellion — a rebellion that would last less than a week, that would be crushed without a proper battle, and that would nonetheless leave two more bodies on the road — Amasa in the dust of Giv'on, and Sheva's own head flying over the wall of Avel Beit Ma'akhah — before the kingdom would finally begin to heal.

That is the story of the next chapter.
