Chapter Four

Noon in Machanayim

The fire had burned down to embers.

Rechav sat on one side of it, sharpening a short blade against a flat stone. Ba'anah sat on the other side, watching his brother work. The barracks courtyard was empty at this hour — most of the garrison was asleep — and the only sounds were the scrape of metal on stone and, far off, the bleating of a goat somewhere down in the town.

"How long," Ba'anah said finally, "has it been since our grandfather fled from Be'erot?"

Rechav did not look up.

"Sixty years. Maybe more."

"And why did he flee?"

"You know why."

"Say it anyway."

Rechav paused in his sharpening. He turned the blade over in his hand and held it up to the firelight, inspecting the edge.

"Because Shaul sent men into Be'erot with swords. Because the men of Be'erot who survived ran to Gittayim and they have been strangers there ever since."

"And why did Shaul send men?"

"Because we were Giv'onim. Because Yehoshua had sworn an oath to our fathers, and Shaul decided the oath did not apply to him."

Ba'anah poked the embers with a stick.

"Do you know what I have been thinking, brother?"

"Tell me."

"I have been thinking that Ish-Boshet is alone. Avner is dead. The old general is not coming back to protect him. The walls of Machanayim are guarded by men we command. The corridors of the palace are walked by officers who know our faces." Ba'anah paused. "I have been thinking that the last son of Shaul is asleep in a room at the top of a hill, and there is no one between him and us except a few lazy guards."

Rechav set down the whetstone.

"You are saying what I think you are saying."

"I am saying it."

"Say it plainly."

Ba'anah looked across the embers at his brother.

"The last son of Shaul should not outlive our grandfather's grief."

Rechav was silent for a long moment. Then he picked up the whetstone and went back to sharpening the blade. The scraping sound started again — slow, even, patient — and he spoke without looking up.

"Tomorrow at noon."

"Why noon?"

"Because the guards grow lazy in the heat. Because Ish-Boshet sleeps in his inner chamber during the shichvat hatzahorayim. Because if we go at noon we will be two officers carrying sacks, and no one will look twice."

"Sacks of what?"

"Wheat."

* * *

The Scripture pauses, at this point in Chapter 4, to give us a piece of family history that seems at first glance to be a digression.

"And Be'erot was also counted among Binyamin, because the Be'erotim fled to Gittayim, and they have been sojourners there until this day."

The Yeshivat Har Etzion commentary unpacks this strange parenthetical. Be'erot was one of the four Giv'onite cities — part of the coalition that had tricked Yehoshua into making a covenant with them back in the days of the conquest. The Giv'onim had sworn themselves to Israel's service forever. They were not technically Israelites. They were a protected caste, bound to Israel by oath.

At some point during the reign of Shaul, the Giv'onim had been driven from their cities. The Be'erotim had fled to Gittayim. Many of their young men had joined the army of Shaul in the next generation, because the tribe of Binyamin had absorbed them, and because in a world where a man had to choose between exile and service, service was the better choice.

But not every son forgets his grandfather's grief.

Rechav and Ba'anah had grown up in Shaul's camp. They had worn Ish-Boshet's uniform. They had captained raiding parties. They had been trusted enough to walk into Ish-Boshet's palace without being challenged at the door. And they had been harboring a grievance against the house of Shaul for as long as they had been alive.

When Avner ben Ner died in the gateway of Chevron, the two brothers had looked at each other across a campfire and understood that the moment had come.

* * *

Machanayim was an old city east of the Yarden, built on a hill, with a palace complex at the top and a walled garrison below. In the middle of a spring day, when the sun beat down on the stones of the courtyard and the air shimmered with heat, the guards grew lazy. Ish-Boshet himself, like most men in that era, took a long midday rest — what the chapter calls shichvat hatzahorayim, the noontime lying-down. The king retired to a cool inner chamber and slept on a cushioned bench until the worst of the heat had passed.

The king's hands had gone slack since Avner's death. He was not eating well. He was not governing well. A cousin of his had gone to him the morning after the news arrived from Chevron and found him sitting by a window with a cup of wine that had not been touched.

"My lord," the cousin had said. "You have not slept."

"No."

"You must eat something. The kingdom cannot run on a fasting king."

Ish-Boshet had looked up at him with eyes that did not seem to see him.

"Tell me," he had said. "If Avner is gone — who now guards the gate?"

The cousin had had no answer.

* * *

Rechav and Ba'anah walked up the path to the palace just before noon.

They wore their uniforms — the captain's sash, the leather cuirass, the short cloak of an officer — and they each carried a large sack over one shoulder. The guard at the lower gate knew both of them by name. He raised a hand in greeting as they approached.

"Rechav. Ba'anah. What's in the sacks?"

"Wheat for the king's stores," Rechav said.

"Wheat from where?"

"The eastern granary. The quartermaster sent us."

"At noon?"

"He says the flour-women need it by this evening. Something about bread for the household."

The guard squinted at the sacks. They sagged heavily. They were well-tied at the top with a leather cord.

"Go on, then."

They walked past him.

The inner gate was manned by a single soldier who was, at the moment they arrived, leaning on his spear with his eyes half closed. He waved them through without a word. They crossed the outer courtyard, passed the cistern, climbed the stone steps to the palace hall, and walked down the corridor that led toward the king's private chambers.

The Rashi on the key verse, following an older tradition, explains that the wheat story was their cover. The Metzudat David suggests they may have walked in alongside genuine wheat-sellers, using the cover of routine delivery to blend in. Either reading works. The point is the same: they moved through the palace without raising a single alarm.

They stopped outside the door of the king's chamber.

Ba'anah whispered, "Are you ready?"

"I am ready."

"Then open it."

* * *

Ish-Boshet was asleep when they came in.

He was lying on his side on the cushioned bench, one arm under his head, the other resting across his stomach. The afternoon light fell through the lattice window and made patterns on the floor. His chest rose and fell slowly. He looked, in that moment, more like a tired shepherd than a king.

Rechav drew his sword.

Ba'anah drew his.

Neither brother spoke. There was nothing to say. Ish-Boshet did not wake. The blades went in together, one from each side, at exactly the place where every practiced soldier learns to strike: the fifth rib, the soft space where the ribs do not protect the heart, the killing spot.

The Scripture records the moment with a surgeon's precision.

"And they came into the midst of the house, as though to fetch wheat. And they smote him in the fifth rib. And Rechav and Ba'anah his brother escaped."

The fifth rib.

It is a phrase that has already appeared in this book. Avner ben Ner had driven the butt of his spear into Asa'el's fifth rib at Giv'on. Yoav had driven his sword into Avner's fifth rib in the gateway of Chevron. Now Rechav and Ba'anah drove their weapons into Ish-Boshet's fifth rib in the inner chamber of his palace in Machanayim. The Scripture is making a point by repetition. The same spot. The same fatal strike. The same targeted place where a practiced soldier drives a blade if he wants to end a life quickly and without noise.

Shmuel Bet is the book of the fifth rib.

* * *

Ish-Boshet did not cry out.

Maybe he was asleep when the blade went in. Maybe he woke for just long enough to see the face of his own officer above him. Whatever happened, no sound came from the bed. The brothers waited for a few seconds — listening for footsteps in the corridor, listening for any sign that someone had heard — and when there was nothing, Rechav gestured to his brother.

"Take the head."

"Why?"

"Because David will not believe us without it. Take it."

Ba'anah hesitated. Then he pulled a longer knife from his belt and bent over the bed. Rechav turned his back and stood by the door, listening.

When it was done, they wrapped the head in a linen cloth and dropped it into one of the empty wheat sacks. They left the other sack on the floor of the chamber. They closed the chamber door behind them. They walked back down the corridor, past the cistern, through the inner gate, through the outer gate, and out into the bright afternoon sun of Machanayim.

The lower-gate guard was sitting now, chewing on a piece of dried fig.

"Done already?" he called.

"The quartermaster wants us back," Rechav said. "One of the women said the wheat was wrong."

"Wrong how?"

"I don't know. Ask her."

The guard shrugged and went back to his fig.

The brothers walked down the hill road until they were out of sight of the walls, and then they broke into a run. They ran west, down the slope of the hill country, toward the Yarden River. They crossed at a ford they knew. They ran across the plain on the far side. They ran all night, taking turns carrying the sack, stopping only to drink at streams and to catch their breath. By the time the sun rose over the hills of Yehuda, they were on the road to Chevron.

They arrived at the city gate just after dawn.

* * *

The throne room at Chevron was already in session when they arrived.

David was hearing petitions — a dispute between two farmers over a boundary stone, a widow who needed grain from the royal storehouse, the usual morning business of a king. Yoav was standing beside the throne with his arms crossed, his face set in its usual grim expression. Evyatar the kohen sat at a small table to the left, making notes with a reed pen.

The doors at the far end of the hall burst open.

Two dusty, travel-worn men strode up the center of the hall without waiting to be announced. A chamberlain started forward to stop them, but David raised a hand, and the chamberlain fell back. The two men walked all the way up to the foot of the throne. Each carried a sack. They dropped the sacks onto the stone floor at David's feet.

Ba'anah opened the first sack and tipped it out.

The head of Ish-Boshet, son of Shaul, rolled across the polished stone and came to rest a few feet from David's sandals.

The entire throne room went silent.

David did not move.

Ba'anah stepped forward. He had rehearsed this speech all night as he ran across the plain. His voice rang out in the silent hall.

"Behold the head of Ish-Boshet the son of Shaul, your enemy, who sought your life! And Hashem has given my lord the king vengeance this day on Shaul and on his seed!"

He paused for effect.

He was waiting — exactly like the Amalekite at Tziklag had waited — for the reward.

David stared at the head on the floor.

He stared at it for a long time.

Yoav, standing beside the throne, had gone very still. The kohen Evyatar had set down his reed pen. Every man in the hall was watching the king's face, and the king's face was unreadable.

"Where did you come from?" David said quietly.

"From Machanayim, my lord."

"At what hour did you do this thing?"

"At the hour of the noonday rest."

"He was sleeping?"

"He was sleeping, my lord. We struck quickly. He did not suffer."

David looked up slowly from the head on the floor to the faces of the two men standing before him.

"You struck him while he was sleeping," David repeated.

"For your sake, my lord. To give you the throne."

And then David spoke, and his voice was colder than anyone in the throne room had ever heard it.

* * *

"As Hashem lives," David said, rising from the throne, "who has redeemed my soul from all distress — "

He stepped down from the dais and walked around the head on the floor, circling it slowly, his eyes fixed on the two brothers.

" — when one told me, saying, Behold, Shaul is dead, and he thought he was bringing good news, I seized him, and I killed him in Tziklag. That was the reward I gave him for his news."

Rechav's face went white.

"How much more," David continued, his voice rising now, "when wicked men have slain a righteous man in his own house, on his bed — "

"My lord — " Ba'anah began.

" — shall I not now require his blood from your hand?"

"My lord, listen — "

" — and remove you from the earth?"

David turned to the young men standing at the edges of the hall — the same young men who had executed the Amalekite at Tziklag.

"Kill them."

The young men drew their swords.

Rechav turned to run. He did not get three paces. Ba'anah opened his mouth to speak and a sword went through his chest before he could say another word. Within a minute, both men were dead on the floor of the throne room, a few feet from the head of the king they had murdered.

David was not finished.

"Take the bodies," he said to his men. "Cut off their hands and their feet. Hang them by the pool at the gate of the city, so that every man who comes to Chevron will see what happens to those who murder anointed princes in their beds."

There was a pause. The captain of the guard hesitated.

"And the head, my lord?"

David looked down at the head of Ish-Boshet.

"Carry it," he said, "with honor. Wash it. Wrap it in fine linen. And bury it in the tomb of Avner ben Ner, in this city. The man who crowned him and the son of Shaul will lie together."

* * *

The man who had crowned Ish-Boshet was buried alongside the head of the man he had crowned. Two victims of the civil war — one a general, one a puppet king — lying side by side in the same grave in David's capital. The political symbolism was unmistakable. David was not celebrating Ish-Boshet's death. David was closing a wound.

* * *

That evening, Yoav came to David in the inner chamber of the palace. The two men had not spoken since the executions.

"My lord."

"Yoav."

"A question."

"Ask it."

"You cursed me, after Avner died. You said my house would never lack a zav or a leper or a man who falls by the sword. And yet you did not execute me for what I did to Avner. You executed two Binyaminim for what they did to Ish-Boshet. Why?"

David looked at him for a long moment.

"Sit down, Yoav."

Yoav sat.

"When you killed Avner," David said, "you had a claim. A wrong claim, but a claim. Avner had killed your brother. You had the standing of a go'el ha-dam. I cursed you because what you did was wrong — you broke the safe conduct of the king, you killed a man who had surrendered, you turned a Torah discussion into a trap. But you had a reason. These two men had no reason. Ish-Boshet had not wronged them. He was sleeping. They came for a reward."

"You executed the Amalekite at Tziklag for the same thing."

"I did."

"And I did not understand then either."

David stood and walked to the window. He looked out across the rooftops of Chevron, toward the hill country of Binyamin.

"Yoav. Listen to me. Every king who has ever lived has needed men like you. Men who will do the hard things. Men who will not flinch. I am not going to pretend I can rule Israel without such men." He turned back. "But the throne itself cannot rest on blood that I have paid for. If I pay men to kill my rivals, then from the first day of my reign, the throne of David is a bargain with murderers. And every ambitious officer in Israel will see that bargain and draw a lesson from it. Do you understand?"

"I think so."

"The throne has to come to me clean. Even if the path to it is full of blood — none of that blood can be on my ledger. The Amalekite, Rechav, Ba'anah — none of them acted on my orders. None of them believed they were acting on my orders. And yet each of them expected a reward. That expectation was the disease. If I had rewarded even one of them, the disease would have spread."

Yoav was quiet.

"I cursed you for killing Avner," David said, "but I did not execute you, because Avner's blood was already owed to you by law. It was wrong to collect it the way you did, but the debt was yours. The brothers today had no debt. They walked into another man's house and killed him in his sleep."

"And Mefivoshet?"

David turned sharply.

"What about Mefivoshet?"

"The lame boy. Yehonatan's son. He is still alive somewhere across the Yarden."

"You know where he is?"

"I have heard rumors. A town called Lo-Devar. In the household of a man named Makhir ben Ammiel."

David nodded slowly.

"Do not touch him, Yoav. Do not send men for him. Do not even speak of him except to me. One day I will ask about him again. That day is not today."

"Understood, my lord."

* * *

The Malbim emphasizes the moral dimension of the whole episode. David was not being cruel in his response to the brothers. He was being just. Rechav and Ba'anah were not soldiers who had killed an enemy in battle. They were household officers who had walked into the inner chamber of their own sleeping lord and murdered him in cold blood. In the moral vocabulary of the Torah, this is among the lowest forms of killing — a betrayal of trust, a violation of the bread-and-salt covenant between a man and the people he has sworn to protect.

And there was another layer.

The Yeshivat Har Etzion commentary, following an older midrashic line, suggests that Rechav and Ba'anah may have been acting on more than personal ambition. If they were descendants of the Giv'onim who had been driven from Be'erot during Shaul's reign, then killing Ish-Boshet was not merely opportunism. It was revenge. They were striking at the last surviving son of the king who had destroyed their grandfather's city. The Scripture's strange parenthetical about Be'erot being counted among Binyamin and the Be'erotim fleeing to Gittayim is a quiet hint: these men had a grievance, and they waited for their chance.

David understood this too. He did not excuse it. A just grievance does not authorize murder in a bed.

* * *

The political effect of David's response was immediate.

The elders of Israel had been watching Chevron very carefully since Avner's murder. They had seen David's public mourning for Avner. They had heard his lament at Avner's graveside. They had heard the curse on Yoav. And now they were hearing about what David had done to Rechav and Ba'anah.

A pattern was emerging.

This king did not condone assassinations. He executed assassins. He did not celebrate the deaths of his rivals — he wept for them, or at least for the ones who deserved mourning. He had never raised a hand against Shaul when he had the chance. He had refused to profit from Shaul's death. He had refused to profit from Avner's death. He had refused to profit from Ish-Boshet's death. Every path by which David might have bought the throne cheaply, he had closed with blood.

The message to the tribes was: If you want this king, you will have to give him the throne yourselves. He will not take it from you.

And the tribes began to think about giving.

* * *

One more detail from the end of Chapter 4 cannot be skipped, because it plants a seed that will bloom painfully many chapters later.

In the middle of the civil war narrative, in the space of a single parenthetical verse, the author of Shmuel Bet introduces a child.

"And Yehonatan the son of Shaul had a son lame in his feet. He was five years old when the news from Yizre'el came about Shaul and Yehonatan, and his nurse picked him up and fled. And as she hurried to flee, he fell, and became lame. And his name was Mefivoshet."

One sentence. One child.

Picture the scene that Scripture compresses into half a verse. The palace at Yizre'el is in an uproar. A messenger has just ridden in from the north with his tunic torn and dust on his head, and the women of the household have begun to scream. The nurse — a woman whose name we are never given — is in the nursery with the five-year-old son of the prince. The boy is playing with wooden animals on the floor.

"Come!" the nurse says, snatching him up. "We have to leave!"

"Why?"

"Because your grandfather is dead. Because your father is dead. Because the Pelishtim will be here by nightfall. Come!"

"Where are we going?"

"Across the Yarden. To people your father trusted. We cannot stay here."

She runs with him in her arms. She runs down the corridor, through the courtyard, out the back gate of the palace, onto the road that leads east. She runs as fast as she can — and she is not a young woman, and the boy is heavy, and her feet are slipping on the stones of the road.

She stumbles.

He falls.

The chapter tells us only this: "And as she hurried to flee, he fell, and became lame." It does not tell us whether she stopped to lift him, whether she cried out, whether she carried him the rest of the way on her back with his broken feet dragging in the dust. It only tells us that he fell, and that he never walked normally again, and that his name was Mefivoshet.

* * *

He survived.

But he would never walk properly again.

His name was Mefivoshet — originally Merib-ba'al, but Scripture changes the ba'al to boshet for the same reason it calls Ish-Boshet by a different name than the one his mother used. Mefivoshet means something like "from the mouth of shame" — and the name, like his uncle's, was an editorial decision rather than his actual birth name.

Why does the verse appear here?

Rashi and the Radak explain: the verse is here because Chapter 4 is the chapter in which the house of Shaul comes to an end. Shaul and his three elder sons are dead at Gilboa. Ish-Boshet, the youngest son, has just been murdered in his bed. Of the royal line, only one male descendant remains alive — the lame grandson hiding in a household east of the Yarden.

Mefivoshet is the last survivor. And he cannot walk.

The kingdom of Shaul, which had begun with a tall handsome king who stood head and shoulders above everyone else in Israel, ends with a five-year-old child lying on the ground with broken feet.

* * *

David, sitting on his throne in Chevron, knew about Mefivoshet — as his conversation with Yoav that evening had just shown. But he had bigger things on his mind that week. The death of Ish-Boshet had created a vacuum in the north. The tribes were in disarray. Avner was dead. The house of Shaul was extinguished. And the anointed king of Yehuda was waiting in Chevron with his army and his household and his seven-year record of refusing to take the throne by force.

The elders were already on the road.
