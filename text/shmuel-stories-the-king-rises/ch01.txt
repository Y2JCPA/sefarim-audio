Chapter One

The Amalekite at Tziklag

## Volume I — The King Rises ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

The stranger was still two fields away when the watchman on the wall began shouting.

"Rider! Rider coming from the north!"

Avishai ben Tzeruyah was on the roof of the guardhouse within seconds. He shaded his eyes against the late-morning sun and squinted across the burned landscape. Tziklag had been burned to the ground only two weeks earlier by Amalekite raiders, and the walls were still half-rebuilt, the houses half-roofed, the people half-crazed with grief and relief and the strange exhausted joy of men who had lost everything and then somehow got it back. The king — or the one who would be king — was inside the city. He had been pacing for three days.

"One man?" Avishai called down to the watchman.

"One man. On foot. Staggering."

"Weapons?"

"None that I can see."

Avishai glanced at his brother Yoav, who had come up beside him. Yoav's face was hard as flint, the way it always was, and his hand was already resting on the hilt of his sword.

"Amalekite?" Yoav asked.

"Can't be sure."

"He's coming from the direction of Gilboa."

"I know."

The two brothers looked at each other. For three days, Tziklag had been holding its breath. Word had reached them from the north that a great battle had been fought between Israel and the Pelishtim on the slopes of Mount Gilboa. No official messenger had come. But travelers had whispered things — things about chariots, and a retreat, and a king who had fallen on his sword. The whispers had multiplied like flies around a wound. And David had not eaten since he heard the first one.

"Should I tell him?" Avishai asked.

Yoav was quiet for a long moment. Then he said, "No. Let the stranger tell him. If the news is what we think it is, David should hear it from a witness."

* * *

David was sitting on the stump of a burned cedar in what had once been the central square of Tziklag. His head was bent. His fingers were twisted together in his lap. A harp lay on the ground beside him, untouched.

He had not touched the harp in three days.

The men of his company — the six hundred who had followed him through every wilderness and every cave, through Pe'or and Ein Gedi and Ma'on, through the long nightmare of Shaul's pursuit — stood at a respectful distance around the edges of the square. They did not speak. They did not look directly at him. They watched him the way men watch a fire they are afraid to approach.

Yoav crossed the square with the stranger at his side.

"My lord."

David looked up. His eyes were red. His beard, usually trimmed, had grown wild in the days since the raid on the city. He looked at Yoav, and then he looked at the stranger, and the stranger fell flat on the ground and pressed his face into the dirt.

"Who are you?" David asked.

The stranger lifted his head just enough to speak. "I have come from the camp of Israel, my lord."

David's whole body changed in an instant. The weariness dropped away. His back straightened. His hands unclenched.

"What news?" he said. "Tell me."

The stranger swallowed. "The people have fled from the battle," he said. "Many have fallen. Many are dead. And also" — he paused, and his voice caught — "also Shaul and Yehonatan his son are dead."

For a long moment, David did not move.

"How do you know," David said slowly, "that Shaul and Yehonatan his son are dead?"

"I was there, my lord. I was on Mount Gilboa by chance, and I saw it with my own eyes. I saw Shaul leaning on his spear, and the chariots of the Pelishtim and their horsemen were closing in on him. He turned around and saw me, and he called me to him. I said, 'Here I am.' He said, 'Who are you?' And I answered him, 'I am an Amalekite.'"

David's jaw tightened at the word.

"Amalekite."

"And what did he say to you?"

"He said, 'Stand over me and kill me, for the convulsion has seized me, and yet my life is still in me.'" The stranger's voice was rising now, becoming almost eager, as if he sensed an opportunity and was reaching for it. "So I stood over him and killed him, for I knew that he could not live after he had fallen. And I took the crown that was on his head, and the bracelet that was on his arm, and I have brought them here to my lord."

He reached into the leather satchel at his side and drew out two objects, and held them up in the sunlight, and laid them in the dust at David's feet.

The crown of Shaul.

The royal bracelet.

* * *

David did not touch them.

He stared at them for the space of a dozen heartbeats, and then he reached up with both hands and took hold of the front of his own tunic and tore it from neck to waist in a single violent motion. The sound of the tearing fabric echoed in the square.

Every man in the company did the same. Six hundred tunics were rent in a single moment. Avishai. Yoav. Asa'el. The Cherethi and the Pelethi. The gibborim. The whole company. The sound was like a forest falling in a wind.

Then David sank to his knees in the dirt and he wept.

The Gemara in Moed Katan would later point to this moment as the source for a halacha. From where do we learn that one must rend his garment upon hearing of the death of the Nasi, the head of the Sanhedrin, and the like? Rabbi Abbahu asked. From the verse, "And David took hold of his garments and tore them." And the measure of the tear — a handbreadth — Rabbi Abbahu derived from the word "vayachazeik" — he took hold — because one cannot truly take hold of a garment that is less than a handbreadth wide. The tearing had to be felt in the hands. It had to be real. It had to hurt.

David wept for Shaul.

The man who had hunted him for years through the wilderness of Yehuda. The man who had thrown a spear at him across a banquet hall. The man whose shadow had fallen across every moment of David's young adulthood. The man whose daughter David had loved. The man whose son David had loved more. The man who, even at the end, had been unable to break the covenant of friendship that bound him to the sweet singer of Bethlehem — "Return, my son David, for I will do you no harm anymore, because my soul was precious in your eyes this day." David had heard those words in a cave and had not believed them, and he had been right not to believe them, but he had never stopped loving the man who had spoken them.

He wept for Yehonatan.

The friend whose soul had been bound to his own soul. The prince who should have inherited the throne and had willingly surrendered the claim. The warrior who had stood beside his father in battle because honor demanded it, even though his heart was in exile with David. The man who had told him, "Go in peace, because we have sworn, the two of us, in the name of the Lord, saying: Hashem shall be between me and you and between my seed and your seed forever."

Now Yehonatan was dead on the slopes of Gilboa.

And David was weeping.

And the men around him were weeping.

And the stranger was still lying flat on his face in the dirt, waiting for his reward.

* * *

They fasted until evening.

For Shaul, David fasted — because Shaul was the anointed of Hashem, the king of Israel, the head of the people. For Yehonatan he fasted — because Yehonatan was his friend. And the chapter records that they fasted "for the people of Hashem and for the house of Israel, because they had fallen by the sword."

The Gemara reads this verse with exquisite care. "For Shaul" — this is the Nasi. "For Yehonatan his son" — this is the Av Beit Din. "For the people of Hashem and the house of Israel" — these are evil tidings in general. The verse is not merely a report of David's grief. It is a halachic source, a precedent. From David's tears, generations of Jews would learn how to mourn a king, how to mourn a judge, how to mourn bad news. The tears of David became Torah.

When the sun began to sink, David rose to his feet and turned to the stranger, who was still prostrate.

"Stand up," he said.

The stranger stood.

"Where are you from?" David asked.

"I am the son of a stranger, an Amalekite."

David looked at him for a long time. The stranger's eyes were darting, calculating. He was waiting for the reward he believed was coming. He had brought the crown and the bracelet. He had removed the king who had pursued David like a dog. He had come all this way to bring the good news. Surely the new king would be grateful.

"How is it," David said slowly, "that you were not afraid to stretch out your hand to destroy the anointed of Hashem?"

The stranger's face changed.

"My lord — I — he was already dying — he asked me — "

"Your blood is on your head," David said. "Your own mouth has testified against you, saying, 'I killed the anointed of Hashem.'"

He turned to one of the young men standing near him.

"Kill him."

The young man drew his sword. The stranger opened his mouth to protest, but no words came out. A single stroke, and it was over. The body fell into the dust beside the crown and the bracelet.

The men watching the scene were silent.

There is a tradition preserved in the Midrash that this Amalekite was no ordinary man. Some say he was the son of Doeg the Edomite — the same Doeg who had been Shaul's chief herdsman, who had betrayed the priests of Nov to Shaul's wrath, who had stood by while eighty-five kohanim wearing the white linen ephod were slaughtered in a single day. Doeg's own son, this tradition suggests, had circled back at the end to finish what his father had begun. He had finished Shaul — the king who had spared Agag and thereby lost the kingdom. Amalek had taken its revenge on the House of Shaul from the beginning of Shaul's reign to the very end of it.

But in doing so, the Amalekite had sealed his own fate.

The Yalkut Shimoni asks a painful question on this scene. When Shaul told the Amalekite, "The shabatz has seized me," — "my convulsion has seized me" — what did he mean? The word shabatz is unusual. The Targum translates it simply as retita — trembling. But the Midrash notes something else: the word tashbetz appears only one other time in all of Tanach. It appears in the description of the priestly garments. "Ketonet tashbetz" — the woven tunic of the kohanim. And the Midrash reads Shaul's final words as a confession and a lament: The tashbetz has seized me. The kohanim of Nov have come back for me. The robes of the priests I killed are strangling me now.

Shaul died with the ghosts of the kohanim around his neck.

And the son of Doeg — the Amalekite — came to gloat, and fell to David's sword, and joined his father and his king and the ghosts of Nov in whatever dark place traitors and murderers go.

* * *

When night fell over Tziklag, David took up his harp.

For three days he had not touched it. Now he took it in his arms the way a man takes up a sleeping child, and he held it, and he tuned it, and he began to play.

The song he composed that night is preserved in the opening chapter of Shmuel Bet. The mefarshim call it Kinat David — David's lament — or sometimes simply Keshet, "the Bow," because David ordered that the men of Yehuda be taught it, that they might remember, that the song might pass from father to son like a piece of equipment a soldier carries into battle.

It is one of the greatest elegies in all of Hebrew literature.

"The glory, O Israel, is slain upon your high places! How are the mighty fallen! Tell it not in Gat, announce it not in the streets of Ashkelon, lest the daughters of the Pelishtim rejoice, lest the daughters of the uncircumcised exult.

Mountains of Gilboa — let there be no dew, no rain upon you, nor fields of offerings, for there the shield of the mighty was defiled, the shield of Shaul, as though he had never been anointed with oil.

Shaul and Yehonatan, beloved and pleasant in their lives, and in their death they were not divided. They were swifter than eagles, they were stronger than lions.

Daughters of Israel, weep over Shaul, who clothed you in scarlet with ornaments, who put ornaments of gold upon your garments.

How are the mighty fallen in the midst of the battle! Yehonatan, slain upon your high places — I am distressed for you, my brother Yehonatan, very pleasant have you been to me. Your love to me was wonderful, surpassing the love of women.

How are the mighty fallen, and the weapons of war perished."

The Talmud draws attention to one line: "Daughters of Israel, weep over Shaul, who clothed you in scarlet." Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Nechemyah debate its meaning. Rabbi Yehudah says: this refers to the literal daughters of Israel, whose husbands went out to war and for whom Shaul provided, clothing them in fine garments. But Rabbi Nechemyah says something deeper: "The daughters of Israel" — these are the sages of the Sanhedrin. They should weep for Shaul, because whenever Shaul would hear a chiddush from a Torah scholar, he would rise from his throne and kiss the scholar on his mouth.

This is who Shaul was.

This is the man David was weeping for.

Not a failed king. Not a persecutor. A lover of Torah, a warrior, a prince of the people, a man who kissed the mouths of Torah scholars because he loved what they said so much. That Shaul was gone. And David, who had been hunted by him, who had twice held his life in his hand and refused to take it, who had torn the corner of his robe in a cave and regretted even that — David understood what the nation had lost.

The harp fell silent in David's hands.

Behind him, the sun set over the charred walls of Tziklag.

Ahead of him — though he could not yet see it — lay the throne.
