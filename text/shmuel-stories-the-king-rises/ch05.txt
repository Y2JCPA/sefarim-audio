Chapter Five

The Road to Chevron

The first of them arrived while the bodies of Rechav and Ba'anah were still hanging beside the pool.

They came from Binyamin — a delegation of seven elders, led by an old man named Shimei who had served as a judge in the outer villages during the last years of Shaul's reign. They rode into Chevron on donkeys at mid-morning, and when they reached the square where the pool stood, they reined in their mounts and sat for a long moment looking at the two mutilated corpses swinging in the breeze.

Shimei spoke first, to the man riding beside him.

"He killed them."

"Yes."

"He did not reward them."

"No."

Shimei pulled at his beard. He was a cautious man. He had served Shaul for thirty years. He had fought in Shaul's campaigns against the Ammonim and the Moavim. He had sat on benches of judgment in the tribal territory that had produced Shaul, and he had judged Shaul's people according to Shaul's law. His loyalty to the house of Shaul had never wavered — not even during the seven years when Ish-Boshet's throne at Machanayim had been held up by nothing but Avner's sword.

But Shimei was also an honest man. And an honest man looking at the pool of Chevron could not pretend he did not understand what he was seeing.

"He killed the Amalekite at Tziklag," Shimei said slowly. "He killed the two brothers here. He mourned Avner in the streets. He walked behind the bier. He cursed Yoav for the murder. Three times he has refused the throne when it was handed to him on a plate."

"Yes."

"A man who will not accept the throne from murderers," Shimei said, "is a man who should be given the throne by free men."

He turned his donkey toward the gate of the city.

"Let us go to him."

* * *

That was the first delegation. Others followed within days.

The Chronicler, writing centuries later in Divrei HaYamim, preserved a catalog of the tribes that came to Chevron in the weeks and months following Ish-Boshet's death. It is one of the most remarkable passages in Tanach — a list of warriors, tribe by tribe, counted out in numbers so large that the mind has difficulty holding them.

From Yehuda, six thousand eight hundred men armed for war, from the tribe that had been David's base for seven and a half years. Why only six thousand? Because David did not need to count Yehuda the same way he counted the others; Yehuda had been with him from the beginning. Six thousand was symbolic — a representative force of tribal leaders, not the full muster.

From Shimon, seven thousand one hundred mighty men of valor.

From Levi, four thousand six hundred — and three thousand seven hundred more under a man named Yehoyada, who was the prince of Aharon's house. Yehoyada was the father of Benayahu — the same Benayahu who would one day become David's most formidable warrior, the one who killed lions in pits and Egyptian giants and whose deeds would fill a dedicated chapter in the sefer of the gibborim. But that comes later. At Chevron, Benayahu's father was leading three thousand seven hundred kohanim and Levi'im to declare for David.

From Binyamin — Shaul's own tribe — three thousand. Only three thousand, the Chronicler notes pointedly, "for until then the majority of them had kept allegiance to the house of Shaul." Even now, many Binyaminim were holding back. The old loyalty died hard. But three thousand was enough.

From Ephraim, twenty thousand eight hundred mighty men of valor, famous throughout their father's house.

From half of the tribe of Menashe, eighteen thousand.

And then the verse that has caught the attention of every reader of Divrei HaYamim since the book was compiled — a description of the men of Yisachar:

"And of the sons of Yisachar, men who had understanding of the times to know what Israel should do, their heads were two hundred, and all their brothers were at their command."

Two hundred.

That was all. Two hundred men from the tribe of Yisachar. No thousand, no ten thousand, no eighteen thousand like their cousins in Menashe. Two hundred leaders — and every man in the tribe followed them because those two hundred knew what Israel should do.

The mefarshim seize on this phrase. Yod'ei binah la'ittim — "those who understood the times." Rashi explains that the men of Yisachar were masters of the calendar, experts in the intercalation of months and the sanctification of the new moon, and that this astronomical knowledge extended metaphorically to a kind of political wisdom: they knew when the hour had arrived for action, and when it had not.

The Talmud goes further. The Gemara in Berachot and elsewhere identifies the sons of Yisachar as the great Torah scholars of the era — the men who filled the batei midrash, who ruled on halacha, who guided the other tribes in decisions that required discernment rather than muscle. Zevulun, their neighbor and partner, supported them materially while they studied. And when the moment came for all Israel to decide whether to crown David, the other tribes looked to Yisachar the way a confused army looks to its general staff.

What should we do?

Crown him, the sons of Yisachar said. The hour has come.

And the other tribes followed.

* * *

The Chronicler continues his list. From Zevulun, fifty thousand warriors, "keeping rank, not of double heart." From Naftali, a thousand captains and thirty-seven thousand men with shield and spear. From Dan, twenty-eight thousand six hundred. From Asher, forty thousand trained for battle. And from the tribes east of the Yarden — Reuven, Gad, and the other half of Menashe — one hundred and twenty thousand men, armed for every kind of war.

The total, when the Chronicler has finished counting, comes to more than three hundred and forty thousand men standing in the fields around Chevron.

Three hundred and forty thousand.

The little city of Chevron, with its ancient stone walls and its cave of the Avot, had never seen anything like it. The army filled the hillsides for miles in every direction. Campfires dotted the valleys all the way to the horizon. The sound of the camp — the talking, the horses, the sheep, the children, the cooking fires — was like the sound of the sea.

And the Chronicler notes, with a kind of quiet joy, that they came "with a whole heart to make David king over all Israel, and all the rest of Israel were of one heart to make David king."

One heart.

It is a phrase Chazal love. The assembly at Chevron echoes the assembly at Sinai, where Israel stood k'ish echad b'lev echad — "as one man with one heart." At Sinai, the heart was united in receiving the Torah. At Chevron, the heart was united in receiving the king. The two moments — the Torah and the monarchy — are twins in the Jewish imagination. The Torah is the soul of the nation. The king is its body. Without both, Israel is incomplete.

And for the first time in a generation, Israel was whole.

* * *

The elders came to David in the evening.

They had been deliberating among themselves for three days — three days of feasting, three days of eating and drinking in the fields around Chevron, three days of waiting for the moment to be right. The Chronicler notes the provisioning: flour, figs, raisins, wine, oil, cattle, and sheep, brought by the tribes themselves and by their neighbors as far as Yisachar and Zevulun and Naftali, loaded onto donkeys and camels and mules and oxen. "For there was joy in Israel."

But the deliberation was not a simple thing.

On the second night of the feast, a council of the tribal elders gathered in a large tent on the outskirts of the camp. Shimei of Binyamin was there. The princes of Reuven and Gad. An old captain from Ephraim named Ammihud. Two of the men of Yisachar, the ones Scripture would later describe as "those who understood the times." The air in the tent was thick with lamp smoke and the smell of roasted lamb from the feast still going on outside.

Ammihud of Ephraim spoke first.

"Let us be honest with each other, brothers. Yehuda has been at this man's side for seven and a half years. The rest of us have been at Ish-Boshet's side for most of those years, even though we knew in our hearts that Ish-Boshet was a shell of a king. Why did we hesitate? Shall we admit it?"

"Admit what?" Shimei said.

"That the house of Shaul was ours. That we followed Shaul because Shaul was the first. That the north has always been suspicious of the south, and Yehuda has always been the south. That we were afraid a Yehudite king would mean a Yehudite kingdom — and the rest of us would be tenants on our own land."

One of the Yisachar elders — a thin man with a white beard — leaned forward.

"And is David such a king? Has he shown himself to be that kind of man?"

Ammihud shook his head slowly. "No. He has not."

"Then why do we hesitate now?"

"Old habits, brother. Old habits of the heart."

Shimei set down his cup.

"May I say something to this council?"

"Speak."

"I served Shaul for thirty years. I judged his people in the outer villages. I buried sons of Binyamin who fell in Shaul's wars. There is no man here who is more bound to the house of Shaul than I am." Shimei paused. "And yet I tell you what I saw when I rode into Chevron two weeks ago. I saw two men hanging by the pool — Rechav and Ba'anah, the killers of my last lord. I saw David's curse on Yoav still hanging in the air over this city. I saw a king who had refused every shortcut to the throne."

"And?"

"And I asked myself: is this the kind of king I want my grandchildren to live under? And the answer, brothers, came back to me very clearly. Yes. I will lose no sleep passing the loyalty of my tribe to a man who will not murder to rule."

The Yisachar elder nodded slowly.

"The hour has come," he said. "This is what I have been saying to my brothers for three days. The hour has come. Delay now is not loyalty — it is cowardice dressed in old robes. We have to go to him."

"Tonight?"

"Tomorrow evening. After the third day of the feast. We walk in as one body. We do not let Yehuda do it for us, and we do not let Binyamin do it alone. We go as the twelve tribes, and we make the covenant in the presence of Hashem."

There were murmurs of assent around the circle.

Ammihud of Ephraim lifted his cup.

"To the third day, then. To the twelve tribes."

"To the king," Shimei said.

"To the king."

* * *

On the third evening, the elders of all the tribes gathered and walked together into David's hall.

Shimei of Binyamin led them. Behind him came the princes of Reuven and Gad and Yehuda and Ephraim and Menashe and all the others. They filled the hall. They stood before the throne. David rose when they entered.

The oldest of them spoke — a voice thinned by age but steady.

"Behold, we are your bone and your flesh."

David inclined his head.

"Also in time past," the elder continued, "when Shaul was king over us, you were the one who led Israel out and brought them in. And Hashem said to you, You shall shepherd My people Israel, and you shall be a ruler over Israel."

The hall was completely silent.

David knew what the elder was doing. He was laying out the three legitimations of Davidic kingship, one by one, in the order that mattered.

We are your bone and your flesh. Tribal legitimacy. David was a son of Yehuda. He was flesh of their flesh. He belonged to them.

When Shaul was king, you led us in battle. Military legitimacy. David had fought for Israel before he ruled it. They had seen him bleed for them. They had seen him bring them home in safety.

Hashem said to you, You shall shepherd My people. Divine legitimacy. The word of the prophet, spoken years ago in the courtyard of Yishai's house, now confirmed by the elders of every tribe in Israel.

Three-fold. Tribe, sword, and prophecy.

David bowed his head.

"Let Hashem establish the covenant He has spoken," he said.

* * *

A brit — a covenant — was cut that night in the presence of Hashem.

The nature of this covenant has been debated by the mefarshim for centuries. The simplest reading is that David swore to rule Israel according to the laws of the Torah, and the elders swore to accept his rule and his descendants' rule in return. The Malbim adds that the covenant included a specification of the limits of kingship — the restrictions laid down in Devarim 17:14-20, where the Torah forbids the king to multiply horses, gold, or wives beyond what is necessary. The elders of Israel wanted it written down, sworn before Hashem, that David would not become another Shaul — that he would not forget he was the servant of a Torah that was above him.

David agreed to all of it.

Oil was brought. Tzadok the kohen — or some have it as Evyatar, the survivor of Nov — took the horn of oil and poured it over David's head. The oil ran down into his beard the way oil runs down the beard of a high priest at his consecration. It was the third time David had been anointed, and the significance of the number is not lost on the mefarshim. The Talmud preserves a tradition that David's three anointings corresponded to three separate phases of kingship:

The first anointing, by Shmuel in the courtyard of Yishai, was private. It was the seed.

The second anointing, by the elders of Yehuda in Chevron seven years earlier, was partial. It was the sprout.

The third anointing, by the elders of all Israel on this night in Chevron, was complete. It was the flower.

Three anointings, over fifteen years, ending in the fullness of the crown. The seed had been planted when David was a boy with a harp tending sheep in the hills around Beit Lechem. The flower had bloomed now, when David was thirty years old and the father of six sons and the commander of an army of six hundred who had followed him through the wilderness and the cave and the battles of the civil war and the mourning at Chevron.

The elders stood and shouted as one voice.

"Yechi HaMelech! Yechi David Melech Yisrael!"

Long live the king! Long live David, king of Israel!

The shout went out of the hall and into the square and into the fields beyond the walls of Chevron, and it was picked up by the thousands of men camped on the hillsides, and it passed from one voice to the next until the entire countryside was ringing with it, and the hills of Chevron echoed the name of David the son of Yishai from the tribe of Yehuda, the anointed of Hashem, the king of all Israel.

* * *

There is a teaching in the classical sources that bears on this moment.

The sages ask: why was David made king in Chevron, and not in Yerushalayim or in Beit Lechem or in Shilo? Why this city, with its ancient stones and its cave of the patriarchs and its memory of Avraham purchasing burial ground from the Chittim?

And the sages answer: because Chevron is the city of the fourth leg of the Throne.

Chazal teach that the Merkavah — the Throne of Glory on which the Shekhinah rests — stands upon four legs. The first three legs are Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov: the three Avot, the three pillars of Israel's existence. The fourth leg was missing for a very long time. A throne with only three legs cannot stand upright. It wobbles. It tips.

The fourth leg was David.

Not David alone, but David and the Davidic dynasty — the kingdom that would eventually produce Mashiach ben David, the king who would restore the world to its original purpose. And the fourth leg had to be installed where the other three were buried. It had to be installed in Chevron, in the city of the cave, in the presence of the bones of the Avot. Only there could the throne be completed.

The night David was crowned in Chevron, the Throne of Glory itself became stable. Three legs became four.

And the mourning that had hung over the last years of Shaul's reign — the mourning that had begun with the killing of the kohanim at Nov and had continued through Gilboa and the civil war and the murder of Avner and the death of Ish-Boshet — that mourning finally ended. Not because no one grieved anymore. But because the Throne was complete, and when the Throne is complete, Israel can weep without despair, because the foundation is no longer shaking.

* * *

David reigned in Chevron over all Israel for six more months.

Scripture records the duration with care. "In Chevron he reigned over Yehuda seven years and six months. And in Yerushalayim he reigned thirty-three years over all Israel and Yehuda." The seven and a half years in Chevron divided into two unequal parts: seven years as king of Yehuda alone, and six more months as king of all Israel from the same city. Why the extra six months? Because David did not immediately move his capital. He did not leap up from the anointing and rush north to a new city. He stayed in Chevron — the city of the Avot, the city where the covenant had been cut, the city of his grandfathers — for another half-year.

Why?

The Radak suggests that David was preparing. He was consolidating the tribes. He was integrating the armies of Israel with the armies of Yehuda. He was building the administrative apparatus of a unified kingdom. A king cannot simply move his throne from one city to another; he has to bring his people with him, and he has to decide where he is going.

And David had already decided where he was going.

He was going to Yerushalayim.

The city that had resisted conquest since the days of Yehoshua. The city that sat on the border between Binyamin and Yehuda, politically neutral, claimed by neither tribe. The city that had belonged to the Yevusi for centuries, and that every Israelite general who had looked at it had dismissed as unconquerable. The city with the spring of the Gichon at its base and the heights of Moriah at its crown. The city where — though David himself did not yet know the full extent of this — Adam had been created from the dust, Noach had offered his first sacrifice, Avraham had bound his son on an altar, and the fourth leg of the Throne would eventually rise above the Holy of Holies.

Yerushalayim was waiting.

And David was coming.

* * *

The last scene of Chapter 5 belongs to a man who was not in Chevron on the night of the crowning. He was on the other side of the Yarden, hiding in a small house in a town called Lo-Devar, in the household of a wealthy man named Makhir ben Ammiel. He was eleven years old. He had been there for six years, ever since the terrible day when his nurse had picked him up and run from Yizre'el and dropped him on the rocks of the road.

Mefivoshet ben Yehonatan was the last son of the house of Shaul.

He could not walk properly. He had been hidden by friends of his father's house since the day of Gilboa, because his life would have been in danger from anyone who wanted to clear the field for a new dynasty. He had grown up knowing that his grandfather had been king, his father had been a prince, and he himself was nothing. Nobody. A crippled child in a borrowed house in a town whose name meant "no word" — Lo-Devar, the place that had nothing to say.

When the news reached Lo-Devar that David had been crowned king of all Israel in Chevron, Makhir ben Ammiel called the boy to him.

"Mefivoshet."

"Yes, master."

"You know what this means."

The boy nodded.

"It means you must never leave this house. Do you understand? Not for any reason. If David sends men to find you, they will find you. But you will not go looking for him. And if a stranger comes asking who you are, you will say you are a servant of the house of Makhir. You will not speak your father's name. You will not speak your grandfather's name. You will be invisible."

"Yes, master."

Makhir looked at the boy for a long time.

"Your father loved David," he said finally. "Did you know that?"

Mefivoshet shook his head.

"He loved him more than a brother. He made a covenant with David. The last thing he said to him was, Go in peace, because we have sworn, the two of us, in the name of Hashem. Do you know what that means?"

"No, master."

"It means," Makhir said, "that one day — maybe not this year, maybe not next year — a messenger may come from David the king, asking if any of the house of Yehonatan is still alive. And if that day comes, you will not be afraid. Because the covenant between David and your father is still binding, even though your father is dead. And David is a man who keeps covenants."

Mefivoshet said nothing.

He was eleven years old. He did not understand about covenants. He only understood that his grandfather was dead, his father was dead, his uncles were dead, and he lived in a borrowed house in a town called No Word.

But many years from now, a messenger would come. And the boy who could not walk would be carried to the palace in Yerushalayim and seated at the king's table, and he would eat the king's food every day for the rest of his life, because David ben Yishai kept the covenant he had sworn to Yehonatan ben Shaul on a moonless night outside the walls of Shaul's camp. That scene belongs to a later chapter — Chapter 9 of Shmuel Bet — and we are not there yet.

But it is coming.

Because the king who was crowned at Chevron was not the kind of king who forgets his promises.
