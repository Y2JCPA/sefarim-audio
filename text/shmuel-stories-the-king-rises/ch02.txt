Chapter Two

The Pool at Giv'on

The journey from Tziklag to Chevron took three days.

David rode at the head of the column on a dark mule. His two wives rode behind him — Achinoam of Yizre'el and Avigayil, who had once been Naval's wife and was now his — and behind them came the six hundred, and behind the six hundred came the households of the six hundred, the wives and children and herds and servants, a small nation on the move. They did not sing as they traveled. The ashes of Gilboa were still in their throats.

Before setting out, David had done what he always did when the path forward was unclear.

He had asked.

"Shall I go up into one of the cities of Yehuda?"

Aleh. Go up.

"Where shall I go?"

Chevronah. To Chevron.

The Urim VeTumim had spoken through the ephod of Evyatar the kohen — Evyatar, the sole survivor of the slaughter at Nov, the young man who had fled to David with the ephod clutched in his hand while his father and brothers were dying on Shaul's command. Evyatar had been David's spiritual counselor ever since. And now the ephod directed David to Chevron.

Why Chevron?

The mefarshim ask the question, and their answers weave together like the cords of a rope. Chevron was the burial place of the Avot — Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, and their wives. It was the city that had been promised to Kalev for his loyalty in the desert. It was the geographical and spiritual heart of the tribe of Yehuda. And, the Gemara adds something more remarkable: Chevron was the city where David would begin to complete the throne.

The Throne of Glory, Chazal teach, stands on four legs. Three of the legs were planted by the Avot — Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov. But the fourth leg had been waiting. It had been waiting for a very long time. It would be planted by David ben Yishai, the shepherd from Beit Lechem who was now riding toward Chevron with the ashes of Tziklag still on his sandals.

The first act of planting the fourth leg would take place in the city of the patriarchs.

* * *

They arrived on the third day.

The elders of Yehuda came out to meet them. They had heard of the battle at Gilboa. They had heard of David's lament. They had heard — and this was no small thing — how David had dealt with the Amalekite who brought the crown. A man who kills a messenger for touching the anointed of Hashem is a man who takes the anointing seriously. The elders of Yehuda took it seriously too.

The anointing ceremony took place in the square beneath the walls of the city.

An elder named Avinadav — Radak identifies him as one of the leading men of the tribe — stepped forward with a horn of oil. He tipped the horn over David's head. The oil ran down David's temples and into his beard.

"Long live David," Avinadav said, "king over the house of Yehuda."

The cry went up from the gathered men.

"Long live the king!"

David was thirty years old.

He had been anointed once before — by Shmuel, in the courtyard of Yishai his father, while his brothers looked on in astonishment. That first anointing had been private, secret, prophetic. It had been the seed. This second anointing, in Chevron, was the first sprout breaking the soil.

There would be a third anointing seven years later, when all Israel would come to Chevron. But that is the end of the arc. We are at the beginning.

* * *

The news of David's anointing spread fast.

It reached the ears of the elders of Yavesh Gil'ad, the city across the Yarden that had retrieved Shaul's mutilated body from the wall of Beit Shan and given it honorable burial. David sent messengers to them with a message of gratitude: "Blessed are you of Hashem that you have shown this kindness to your lord Shaul and have buried him. And now, may Hashem show kindness and truth to you, and I also will do you this good because you have done this thing."

The gesture was perfect. It was also shrewd. Yavesh Gil'ad was Shaul's territory, loyal to his house, emotionally bound to his memory. David was reaching across the grave of Gilboa and extending his hand.

But the hand was not taken.

Because the news of David's anointing had also reached a man named Avner ben Ner, and Avner ben Ner was not interested in extending hands across graves. Avner ben Ner was interested in keeping Shaul's kingdom in Shaul's family, and the kingdom of Shaul in the hands of Avner ben Ner.

* * *

Avner was Shaul's cousin. He was also the commander of Shaul's army — the man who had trained the troops, led the campaigns, sat at the king's right hand during battle, and ridden ahead of the column when the king moved between cities. When David had killed Golyat in the valley of Elah, it was Avner who had brought him into Shaul's presence. When Shaul had pursued David through the wilderness, it was Avner who had slept beside the king, within the circle of the camp, and had failed to notice when David and Avishai crept between them in the night and took the king's spear and water jug without harming a hair on his head.

David had mocked Avner that night.

"Are you not a man?" he had called across the valley. "And who is like you in Israel? Why then have you not kept watch over your lord the king? For one of the people came in to destroy the king your lord. This thing that you have done is not good. By the life of Hashem, you are sons of death, because you have not guarded your lord, the anointed of Hashem. See now, where is the king's spear, and the jug of water that was at his head?"

The Gemara in Sanhedrin reads this moment as the beginning of Avner's doom. For Avner, the Gemara says, was a great man. A mighty warrior. Ish milchamah mi-ne'urav. And yet — with all his greatness — Avner had failed in one terrible way. He had not protested the killing of the kohanim at Nov. When Shaul had ordered Doeg the Edomite to slaughter the eighty-five men wearing the linen ephod, Avner had stood by in silence. He had not said a word to stop it. And the Gemara declares: For this, Avner would die.

Silence in the face of injustice has a cost. Avner would pay it — not yet, but soon.

For now, Avner had another project.

He found Ish-Boshet in a small house on the outskirts of the camp at Yavesh Gil'ad, where the son of Shaul had been staying since the burial of his father's bones. Ish-Boshet was sitting on a low stool, staring at the wall.

"Cousin," Avner said.

Ish-Boshet looked up. His eyes were red.

"Cousin, stand up. We are leaving."

"Where are we going?"

"To Machanayim."

"Why?"

"Because Machanayim is on the far side of the Yarden, and the Pelishtim cannot reach you there. Because the tribes of Gil'ad are loyal to your father's house. Because we need a place to rebuild."

"Rebuild what, Avner? My father is dead. My brothers are dead. The army is scattered. There is nothing to rebuild."

Avner crouched down in front of him so their eyes were level.

"There is a kingdom to rebuild. You are the last son of Shaul. The crown of your father is not going to pass to a shepherd from Beit Lechem while you are still alive."

"David has already been anointed king of Yehuda. I heard it yesterday."

"Of Yehuda only. One tribe out of twelve. Eleven tribes are still waiting. They will follow me if I tell them to follow me. And I will tell them to follow you."

"I do not want the crown, Avner."

Avner's voice hardened.

"That is not a thing you get to decide. Your father died wearing it. You are his son. Stand up."

Ish-Boshet stood up slowly.

"Good. Now listen to me. When we reach Machanayim, I will call together the elders of Gil'ad and the remnant of the army. You will stand before them. You will say the words I tell you to say. You will put on the crown when I hand it to you. And you will not weep in front of them. Do you understand?"

"Yes, cousin."

"Say it again. With your back straight."

"Yes, cousin."

"Better."

* * *

In Machanayim — the place where Yaakov Avinu had seen the angels of Hashem encamped on both sides of him — Avner gathered the elders in the square outside the governor's hall. They were old men, most of them, veterans of Shaul's early campaigns. They remembered Shaul the way a whole generation of Israelites remembered him: as the first king, the tall one, the warrior who had delivered them from the Ammonim, the man who had kissed Torah scholars on the mouth for sharing a good word.

Avner placed a circlet of gold on Ish-Boshet's head.

"Behold," Avner said, and his voice was louder than it needed to be in that small square. "The king of Israel."

The elders were silent for a long moment. Then one old man — a captain named Ittai, who had fought under Shaul at Michmas — stepped forward and went down on one knee.

"Yechi hamelech," he said.

"Long live the king," the others repeated after him.

Ish-Boshet stood in the center of the square with the crown on his head and his hands trembling at his sides.

He did not say a word.

* * *

Ish-Boshet was not a king.

He was Shaul's youngest surviving son, and his true name was Eshba'al — "man of the master" — but Scripture calls him Ish-Boshet, "man of shame," because the name ba'al had been corrupted by idol-worshipers into an association with the Canaanite deity, and the Tanach would not let an Israelite prince be called by a name shared with an abomination. Even in his name, Ish-Boshet carried a kind of shadow.

He was weak.

He had not fought at Gilboa. He had not stood with his father at the last battle. Whatever his reasons — perhaps Shaul had left him behind to guard the family, perhaps he had been too young, perhaps Avner had kept him in reserve as a dynastic insurance policy — the result was that Ish-Boshet was alive while his brothers were dead. And now, in Machanayim, with Avner pulling the strings, Ish-Boshet wore a crown that had never belonged to him.

The Malbim, with characteristic precision, points out the asymmetry. David had been chosen by Hashem, anointed by a prophet, proven in battle, and confirmed by the ephod. Ish-Boshet had been chosen by his cousin, anointed by a general, proven in nothing, and confirmed by nobody. The two kings were not of the same species.

But the people of Israel did not know that yet. Most of them had never seen David. They had seen Shaul. They had fought under Avner. They had grown up in a kingdom where the name of Shaul meant king. When Avner stood up and said Ish-Boshet is your king, eleven tribes followed him — not because they loved Ish-Boshet, not because they despised David, but because Avner told them to, and they had been following Avner all their lives.

The civil war began without a formal declaration.

It began with two men standing on opposite banks of a river, each wearing a crown.

* * *

For a while, nothing happened.

The author of Shmuel Bet tells us that David reigned over Yehuda in Chevron for seven years and six months, and Ish-Boshet reigned over Israel for two years. The discrepancy is famous. How can David reign for seven and a half years and Ish-Boshet for only two? The mefarshim offer a simple answer: for the first five and a half years, the throne of Israel stood empty. Ish-Boshet was not crowned immediately. Avner spent those years consolidating power, moving Ish-Boshet between towns, building alliances, waiting for the moment to strike. Only in the sixth year did Avner bring Ish-Boshet to Machanayim and crown him. And then — only then — the war began in earnest.

The first battle took place at a pool.

A place called Giv'on, in the territory of Binyamin, a few miles north of Yerushalayim. There was a pool there — a deep rock-cut cistern that held water year-round — and it was by this pool that Avner ben Ner and Yoav ben Tzeruyah met one spring afternoon, each leading a detachment of soldiers, each unwilling to back down, each aware that the tension between the two kingdoms could not be sustained much longer.

They sat on opposite sides of the pool and stared at each other across the water.

Avner spoke first.

"Let the young men rise up," he called, "and play before us."

Yoav understood him immediately. It was an ancient kind of proposal — a battle of champions, a contained duel, a way to resolve a conflict without throwing two full armies at each other. Golyat had once proposed the same thing to the army of Shaul in the valley of Elah. Avner was proposing it now.

"Let them rise up," Yoav called back.

Twelve men from each side rose and walked around the pool and stood face to face in the open ground between the two armies. Twelve from the servants of Ish-Boshet, all from the tribe of Binyamin. Twelve from the servants of David. The number twelve was not accidental. Avner was saying, in the language of symbol: We represent all Israel. Not you. We are the twelve tribes.

The men from Yehuda and the men from Binyamin stared at each other across the grass. Some of them knew each other. Israel was a small country; a man from the hill country of Yehuda might easily have a cousin from the hill country of Binyamin, and more than one of the twenty-four had grown up in villages close enough that they had once shared festival meals.

One of the Binyaminim — a broad-shouldered man with a scar across one cheek — spoke first.

"Micha'el. Is that you?"

On the other side, a man from Yehuda lowered his spear slightly.

"Yehonadav?"

"It is."

"I thought you were in Shilo."

"I was. I came home when Shaul called us."

Micha'el shifted his weight. "My mother's sister is still married to your uncle."

"I know."

"Yehonadav — we do not have to do this."

"Micha'el. Look around us. Two armies. Avner on one side, Yoav on the other. If we do not do this, they will do it with us in it. We are the lucky ones. At least we will die quickly."

"We could refuse."

"And be executed by our own commanders for cowardice? No. Come. Let us finish it."

The twenty-four men drew their swords.

Each man grabbed his opponent by the beard with his left hand. Each man drove his sword into his opponent's side with his right. They did it at the same moment. Every single one of them.

Twenty-four bodies fell by the pool at the same instant.

The chapter records that the place was called afterward Chelkat HaTzurim — the Field of the Sharp Edges — because of what happened there. The Ralbag offers a chilling interpretation: they gripped each other's beards so that neither could retreat, and they stabbed each other so precisely that no one survived to claim victory. It was a duel in which everyone lost.

And when the twenty-four bodies had fallen, and neither side could claim the championship, the two armies did what armies do when the rules fail. They charged each other.

* * *

The battle that followed was long and bloody and indecisive.

By the end of the day, David's men had the upper hand. Three hundred and sixty of Avner's men lay dead on the field. Nineteen of Yoav's men had fallen. It was a clear tactical defeat for Avner, but not a decisive one. And then, as the sun was sinking and Avner's forces were in retreat, a young warrior came running across the field toward Avner with the speed of a hunting cat.

His name was Asa'el.

He was Yoav's brother. The third of the sons of Tzeruyah, David's sister. And the Scripture describes him with a memorable phrase: "Asa'el was light-footed, like one of the gazelles in the field." He could run faster than any man in either army. When he saw Avner fleeing, he made up his mind: he would catch Avner, and he would kill Avner, and his name would be the name that ended the war.

Avner glanced over his shoulder and saw the gazelle gaining on him.

"Is that you, Asa'el?"

"It is."

"Turn aside from following me. Turn to your right or your left, and take hold of one of the young men, and take his armor for yourself."

It was a warning. It was an act of mercy. Avner, older and more experienced, was telling the younger man: I do not want to kill you. Go find an easier opponent.

But Asa'el would not turn aside.

Avner tried again.

"Turn aside from following me! Why should I strike you to the ground? How would I show my face to Yoav your brother?"

Asa'el kept running.

And when Asa'el was within a spear's length of Avner, Avner did a thing that every soldier present would remember for the rest of his life. He did not turn around and fight. He did not slash with his sword. Instead — without even looking back — he thrust backward with the butt end of his spear, the blunt rear end, driving it behind him at waist level.

The butt of the spear went straight through Asa'el's belly and out the other side.

The gazelle fell.

The men who came running after — Yoav and Avishai at the head of a small company — found Asa'el lying in his own blood beside the spear that had killed him. He was already dead. They stood there looking down at him, and all the men who came up to that place stopped and stood still, because something about Asa'el's body lying there made men stop.

Yoav did not weep.

Yoav did something worse than weeping. Yoav looked down at his brother's body and he memorized the face of Avner ben Ner in his mind, and he made a promise to himself, silent and private, that he would keep for the rest of his life.

Avner had killed Asa'el in self-defense. That was clear. The Gemara in Sanhedrin would later invoke the principle of rodef — the pursuer — to justify what Avner had done. Asa'el had been the aggressor. Asa'el had refused to turn aside. Asa'el had been warned twice. Avner had acted within his rights.

But the Gemara adds something devastating. A rodef, even when he is legitimately killed, leaves a debt of blood. The killer is not a murderer, but the blood still cries out. And the next of kin — the go'el ha-dam — has rights that the Torah does not revoke merely because the killing was justified. Yoav, as Asa'el's brother, became the avenger of the blood in that moment. He had the legal standing to pursue Avner.

He would use that standing.

Not today. Not tomorrow. But one day.

* * *

The sun set over the field of Giv'on.

Avner ordered a retreat, and Yoav — with his brother's body at his feet — did not pursue. The two armies disengaged. The chapter closes with a haunting sentence: "And they walked all night, and they came to Chevron at daybreak." The light rose on David's city as his men carried their dead home. The Midrash reads this line as a prophecy: "And the light rose on them in Chevron" — meaning that even in the darkest hour of the civil war, light was already rising on the Davidic kingdom. The king was in Chevron. And the light was beginning to rise.

But the war was not over.

Far from it. The chapter ends with a grim summary: "And the war was long between the house of Shaul and the house of David, and David grew stronger and stronger, and the house of Shaul grew weaker and weaker." Years of fighting lay ahead. Cities would burn. Men would die. And in Chevron, David would build his household slowly — six sons born to six wives, each one a political alliance, each one a seed of both strength and future trouble.

Yoav was already watching for his moment.

Avner was already planning his next move.

And Ish-Boshet — weak, frightened, the reluctant instrument of his cousin's ambition — was already beginning to unravel.
