Chapter Six

The Blind and the Lame

The city stood on the mountain as it had stood for a thousand years.

From the south, approaching along the ridge road from Chevron, a traveler would see it rising out of the heat-haze in the late afternoon: a fortified hill, narrower than it was long, with the spring of the Gichon at its base and the height of Moriah at its crown. On the western side the valley of Hinnom dropped away sharply. On the eastern side the Kidron valley fell even more sharply. The hill was a natural fortress — water below, cliffs on three sides, a single narrow approach from the north. For a thousand years, no army of Israel had been able to take it.

Not Yehoshua, who had conquered every other stronghold in Canaan but left the Yevusi in their fortress unharmed. Not the tribe of Yehuda in the days of the shoftim, who had burned the lower city but had never dislodged the Yevusi from the heights. Not Shaul, who had marched past the city many times on his way to other campaigns but had never laid siege to it. The Yevusim had sat in their mountain fortress through every political upheaval of the last half-millennium, secure in their walls and — as the mefarshim preserve for us — secure in something older than walls.

They were secure in an oath.

* * *

A few days after the great coronation at Chevron, David summoned his war council.

Yoav was there. Avishai. Benayahu ben Yehoyada, newly arrived from the head of the Levite contingent, already beginning to earn his reputation. Tzadok and Evyatar, the two kohanim, sitting quietly at the far end of the room with the ephod between them. A few tribal captains representing the larger delegations.

"We are going to take Yerushalayim," David said.

There was a long silence.

Yoav broke it. "You know what they say about Yerushalayim."

"I know what they say."

"For five hundred years our fathers have left it alone."

"I know that too."

"And the walls of the metzudah" — Yoav meant the inner citadel, the stronghold on the height — "the walls of the metzudah have never been breached. Not by anyone."

David looked at him. "That is why we will take it."

He stood and walked to the window of the hall. From this angle he could see north, toward the hill country of Binyamin. Somewhere up that road, beyond the next ridge and the next, lay the city he had been thinking about for years — since long before the civil war, since before Gilboa, since he was a young man running from Shaul and hiding in caves and composing psalms about a city of Hashem that he had never actually seen.

"A king needs a capital," David said, still looking north. "A capital that does not belong to any one tribe. If I stay in Chevron, I am the king of Yehuda only, and the other tribes will always see me that way. If I go to Machanayim or to Shekhem, I become the king of Efrayim or Menashe, and Yehuda will resent me. Yerushalayim sits between Yehuda and Binyamin. It belongs to neither. It will belong to the king alone."

"It belongs to the Yevusi," Yoav said.

"Not for much longer."

* * *

The army set out three days later.

The march to Yerushalayim was short — only a day and a half up the ridge road from Chevron. David brought with him the elite of the gibborim, the six hundred who had followed him through the wilderness, reinforced by contingents from the tribes that had been at Chevron for the coronation. The whole force moved quickly, in good order, and they camped on the evening of the second day on the heights opposite the city, looking across the Hinnom valley at the walls of the metzudah.

The Yevusi had seen them coming.

From the gates of the city, across the valley, a group of men emerged and stood on the wall in full view of David's camp. They were shouting — not at close range, but at the kind of distance where men can make themselves heard only by cupping their hands around their mouths and hurling the words across open air.

"You will not come in here!"

The shout carried across the Hinnom. David's men, standing in their camp, heard it clearly.

"Even the blind and the lame will turn you away!"

And then — and this is the part that the mefarshim have puzzled over for a thousand years — the Yevusi did something strange. They carried two objects up onto the wall and placed them in plain view so that everyone in David's camp could see them. Two figures. Life-sized. Made of bronze or copper.

One figure had no eyes — it was blind.

The other figure had withered legs — it was lame.

On the wall of the metzudah of Tzion, the Yevusi had set up two idols. And in the mouths of the idols, as the Midrash preserves it, were written words.

* * *

What did the idols say?

The Radak, citing an older midrashic tradition, gives us the answer. The idols were inscribed with the text of an oath. Not just any oath. Avraham's oath to Avimelech, king of Gerar.

Four hundred years before David was born, the Torah records in Bereishit 21 that Avraham had traveled to the land of the Pelishtim and had made a covenant with Avimelech, the local king. The covenant involved a specific promise: Avraham swore that he would not deal falsely with Avimelech or with his son or with his grandson. Avraham raised his hand to Heaven and swore. The covenant was sealed with seven sheep, and the place was named Be'er Sheva — the Well of the Oath.

The Yevusi of Yerushalayim, according to the midrash, were descendants of Avimelech. They were not, strictly speaking, one of the seven Canaanite nations that Israel had been commanded to destroy. They were descendants of a Pelishti king with whom Avraham had sworn a treaty. And the treaty was still in force.

The mefarshim note the chilling effect of this tradition on the history of the conquest. When Yehoshua led Israel into the land, he conquered most of the territory — but he could not conquer Yerushalayim, because the oath of Avraham was still binding. Not to Avimelech — Avimelech had been dead for centuries. But to his son, and his grandson. At the time of Yehoshua, Avimelech's grandson was still alive. The oath had not yet expired.

By the time of David — four centuries later — the grandson had been dead for a long time. The oath had technically lapsed. But the Yevusi did not know that the oath had lapsed. Or they did not care. Or, worse, they believed that the oath was still binding on David, even if they themselves had no standing to enforce it.

And so they placed the two idols on the wall.

The idol of the blind man was Yitzchak, whose eyes had grown dim in his old age. The idol of the lame man was Yaakov, who had been struck on the thigh by the angel at the ford of the Yabbok and had walked with a limp ever since. Two of the three Avot, represented in their physical defects, standing on the wall of Yerushalayim — and on their mouths, the words of the oath that Avraham had sworn on behalf of all his descendants.

The message to David was as precise as a knife.

You cannot enter this city. Your own grandfather swore it.

* * *

David's men stared at the walls.

A murmur went through the camp. Some of the tribal captains understood the symbolism immediately; others had to have it explained to them. When the full meaning had sunk in, a kind of paralysis settled over the army. The Yevusi had not deployed archers or slingers or walls of fire. They had deployed something older and more terrible than any of those things. They had deployed the word of Avraham Avinu. And what Israelite would want to be the man who broke the word of Avraham?

Yoav looked at David.

"What do we do?"

David said nothing for a long moment. He was staring at the two figures on the wall. Then he spoke.

"The oath was to Avimelech and his son and his grandson. Tell me — does Avimelech's grandson live today?"

"No."

"When did he die?"

"Generations ago. Before the kings of Israel."

"Then the oath has expired."

"They do not seem to agree."

David turned to Evyatar the kohen. "What does the Torah say?"

Evyatar — who had been consulting the ephod quietly in the corner — looked up. "The Torah says what it says. Avraham swore to Avimelech, his son, and his grandson. Three generations. Not four. Not five. The oath has been expired for centuries. The Yevusi have no legal standing. They are squatters on a contract that no longer exists."

"And Hashem?"

Evyatar lifted the ephod. The ephod, with its twelve stones, each bearing the name of a tribe, began to glow in the dim light of the tent. Certain letters lit up — the letters of the Urim VeTumim, spelling out an answer. Evyatar read the answer carefully, and then he looked at David.

"Hashem says: Take the city."

"You are sure?"

"Completely."

David turned to Yoav.

"Take the city."

* * *

The Targum Yonatan — the ancient Aramaic translation of Tanach — reads the phrase "the blind and the lame" in an entirely different way than the pshat seems to suggest. Targum Yonatan translates it as "chata'aya ve-chayavaya" — "sinners and the guilty." In this reading, the Yevusi were not mocking David with a reference to physical disabilities. They were mocking him with a reference to spiritual ones. The message was: You will not enter this city with an army full of sinners. Only the perfect can enter Yerushalayim.

This reading is beloved by the homilists, and it contains a truth that the literal reading misses: Yerushalayim is not merely a city to be conquered by military force. It is the city of the Temple, the city of the Shekhinah, the city where Heaven and earth meet. A king can capture it with swords, but a king can only hold it with righteousness. The Yevusi were daring David to prove that his army was worthy of the city.

And David accepted the dare — in both senses. He would take the city by force, and then he would hold it by righteousness. Both were necessary.

* * *

Yoav called together the captains of the thousands.

"Listen carefully. There is only one way into the metzudah that has never been tried. The Yevusim trust the walls and they trust the oath. They do not trust the water."

"The water?"

"There is a shaft."

Yoav had been studying the topography of Yerushalayim for months — perhaps for years. He was, whatever else he was, one of the most gifted military engineers in the ancient world. He had identified something that no previous commander had noticed or dared to exploit: the spring of the Gichon, at the base of the eastern cliff, was connected to the interior of the city by a vertical water shaft. The shaft had been cut centuries earlier by the Yevusim themselves, so that the women of the city could draw water in safety during a siege. A leather bucket on a long rope would be lowered down the shaft from inside the fortress, filled at the pool fed by the spring, and hauled back up.

The shaft was narrow. It was slick with algae. It rose almost vertically into the heart of the metzudah. And it was unguarded.

"A man who could climb that shaft," Yoav said, "would come up inside the walls."

"A man would have to climb it in the dark."

"Yes."

"And with no armor."

"Yes."

"And if he fell, he would die."

"Yes."

Yoav looked around the tent at the gathered captains.

"David has made an offer. Whoever is the first to strike down a Yevusi will become the head of the army and the commander. That is the promise. I am going to take that shaft tomorrow night."

There was a stirring. Avishai stood up.

"I will go with you."

Benayahu ben Yehoyada stood up next. "And I."

"Good," Yoav said. "Tomorrow night. When the moon is down."

* * *

The climb up the shaft of the Gichon is not described in detail in the Tanach. Scripture gives us only the phrase — "whoever strikes down a Yevusi and reaches the tzinor" — and leaves the rest to the imagination of the reader. The Targum and the Ralbag fill in some details: the tzinor was a water conduit or pipe, possibly a vertical shaft, possibly a horizontal gutter at the base of the wall. Whatever its exact geometry, it was a hidden way into the city, and Yoav took it.

Picture it if you can. A new moon night. Complete darkness. Three men — Yoav, Avishai, and a handful of volunteers — wading through the shallow pool at the base of the Gichon spring in wet clothes, feeling along the cliff face with their hands until they found the opening of the shaft. A rope dropped from above by the Yevusim during the day to bring up water; but at night the rope was pulled in, and the shaft was empty. Yoav drove wooden pegs into the walls of the shaft as he climbed, hammering them home as quietly as he could. His fingers would have been raw within minutes. His arms would have been burning. The stone would have been slick with water and moss.

Meter by meter, he climbed.

Eventually he emerged — wet, exhausted, armed only with a short sword — into the inner courtyard of the metzudah of Tzion. The guards had been posted on the walls, not on the water-shaft. They had not dreamed that anyone could climb that way.

Yoav killed the nearest one before the man could cry out.

Then the next.

Then he ran for the main gate, and behind him came Avishai, and behind Avishai came the others, and when they reached the gate they threw back the bolts and lifted the heavy beam and opened it from the inside.

Outside the gate, David's army was waiting in silence. When the gate swung open, they came in like a flood.

* * *

The fall of the city was over before the sun rose.

The Yevusim, caught inside their own walls, had no time to mount a defense. Some fled through the postern gates and escaped into the hill country. Others surrendered. A few died. The inner citadel — the metzudah of Tzion — was taken with minimal bloodshed, because the Yevusim had trusted the walls and the idols too much to prepare a proper defense for the interior of the city.

As the sun rose over the Kidron valley, David walked through the gate of Yerushalayim for the first time.

He did not stop at the outer walls. He walked all the way to the center, to the heights of the metzudah, and he stood where he had dreamed of standing for years. The idols of the blind and the lame were still on the wall behind him. He would have them torn down later that day — but he did not rush. He walked to the highest point of the city and he looked out across the valleys and the hills and he knew what he was seeing.

He was seeing the place where the fourth leg of the Throne would stand.

North of where he stood, on the heights of Moriah, there was a threshing floor belonging to a man named Aravnah the Yevusi. David did not know yet — or perhaps he did — that the threshing floor was the place where Avraham had bound Yitzchak on an altar, the place where Yaakov had dreamed of a ladder reaching to Heaven, the place where, long before any of this, Adam had been gathered from the dust. The place would not come fully into David's possession until the final chapters of Shmuel Bet, during the plague and the encounter with the angel. That scene belongs to the end of Volume Two.

But the seed was planted on the morning David walked into Yerushalayim.

The city had changed hands. It would never change hands again in the same way. For the next four hundred years, until the day the walls would finally fall to Nebuchadnezzar, Yerushalayim would be the city of David and of David's descendants. The Beit HaMikdash would be built here by David's son. The prophets would walk these streets. The psalms that David was composing would be sung here, generation after generation, by choirs of Levi'im on the steps of the Temple that David would not live to see but would plan for as carefully as he planned this conquest.

* * *

A new name was given to the city that morning.

It had been Yerushalem or Yevus before. David added a word. He called it Ir David — the City of David — and the name stuck. For the next three thousand years, every time a Jew would say the words Ir David, they would be referring to the fortress that Yoav took at night through the water-shaft, under a new moon, against an ancient oath that had already expired.

And the Scripture records the sentence that ends Chapter 5 of Shmuel Bet — a sentence that closes the arc of The King Rises with the quiet solemnity of a door being sealed behind a man who has just come home.

"And David went on and grew great, and Hashem the God of hosts was with him."

* * *

The arc is complete.

David began this arc as a fugitive in Tziklag, sitting on a burned tree stump with a silent harp, weeping for a king who had hunted him and a friend who had loved him. He ends the arc as the anointed king of all Israel, standing on the heights of Yerushalayim, looking north toward the threshing floor where his son would build the House of Hashem.

The road between those two points ran through a great deal of blood. The Amalekite at Tziklag. Asa'el at Giv'on. Avner in the gateway of Chevron. Ish-Boshet in his bed at Machanayim. Rechav and Ba'anah at the pool of Chevron. And now, the nameless Yevusim who fell in the darkness when Yoav came up through the water shaft. Every step of David's path to the throne had been paved with death — but none of the deaths had been David's doing. That was the strange and lonely accomplishment of these five chapters. David had not killed to become king. He had wept, and he had waited, and he had refused every shortcut.

And at the end of the waiting, the throne had come to him.

The next arc of this book — The Aron Comes Home — begins with David in Yerushalayim, determined to bring the Ark of the Covenant up into the city he has just conquered. It is a story with its own terrors and its own triumphs, and it ends with the promise that David's house will endure forever — a promise spoken through the prophet Natan, a promise that would stand against every exile and every destruction and every generation that would follow.

But that arc begins where this one ends.

On the heights of the Ir David, with the morning sun on David's face, and the idols of the blind and lame still silent on the wall behind him.
