Chapter Three

The Gate of Chevron

Ritzpah bat Aiyah was Shaul's concubine.

She was a quiet woman, older than most of Shaul's other women by the time of the battle at Gilboa, and she had borne Shaul two sons — Armoni and Mefivoshet, not to be confused with Yehonatan's lame son of the same name. When Shaul fell at Gilboa, Ritzpah did what widowed concubines of fallen kings had always done: she withdrew into the women's quarters of the palace at Machanayim, covered her head, and waited for someone to tell her what came next.

What came next was Avner ben Ner.

The chapter does not describe exactly what took place between Avner and Ritzpah — Scripture is famously reticent about such matters — but Ish-Boshet came to believe that his cousin, the commander of his army, had taken his father's concubine for himself. In the political grammar of the ancient Near East, this was not a private matter. A king's concubines were part of the royal household. To take them was to claim the throne.

Avshalom, years later, would stage exactly this gesture on the roof of the palace in Yerushalayim, laying with David's concubines in full view of the city to declare himself king. When Avner — or so Ish-Boshet believed — took Ritzpah, it was either an actual attempt to claim the throne or close enough that Ish-Boshet could not afford to ignore it.

One morning in the spring of the sixth year, Ish-Boshet summoned his cousin.

* * *

"Why," Ish-Boshet demanded, "have you gone in to my father's concubine?"

Avner was a large man. He wore his armor even in the presence of the king, even indoors, even during breakfast. His beard was grey at the edges. He had killed men in battle before Ish-Boshet had been weaned. When Ish-Boshet — small, soft-handed, frightened — spoke those words, Avner did not blink. He simply looked at his cousin across the table, and then he spoke, and his voice was very quiet.

"Am I a dog's head?"

"What?"

"A dog's head. Belonging to Yehuda." The quiet was gone now. Avner's voice was rising. "Today I do kindness to the house of Shaul your father, to his brothers and to his friends, and I have not delivered you into the hand of David. And you charge me today with an offense concerning this woman?"

Ish-Boshet opened his mouth. No words came.

"God do so to Avner and more also," Avner thundered, "if I do not do to David as Hashem has sworn to him — to transfer the kingdom from the house of Shaul, and to establish the throne of David over Israel and over Yehuda, from Dan to Be'er Sheva!"

Ish-Boshet's face went white.

"And Ish-Boshet could not answer Avner another word," the verse says, "because he was afraid of him."

The Yeshivat Har Etzion commentary notes the terrible irony buried in this scene. Ish-Boshet was afraid of Avner. He had always been afraid of Avner. Avner was the reason he wore a crown at all. Avner could take the crown from him as easily as he had given it. And now, in a moment of temper, Avner was making a vow — a vow before Heaven — to do exactly that.

The thing that should strike us is what Avner does not say. He does not deny taking Ritzpah. He does not claim innocence. He simply pivots: I have been loyal for years, and this is how you repay me? Watch what I do now.

Even if Avner had in fact taken Ritzpah, the punishment he threatened — handing the kingdom to David — was grotesquely out of proportion. Unless, the commentators suggest, Avner had been preparing to defect all along and had been waiting only for an excuse.

The excuse had just walked into the room on Ish-Boshet's frightened feet.

* * *

Avner sent messengers to Chevron that very night.

The message was brief and shocking.

"To whom does the land belong? Make your covenant with me, and behold, my hand shall be with you to turn all Israel to you."

David's response was also brief, and conditional.

"Good. I will make a covenant with you. Only one thing I require of you: you shall not see my face, unless you first bring Michal, the daughter of Shaul, when you come to see my face."

* * *

Michal.

The name landed on Avner's ears like a stone. Michal, Shaul's younger daughter. Michal, who had loved David when he was a young warrior in her father's court. Michal, who had helped him escape through a window one night when Shaul's soldiers were surrounding the house, who had placed a teraphim in the bed to make it look as if David were sleeping there, who had bought David the hours he needed to flee into the wilderness.

Shaul, in his fury at David's escape, had taken Michal away from him and given her to another man — a man named Palti ben Layish. She had been Palti's wife for years now. They had built a life together.

David was demanding her back.

The mefarshim debate why. Some say David loved her still, and had never stopped. Some say this was pure political calculation — a son-in-law of Shaul had a stronger claim to the throne than an outsider, and by reclaiming Michal, David was reclaiming his dynastic legitimacy. Most likely, both things were true at once. Love and politics often wear the same clothes.

Avner went straight to Ish-Boshet.

"Give me Michal," he said. "The king of Chevron requires her."

Ish-Boshet did not resist. He was past resistance. He sent soldiers to the house of Palti and they took Michal from her husband and they led her away on the road to Chevron. The chapter records a small, unbearable detail: "And her husband went with her, walking and weeping after her, as far as Bachurim. And Avner said to him, Go, return. And he returned."

Palti walked behind his wife weeping. For miles. All the way to Bachurim. Then Avner told him to go home. And Palti went home.

The Gemara preserves a discussion about Palti ben Layish. Some say he had never been married to Michal in the true sense — that he had placed a sword between them in the bed every night and sworn that he would not touch her, because he knew she had been David's wife and that David was still living. If that tradition is correct, then Palti's weeping on the road to Bachurim was the weeping of a man losing not a wife, but a companion — a woman he had protected for years without ever calling her his own.

Avner did not look back.

He escorted Michal to Chevron and brought her before David, and then he turned to the business that had brought him there. He had come to betray Ish-Boshet. He had come to hand over the kingdom.

* * *

David gave Avner a feast.

Twenty men had come with Avner from Machanayim — his personal retinue, his closest officers — and David fed them and spoke gently with Avner and listened to his plans. Avner explained how the transfer of power would work. He would go back to Machanayim. He would call together the elders of all the tribes. He would remind them — as he alone could — of their old loyalty to the house of Shaul, and then he would tell them, one by one, tribe by tribe, that the time had come to transfer that loyalty to David. Avner had the standing to do this. The tribes would listen to him. Avner was the only living man who could deliver Israel to David without another war.

"Go in peace," David said.

Avner left.

Yoav was not there.

Yoav had been away — on some campaign, some raid, the text is unclear — and had not been present when Avner arrived, or during the feast, or when Avner departed. He came home that same evening, and he heard the news from his men.

"Avner ben Ner came to the king, and he sent him away, and he went in peace."

Yoav's reaction was immediate.

He went straight into the throne room.

* * *

"What have you done?"

David looked up.

"Behold, Avner came to you. Why have you sent him away so that he goes away free? You know Avner ben Ner. He came to deceive you, and to know your coming in and your going out, and to know all that you do."

David was silent.

The Yeshivat Har Etzion commentary points out what Yoav was doing in this moment. He was accusing Avner of being a spy — an elaborate double agent who had come to Chevron not to defect but to gather intelligence. It was a plausible accusation. Avner had a reputation for cunning. The story of a defecting general turning out to be a double agent was not unheard of in the ancient world.

But Yoav's true motive was not security.

Yoav's true motive was buried in the memory of a field by a pool in Giv'on, and the body of a gazelle-swift young man lying in his own blood with the butt of a spear through him. Yoav had been waiting for this moment for years. He had made Asa'el a silent promise by that pool. Now Avner had walked into Chevron, a guest at David's table, protected by the king's word, safe from all reprisal.

Yoav was not going to let that protection stand.

Without telling David, he sent messengers after Avner. They caught up with him at a place called the Cistern of Sirah, where Avner had stopped to water his horses, and they delivered a message from Yoav.

"The king wishes to speak with you about one more matter. Return to Chevron."

Avner turned around and rode back.

* * *

He entered the gate of Chevron at dusk.

Yoav was waiting for him in the gateway itself — the wide stone chamber at the base of the city wall where the elders of the city held court, where legal matters were decided, where the community's business was conducted. The gate of the city was, among other things, a place of Torah. It was the place where the shofetim sat (as the Torah says, "Shoftim v'shotrim titen lecha b'chol she'arecha" — "You shall appoint judges and officers in all your gates"). It was where disputes were adjudicated.

The Gemara in Sanhedrin 49a preserves a remarkable tradition about what took place in that gateway.

Yoav, the Gemara says, did not kill Avner with a blunt attack. He engaged him in a Torah discussion.

"I have a halachic question to ask you, Avner ben Ner," Yoav said.

Avner — who was by every Torah source a scholar, a warrior-scholar, the kind of man who sat in the beit midrash between campaigns — was instantly interested. "Ask."

Yoav asked him a complicated question about the laws of chalitzah — the ceremony by which a widow is released from the obligation of levirate marriage to her deceased husband's brother. The question involved a specific detail about how the shoe is removed and who holds what during the procedure.

"A yevamah who is a dwarf," Yoav asked, "how does she perform chalitzah?"

Avner began to explain. The Gemara says he bent down to demonstrate how the shoe is taken with the teeth, the way the ceremony physically works with a woman who is too short to reach the foot with her hands. He was bending over, showing Yoav how the mechanics of the ceremony would play out, when Yoav drew his sword and drove it into Avner's fifth rib — the same place where Avner had driven the butt of his spear into Asa'el's belly at Giv'on.

Avner died in the gate of Chevron with a discussion of chalitzah on his lips.

* * *

The Gemara's reading of this scene is devastating in its precision. Chalitzah is the ceremony of releasing a brother-in-law from a dead brother's wife. It is the legal acknowledgment that the dead brother has no further claim on the family, that his line has been closed, that his widow is free.

Yoav chose the topic on purpose.

He was telling Avner, with terrible wit, that the matter between them was not political. It was not about David, not about the throne, not about Ish-Boshet, not about Avner's defection. It was about a dead brother. Asa'el was Yoav's brother. Asa'el's blood had never been released. Chalitzah had never been performed. The debt remained open.

Now — in the gateway of Chevron, where judges sit — Yoav was closing the case.

It was a ruling, not a murder. At least in Yoav's mind.

In the minds of almost everyone else, it was something much darker.

* * *

When David heard the news, he went white.

He was sitting in his palace when a messenger came running in with the report. Avner ben Ner was dead. Killed by Yoav in the gateway. Unarmed. Under the king's safe conduct. In Chevron.

David rose from his throne so quickly that the men around him took a step back.

"I and my kingdom are innocent before Hashem forever," he said, his voice shaking, "of the blood of Avner ben Ner."

Then he turned, and he pronounced a curse. The text preserves his exact words.

"Let it rest on the head of Yoav, and on all his father's house. May there never cease from the house of Yoav one who has a flow, or a leper, or one who leans on a crutch, or one who falls by the sword, or one who lacks bread."

It was a brutal curse. Five calamities, each worse than the last: a chronic illness, a skin disease, a disability, a violent death, and starvation. David was not merely condemning Yoav. He was condemning Yoav's descendants, generation after generation, to bear the weight of Avner's blood.

But the curse was only the first thing David did.

The second thing David did was stranger. He commanded Yoav himself — the very man whose head he had just cursed — to tear his own garments and put on sackcloth and walk at the head of Avner's funeral procession as the chief mourner. The commander of the army, the killer of the deceased, was forced to perform the outward rites of grief for the man he had murdered. David wanted all of Israel to see it.

And David walked behind the bier.

The king of Yehuda walked behind the coffin of the general of Israel, weeping publicly, fasting all day, refusing to eat until the sun went down. When his advisors urged him to take a little food, David swore an oath: "God do so to me and more also, if I taste bread or anything else until the sun goes down."

And the people saw it. The Scripture is emphatic on this point: "And all the people took notice of it, and it pleased them. For whatever the king did pleased all the people. And all the people and all Israel knew that day that it was not of the king to kill Avner ben Ner."

The funeral became, without David planning it, the most effective political act of the entire civil war.

* * *

At the graveside in Chevron, David stood with his head uncovered and composed a lament — a short one, nothing like the elegy for Shaul and Yehonatan, just four lines, but searing.

"Should Avner die as a fool dies? Your hands were not bound, nor were your feet put into fetters. As one falls before the children of injustice, so you have fallen."

The lament was a public indictment of Yoav without naming Yoav. A fool dies of his own stupidity — Avner had not died that way. A criminal dies in chains — Avner had been free. Avner had fallen "before the children of injustice." There were the children of injustice in the crowd, hearing themselves described. Yoav was standing at the head of the bier, forced by David to play the role of chief mourner, with his own king accusing him in public verse.

The aggadic sources add a poignant detail. Avner, according to rabbinic tradition, had been a Torah giant. One tradition describes him as having been a member of the Sanhedrin. Think of what that means. The same man whose silence at Nov had damned him, who had crowned an illegitimate king, who had driven a spear-butt through a young man's belly — this same man sat in the beit midrash and ruled on questions of halacha, and the sages of his generation accepted his rulings. A human being is never one thing.

David understood that. That is why he wept for Avner.

* * *

And then — as if the chapter had not already seen enough — one more detail is given.

In the city of Machanayim, when Ish-Boshet heard that Avner was dead, "his hands went slack, and all Israel was dismayed."

The only support Ish-Boshet had ever had was gone. The only man who had been holding the house of Shaul together had been struck down in the gateway of his rival's city. Ish-Boshet sat on a throne in a town across the Yarden river with no one to defend him.

The vultures were already gathering.
