Chapter Three

The Wise Woman of Tekoa

Yoav noticed.

It was Yoav's particular gift, and his particular curse, to notice things about the king that the king did not realize were visible. He had noticed, twenty years earlier, that David was secretly in love with Yonatan in a way that went beyond the friendship of soldiers. He had noticed, ten years earlier, that David was about to make a serious mistake by elevating Avner to the position of chief military advisor — a mistake Yoav had prevented by killing Avner in the gateway of Chevron. He had noticed, several years ago, that David was hiding something terrible in the matter of Uriah the Chitite — a terrible thing he had never spoken of with David, but that he had silently understood from the moment the sealed letter arrived in his command tent at Rabbah. Yoav noticed everything. He spoke about almost none of it. And he acted on what he noticed when he believed action was necessary.

What Yoav had been noticing for the past several months was that David was not at peace.

The king had finished his official mourning for Amnon. He had returned to the audience chamber. He had received ambassadors. He had given the orders that needed to be given. He had sat at the council table during the planning sessions for the next year's campaigns. From the outside, David appeared to be a king in full command of his kingdom. But Yoav, who had spent decades studying David's face, could see what no one else could see. The king was preoccupied. The king was thinking about Avshalom. The king's eyes, when they were not focused on the work in front of him, would drift to a middle distance, and the corners of his mouth would tighten in a way that Yoav recognized as the sign of a man arguing with himself.

David wanted Avshalom back.

David could not say so. To say so would be to admit that the king had been wrong to leave the killer of his firstborn in exile for three years. It would be to admit that the longing of his heart was overruling the demands of his justice. It would be to admit, in fact, that the very prophecy Natan had once delivered — the sword shall not depart from your house — was working its way through the household exactly as predicted, and that David had no power to stop it. David could not say any of this aloud. He could not even ask Yoav to help him think through it. The king had to maintain, in his public posture, the position that Avshalom was a fugitive from justice and that no amount of paternal grief would change that fact.

But the king's face was telling Yoav a different story.

And Yoav, who was a fixer in the same way that Yonadav had been a fixer (with the crucial difference that Yoav fixed things in service of David's interests rather than his own), decided that the king needed to be helped.

He decided that Avshalom needed to come home.

He decided that the king needed someone else to make the decision for him — to deliver the indirect persuasion that would let David do the thing he wanted to do without having to admit, even to himself, that he had wanted to do it.

Yoav had seen this kind of indirect persuasion work before. He had watched, several years earlier, when Natan the prophet had walked into the throne room with a story about a poor man and a lamb, and had induced David to render judgment on himself. Yoav had been in the next room during that scene. He had heard the parable through the door. He had watched David stagger out of the audience chamber afterward, his face wet, his eyes empty. Yoav had understood, that day, that the judicial parable was the most powerful tool in the kingdom for moving the king when no other tool would work. A parable framed as a legal case forced the king to render a verdict — and once the verdict was rendered, the king could not retract it.

Yoav decided to construct a parable.

He could not deliver it himself. He was too close to David. The king would see through any parable that Yoav told personally. The parable had to come from a stranger — someone whose motives could not be read in advance, someone whose loyalty was not in question because no loyalty was visible to begin with. The parable also had to come from a woman — because women in the ancient Near East had a particular kind of standing in legal cases that involved family inheritance, and because a woman pleading for the life of her son carried a particular emotional weight that a man could not have carried.

Yoav knew where to find the right woman.

He sent a messenger to Tekoa.

* * *

Tekoa was a small town in the hills south of Yerushalayim, perhaps a half-day's ride from the capital. It was not a famous town. It was best known for its olives and its wisdom. The olives were prized for the high-quality oil they produced, and the local tradition held that the consumption of olive oil increased a person's mental clarity. The town had a reputation for producing wise people — men and women who were known throughout Yehuda for their ability to think through difficult problems and offer measured counsel. Tekoa would later become famous as the hometown of the prophet Amos, who would emerge from its hills a few centuries after these events. But in Yoav's time, the town was known mainly for its wise women — informal village counselors, the kind of older women whom families consulted in disputes, the kind of women who knew how to listen and how to speak.

Yoav's messenger arrived in Tekoa and asked, in the marketplace, where the wisest woman of the town could be found. He was directed to a particular house on the eastern edge of the town. He went to the house. He knocked. An older woman opened the door. She was perhaps fifty years old. She was dressed in the plain clothes of an ordinary villager, but the messenger had been given a description, and he recognized her at once.

"My lady. Yoav the son of Tzeruyah, commander of the army of Israel, sends you this message. He requests that you come to Yerushalayim immediately. He has a task for you that only you can perform. He will reward you generously, but it must be done in absolute secrecy. Will you come?"

The woman looked at the messenger for a long moment.

She did not ask why. She did not ask what the task was. She had heard, like everyone else in Yehuda, the stories of Yoav — the man who killed Avner in the gateway, the man who burned fields when he needed to make a point, the man who was the king's right hand and who served the kingdom with a competence that bordered on ruthlessness. If Yoav was sending for her, the matter was important. If the matter was important, refusing was not really an option.

"I will come."

She gathered a small bag of belongings. She closed the door of her house. She mounted the small donkey the messenger had brought for her. They rode together back to Yerushalayim.

* * *

Yoav met her in a private chamber of the palace, not in any official audience hall.

"My lady. Thank you for coming."

"Why am I here, my lord?"

Yoav explained.

He explained the situation in the king's heart. He explained the three-year exile of Avshalom. He explained the reason that Avshalom could not simply be brought home — the formal requirements of justice, the public expectation that a killer should face consequences, the impossibility of the king reversing his own previous silence without losing face. He explained that the king was suffering and needed a way out that the king himself could not construct.

The wise woman listened in silence.

When Yoav was done explaining, she nodded.

"You want me to deliver a parable."

"Yes."

"Like the one that Natan delivered three years ago?"

Yoav nodded slowly. So she had heard about Natan's parable. The story had spread, as such stories do, beyond the walls of the palace and into the towns of Yehuda.

"Like that one. But not exactly. Natan was a prophet, and his parable was a rebuke. You are a private citizen, and your parable will be a plea. You will come to the king as a widow whose remaining son is in danger of death because of a family feud. You will plead with him to spare your son. He will, I am confident, render a verdict in your favor. Once he has rendered it, you will turn the verdict back on him — show him that the same mercy he is willing to extend to your imaginary son, he should extend to his real son in Geshur. Do you understand the structure?"

"I understand the structure, my lord."

"Will you do it?"

She thought for a moment.

"What you are asking me to do, my lord, is to trick the king. To deliver words that are not true, to frame a case that does not exist, in order to manipulate the king into a decision that he might not otherwise make. Do you understand that this is what you are asking me to do?"

Yoav looked at her steadily.

"I understand."

"And you believe that the decision is the right one? That bringing Avshalom back is the right thing for the kingdom?"

"I believe it is the right thing for the king's heart. The kingdom — that is harder to say. But the king's heart is breaking. I have served him for many years. I cannot watch him break."

The wise woman of Tekoa nodded slowly. She had come from a town where wisdom was valued above strict legalism, where the right answer to a difficult question was often the answer that healed the most wounds rather than the answer that applied the most rules. She was the kind of woman who could see Yoav's request as a service to the king rather than a manipulation of him.

"I will do it. Tell me the words."

Yoav told her the words.

He spent the next two hours coaching her on the script. He told her the story — a widow with two sons, one who killed the other in a field, the surviving son now sought by the avenging family, the widow herself the only one left who could plead for his life. He told her the cadence — slow at first, building to a moment of supplication. He told her where to pause, where to weep, where to look up at the king. He gave her the speech the king would render, more or less, in response — the verdict that would seem to grant her relief but that she would then turn around and use against the king himself.

She memorized everything.

When they were done, Yoav looked at her once more.

"Tomorrow morning. Wear mourning clothes. Do not anoint yourself with oil. Look like a woman who has been weeping for many days. Come to the palace at the second hour. I will arrange the audience."

"Yes, my lord."

She bowed and was led away to a guest chamber to spend the night.

* * *

The audience took place the next morning, exactly as Yoav had arranged.

The wise woman walked into the audience chamber wearing a torn black robe. Her hair was uncombed. Her face was streaked with dust and tears. She had not anointed herself with oil, and she looked like a woman who had been mourning a loss for many weeks. She was a convincing actress. She had also been wise enough to understand that the most effective acting was the acting that drew on real grief — and so she had been thinking, on the way to the audience chamber, about the actual losses she had experienced in her own long life, and her face was wet with the memory of those real losses rather than the tears of a fake performance.

She prostrated herself in front of the throne.

"Help, O king."

David sat up.

"What is troubling you, woman? Speak."

"In truth, my lord, I am a widow, my husband being dead. And your handmaid had two sons. And the two of them strove together in the field, and there was no one to part them, and one struck the other and killed him. And now the whole family has risen up against your handmaid, and they say: Give us the one who struck his brother, that we may put him to death for the life of his brother whom he slew, and we will destroy the heir also. So they will quench my coal which is left, and they will leave my husband neither name nor remnant upon the face of the earth."

She paused. She was weeping now. The tears were running down her face. She was the perfect picture of a desperate widow whose entire family line was about to be exterminated by the rigid application of the law.

David was watching her with full attention.

The case was not unusual. The Torah's law on accidental homicide and family blood-vengeance was complicated, and the application of the law in cases like the one the woman had just described required royal arbitration. David had heard cases like this before. He knew the relevant verses. He knew the relevant rulings.

But there was something about the particular structure of this case that was making him uneasy.

He started to give a vague answer.

"Go home. I will give the necessary order."

The wise woman was ready for this. Yoav had warned her that the king might try to dispose of the case with a vague non-answer. She pressed.

"My lord. My king. Let the iniquity be on me and on my father's house. Let the king and his throne be guiltless."

She was offering, in formal court language, to take the legal consequences of any wrong judgment onto herself. She was telling the king that he could rule in her favor without fearing any blowback. She was eliminating, in advance, any excuse the king might have used to refuse to engage with the case.

David understood that she was not going to accept a vague answer.

He sat up straighter.

"If anyone speaks against you, bring him to me, and he will not touch you again."

"My king. Let the king remember Hashem your God, that the avenger of blood not destroy any more, lest they destroy my son."

"As Hashem lives, not one hair of your son shall fall to the earth."

There it was.

The king had committed himself. He had taken an oath in the name of Hashem that the woman's son would not be killed. He had publicly rendered the verdict. He could not now retract it.

The wise woman lowered her eyes.

She was about to deliver the line that Yoav had coached her on. The line that would turn the parable into a rebuke. The line that would force David to recognize that the case he had just decided was a mirror of the case he had been refusing to decide for three years.

She looked up at the king.

"Why then have you devised such a thing against the people of God? For in speaking this thing, the king is as one who is guilty, in that the king does not bring back his banished one. We must surely die, and are as water spilled on the ground that cannot be gathered up again. Yet God does not take away a soul, but devises means that none of His banished may be banished from Him."

* * *

The room went still.

The wise woman had just done, in two sentences, what Natan had done with a parable about a lamb three years earlier. She had used the king's own verdict to convict the king of his own inaction. She had pointed at the gap between the mercy he was willing to extend to her imaginary son and the mercy he was withholding from his real son. She had quoted one of the most beautiful theological lines in the entire Tanach — yet God does not take away a soul, but devises means that none of His banished may be banished from Him — and she had used it as the central argument for why David should bring Avshalom back from exile.

The line is one of the most extraordinary in all of Tanach. Velo yissa Elohim nefesh, vechashav machshavot levilti yiddach mimenu niddach. "And God does not take away a soul, but devises plans so that the banished one may not be banished from Him." It is a verse that has been read by Jewish tradition for thousands of years as a statement of the deepest possible doctrine of divine mercy. Hashem does not give up on people. Hashem actively devises plans — machshavot, the same word used in Yirmeyahu 29:11 for the divine plans of welfare and not of evil — to bring back the ones who have been pushed out. The wise woman of Tekoa had taken this enormous theological idea and applied it as the lever that would move David's decision about his own son.

The line was beautiful.

It was also a setup.

David recognized, in the silence after the line was delivered, that he had been played. He looked at the wise woman with a sudden sharpness.

"I am going to ask you a question. Do not hide anything from me. Whose hand is in this with you? Is it the hand of Yoav?"

The wise woman bowed her head.

"As your soul lives, my lord the king, no one can turn to the right hand or to the left from anything that my lord the king has spoken. For your servant Yoav — he commanded me, and he put all these words in the mouth of your handmaid. To bring about this change of affairs, your servant Yoav has done this thing. But my lord is wise, according to the wisdom of an angel of God, to know all things that are in the earth."

She had confessed.

The Tanach is careful to record that she did not lie when she was asked the direct question. She had been willing to deliver the parable, but she was not willing to deny Yoav's role when the king asked her about it directly. The line of her ethics was clear. I will speak words that are not literally true if they will heal the king's heart. But I will not lie when I am asked a direct question about the source of those words.

David nodded slowly.

He was not angry. The Etzion commentary is careful to note that David's reaction here is not anger but a kind of resigned acceptance. He had been outmaneuvered. The maneuver had been performed by the man who knew him best — the same Yoav who had stood beside him in every battle since the wilderness years, who had killed Avner without an order, who had received the letter about Uriah and asked no questions, who was in many ways the only man in the kingdom who could see David clearly enough to know exactly which lever to pull.

The maneuver had worked. David was going to bring Avshalom back. The decision had been made for him by the parable, and he could not now retract it without dishonoring the oath he had just sworn in the name of Hashem.

He turned to one of his attendants.

"Send for Yoav."

* * *

Yoav came in.

He had been waiting in an antechamber. He came in quickly. He bowed before the king.

David looked at him.

"Behold now, I have done this thing. Go therefore, bring back the young man Avshalom."

Yoav fell on his face to the ground. He prostrated himself fully. He blessed the king. The Tanach preserves his words: "Today your servant knows that I have found favor in your sight, my lord, O king, in that the king has performed the request of his servant."

The phrase the request of his servant is striking. Yoav was openly admitting, in the same audience, that the wise woman's parable had been his request. He was not hiding it. He was thanking the king for granting his request — the request that had been delivered through the mouth of a strange woman and through the framework of a fictional case, but that had really been Yoav's request all along.

David accepted the thanks. He did not respond. He simply nodded and looked at the wise woman, who was still standing in the corner of the audience chamber.

"You may go home, mother of Tekoa. Take this gift with you" — he gestured to one of his attendants — "and go in peace. You have served the king well."

The wise woman bowed. She was given a small bag of silver. She left the palace and rode back to Tekoa that same afternoon. She is never mentioned again in Tanach. She had appeared, performed her role, and disappeared — like a perfect minor character in a play whose only purpose was to deliver the speech that would turn the plot.

Yoav was already on his way to Geshur.

* * *

He arrived at the court of Talmai four days later.

He carried a sealed letter from David — formal authorization for Avshalom to return to Yerushalayim. He presented the letter to Talmai. Talmai read it. Talmai sent for his grandson.

Avshalom came into the audience hall of his grandfather's palace. He looked at Yoav. Yoav looked at him. The two men had not seen each other in three years. Yoav had been at every meal of Avshalom's childhood, had been the chief military officer of his father's court for as long as Avshalom could remember, had taught him to ride and to fight as a teenager. Now Yoav was looking at him as a man — as the killer of Amnon, as the heir presumptive to the throne, as the prince who was about to be brought back from exile through Yoav's own elaborate maneuver.

"Yoav."

"Avshalom."

"You have come for me?"

"I have come for you. Pack your things. We leave for Yerushalayim in the morning."

Avshalom did not say anything for a long moment. He had been waiting for this day for three years. He had imagined it many times — the return, the reunion with his father, the formal restoration of his place in the order of succession. Now the moment had come. It did not feel quite the way he had imagined it.

"Will my father see me?"

Yoav looked at him.

"That is a question I cannot answer, Avshalom. Your father has agreed to bring you back to Yerushalayim. He has not yet agreed to see you. Those are different things. Be prepared for the possibility that you will live in the city without being received in the palace."

Avshalom's face hardened.

"For how long?"

"I do not know."

* * *

The journey back to Yerushalayim took five days.

They rode together through the territory of Geshur, across the Yarden, through the hills of Efrayim, past the very town of Ba'al-Chatzor where Avshalom had killed Amnon three years earlier. Avshalom did not speak much during the journey. He was thinking. He was counting the days he had spent in exile. He was rehearsing, in his mind, the speech he would deliver to his father at the moment of their reunion. He was preparing himself emotionally for the embrace, the tears, the formal restoration of his status as the king's son.

When they arrived in Yerushalayim, Yoav escorted him to a house on a quiet street in the western part of the city — a house that had belonged to Avshalom before his exile and that had been kept for him during his absence.

"Wait here. I will speak with your father."

Yoav left.

Avshalom waited.

He waited the rest of the day. He waited through the night. He waited through the next day. He waited through the next week. He waited through the next month.

The summons to the palace did not come.

Yoav returned at the end of the first week to deliver the news.

"Your father has spoken. He says: Let him turn to his own house, and let him not see my face."

The Hebrew is u-fanai lo yir'eh. And he shall not see my face. It was the formal language of royal disfavor. David was permitting Avshalom to live in the city. He was not permitting Avshalom to enter the palace. He was not permitting Avshalom to be acknowledged as the heir. He was, in effect, putting Avshalom into a kind of internal exile — physically present in Yerushalayim but socially and politically invisible.

Avshalom stared at Yoav.

"For how long?"

"Until the king changes his mind."

"And when will that be?"

"I do not know, Avshalom. I have done what I could. I brought you back. I cannot make the king kiss you. That is between you and him. You will have to be patient."

Avshalom was not patient.

He waited two years.

* * *

The Tanach records the next sentence with characteristic compression. "So Avshalom dwelled two full years in Yerushalayim, and he did not see the king's face."

Two more years.

This is the same Avshalom who had waited two years between Tamar's rape and Amnon's killing. The Tanach is making us notice the parallel. Two years of silence between a wrong and its bloody answer in the case of Amnon. Two years of silence between an exile and its bloody answer in the case of David. Avshalom was a man who could wait. He was a man who could let resentment build slowly inside him for long periods of time without showing it on the outside. And when the resentment finally became unbearable, he was a man who would act decisively and without warning.

For two years he lived in Yerushalayim. He was the most beautiful man in Israel — the Tanach takes a moment to describe his physical beauty in remarkable detail. "In all of Israel there was no man so beautiful as Avshalom and so much praised. From the sole of his foot to the crown of his head there was no blemish in him." His hair was so long and so heavy that he had to cut it once a year, and the cut hair weighed two hundred shekels by the king's weight — about three pounds of hair from a single annual cutting. He had three sons and one daughter, the daughter named Tamar after the sister whose violation had set the entire chain of events in motion. He was, in every external respect, a prince of Israel. The people of Yerushalayim adored him. The crowds in the marketplace stopped to watch him when he walked by. The young men of the city followed him in groups whenever he appeared in public.

But he could not see his father.

For two years he sent messages to Yoav requesting an intercession. Yoav did not respond. Yoav had brought Avshalom back to Yerushalayim. Yoav was not willing to go any further. Yoav understood, perhaps better than Avshalom did, that David's refusal to receive Avshalom in person was not a mere oversight — it was the king's last attempt to maintain the dignity of justice in a situation where the king's own heart had already surrendered the case. To press David for the formal reception would be to humiliate the king publicly. Yoav was not willing to humiliate the king publicly. He had reached the limit of what he was willing to do.

Avshalom decided to push him.

At the end of the second year, Avshalom sent his servants to Yoav's estate just outside the city.

"Burn his barley field."

The servants stared at him.

"My lord — "

"Burn it. The whole field. Use torches. Make sure it burns to the ground. Then come back and tell me when it is done."

The servants went. They burned the field. The flames spread quickly through the dry barley stalks, and within an hour the entire field was a smoking ruin. The smoke was visible from the city walls of Yerushalayim. Yoav saw it from his own roof.

He understood instantly who had done it.

He rode out to Avshalom's house within the hour. He was furious.

"Why did your servants set my field on fire?"

Avshalom looked at him calmly.

"Because I sent for you, and you would not come. I sent for you twice. Now I have your attention. I will tell you what I want you to tell my father. Why did I come from Geshur? It would have been better for me to be there still. Now therefore let me see the king's face. And if there is iniquity in me, let him kill me."

Yoav stared at him.

It was an extraordinary speech. If there is iniquity in me, let him kill me. Avshalom was demanding that David either receive him formally as a son or execute him formally as a criminal. The half-measure of internal exile was no longer acceptable. Avshalom was forcing the king to commit one way or the other.

Yoav understood the demand. He also understood that he had no choice. The burning of the field had made it impossible for Yoav to ignore Avshalom any longer. Avshalom had done exactly what Shimshon had once done with the foxes and the torches — used fire to force a powerful man to engage with him. The parallel was not lost on Yoav, who had been a student of the Tanach since childhood.

He nodded.

"I will speak to your father."

He rode back into the city.

* * *

He came into the audience chamber and bowed before David.

"My lord. Avshalom your son requests an audience. He has burned my barley field to demand it. He says that if there is iniquity in him, you should kill him; and if not, you should receive him."

David looked at Yoav for a long moment.

He had been hoping, for two years, that the situation would resolve itself. He had been hoping that some passage of time would make the formal reception unnecessary — that Avshalom would marry, settle down, fade into the background of the court, and that the question of whether the king would ever receive him face-to-face could be left permanently unanswered. The burning of Yoav's field had ended that hope.

David nodded.

"Bring him."

Yoav left. He returned a few hours later with Avshalom.

The reunion took place in the audience chamber, with no other witnesses except the prophet Natan (who had been called in for protocol) and a small handful of attendants. Avshalom walked into the chamber. He saw his father on the throne for the first time in five years. He prostrated himself fully on the floor — face down, arms outstretched, the formal posture of complete submission.

David rose from the throne.

He walked down to where Avshalom was lying on the floor. He bent down. He took his son by the shoulders and lifted him up. He looked at his son's face — the face he had not seen in five years, the face of the boy who had killed his other son, the face of the heir presumptive whom he had been refusing to receive for two years. The face was the face of his Avshalom. The face was beautiful, the way Avshalom's face had always been beautiful. The face was the face of his son.

David kissed him.

The Tanach records the kiss in a single Hebrew word. Vayishak. He kissed him.

That was all. The chapter ends there. There is no recorded conversation between father and son. There is no formal restoration of Avshalom to the order of succession. There is no public ceremony. There is just the kiss — the kiss of a father who had been waiting for five years to feel his son's face against his own, the kiss that ended the formal exile and that should have been the beginning of a real reconciliation.

It was not the beginning of a reconciliation.

It was the beginning of the next phase of the story.

Avshalom had waited two years to be received. He was now received. And the moment he was received, his thoughts turned in a new direction. The kiss had not really healed anything. The kiss had been five years too late. Avshalom had spent those five years watching his father refuse to acknowledge him, and the refusal had hardened something in him. He no longer wanted to be his father's son. He no longer even wanted to be his father's heir.

He wanted to be his father's replacement.

The next chapter will tell the story of how he set out to make that replacement happen.
