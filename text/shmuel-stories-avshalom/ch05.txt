Chapter Five

Across the Kidron

The exit from Yerushalayim took less than two hours.

David's household — the wives, the concubines, the children, the personal servants, the inner circle of officers and counselors — gathered what they could carry in the time that David had given them. They left behind the furniture, the wall hangings, the ceremonial objects, the libraries, the wardrobes. They took clothes, money, weapons, food for two days, and the children. They moved through the corridors of the palace at a controlled pace that was fast enough to be effective and slow enough not to start a panic in the streets. By the time the procession was assembled in the main courtyard, the sun was already low in the western sky.

David stood at the front of the procession in a plain cloak — no purple, no royal embroidery. He had taken off the crown an hour earlier and left it on a table in his private chamber. He was, for the next several days, going to be a fugitive, and a fugitive does not wear a crown.

He turned to his wives and concubines.

"I am going to leave ten of the concubines here, in the palace, to keep the house. The rest of you will come with me. The men with weapons will travel at the front and the rear. The women and children will travel in the middle. We will not run unless we have to. We will walk steadily and we will keep together. Once we are out of the city, we head east."

There was a small whimper from one of the younger children. A nursemaid hushed it.

David turned to his head steward.

"Open the eastern gate. Then close it behind us. The city is Avshalom's now."

The steward bowed and ran ahead.

The procession began to move.

* * *

They walked out of the palace courtyard, through the inner streets, past the marketplace, past the houses of the elders, past the synagogue courtyard, past the public well, and out through the eastern gate of Yerushalayim. The people of the city stood in their doorways and watched. Some of them wept openly. The Tanach uses a particular phrase: vekol ha-aretz bochim kol gadol. "And all the land was weeping with a loud cry." The weeping spread from house to house as the procession passed. Yerushalayim had been David's city for many years. The people in the streets had loved him, even if some of their hearts had been stolen by Avshalom over the past four years. They were watching their king walk out of his own capital in defeat, and they could not believe what they were seeing.

David did not speak to anyone as he walked through the city.

He kept his head covered. He kept his eyes down. The Tanach says: velo amad ish. "And no one rose up." Nobody tried to stop him. Nobody tried to rally to his defense. Nobody barricaded a street or blockaded a gate. The whole city watched him leave in silence and weeping, and not a single hand was raised to keep him there.

He walked through the eastern gate.

The gate closed behind him.

He was, for the first time in many years, outside the walls of his own capital with no plan to return.

* * *

Beyond the gate, the road descended into the Kidron valley.

The Kidron is the deep ravine that runs along the eastern side of Yerushalayim, separating the city from the Mount of Olives on the other side. In the spring, when the rains have ended, the Kidron is dry — a stony bed where a small stream flows in the winter and where shepherds graze their flocks in the warmer months. David's procession reached the bottom of the Kidron at sunset and began to climb the slope on the far side, the slope that leads up to the Mount of Olives.

It was at this point that the first of several extraordinary encounters took place.

* * *

Itai the Giti was waiting at the side of the road.

Itai was a Pelishti — a man from Gat, the city that David had once lived in for sixteen months when he was a fugitive from Shaul. Itai had joined David's service relatively recently, perhaps within the past year or two, bringing with him six hundred armed men of his own clan from Gat. They were a mercenary unit, technically — paid soldiers who served the king of Israel for wages and protection, but who were not Israelites by birth. Itai himself was a convert to the faith of Israel. His men were partly converts and partly still pagans who served their leader without sharing his religious commitments.

He had heard about the rebellion. He had brought his entire unit to the road outside Yerushalayim — six hundred armed men, with their wives and children — and he was waiting at the edge of the Kidron when the king's procession came down from the gate.

David stopped when he saw Itai.

"Itai. What are you doing here?"

"My lord. I am coming with you. Wherever you go, my men go also."

David shook his head.

"You should not come with me, Itai. You are a stranger to this land. You are a Pelishti by birth. You arrived only recently. You have no quarrel with Avshalom. He will not harm you. Go back to the city. Stay with the new king. Take your men and your wives and your children, and live in peace under Avshalom's rule. You owe me nothing, Itai. You owe yourself a peaceful life. Go back."

Itai listened with his head bowed. When David had finished, he raised his head and spoke. The Tanach preserves his speech word for word, and it is one of the most beautiful expressions of personal loyalty in all of Tanach.

"As Hashem lives, and as my lord the king lives, surely in whatever place my lord the king shall be, whether for death or for life, even there also will your servant be."

The Tanach uses a striking Hebrew formulation: im le-mavet im le-chayim. "Whether for death or for life." Itai was telling David that his choice was already made. He was not weighing the options. He was not calculating his odds. He had pledged himself to David's person, and the pledge held regardless of whether David was on the throne or in the wilderness. Death or life. Either one. He would be there for both.

David looked at the foreign mercenary captain who was offering him a kind of loyalty that not even some of his Israelite subjects had offered.

He nodded.

"Then come and pass over."

Itai signaled to his men. The six hundred warriors of Gat fell in behind David's household at the rear of the procession. Their wives and children walked with them. They added their armed strength to a procession that, an hour earlier, had been short on armed strength. They became, that evening, the rearguard of the king's flight.

The Tanach makes a point of emphasizing how unusual this was. A foreign mercenary unit, brand new to the kingdom, showing more devotion to David than the Israelites of his own city. The mefarshim treat Itai's loyalty as a kind of moral commentary on the loyalty of David's own people. The men whose hearts Avshalom had stolen at the city gate had been Israelites. The man whose heart could not be stolen was a Pelishti from Gat.

* * *

The procession continued up the slope of the Kidron.

A few minutes later, the second extraordinary encounter took place.

Tzadok the kohen and Evyatar the kohen were coming up the road behind the procession with a contingent of Levi'im. The Levi'im were carrying the Aron of Hashem.

David turned around when he saw them coming. He stopped. He walked back down the slope to meet them.

"Tzadok. What is this?"

"My lord. We are bringing the Aron with you. Wherever you go, the Aron of Hashem should go with you. We will not let it remain in a city that has been taken by your enemies."

David looked at the Aron — the ancient gold-overlaid box that had once been carried through the wilderness by his ancestors, the same Aron that had killed Uzza at Peretz Uzza and that had blessed the household of Oved-Edom and that had been brought up to Yerushalayim with David dancing in front of it in a linen ephod many years earlier. The Aron that he had built a tent for in the heart of his city. The Aron that he had wanted to build a Beit HaMikdash for, until Natan had told him that the Beit HaMikdash would be built by his son. The Aron that was, in the deepest sense, the throne of Hashem in the kingdom of Israel.

He looked at the Aron, and he made a decision that no other king of Israel before him would have made.

"Take it back."

Tzadok blinked.

"My lord — "

"Take it back to the city. The Aron belongs in Yerushalayim. It does not belong with me on the road. If I find favor in the eyes of Hashem, He will bring me back, and I will see the Aron again in its place. If He says: I have no pleasure in you — then here I am, let Him do to me as seems good to Him. The Aron stays in the city."

The Tanach preserves the speech word for word, and the mefarshim have read it for centuries as one of the most spiritually mature statements in all of Tanach. David was telling Tzadok, in effect: The Aron is not a magic object. The Aron is not a portable source of military protection. The Aron is the throne of Hashem, and Hashem decides whether I deserve to be near it. If I am being punished — if Avshalom's rebellion is the working-out of Natan's prophecy that the sword would not depart from my house — then I should not be carrying the symbol of divine favor with me on my flight. The symbol of divine favor stays where it belongs, in the place Hashem chose, and I go where Hashem sends me, and we will see whether He brings us back together.

It was an act of profound theological humility. David was refusing the temptation to use the Aron as a talisman. He was accepting his exile as a divine judgment without trying to manipulate the symbols of the relationship.

Tzadok stared at him.

"My lord — what about the spy ring? Who will tell you what is happening in the city?"

David nodded. He had been thinking about this. He had been thinking about it since the moment he gave the order to flee.

"I am going to leave you and Evyatar in the city. The two of you, and your two sons — Achimaatz the son of Tzadok, and Yonatan the son of Evyatar. The four of you will be my eyes in Yerushalayim. You will hear what Avshalom plans. You will hear what his counselors advise. And your sons will carry the news to me. They will use the well at En Rogel as the relay point — they will stay outside the city, and a maidservant will bring them messages, and they will ride to me with whatever they learn. Do you understand?"

"I understand, my lord."

"Then go. Take the Aron back. Set up the spy network. And — Tzadok — be careful. Avshalom is not stupid. He may suspect you. Act as if you have always been loyal to whoever sits on the throne in Yerushalayim. Do not draw attention to yourself."

Tzadok bowed. He turned. He gave the command to the Levi'im. The procession of priests carrying the Aron reversed itself and began to climb back up toward the city of Yerushalayim. David watched them go until they disappeared over the rim of the Kidron.

Then he turned and continued up the slope of the Mount of Olives.

* * *

They reached the summit of the Mount of Olives a little after dark.

The Tanach uses an extraordinary phrase to describe David's ascent: vehu oleh be-ma'aleh ha-zeitim oleh u-vocheh, ve-rosh lo chafui, ve-hu holech yachef. "And he was going up by the ascent of the Mount of Olives, weeping as he went, with his head covered, and walking barefoot."

Read those words carefully. The king of Israel was climbing a mountain in his own kingdom, in the dark, barefoot, with his head covered like a mourner, weeping as he went. The mefarshim read this as the deepest moment of David's personal humbling in the entire book. He was not just losing a battle. He was performing — physically, in his own body — the gestures of a man in mourning for his own kingship. The covered head was the gesture of a mourner. The bare feet were the gesture of a man stripped of dignity. The weeping was the gesture of a man who had nothing left to defend.

The people who walked behind him copied his gestures. The Tanach says: vechol ha-am asher ito chafu ish rosho, ve-alu aloh u-vocho. "And all the people that were with him covered every man his head, and they went up, weeping as they went up."

The procession became, in those minutes on the slope, a procession of mourners. They were all walking with their heads covered. They were all weeping. They were performing the rites of mourning while walking through the night, climbing a mountain, leaving behind the city that none of them was sure they would ever see again.

It was at the top of the Mount of Olives that the news about Achitofel reached David.

* * *

A messenger came up the slope from behind, running. He pushed his way through the procession until he reached David.

"My lord."

David stopped.

"Speak."

"My lord — Achitofel has joined the conspiracy. He is with Avshalom in Chevron. He is among the conspirators."

David's face changed.

The Tanach records his reaction not with a description but with a prayer. He raised his eyes to the sky, and he said five Hebrew words: Sakel-na et atzat Achitofel, Hashem. "Make foolish, please, the counsel of Achitofel, O Hashem."

It is a prayer of pure desperation. David did not pray for Achitofel to be killed. He did not pray for Achitofel to repent. He prayed for the counsel itself to be turned into foolishness — for whatever Achitofel might advise Avshalom in the coming days to be somehow garbled, misheard, rejected, or undone, before it could destroy the king and his cause.

The mefarshim explain why David was so afraid of Achitofel specifically. Achitofel was David's own counselor. He had been the most trusted advisor in the royal court for years. He had advised David through every major decision of David's reign — the coronation, the war with Ammon, the conquest of Yerushalayim, the building of the palace, the administration of the new kingdom. The Tanach says of him in a famous later verse: "And the counsel of Achitofel, which he counseled in those days, was as if a man had inquired of the word of God." That is a remarkable thing for the Tanach to say about any human counselor. Achitofel's advice was so reliably correct that it was treated, in the court of David, as if it were prophecy.

If a counselor of that caliber was now advising Avshalom, the rebellion was no longer a matter of military force. It was a matter of strategy, and Avshalom now had access to the best strategist in the kingdom.

David needed a counter.

He needed someone who could go back into Yerushalayim and frustrate Achitofel from the inside — someone who could undo Achitofel's counsels by giving Avshalom different counsels at the critical moments. David needed his own man inside Avshalom's inner circle.

And then, at the very moment David was praying this prayer at the top of the Mount of Olives, the Tanach says: behold, Chushai the Arkhi came to meet him.

* * *

Chushai the Arkhi was an old friend.

He was re'eh ha-melech — "the friend of the king." It was an actual title in David's court, a position with specific responsibilities. The friend of the king was the king's personal confidant, the man who served as his private counselor on personal rather than state matters. The position required absolute trust. Chushai had held it for many years.

He was now coming up the slope of the Mount of Olives, an old man with his clothes torn and his head covered with dust — performing the gestures of a man mourning the catastrophe of the rebellion. He had come to join David in exile.

David looked at him for a moment. The mefarshim treat the timing as miraculous. David had just prayed for Achitofel's counsel to be confused, and at that very moment, the man Hashem had prepared as the answer to that prayer appeared on the path in front of him. Chushai was exactly the person David needed, and Chushai had arrived without being summoned, at the exact second David realized he needed him.

David spoke quickly.

"Chushai. Listen to me. Do not come with me into exile. If you come, you will be a burden on the road. You are old. You will slow us down. But if you go back into the city — if you go to Avshalom and offer him your services as a counselor, the way you have always served me — then you can do for me what no one else can do. You can defeat the counsel of Achitofel."

Chushai listened.

"Tell Avshalom: I am the friend of the king. Yesterday I served David. Today I will serve you. As I served the father, so I will serve the son. Whoever is anointed by Hashem and chosen by the people, I belong to him. Make him believe you. He may not believe you immediately. Press the case. Be patient. Eventually he will let you into his council. And when he does — when Achitofel gives him advice that would destroy me — you give him different advice. Find a way to make Avshalom take your advice instead of Achitofel's. Buy me time. Just buy me time, Chushai. Time is all I need. If I have time to gather my forces in the wilderness across the Yarden, I can come back."

Chushai bowed.

"My lord — what about communications?"

"Tzadok and Evyatar are still in the city. Their two sons, Achimaatz and Yonatan, will be at the well of En Rogel. Whatever you hear in Avshalom's council, tell the kohanim. They will send the news through the boys. The boys will ride to me. We will have a relay system."

"I understand, my lord."

"Then go, Chushai. Go quickly. Avshalom will be entering the city tomorrow. Be there when he arrives. Make him believe you. Save my kingdom."

Chushai turned and walked back down the slope. The Tanach does not record his thoughts during the descent, but the mefarshim imagine the weight of what he had just been asked to do. He was an old man being sent to spy. He was a lifelong loyalist being asked to lie convincingly to the new king. He was being asked to play the role of a traitor to David in order to save David's life. If Avshalom did not believe him, he would be killed within hours. If Avshalom did believe him, he would have to live with the daily humiliation of pretending to serve a man he despised. Either way, Chushai's life was about to become very dangerous.

He walked back to Yerushalayim alone.

* * *

David's procession continued east from the Mount of Olives.

They walked through the night, then rested briefly at dawn, then walked again through the morning. They were heading for the Yarden River and the territories beyond — Machanayim, in particular, which had once been the capital of Ish-Boshet's short-lived kingdom and which now offered the best fortified position in the trans-Jordan that was loyal to David.

On the way out of the territory near Yerushalayim, two more encounters happened.

The first was with Tziva, the servant of Mefivoshet.

Mefivoshet, the reader will remember from Volume I, was the crippled grandson of Shaul, the son of Yehonatan, whom David had sworn to protect and had brought to Yerushalayim and given a place at the royal table. Mefivoshet had been living in the city for many years under David's care. Tziva was the steward who managed Mefivoshet's lands and household.

Tziva came up the road carrying donkeys loaded with food — bread and raisins and figs and a jar of wine. He bowed before David.

"My lord."

"Tziva. Where is your master? Where is Mefivoshet?"

"My lord — he stayed in Yerushalayim. He said: Today the house of Israel will restore the kingdom of my father to me. He is hoping to be made king by Avshalom in the chaos of the rebellion. He betrayed you the moment you walked out of the gate."

David's face hardened.

"Are you telling me the truth, Tziva?"

"As Hashem lives, my lord, I am telling you the truth. My master has decided that this is his moment. He thinks the kingdom can be restored to the house of Shaul if he stays behind."

David stood for a moment looking at Tziva. He had no way of verifying the accusation. Mefivoshet was not present. Tziva was a servant whose own loyalty was, by definition, to whichever master would best reward him. The Tanach later reveals — in Chapter 19, when David returns to Yerushalayim — that Tziva's accusation was a lie, and that Mefivoshet had genuinely tried to flee with David but had been prevented by Tziva himself, who had taken the donkeys for his own use rather than for his crippled master. But David did not know this in the moment. He could only judge by what Tziva was telling him in the road.

He made a decision he would later regret.

"Everything that belonged to Mefivoshet now belongs to you, Tziva."

Tziva bowed deeply.

"My lord. May I find favor in your eyes always."

It was a hasty judgment, and a wrong one. David was upset, frightened, and surrounded by enemies. He gave a property judgment without hearing the other side of the case. The Talmud later reads this scene as an example of a moment when even a great king can be deceived by a clever servant who takes advantage of his master's distracted moment. But the judgment was made, and Tziva walked away with the food and the donkeys and the legal rights to all of Mefivoshet's lands, and the matter would not be revisited until David came back to Yerushalayim months later.

* * *

The second encounter was even harder.

A little further down the road, in a place called Bachurim, a man came out of the bushes at the side of the path. He was an older man, of the tribe of Binyamin, and he was — the Tanach makes this explicit — a relative of the house of Shaul. His name was Shimi the son of Gera. He had been a quiet but bitter critic of the Davidic dynasty for many years. He had never forgiven David for replacing Shaul as king. He had been waiting, all this time, for an opportunity to express what he really thought of the king of Yehuda, and the rebellion of Avshalom had given him the perfect opportunity.

He stepped out into the road. He started throwing stones.

He threw stones at David. He threw stones at the men around David. He threw stones at the women and children. He shouted curses as he threw.

The Tanach preserves the curses word for word. They are some of the harshest words ever shouted at any king of Israel.

"Get out, get out, you bloody man, you man of Belial! Hashem has returned upon you all the blood of the house of Shaul, in whose place you have reigned. Hashem has delivered the kingdom into the hand of Avshalom your son. And behold — you are taken in your own evil, because you are a bloody man!"

The accusations were specific. You bloody man. Hashem has returned the blood of the house of Shaul upon you. Shimi was accusing David of having achieved the throne through the bloodshed of Shaul's family — through the killings of Avner and Ish-Boshet, neither of which David had ordered but both of which had benefited him. He was accusing David of being a usurper. He was accusing him of having gained his kingdom through blood and now of losing it through the consequences of that same blood.

He kept throwing stones as he shouted.

Avishai ben Tzeruyah — Yoav's brother, the second of the three sons of Tzeruyah, and one of David's most loyal warriors — could not stand it. He drew his sword. He stepped toward Shimi.

"My lord. Why should this dead dog curse my lord the king? Let me go over and take off his head."

David stopped him.

The Tanach preserves David's response, and it is one of the most theologically important responses in the entire chapter. David said:

"What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah? Let him curse. If Hashem has told him curse David — then who can say why have you done this thing? Behold, my own son, who came forth from my own body, seeks my life. How much more then this Binyamini? Let him alone, and let him curse. Perhaps Hashem has told him to do it. It may be that Hashem will look upon my affliction, and that Hashem will repay me good in place of his cursing this day."

Read those words carefully. David was telling Avishai not to silence Shimi because — for all Avishai or David knew — Shimi might be a messenger of Hashem. Perhaps Hashem has told him to curse. If Hashem was using Shimi to administer a part of the punishment that Natan had prophesied, then to silence Shimi would be to rebel against Hashem's chastisement. David was accepting the curse as part of his exile. He was treating Shimi not as an enemy to be killed but as a possible instrument of divine judgment.

The mefarshim treat this as the spiritual high point of David's flight. David, in this moment, was the same David who had refused to kill Shaul in the cave at Ein Gedi years earlier. He was the same David who had received Natan's rebuke without arguing. He was the same David who had said I have sinned against Hashem and had accepted the death of his first child without protest. He was now accepting the curses of a Binyamini stranger because he understood that the worst things that were happening to him were deserved, and that the ones who were inflicting them might be doing Hashem's will.

Avishai sheathed his sword reluctantly. The procession kept walking. Shimi followed alongside the road, shouting curses and throwing stones, for as long as he could keep up. Eventually the procession outpaced him, and his curses faded behind them, and David walked on toward the Yarden in silence with his head still covered and his face still wet.

* * *

They reached a resting place at the end of the day. The Tanach calls it simply ayefim — "the weary ones." The exhausted procession stopped at a place by the side of the road and rested.

The Tanach closes the chapter with that single word: ayefim. They were weary. The king of Israel and his household were weary, and the day had brought them three encounters with people who had revealed the moral landscape of the rebellion. Itai the foreign mercenary had pledged absolute loyalty. Tziva the servant had told a clever lie. Shimi the Binyamini had thrown stones and cursed. Each encounter had added another piece to the picture of who would be with David and who would be against him in the days ahead.

Ahead of them, beyond the horizon, was the Yarden River and the territory of Machanayim and the prospect of regrouping. Behind them, in the city they had left, Chushai was about to enter Avshalom's audience chamber and begin the most dangerous performance of his life.

The next chapter belongs to the council of war between Achitofel and Chushai.

But this chapter ends with an exhausted procession lying down on the bare ground at the side of a road, the king of Israel still wrapped in his mourner's cloak, his bare feet bleeding from the rocks of the Mount of Olives, the prayer for the confusion of Achitofel's counsel still on his lips, and the day's three encounters playing through his head as he tried to fall asleep in the dust under the open stars.
