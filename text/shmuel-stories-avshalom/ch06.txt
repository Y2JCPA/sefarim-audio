Chapter Six

The Counsel of Achitofel

Avshalom entered Yerushalayim the next morning.

He came up the road from the south with his army — the men who had gathered to him at Chevron, the network of supporters who had risen up in the towns of Yehuda when the shofar sounded, plus the prestigious two hundred men from Yerushalayim whom he had brought along as cover and who had now been swept into the rebellion whether they liked it or not. By the time he reached the gates of his father's old capital, he was leading several thousand armed men.

The gates were open.

There was no one to defend them. David had taken his loyalists with him in the flight to the wilderness. The remaining residents of the city — those who had stayed behind because they were too old to flee, or because they had children, or because their hearts had been stolen by Avshalom over the past four years and they actually preferred the new king — opened the gates and watched the rebellion enter.

Avshalom rode through the streets in his chariot, with his fifty runners going before him. He came up the main street to the palace of his father. He dismounted in the courtyard. He walked through the corridors he had grown up in. He entered the audience chamber. He looked at the throne — the throne his father had sat on for the past thirty years.

He sat down on it.

The Tanach does not record his thoughts in this moment. The mefarshim imagine various things. Some imagine him feeling triumphant. Others imagine him feeling, for a brief and uncomfortable second, the weight of what he had just done — the realization that his father had been the king of this kingdom for as long as Avshalom could remember, and now Avshalom was sitting on his father's chair. Others imagine him feeling nothing at all, only the cold practical satisfaction of a man whose plan had worked and who was now beginning the next phase of execution.

Whatever he felt, he did not feel it for long.

Within minutes, his counselors were filing into the audience chamber to begin the work of consolidating the rebellion.

Achitofel was the first to enter.

* * *

Achitofel was perhaps in his sixties at this point. He had been David's chief counselor for many years. He had advised David through every major decision of the reign. He was, by reputation, the wisest man in the kingdom — the kind of advisor whose counsel was treated as if it were prophetic word. The Tanach, in Chapter 16, makes this explicit in a verse that we have already quoted: "And the counsel of Achitofel, which he counseled in those days, was as if a man had inquired of the word of God."

He bowed before Avshalom.

"My lord the king."

"Achitofel. You are the most welcome of all my councilors. Sit down. I need your advice immediately."

Achitofel sat.

Avshalom leaned forward on the throne.

"My father has fled. He has taken his household and his core loyalists with him. He left ten of his concubines behind in the palace. He is now somewhere east of the Mount of Olives, heading for the Yarden. The kingdom is mine — for the moment. What should I do first?"

Achitofel did not hesitate.

The Tanach preserves his reply word for word, and it has been one of the most disturbing passages in Shmuel Bet for centuries. He said:

"Go in to the concubines of your father, the ones whom he has left to keep the house. And let all Israel hear that you have made yourself abhorred by your father. Then the hands of all who are with you will be strong."

Read those sentences carefully. Achitofel was advising Avshalom to publicly take his father's concubines — the women who had been left behind in the palace — and to do so in a way that would be visible to the entire city. The recommendation was not based on lust. It was based on a precise political calculation. In the political culture of the ancient Near East, the harem of a king was the most personal and intimate symbol of his power. To take the harem of a previous king was to claim the kingship itself. It was a gesture that announced, irreversibly, that the previous king was finished. It was also — and this was the deeper point of Achitofel's recommendation — a gesture that closed off any possibility of reconciliation. As long as Avshalom had not violated his father's intimate space, there was always a chance that the rebellion could end in negotiation, with David returning to power and Avshalom being forgiven. But once Avshalom had publicly taken his father's concubines, no such reconciliation would be possible. The act would be unforgivable. Avshalom and David would be permanent enemies. Avshalom's followers, knowing that there was no way back, would fight harder. "The hands of all who are with you will be strong."

It was, on its own brutal terms, sound advice.

Avshalom took it.

The Tanach reports the act with shocking brevity: "So they spread Avshalom a tent on the roof, and Avshalom went in to the concubines of his father in the sight of all Israel."

On the roof. The same roof from which David had once seen Batsheva bathing in her courtyard many years earlier. In the sight of all Israel. Public, visible, deliberate. The mefarshim universally read this verse as the literal fulfillment of one of Natan's prophesied punishments from Chapter 12: "I will take your wives before your eyes, and give them to your neighbor, and he shall lie with your wives in the sight of this sun. For you did it in secret, but I will do this thing before all Israel, and before the sun."

The roof of David's palace had now witnessed both halves of the cycle. David had stood on that roof and looked down into Batsheva's courtyard and begun the chain of events that brought down his house. Avshalom now stood on the same roof and acted out the punishment that Natan had prophesied. The sin and the consequence happened in the same place. The Tanach is making the point in the loudest possible architectural language.

* * *

Achitofel's first piece of advice was carried out that day. The next day, Avshalom convened his council of war for the second piece of advice — and this was the one that David's prayer at the top of the Mount of Olives was specifically designed to frustrate.

Achitofel rose to speak.

"My lord. I have a proposal. Let me choose twelve thousand men. Let me arise and pursue David tonight. I will come upon him while he is weary and weak-handed. I will strike at him in the night, and I will throw his men into a panic, and his people will flee. Then I will strike only the king — David alone — and I will bring all the people back to you. You will then be able to receive the people the way a bride is brought to her wedding. The whole nation will be at peace. There will be no civil war. There will be only one death — the death of the man you sought."

The Tanach preserves this speech word for word, and the mefarshim read it as a masterpiece of military counsel. It was almost perfect. Achitofel had identified every key factor. David was now — at this exact moment, on the night after the flight — at his most vulnerable. He was tired. He was scared. He was on the move. His men were not yet organized into a defensive formation. A surprise night attack with twelve thousand picked men would catch him before he could recover. The attack would be focused — only David would be killed, no one else, in order to minimize the cost in Israelite lives and to make the absorption of David's followers into Avshalom's army easier in the days that followed. Achitofel was thinking three steps ahead. He was thinking about what came after the killing — about how to reunify the kingdom under Avshalom's rule with the minimum amount of lasting bitterness. He was treating the entire rebellion as a strategic problem to be solved with maximum efficiency.

It was the right counsel.

The Tanach explicitly says so: "And the saying pleased Avshalom well, and all the elders of Israel."

One of the elders — a man from the tribe of Yissachar who had been one of the first to join the rebellion — spoke up.

"My lord, the counsel is sound. The timing is everything. If we wait, David will gather the eastern tribes. Gad, Reuven, and half of Menasheh will come to him. By next week he will have an army."

Another elder nodded.

"The loss of only one man — only David — is a mercy to the nation. There will be no long war. No village will burn. The rebellion ends in one night."

A third spoke.

"Achitofel has been advising kings since before some of us were born. He was advising your father when we were still boys. If he says tonight is the night, then tonight is the night."

Avshalom listened to each of them. The elders of Israel — the council of advisors who were now serving Avshalom — heard the proposal and approved it. Avshalom himself was inclined to accept it. The matter was on the verge of being decided.

And then Avshalom said something that changed the course of the entire rebellion.

"Call now Chushai the Arkhi also. Let us hear what he has to say."

The elders looked at each other. The request was strange. Chushai had only just joined the court. His loyalty was still being tested. He had not yet been made a member of the inner council. To interrupt a decision that Achitofel and the elders had already approved, in order to consult a newcomer of questionable loyalty, was — from a political perspective — baffling.

But Avshalom was the king. The elders nodded. A servant was sent to bring Chushai.

* * *

The mefarshim have wondered for centuries why Avshalom hesitated.

Achitofel had given perfect advice. The elders had approved it. There was no obvious reason to seek a second opinion. Yet Avshalom did. He asked for Chushai to be summoned. The Etzion commentary suggests several possible reasons. The first is psychological. Achitofel's speech, as preserved in the Tanach, contained an unusual feature — it was full of first-person verbs. Let me choose. I will arise. I will pursue. I will strike. I will smite the king only. Achitofel had used some form of I seven times in three verses. He was emphasizing his own role in the upcoming action. He was, perhaps without realizing it, putting himself at the center of the operation rather than putting Avshalom at the center.

For a young king who had just seized his father's throne and who was hyper-sensitive to questions of personal authority, this might have been just enough to register as a warning. Why is Achitofel saying "I"? Why isn't he saying "you, my king"? Is Achitofel planning to take credit for the operation? Is Achitofel positioning himself to become the de facto ruler of the kingdom while I become a figurehead?

The second possible reason is more practical. Avshalom may simply have wanted a second opinion because the stakes were so high. A surprise attack on David was a one-time gamble. If it failed, the rebellion would be in serious trouble. A second opinion was a normal precaution.

The third possibility is the providential one. The mefarshim treat Avshalom's request for Chushai as the answer to David's prayer at the top of the Mount of Olives. David had prayed: Make foolish the counsel of Achitofel, O Hashem. And here, in the audience chamber of Avshalom's palace, the answer was unfolding. Avshalom — without knowing why — was about to seek a second opinion from the man Hashem had specifically planted in the room to deliver a counter-counsel that would defeat Achitofel.

Whatever the reason, Avshalom called for Chushai.

* * *

Chushai was summoned in.

He had been waiting in an anteroom for hours. He had successfully passed his first test — when he had arrived in Yerushalayim and gone to Avshalom in the throne room and pledged his loyalty to the new king. Avshalom had been suspicious at first. "Is this your kindness to your friend? Why did you not go with your friend David?" Chushai had answered with practiced smoothness: "No. I will be the servant of whoever Hashem and this people have chosen. I served the father; now I will serve the son. As I have been your father's servant in time past, so I will be yours." The answer had flattered Avshalom by treating him as the legitimate continuation of the Davidic line rather than as a usurper, and Avshalom had — provisionally — accepted Chushai's offer of service. He had not yet given Chushai full access to the inner council, but he had not killed him, either, which was the main thing.

Now Chushai was being summoned in for the most important conversation of his life.

He came into the chamber and bowed.

"My lord."

Avshalom did not waste time.

"Achitofel has just proposed something. He wants to take twelve thousand men and pursue my father tonight, kill him in a surprise attack, and end the war. The elders have approved his plan. I want your opinion. Is Achitofel's counsel good, or is there a better way?"

Chushai stood very still. He had been preparing for this moment all morning. He had thought through several possible scenarios. He had not quite anticipated this scenario — Achitofel proposing an immediate surprise attack with a small force — but he had thought through enough variations that he could improvise from his prepared material.

He needed to delay David's destruction.

Every additional day David had to gather his forces in the trans-Jordan was a day in which the rebellion's chances diminished. Achitofel's plan, if executed, would end the war within twenty-four hours. Chushai had to derail it. He had to convince Avshalom to choose a slower, larger, more elaborate plan — one that would buy David at least several more days.

The trick was to convince Avshalom to do this without sounding like he was contradicting Achitofel for the sake of contradiction. Chushai had to make his counter-proposal seem objectively better.

He spoke.

The Tanach preserves his speech in detail. It is one of the great rhetorical performances in all of Tanach.

* * *

"My lord. The counsel that Achitofel has given on this occasion is not good."

He paused. He let the strong opening sit. He had begun with a direct contradiction of the wisest counselor in the kingdom. He had the council's full attention.

"You know your father. You know the men around him. They are gibborim — mighty warriors. They are bitter of soul like a bear robbed of her cubs in the field. Your father is a man of war. He will not lie down with the people at night. He will be hidden somewhere, in some pit, in some cave, in some refuge that even his own men will not know. And it will come to pass that when the first attack falls — when even some of his men fall in the first wave — then the rumor will spread: there has been a slaughter among the people who follow Avshalom. And then even the bravest of your supporters, whose heart is like the heart of a lion, will utterly melt. For all Israel knows that your father is a mighty man, and they that are with him are valiant men."

He paused again. The image of the bear robbed of her cubs was particularly effective. Every Israelite knew what an enraged mother bear was like. Achitofel had described David as weary and weak-handed. Chushai was now describing him as bitter of soul like a bear robbed of her cubs. Two completely different pictures of the same man on the same night. Avshalom was being asked to choose between them.

Chushai was not done. He continued.

"Therefore I counsel that all Israel be gathered to you, from Dan to Be'er Sheva — as the sand that is by the sea for multitude. And that you yourself go to battle in person, in the midst of the army. Then we shall come upon him in some place where he shall be found, and we will fall upon him as the dew falls on the ground. And of him and of all the men that are with him, we will not leave so much as one. And if he has gathered into a city, then all Israel shall bring ropes to that city, and we shall draw it into the river, until there shall not be one small stone found there."

Read those last sentences carefully. Chushai's counter-proposal was elaborate, dramatic, and — crucially — grand. Rather than a surgical strike with twelve thousand men, he was proposing a national mobilization. All Israel. From Dan to Be'er Sheva. As the sand of the sea for multitude. He was telling Avshalom that this rebellion deserved the participation of every fighting-age male in the kingdom, that the king himself should lead the army personally, and that the eventual victory would be so total that David's followers would be wiped out completely and David's stronghold (if he found one) would be torn down stone by stone with ropes.

The proposal was almost ridiculous on military terms. It would take weeks to gather all of Israel from Dan to Be'er Sheva. It would take additional weeks to organize them into a fighting force. It would give David exactly the time he needed to consolidate his own position, gather his own forces, and prepare a defensive battle on his own terms.

But the proposal had a quality that Achitofel's proposal did not have. It was flattering to Avshalom. Achitofel had proposed that he — Achitofel — would lead twelve thousand picked men in a quick strike. The personal glory would belong to Achitofel. Chushai, in contrast, was proposing that Avshalom himself would lead all Israel in a national campaign. The personal glory would belong to the king. Avshalom would ride at the head of the largest army that Israel had ever fielded. He would conquer his father not through a covert nighttime ambush but through a triumphant public campaign.

For a young king who loved displays of honor and who had spent the past four years building his image as the new ruler of Israel, this was an irresistible vision.

Chushai had read his audience perfectly.

* * *

The Tanach does not preserve a debate. It simply records the outcome.

"And Avshalom and all the men of Israel said: The counsel of Chushai the Arkhi is better than the counsel of Achitofel."

The Tanach adds an editorial comment that the mefarshim have read with great care: "For Hashem had ordained to defeat the good counsel of Achitofel, to the end that Hashem might bring evil upon Avshalom."

This is the key theological note in the entire chapter. The Tanach is telling the reader, explicitly, that Avshalom's choice was not an ordinary political decision. It was a divine intervention. Hashem had ordained — tzivah — that the good counsel of Achitofel would be defeated, in order to bring the destruction that the prophecy required. David's prayer at the top of the Mount of Olives had been heard. The answer was now being delivered in the audience chamber of Avshalom's stolen palace, through the mouth of an old man who had been planted by David at exactly the right time and the right place. The double causality that the Etzion commentary noted in the previous chapter — the principle that human action and divine providence work inseparably — was on full display. Chushai was using rhetorical skill. Avshalom was using political judgment. The elders were using military assessment. And underneath all of it, Hashem was directing the outcome.

The decision was made.

Avshalom rejected Achitofel's plan. He accepted Chushai's plan. The kingdom would be mobilized for a national campaign. The pursuit of David would wait until the army was ready.

The room emptied of councilors.

* * *

Achitofel walked out of the chamber alone.

He did not protest the decision. He did not argue. He did not try to change Avshalom's mind. He had been a courtier for too long to engage in pointless political fights. He had read the room. He had seen Avshalom's face when Chushai was speaking. He had understood, in the second half of Chushai's speech, that the decision was already going Chushai's way and that nothing he could say would change it.

He also understood something else.

He understood that his own life was now over.

The Tanach preserves what Achitofel did next with chilling brevity. "And when Achitofel saw that his counsel was not followed, he saddled his donkey, and arose, and got him home, to his city. And he set his house in order, and hanged himself, and died, and was buried in the sepulchre of his father."

Read those sentences carefully. Every clause is a small piece of a man's final hours.

He saddled his donkey. He left the palace. He did not say goodbye to Avshalom. He simply left.

He arose, and got him home, to his city. His city was Gilo, a town in Yehuda. The journey from Yerushalayim to Gilo would have taken several hours. Achitofel made the journey alone, on his donkey, in the long shadows of the late afternoon.

When he arrived at his estate, he called his steward into the main hall.

"Bring me my son. Bring me my two grandsons. Bring the scribe. And bring a clean scroll and ink."

"My lord — "

"Do it now."

The steward ran to fetch them. Within the hour, the son and the grandsons and the scribe were gathered in the main hall. Achitofel looked at each of them. His face was perfectly calm.

"I am going to die tonight. By my own hand. Do not try to stop me. Do not argue with me. I have read the situation and I know what is coming. The rebellion will fail. David will return to Yerushalayim within the month. When he returns, he will kill every man who supported Avshalom. I will not give him the pleasure of killing me. I will go before him."

The son — Eliam, Batsheva's father — stared at his own father. He did not speak. He had not spoken to Achitofel about the rebellion at any point. He had not joined it. He had remained in Yerushalayim throughout. He had known, the way everyone in the family had known, that his father's defection to Avshalom was rooted in the old wound of Batsheva, but he had never been able to talk his father out of it.

"Father — "

"Listen, Eliam. When David comes back, swear allegiance to him immediately. Bring the grandsons with you. Do not mention my name. Let him think I died of grief. Do not tell him anything about the counsel I gave at the palace. He will find out eventually, through Chushai, but by then you will have established yourselves as loyal subjects. The house of Achitofel will survive. My mistake will not become yours."

"Father, please — "

"Enough, Eliam. Scribe — write this down. I am leaving the estate at Gilo to my son Eliam. The olive groves on the western slope go to my elder grandson. The vineyards on the southern slope go to my younger grandson. The silver in the strongbox is to be divided equally among the three of them after my burial costs are paid. I am to be buried in the tomb of my father. Do not delay the burial. Do not mourn me in the public square. Do not let the women of the village tear their garments. The death is to be quiet. Do you have all of that?"

"Yes, my lord," the scribe said.

"Read it back to me."

The scribe read it back.

"Good. Give me the scroll."

Achitofel took the scroll. He signed it. He pressed his seal into the wax. He handed it back to the scribe.

"File it with the elders of the town tomorrow morning."

He turned to his son one last time.

"Eliam. Remember: David will win. Swear to him. Kiss his hand. Do not mention me. Survive."

Eliam was crying now, silently. Achitofel did not acknowledge the tears. He turned and walked out of the main hall and up the stairs to the upper chamber of his own house.

He closed the door.

He hanged himself. The Hebrew is vayechanak — he strangled himself. Most translators render this as "he hanged himself," and the standard interpretation is that Achitofel tied a rope to a beam and ended his own life. He is one of only a small handful of suicides in the entire Tanach. The others include Shaul, who fell on his sword on Mount Gilboa; Shimshon, who pulled down the temple of Dagon; and Zimri, who burned his palace down around himself in Melakhim Aleph 16.

He was buried in the sepulchre of his father. He had arranged the burial in advance. He had returned to his ancestral village to die. He was buried with his ancestors, in the family tomb, in the proper place for an Israelite of his standing.

He was, in his own self-controlled way, a man who had calculated his death the way he calculated everything else.

* * *

Why did Achitofel kill himself?

The mefarshim offer several explanations. The most direct one is the practical one: Achitofel knew that his counsel would have worked. He knew that if Avshalom had taken his advice, David would be dead by now and the rebellion would be over. He also knew that since Avshalom had taken Chushai's advice instead, the rebellion was now going to fail. He had read the strategic situation clearly enough to see exactly what was about to happen. David would have time to gather his forces in the trans-Jordan. Avshalom's huge mobilized army would meet David's smaller but disciplined force somewhere east of the Yarden. The battle would go badly for Avshalom. David would return to Yerushalayim. And when David returned, he would round up the conspirators and execute them — and Achitofel, the most prominent and most personally treacherous of all the conspirators, would die a humiliating public death.

Achitofel chose to die at home, by his own hand, in dignified silence, rather than die in shame at David's hand.

That is the practical explanation, and it is sufficient on its own.

But the mefarshim add deeper readings. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 106b) treats Achitofel as one of the great cautionary figures in Jewish history — a man whose Torah scholarship was so deep that he could have been a member of the Sanhedrin, but whose personal grievance against David poisoned his wisdom and turned him into a traitor. According to several midrashic traditions, Achitofel's grievance against David was specifically rooted in the Batsheva episode. Achitofel was the grandfather of Batsheva — his son Eliam was Batsheva's father, and Batsheva was therefore Achitofel's granddaughter. (See Shmuel Bet 11:3 for Batsheva's parentage and Shmuel Bet 23:34 for Eliam's relationship to Achitofel.) When David had taken Batsheva and arranged the death of her husband Uriah, he had not just committed a sin against the family of one of his Thirty. He had committed a sin against the family of his own chief counselor. Achitofel had carried the wound of that violation for years. His defection to Avshalom was not the opportunism of a man seeking power. It was the long-delayed vengeance of a grandfather whose granddaughter had been stolen by the man he served.

If this midrashic reading is correct, then Achitofel's final hours had a deeper bitterness than even the practical reading suggests. He had spent years planning his vengeance. He had finally seen his opportunity when Avshalom rebelled. He had given his counsel — counsel that, if accepted, would have killed David and completed the vengeance. And his counsel had been rejected. Not because it was wrong, but because Hashem had arranged for Chushai to be in the room with a better-sounding alternative. Achitofel had been outmaneuvered by an old man who was operating on direct instructions from David, who in turn had been operating on direct prayer to Hashem.

Achitofel rode home that afternoon understanding that he had been beaten not by Chushai but by Hashem. He understood that his vengeance had been denied, that his rebellion would fail, that his life was over, and that Hashem had decided which side would win.

He went home. He arranged his affairs. He hanged himself.

The Tanach gives his death two terse sentences and then moves on to the next thing.

* * *

That night, while Achitofel was riding home to die, Chushai was sending an urgent message to David.

He went to Tzadok and Evyatar in the city. He told them what had happened in the council. He told them that Avshalom had — for the moment — accepted his counsel rather than Achitofel's, but that Chushai was not entirely sure the decision would hold. Avshalom might change his mind. Achitofel might find a way to reverse the decision before he left the palace. David could not afford to assume anything. He had to keep moving, and he had to cross the Yarden tonight, before the situation in Yerushalayim could change.

The two priests gathered the message. They sent for the maidservant who served as the link between the priests and the spy boys at En Rogel. The maidservant carried the message to the well. Achimaatz the son of Tzadok and Yonatan the son of Evyatar received it. They prepared to ride.

But Avshalom's men had eyes everywhere. A young informant had seen the maidservant going to the well. He reported it to the palace. Avshalom dispatched soldiers to capture the spies.

The boys were warned in time. They ran. They reached the house of a friendly woman in the village of Bachurim — the same village where Shimi ben Gera had thrown stones at David the day before. The woman hid the two boys in a well in her courtyard. She covered the mouth of the well with grain so that it looked like a normal storage area. Avshalom's soldiers arrived at her house, demanded to know if she had seen the spies, were told they had passed through quickly and were probably already across the brook of water by now, and rode off in the wrong direction.

The boys came up out of the well after dark.

They ran the rest of the way to David's camp. They reached him in the small hours of the morning, breathless and covered in dust.

"My lord. Chushai sends you this message: Do not lodge tonight in the plains of the wilderness. Cross the Yarden immediately, lest the king and all the people that are with him be swallowed up."

David did not hesitate.

The Tanach says: "And David arose, and all the people that were with him, and they passed over the Yarden. By the morning light there was not one of them left who had not gone over the Yarden."

By dawn, David's entire household had crossed to the eastern side of the river. They were now in territory that Avshalom's army would have to cross a major water obstacle to reach. They had bought themselves the time they needed.

Chushai's counsel had worked.

David's prayer had been answered.

Achitofel was dead in his father's tomb in Gilo, and the rebellion that had seemed unstoppable a few days earlier was now a rebellion whose own master strategist had committed suicide and whose army had not yet even been mobilized.

The next chapter — the last and longest chapter of this volume — would be the chapter of the battle in the forest of Ephrayim, the death of Avshalom hanging from an oak, and the moment when David, locked in a small room above the gate of Machanayim, would receive the news that his rebel son was dead and would weep the most famous lament that any father ever wept for any son.

But this chapter ends in the small hours of the morning, with David's household stumbling across the shallows of the Yarden in the gray light before dawn, every person accounted for, the king himself among the last to cross, and the rebellion of Avshalom — though no one yet knew it — already past its high-water mark and beginning the slow descent toward the disaster in the forest.

* * *
