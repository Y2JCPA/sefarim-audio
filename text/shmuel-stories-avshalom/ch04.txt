Chapter Four

The Long Hair

The kiss had healed nothing.

That is the central fact of the next chapter, and the entire chapter exists to demonstrate it. David had brought Avshalom back from exile. David had received him formally. David had kissed him in the audience chamber. By all the visible measures of royal protocol, the matter of Avshalom and Amnon was now closed — the killer had been forgiven, the king had reconciled with his son, the family had been restored. Anyone walking through the streets of Yerushalayim in the days after the kiss would have said that Avshalom was once again a prince of Israel in good standing with his father.

But Avshalom had been waiting for that kiss for five years, and the kiss, when it came, was not enough.

Five years is a long time to wait for anything. Five years is especially a long time to wait for the simple act of being seen by your own father. During those five years, Avshalom had built up a complicated set of expectations about what the kiss would feel like and what it would mean. He had imagined the reunion many times. He had pictured the tears, the embrace, the formal restoration of his title as crown prince, the public acknowledgment that the past was past and the future was open. He had pictured David walking arm-in-arm with him through the streets of Yerushalayim, the crowds cheering, the elders of the tribes coming forward to congratulate him on his return. He had pictured a kingdom that, after the kiss, would treat him as the rightful heir.

The actual kiss had been brief. It had taken place in an empty audience chamber. There had been no public ceremony, no procession, no announcement to the elders, no restoration of title. David had kissed his son and then sat back down on the throne and dismissed the audience. The next morning, the routines of the palace had continued as if nothing had changed. Avshalom was permitted in the palace now. He could attend the council meetings. He could eat at the family table. But he was not formally named the heir. He was not given any official position. He was not consulted on any decision of importance. He was, in every practical sense, one of the king's sons among many sons, and not the next king that he had been expecting to be recognized as.

This was unbearable to Avshalom.

He had been the firstborn-by-circumstance ever since Amnon's death. With Amnon dead and Kilav also dead (Kilav, David's second son, had died young — the Tanach mentions him briefly in Shmuel Bet 3:3 and never again), Avshalom was now the eldest surviving son of David. By the natural order of succession, he should have been the obvious heir. The fact that David was not naming him the obvious heir could only mean one thing: David was holding the killing of Amnon against him, was withholding the formal designation as a continuing punishment for the original crime.

Avshalom decided that if his father would not name him the heir, he would simply make himself the heir.

He decided that he would seize the throne.

* * *

The Tanach records the beginning of his preparation in a single sentence that is one of the most quietly ominous sentences in the entire book.

"And it came to pass after this, that Avshalom prepared for himself a chariot and horses, and fifty men to run before him."

A chariot and horses, and fifty men to run before him.

The image is specific. Chariots, in the time of David, were the prestige vehicles of foreign kings — the Egyptians, the Pelishtim, the Aramim. Israel under David did not yet have a royal chariot corps; the army was still based on infantry and cavalry. For an Israelite prince to ride a chariot through the streets of Yerushalayim was to make a deliberate statement: I am acting like a foreign king. The fifty runners — young men who would jog ahead of the chariot to clear the way and to announce the arrival of the rider — were the standard royal entourage of the Egyptian and Persian courts. The whole display was foreign, ostentatious, and unmistakably royal.

Avshalom was making himself look like a king before he was a king.

The Talmud in Sotah 9b notes the irony: Shmuel had warned the people of Israel, in Shmuel Aleph 8:11, that if they insisted on having a king they would get exactly this — a king who would take your sons and appoint them for himself, for his chariots and to be his horsemen, and they shall run before his chariots. The warning had been delivered seventy years earlier. The people had insisted on having a king anyway. And now, in the person of Avshalom, the warning was being fulfilled. The prince who was preparing to seize the throne was doing so by adopting exactly the trappings that Shmuel had warned would be the marks of a foreign-style monarchy. The Hebrew word that Shmuel had used in the warning — ratzu lefanav, "they will run before him" — is the very same word the Tanach now uses for the fifty men of Avshalom's entourage.

Avshalom was the king the people had been warned against. They did not yet know it.

* * *

The chariot was just the beginning.

Avshalom's real strategy was much more sophisticated than mere display. He had learned, watching David rule for the past several years, that the ordinary Israelite did not particularly care about the formal trappings of monarchy. The ordinary Israelite cared about justice — about whether the king's courts were ruling fairly on the disputes that the people brought to them. The ordinary Israelite, when his case was decided in his favor, walked out of the court satisfied with the king. When his case was decided against him, he walked out muttering that the king did not really care about ordinary people. Avshalom had observed that David, increasingly, was leaving the daily judicial work to the lower courts. David himself was no longer hearing every petitioner who came to the gate of the city. The petitioners were being processed by clerks. The clerks were efficient. They were also impersonal. And every day, several dozen frustrated petitioners walked out of the lower courts feeling that the king himself had not heard them and that their case had been decided by a stranger who did not understand the particulars.

Avshalom saw an opening.

He began to rise early in the morning. Every morning. He would put on a simple robe — not his royal garments, just the kind of robe that a wealthy private citizen might wear — and he would walk down to the gate of the city and stand near the place where the petitioners were lining up to bring their cases to the court.

He would identify the men who looked most frustrated, most uncertain, most likely to be turned away by the clerks. He would approach them.

A typical exchange went something like this. A man from the tribe of Naftali had ridden three days to Yerushalayim with a complaint about a stolen ox. He had been waiting at the gate for two hours. He was tired and hungry and beginning to think he should have stayed home. Avshalom approached him.

"Good morning, friend. Where are you from?"

The man looked up, surprised that anyone was speaking to him.

"From a small village in Naftali. Three days' ride."

"Three days. That is a long ride. And what is your case?"

"My neighbor's son took my ox. The ox was tied in my field. The boy untied it and led it back to his father's house. When I went to the father to ask for the ox back, the father said the ox had been his all along, and that I was the thief. The local elders said it was my word against his and they could not decide. So I came to Yerushalayim."

"And what does the boy say?"

"The boy says he untied the ox by mistake. He says he thought the ox was his father's. But he is twelve years old and he knew exactly which ox he was untying. The elders are all friends of his father. They would not call him a liar in front of the village."

Avshalom nodded. He frowned at the right moment. He waited until the man finished. Then he delivered the killing line.

"Friend, your case is good. Your case is right. You have been wronged. The truth is on your side. But there is no man appointed by the king to hear you. You will wait here all day. The clerk will hear you for ten minutes. He will tell you to go home and sue your neighbor in the lower court of Naftali. The case will go nowhere. You will lose your ox forever."

The man stared.

"What can I do?"

Avshalom smiled. He put his hand on the man's shoulder.

"Mi yesimeni shofet ba-aretz!" he said. "Oh, that I were made judge in the land! Then every man who had any suit or cause might come to me, and I would do him justice. I would call your neighbor before me. I would call the boy before me. I would have the truth out of them in an hour. I would give you back your ox and add a sheep to it for your trouble."

The man's eyes were wide.

"My lord — "

"I am no one's lord, friend. I am only Avshalom the son of David, a man who would like to see justice done."

He drew the man into a brief embrace. He kissed him on the cheek — the formal greeting between equals, not the bow of subject to king. The man — who had come to Yerushalayim expecting to be processed by an indifferent clerk — suddenly found himself being treated as a peer by a beautiful, well-dressed prince of the royal family. The shock of the unexpected attention burned the moment into the man's memory forever.

Then Avshalom let him go and moved on to the next petitioner.

The Tanach preserves this detail with care: "And it was so, that when any man came near to do him obeisance, he put forth his hand, and took hold of him, and kissed him."

The kiss was the trick. The petitioner who had been kissed by the prince walked back to his hometown a transformed man. He told everyone he met about it. The king's son kissed me on the cheek. The king's son said my case was just. The king's son said he wished he were the judge instead of his father. The king's son acted like he knew me. The king's son understood my case perfectly. If only Avshalom were the king instead of David, my case would have been decided in my favor.

Within months, the rumor was running through every village in Yehuda and Israel. Within a year, the rumor was a movement. Within two years, it was a force.

The Tanach summarizes the result with another devastating sentence: "So Avshalom stole the hearts of the men of Israel."

The verb is vayganev. He stole. He did not earn the loyalty of the people. He did not win it through legitimate political action. He stole it. He took it from his father by deception, by performance, by the careful application of false sympathy and false promises. The line is one of the most precise observations in the entire Tanach about how political insurgents operate. Steal the hearts. The phrase has become a staple of Hebrew political vocabulary in every century since.

David, sitting in the palace, did not notice.

The Tanach is careful to record that David did not notice. The text gives us no indication that David was monitoring Avshalom's activities, that anyone in the king's circle was reporting back to the throne about what was happening at the gate of the city. David was preoccupied with the affairs of the kingdom. He was older now — perhaps sixty or sixty-five years old by this point in the narrative. He was tired. He was losing his grip on the daily details that he had once managed effortlessly. And his son was using exactly that distance — the distance between the king and the daily life of the people — as the lever for the takeover that was being prepared in plain sight.

* * *

Forty years.

The number is preserved in the Tanach in a phrase that is famously difficult to interpret. "And it came to pass at the end of forty years that Avshalom said to the king…"

Forty years from what?

David's total reign was forty years (seven and a half in Chevron, thirty-three in Yerushalayim — see Shmuel Bet 5:5). So the rebellion of Avshalom cannot have been forty years into David's reign — that would be the very last year of his life, after he was already an old man unable to leave his bed, which contradicts everything else we know about the chronology. The mefarshim have struggled with the verse. Rashi, citing the Gemara in Temura 14b, suggests that the forty years are counted from the moment when Israel first asked Shmuel for a king (Shmuel Aleph 8). According to this reading, the rebellion of Avshalom was the natural fulfillment of the warning Shmuel had given the people forty years earlier — the warning about how a king would take your sons and appoint them for chariots. The forty years were counted from the first request for a king to the moment when the kind of king Shmuel had warned about finally appeared in the form of Avshalom.

The Ralbag offers an alternative reading: the forty years are counted from the anointing of Shaul, since Shaul's reign and David's reign overlap with each other and with the period of Shmuel's judging. Other commentators suggest the forty years should be read as a round number, a literary device meaning "a full generation," rather than as a precise count.

Whatever the exact arithmetic, the point of the verse is clear: Avshalom's rebellion was not a sudden event. It had been a long time in coming. The patient seduction of the people at the gate had taken years. The chariot and the runners had been displayed for years. The kisses, the false promises, the stealing of the hearts — all of it had been the slow, methodical work of a man who had been planning his takeover since the day his father had failed to receive him for two years after his return from Geshur. By the time the actual move came, the groundwork was so deep that the rebellion was not really a rebellion at all. It was a coronation that needed only a place to happen.

Avshalom chose Chevron.

* * *

He came to David's audience chamber one morning with a request.

"My father."

"Avshalom. Come in."

"My father. I have a small request to make."

"Speak."

"I made a vow when I was in Geshur. In the years of my exile, when I was uncertain whether I would ever see Yerushalayim again, I vowed that if Hashem brought me back to the city of my father, I would offer sacrifices in Chevron — the city where my father was first crowned. I would like permission to go down to Chevron now and fulfill my vow."

David smiled. He had been smiling at his son a lot recently. The reconciliation, in his own mind, had been growing slowly more complete. He had been watching Avshalom around the palace and around the city, and he had been pleased by what he saw — a young man who had taken his exile seriously, who had returned humbled, who was now living quietly in Yerushalayim and (in David's reading) making peace with his place in the order of succession. The vow about the sacrifices in Chevron struck David as exactly the kind of pious gesture a returning exile would make. A vow made in a foreign land, fulfilled in the city of his father's first coronation. Beautiful. Proper.

"Go in peace, my son."

"Thank you, my father."

Avshalom bowed.

He left the audience chamber and walked back to his own house, and within an hour he was riding south toward Chevron with a small honor guard of twenty men. He had also sent — secretly, the day before — runners to every tribe in Israel with a single coded message: When you hear the sound of the shofar, then say: Avshalom is king in Chevron.

The runners had fanned out across the country. They had reached every major town. The men whom Avshalom had been kissing at the gate for two years had been waiting for this signal. They had organized themselves into local cells. They had identified the elders of their towns who were sympathetic to Avshalom's cause. They had pre-positioned themselves to make the proclamation as soon as the signal came.

When Avshalom arrived in Chevron, two things happened in quick succession.

The first thing was the formal ceremony of the sacrifices. Avshalom went to the altar at Chevron and offered the burnt offerings he had vowed in Geshur. The ceremony was witnessed by the local elders of Chevron and by his honor guard. It was, in every external respect, exactly the kind of vow-fulfillment that David had blessed.

The second thing was the proclamation.

While the smoke of the sacrifices was still rising over the altar, Avshalom turned to the men of his honor guard and gave a signal. One of the men — a Levite musician who had been brought along for exactly this purpose — raised a shofar to his lips and blew a long, ringing blast that echoed off the walls of the city.

The blast was the agreed-upon signal.

In every village and town in Israel, the sympathizers of Avshalom heard the blast or received the news of the blast within a few hours. They went to the central square of their town. They proclaimed the words they had been told to proclaim: Avshalom is king in Chevron. In some towns, the proclamation was greeted with confusion and uncertainty. In other towns — the towns where Avshalom's preparations had been most thorough — the proclamation was greeted with immediate acclamation and the people poured out into the streets in support.

The kingdom split overnight.

By the end of the first day, half of Israel was declaring its loyalty to Avshalom in Chevron, and the other half was waiting nervously to see what David would do.

David was about to find out.

* * *

The news reached the palace at the second hour of the second day.

A messenger came galloping into Yerushalayim from the south. He came directly to the palace gate. He demanded an audience with the king. He was admitted within minutes.

He bowed before David and delivered four words that had not been spoken in the kingdom of Israel for forty years.

"The hearts of the men of Israel have gone after Avshalom."

David rose from the throne.

"Say that again."

"My lord — the hearts of the men of Israel have gone after Avshalom. He has been proclaimed king in Chevron. The shofar has been blown. Messengers have ridden out to every tribe. The southern tribes are already declaring for him."

"How many men?"

"My lord — I do not know the count. The reports are coming in from every direction. The whole south is rising."

"Yoav?"

"Not yet known. I rode here directly when I heard the shofar. I have not had time to find out where Yoav stands."

David sat back down on the throne.

He understood, in the space of about ten seconds, several things at once.

He understood that his son Avshalom had been planning this for years and that he himself had not noticed. He understood that the seduction of the people at the gate — which Avshalom had been doing in plain sight — had been the preparation for exactly this moment. He understood that the chariot and the runners had not been youthful vanity but tactical positioning. He understood that the kiss in the audience chamber two years earlier had not healed anything, that Avshalom had walked out of that audience and immediately begun to plan a coup, and that David's failure to see the coup coming was a direct consequence of his own desire to believe that the reconciliation was real.

He understood, also, that Avshalom now had the initiative.

If Avshalom had built up enough support in the south — if Chevron and Yehuda and the southern tribes were really behind him — then Avshalom would be marching on Yerushalayim within days. The capital was not well-defended against a force coming from the south. The palace guard was small. The walls of the new metzudah were strong but they had not been designed to withstand a siege from a unified Israelite army. If Avshalom reached Yerushalayim with a real force behind him, the city would fall, and David and his household would be killed in the streets.

David did the calculation in about thirty seconds.

He stood up.

"Yoav. Avishai. Benayahu. All the captains. Front of the chamber. Now."

Within minutes, the captains were in the audience chamber.

"Avshalom has risen in Chevron. Achitofel is with him. The southern tribes have declared for him. We are leaving the city within the hour."

Yoav stared at him.

"Leaving the city? My lord, we can hold Yerushalayim. The walls are strong."

"No, Yoav. Not against a united Israelite army with Achitofel advising it. And not with the Aron here, and the whole of the royal household here. If we stay, Avshalom will besiege us, and the city will burn. I will not let Yerushalayim become a battlefield. We move east. We regroup across the Yarden, where the eastern tribes are still loyal."

"My lord — "

"Yoav. I have decided. Get the men moving. Bring only what can be carried. The city is Avshalom's for now."

Benayahu stepped forward.

"My lord. Who stays behind?"

David thought for a moment.

"Ten of the concubines. They will keep the house. Everyone else comes with us."

"Yes, my lord."

David turned to one of the scribes.

"Send a runner to Tzadok and Evyatar. Tell them to bring the Aron and meet me at the Kidron. Send another runner to Chushai the Arkhi at his house. Tell him I need him to come to me as well."

The captains bowed and scattered to give their orders.

David stood alone in the audience chamber for a moment longer. He looked around at the walls, at the throne, at the mosaics on the floor. He had sat in this chamber for thirty-three years. He was about to walk out of it without knowing whether he would ever walk back in.

The Tanach records his words to his servants, spoken as he came out of the chamber into the courtyard where the household was already beginning to assemble:

"Arise, and let us flee, for else there will be no escape for us from Avshalom. Make speed to depart, lest he overtake us suddenly, and bring evil upon us, and strike the city with the edge of the sword."

There is something extraordinary about that speech. Lest he… strike the city with the edge of the sword. David is not flying because he is afraid for himself. He is fleeing because he is afraid that Avshalom will sack the city if he finds David inside it, and David does not want Yerushalayim — the city he had spent three decades building — to be the site of a battle between his men and his son's men. David is willing to give up the city, give up the palace, give up the throne, give up everything, to spare the city from being destroyed in the fighting.

It is one of the most David-like decisions in the entire book.

It is also one of the most painful.

The chapter ends with David giving the orders for the flight. The next chapter — and the rest of this volume — will tell the story of what happened next: the long walk out of the city, the crossing of the Kidron, the climb up the Mount of Olives, the curses of Shimei, the prayer about Achitofel, the meeting with Hushai, the crossing of the Yarden, the arrival at Machanayim, the battle in the forest of Efrayim, and the death of Avshalom under the terebinth.

The kiss in the audience chamber had been five years too late.

Avshalom's hair, which had grown so heavy that he had to cut it once a year, was about to find its final use in the branches of a tree.

But that is the next chapter.
