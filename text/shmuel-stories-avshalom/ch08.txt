Chapter Eight

My Son, My Son

The two runners left the forest at sunset.

The first runner was Achimaatz, the son of Tzadok the priest. He was a young man — fast on his feet, well-trained, and one of the spy-network couriers who had been carrying messages between Yerushalayim and David's camp throughout the rebellion. Achimaatz had asked Yoav for permission to be the one to run the news of the victory back to Machanayim. The reason he wanted to run was emotional. He had grown up in David's court. He had played in the gardens of the palace as a child. He loved David. He wanted to be the one to bring the news of the king's victory to the king himself.

Yoav refused him.

"Not today, Achimaatz. You have been the bearer of good news many times. This is not a day for good news. Some other day."

"Why not today? What is wrong with today?"

Yoav looked at him.

"The king's son is dead."

Achimaatz did not understand at first. The reflexive thought was that of course the king's son was dead — that was the good news. The rebellion was over. The traitor was dead. The king could come home.

Then Achimaatz looked at Yoav's face, and he understood.

He understood that David was not going to receive the news of Avshalom's death as good news. He understood that David would receive it as the worst news of his life — that David would weep, that David would tear his clothes, that David would forget the victory and remember only the loss. He understood that the runner who delivered the news would be the runner who would be associated, in David's mind, with the moment of supreme grief. The king might not punish the runner. But the king would never look at that runner again the same way. Yoav was protecting Achimaatz from a memory that would mark him for life.

Yoav called for the Cushi instead.

The Cushi was a Cushite — a black African man, perhaps from Ethiopia or Nubia, who was serving in David's army as a messenger. He was a foreigner. He had no personal relationship with David. He could deliver the news without being broken by the delivery.

"Go, tell the king what you have seen."

The Cushi bowed and left at a fast jog.

Achimaatz stood watching him go. After a few seconds, Achimaatz turned back to Yoav.

"My lord. Please. Let me run also. After the Cushi. I will not arrive first. I will not be the bearer of the bad news. But let me run."

Yoav looked at him again.

"Why?"

"Because I want to be there."

Yoav considered. The request was strange. It was not a tactical request. It was an emotional one. Achimaatz wanted to be present at the moment when David received the news, even if he was not the one delivering it. There was something almost protective about the request — as if Achimaatz could not bear the thought of David being alone with the news from a stranger.

Yoav nodded.

"Run, then. But you will arrive after the Cushi."

Achimaatz did not answer. He bowed and was already moving. He took a different route from the Cushi — not the main road through the hills, but a shorter route through the plain along the Yarden, a route that the Cushi did not know about because the Cushi was a foreigner who did not know the local geography. The route through the plain was longer in straight-line distance but it was easier going underfoot, with no hills to climb, and a fast runner could cover the distance in less time than it would take the Cushi to climb the hills. Achimaatz was the faster runner of the two, and he was taking the easier route. By the time he was a mile out of the forest, he was already gaining on the Cushi.

He passed the Cushi at the edge of the river.

He arrived at Machanayim before the Cushi did.

* * *

David was sitting in the small upper room above the gate of Machanayim.

The upper room above the gate was the standard observation post of an ancient Israelite town. It was a small chamber built into the gate complex itself, with a window looking out toward the road by which messengers approached. David had been sitting in this room for hours. He had not eaten. He had not slept. He had not asked for any reports from the field — he had instructed his men that he wanted no reports until the news of the battle was final. He was waiting in silence, alone except for a single watchman who stood on the roof above him, scanning the horizon for the dust of approaching messengers.

The watchman called down through the opening in the roof.

"My lord. A man running. Alone."

David looked up.

"If he is alone, he carries news in his mouth."

The Hebrew is im levaddo besorah be-fiv. If he is alone, the news is in his mouth. The phrase is the technical military formula of the time. A single runner carried specific news. Multiple runners meant a chaotic retreat. A single runner meant something definite — either a victory or a confirmed death. David, sitting in the upper room, knew that a single runner approaching at full speed was carrying some particular piece of news.

The runner came closer. The watchman called again.

"My lord. I see another running behind him."

David nodded.

"He also brings news."

The watchman waited a long minute. The runners came closer. The first one was now within range of recognition. The watchman called down again.

"My lord. The first runner — by the way he runs, I think it is Achimaatz the son of Tzadok."

David sat up.

"He is a good man, and he comes with good news."

The Hebrew is striking. Ish tov hu, ve-el besorah tovah yavo. "He is a good man, and he comes with good news." David's mind, in the moment of recognizing Achimaatz, had jumped to the assumption that good news was approaching. He knew Achimaatz. He had known him since the boy was a child running through the palace gardens. He could not imagine Achimaatz running toward him with bad news. The very fact that Achimaatz was the runner was, in David's mind, evidence that the news must be good.

He stood up. He walked to the window of the upper room.

Achimaatz reached the gate. He bowed before the king with his face to the ground, and he delivered the formal opening line of a victory report.

"All is well! Blessed be Hashem your God, who has delivered up the men who lifted up their hand against my lord the king."

David nodded. He smiled slightly. The good news he had been hoping to hear had been delivered.

He asked the question he was waiting for the answer to.

"Is it well with the young man Avshalom?"

* * *

Ha-shalom la-na'ar le-Avshalom?

Six Hebrew words. Is it well with the young man, with Avshalom?

The question is one of the most heartbreaking in all of Tanach. Read it again. The king of Israel has just been told that the rebellion against him is over, that his enemies have been defeated, that his throne is secure. His first response is not to thank the runner, not to celebrate the victory, not to ask about the casualties or the captured equipment or the next steps of the campaign. His first response is to ask about the young man. The same word he used when he gave the order to his commanders before the battle. The young man. Not the rebel. Not the traitor. Ha-na'ar. The young man. The boy he had held on his lap thirty years earlier. The boy whose hair he had once stroked.

Achimaatz heard the question.

Achimaatz had been planning, on the run from the forest, to give a careful, indirect answer that would buy time. He had been thinking about what to say for the entire run. He had not been sure he would be able to deliver the truth. Now, hearing the king ask the direct question, he found that he could not deliver the truth at all. The grief on the king's face was already there, anticipating the answer. Achimaatz could not be the one to confirm it.

He gave the evasive answer he had prepared.

"When Yoav sent the king's servant — and your servant me — I saw a great tumult, but I did not know what it was."

David nodded slowly.

"Step aside, and stand here."

Achimaatz stepped aside.

He had bought himself the half-minute he needed. The Cushi was now visible, running up the road toward the gate. He would arrive in less than a minute. The Cushi was the one who had been sent by Yoav to deliver the news. The Cushi did not have a personal relationship with the king. The Cushi could deliver the truth that Achimaatz could not.

The Cushi reached the gate. He was breathless. He bowed. He delivered the formal opening line.

"Tidings, my lord the king! For Hashem has avenged you this day of all those who rose up against you."

David asked the question again.

"Is it well with the young man Avshalom?"

The Cushi was a foreigner. He did not know the protocol of indirection that an Israelite courtier would have used. He did not understand the loaded weight of the question David was asking. He delivered his answer in straightforward technical language.

"May the enemies of my lord the king, and all who rise up against you to do you harm, be as that young man is."

* * *

The chamber went silent.

The Cushi was still standing in front of the king with his head bowed. He had not yet realized that he had delivered a death sentence. He had thought he was delivering a victory report. The implication of his answer was clear to anyone listening — as that young man is meant as Avshalom is, and as Avshalom is meant dead. The Cushi had announced, in the formal language of military reporting, that Avshalom had been killed in the battle.

David did not respond at first.

He stood in the upper room for several seconds. His face did not change. He did not weep. He did not fall down. He did not tear his robe.

Then he turned away from the runners and walked across the upper room.

He climbed the small stairway that led from the upper room to the room above the gate — the smaller, even more private chamber that served as a kind of attic. He climbed the stairs slowly, like a man whose legs were not quite working. He reached the upper chamber. He went inside. He closed the door behind him.

And then, alone in the small upper chamber above the gate of Machanayim, with no witnesses except the walls of the room — David began to weep.

The Tanach records what he said, the words that came out of him in the weeping. They are perhaps the most famous words spoken by any father about any son in any human language.

"My son Avshalom! My son, my son Avshalom! Would I had died instead of you, Avshalom, my son, my son!"

Beni Avshalom, beni veni Avshalom. Mi yiten muti ani tachtekha, Avshalom beni veni.

The Hebrew is shattering. Beni — my son — appears five times in the lament. Once, twice, three times, four times, five times. He cannot stop saying it. He cannot find any other word for what he is feeling. The boy is gone. The boy who tried to kill him. The boy who killed his other son. The boy who broke his heart by being who he was. The boy who was nonetheless his son. Beni. Beni. Beni. Beni. Beni.

Mi yiten muti ani tachtekha. Would that I had died instead of you. It is the cry of every parent who has ever outlived a child. It is also the cry, specifically, of a father who knows that he himself was the cause of his son's death — that if he had been a different king, a different father, a different man — if he had punished Amnon, if he had welcomed Avshalom back from Geshur immediately, if he had not let the sin of Batsheva sit on the kingdom for a decade — then his son would still be alive. Would that I had died instead of you. David was offering, retroactively, his own life in exchange for Avshalom's, knowing that the offer was useless, knowing that no such exchange was possible, knowing that the words were nothing but the unbearable expression of a grief that had no outlet.

He wept in that small upper room for a long time.

The men below the gate could hear him.

* * *

Outside the gate, the first squads of the returning army began to arrive.

They were tired. They were covered in the dirt and blood of the forest. They were expecting the welcome that victorious armies always expect — the king coming out to meet them, the wine, the bread, the shouted blessing, the clasping of hands. They came up to the gate and they stopped, because something was wrong.

One of the first soldiers through the gate caught a servant by the arm.

"Where is the king? Why is there no one out to meet us?"

The servant looked at him, embarrassed.

"The king is in the upper chamber above the gate."

"Why is he in the upper chamber? Why is he not at the gate?"

"Because — because of the news the runners brought."

"What news?"

"The young man is dead."

"Well — yes. The young man is dead. That is why the war is over. That is why we are home. Why is the king in the upper chamber?"

The servant did not know how to answer.

Another soldier came up to the group.

"Can you hear that?"

They listened. From the upper chamber above the gate, through the thick walls, they could hear a faint, repeated, muffled sound. It was the sound of a man crying beni, beni — my son, my son — over and over.

The first soldier stared at the second.

"He is weeping for Avshalom?"

"Yes."

"Avshalom? The one who tried to kill him? The one we have been fighting?"

"Yes."

"I do not understand."

"Neither do I. But the king is up in that chamber crying for his son, and nobody wants to come out and greet us because nobody wants to be the one to interrupt him."

The first soldier did not say anything. He looked at his own hands. They were still dark with the dust of the forest. He had been on the march for three days and he had been fighting for most of today, and his arms ached, and he was hungry, and he had just lost two friends from his squad in the chaos of the fight near the ravine. He had been looking forward to a welcome from his king. He had been looking forward to being told that what he had done had mattered.

Instead, his king was weeping in an upper chamber for the man who had tried to kill him.

The soldier walked into the city with his head down.

The Tanach records the moment with extraordinary precision. "And the victory was turned that day into mourning for all the people, for the people heard say on that day: The king grieves for his son. And the people stole into the city that day, as people who are ashamed steal away when they flee from a battle."

The people stole into the city the way ashamed soldiers steal away from a defeat.

The verb vayitganev — they stole — is the same root as the verb vayganev used earlier in the volume for Avshalom stealing the hearts of the men of Israel. The Tanach is making us notice the parallel. Avshalom stole the hearts at the gate. David's grief stole the victory at the gate. The same verb is being used twice in the same volume to describe two opposite acts of theft. Avshalom had stolen love from his father. David's grief was now stealing victory from his soldiers.

The soldiers walked into the city in silence. They did not cheer. They did not laugh. They did not sing. They walked through the streets of Machanayim looking at the ground, the way a defeated army walks back to its base after losing a battle. They had won. But the king was weeping for his son in the upper room above the gate. They could not celebrate while their king was weeping. So they did not celebrate.

Yoav watched this from his position at the rear of the army. He saw what was happening. He saw the men entering the city in shame. He understood, immediately and without anyone having to tell him, what was wrong.

He turned around and went up to the gate.

* * *

He climbed the stairs to the upper room.

He did not knock. He pushed the door open and walked into the small chamber where David was sitting on the floor with his face in his hands, his beard wet with tears, his robe streaked with dust.

Yoav stood over him.

He spoke without preamble.

"You have shamed today the faces of all your servants who have today saved your life, and the lives of your sons and your daughters, and the lives of your wives, and the lives of your concubines. You love those who hate you, and you hate those who love you. For you have declared today that you regard neither princes nor servants. For today I see clearly that if Avshalom had lived and all of us had died today, then it would have pleased you well. Now therefore, arise. Go out and speak comfort to your servants. For I swear by Hashem: if you do not go out, not one man will remain with you this night. And that will be worse for you than all the evil that has come upon you from your youth until now."

The speech is one of the most extraordinary speeches in all of Tanach.

Yoav was not consoling David. Yoav was rebuking David. He was telling the king, in the most direct possible language, that the king's grief was destroying his army. He was telling the king that the men who had risked their lives in the forest of Efrayim were now being humiliated by a king who acted as if their victory was a defeat. He was telling the king that the army would mutiny — not one man will remain with you this night — if the king did not immediately get up, dry his face, walk down to the gate, and address his soldiers as victors.

Yoav was right.

David knew he was right.

The Tanach records David's reaction in a single verb: vayakam ha-melekh. And the king arose.

He got up from the floor. He wiped his face. He straightened his robe. He walked down the stairs from the upper chamber to the room above the gate, and from the room above the gate down to the ground level, and out through the gate into the open square where his men were now gathering.

He sat down on a stone bench by the gate.

The men of his army began to come past him. Each one bowed as he passed.

The first one was a young soldier from the tribe of Efrayim, perhaps nineteen years old, with a bandage on his left arm.

"My lord."

"What is your name, son?"

"Yigal ben Natan, my lord."

"Yigal. Is your wound bad?"

"No, my lord. A cut from a branch, not a sword."

"Good. Go find the physician and have it dressed properly. And eat something. You have earned it."

"Yes, my lord. Thank you, my lord."

The young man bowed and moved on. The soldier behind him stepped forward.

"My lord."

"Your name?"

"Hanan ben Yoel, my lord, of the third command."

"Hanan. Thank you for your service today. You fought well."

"Yes, my lord."

Another soldier. Another. Another. David thanked each one by name when he could, by description when he could not. He laid his hand on the shoulder of certain captains and called them by name. He asked about their wounds. He asked about their men. He performed the duties of a king receiving a victorious army.

The men, seeing that the king was now performing the duties he was supposed to perform, slowly began to relax. The shame in their faces was replaced by a tentative pride. The tentative pride was replaced by the more confident pride of soldiers who had been recognized by their king. The mood of the army began to recover.

But David never recovered the easy joy of the victory. He did the duties. He smiled when he had to smile. He spoke when he had to speak. But the grief for Avshalom was now permanent in his face, and every man who looked at him — that day, and the next day, and every day for the rest of his life — could see it.

* * *

The journey home took several days.

David did not march on Yerushalayim immediately. He waited in Machanayim for a formal invitation to return — an invitation from the elders of Yehuda and the elders of Israel, asking him to come back to the throne. The invitation was complicated by the politics of the rebellion. Many of the people who had supported Avshalom were now afraid of what David would do to them when he returned. Many of the elders who had remained loyal to David were angry at the elders who had defected and were demanding that David punish them. The kingdom was fractured. The coming-back was not going to be a simple homecoming.

David handled it the way he handled most things in the later years of his reign: with patience, with calculated mercy, and with a willingness to absorb personal humiliation in the interest of the kingdom's stability.

He sent a message to Tzadok and Evyatar the priests, who had remained in Yerushalayim during the rebellion: Speak to the elders of Yehuda. Tell them: Why are you the last to bring back the king to his house? You are my brothers, my bone and my flesh. Why are you the last to bring back the king? The southern tribes — the tribes whose support had originally given Avshalom his momentum — were the ones David targeted first. He wanted them to invite him back, not the other way around. He wanted the homecoming to begin in the place where the rebellion had begun. The symbolism was important. The reconciliation had to start where the wound had started.

The elders of Yehuda received the message. They responded by sending a delegation to invite David home.

David crossed the Yarden at the same place where he had crossed it during the flight, and he began the slow walk back through the hill country toward Yerushalayim.

* * *

Three encounters marked the journey home, and the Tanach preserves each of them with care.

The first encounter was with Shimei ben Gera.

Shimei was the same man who had cursed David at Bachurim during the flight from Yerushalayim — the man from the house of Shaul who had walked alongside the procession of refugees throwing stones and shouting "Get out, get out, you man of blood!" He had cursed David repeatedly during the flight. David had refused to let his men kill Shimei at the time, saying "Let him curse, for Hashem has told him to curse" — accepting the curses as part of the divine punishment for the sin of Batsheva, exactly as the midrash on Tehillim 3 reads the scene.

Now Shimei was at the head of a large delegation of men from the tribe of Binyamin, waiting at the bank of the Yarden as David was about to cross.

Shimei threw himself face-down on the ground.

"My lord — do not impute iniquity to me, and do not remember what your servant did wrong on the day my lord left Yerushalayim. I know I have sinned. Therefore I have come this day, the first of all the house of Yosef, to come down to meet my lord the king."

Avishai, Yoav's brother, was standing beside David.

"My lord — should not Shimei be put to death for this? He cursed the anointed of Hashem!"

David turned on Avishai.

"What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah? Why should anyone in Israel be put to death today? Do I not know that today I am the king of Israel?"

He turned to Shimei.

"You shall not die."

He swore the oath formally. Lo tamut. You shall not die. Shimei was forgiven. The man who had cursed him in his moment of supreme weakness was forgiven on the day of the supreme victory. David's mercy in this moment is one of the most generous moments in his entire reign.

The mercy was not, in the end, complete. Many years later, in the closing chapters of David's life, on his deathbed, David would whisper to his son Shlomo "deal with Shimei according to your wisdom" — a quiet reminder that he had sworn the oath himself but had not committed Shlomo to the same oath. Shimei would eventually be executed by Shlomo. But that is for a much later volume. On this day, David forgave Shimei without reservation.

The second encounter was with Mefivoshet ben Yonatan.

Mefivoshet was the lame son of Yonatan whom David had taken into his palace many years earlier — the boy who could not walk because his nurse had dropped him as a baby during the flight after Gilboa, the boy David had specifically promised to protect because of his love for Yonatan. During the rebellion, Mefivoshet had not fled with David. He had remained in Yerushalayim under the care of his servant Tziva. Tziva had brought David food and supplies during the early days of the flight, and had told David that Mefivoshet had stayed behind because "Today the house of Israel will restore to me the kingdom of my father." David, hearing this, had given Tziva all of Mefivoshet's lands as a reward.

Mefivoshet now came to meet David at the river. He was disheveled. His feet had not been washed since the day of the flight. His clothes had not been changed. His beard was untrimmed. He had been performing all the signs of mourning continuously throughout the rebellion.

David looked at him.

"Why did you not come with me, Mefivoshet?"

"My lord. My servant deceived me. I told him to saddle my donkey so that I could ride out with the king — for your servant is lame — and he saddled it for himself instead and rode away with the food and the supplies. He has slandered me to my lord the king. But my lord the king is like an angel of God. Do what is right in your eyes."

David looked at him for a long moment.

The truth was unclear. Mefivoshet's story contradicted Tziva's story. David did not know which version to believe. He could not investigate the matter properly with the army still on the road. He made a quick, pragmatic decision that satisfied neither party.

"Why are you still talking about this? I have decided. You and Tziva will divide the land between you."

Mefivoshet shook his head.

"My lord. Let him take it all, since my lord the king has come back in peace to his own house."

It was the answer of a man whose loyalty had never been about land. Mefivoshet was telling David that he did not care about the property — all he cared about was that David was safely returning home. The Tanach preserves the answer because the answer is the proof. Mefivoshet was the loyal one. Tziva was the deceiver. But David, distracted by the weight of the homecoming, did not have the energy to fully investigate. He left the matter half-resolved, and the half-resolution would haunt the memory of his treatment of the house of Yonatan for the rest of his reign.

The third encounter was with Barzillai the Gil'adi.

Barzillai was the wealthy old man from Roglim who had brought supplies to David at Machanayim during the early days of the flight. He had been one of the three men who had fed David's household during the rebellion. He was now eighty years old. He had walked with David all the way from Roglim to the bank of the Yarden, and now David was about to invite him to come the rest of the way to Yerushalayim and to live the remainder of his life in the royal palace as an honored guest of the king.

Barzillai refused.

"My lord. How long have I to live, that I should go up with the king to Yerushalayim? I am eighty years old today. Can I tell what is sweet from what is sour? Can your servant taste what I eat or what I drink? Can I hear the voices of the singing men and singing women anymore? Why should your servant be a burden to my lord the king? Let your servant turn back, that I may die in my own city, by the grave of my father and my mother. But here is your servant Kimham — let him go over with my lord the king, and do for him what seems good to you."

David nodded.

He embraced the old man. He kissed him. He blessed him. He let him go home to die in his own town. He took Kimham — Barzillai's son or grandson — back to Yerushalayim with him as a permanent member of the royal household.

* * *

The journey ended at the gate of Yerushalayim.

The crowds were waiting. The city had been preparing for several days. The streets were lined with people — the same people, in many cases, who had stood weeping in the same streets several weeks earlier as David walked out of the city in defeat. Now they were watching him walk back in. They were not cheering, exactly. The mood of the homecoming was complicated. The rebellion had been a national wound, and the wound was still fresh. The crowds were quiet, watching the king pass, taking in the moment without celebrating it.

David walked through the gate of his city.

He went directly to the palace. He went into the palace courtyard. He went into the inner chambers. He found the ten concubines whom he had left behind at the moment of the flight — the ten women whom Avshalom had taken on the roof in the public fulfillment of Natan's prophecy from many years earlier. The Tanach records the moment with terrible compression: "And the king took the ten women, the concubines whom he had left to keep the house, and put them in a house of guarding, and provided for their needs, but he did not go in to them. So they were shut up unto the day of their death, living in widowhood."

Almenut chayut. Living widowhood. The same phrase that had been used about Michal in Volume II — almanat chayim, the living widow whose marriage had been destroyed by contempt. Now the same phrase was being used about ten more women. They had been violated by Avshalom on the roof of the palace. They were not legally able to return to David's bed after what had happened, because the violation had altered their halachic status. So David provided for them — fed them, housed them, kept them safe — but never went in to them again. They lived in the palace as widows, even though their husband was the king and the king was alive.

The fulfillment of Natan's prophecy was now complete. "For you did it in secret, but I will do this thing before all Israel and before the sun." The original sin of the roof had been answered with a prophesied punishment, and the prophesied punishment had been carried out, and the women who had been the instrument of the punishment were now living the rest of their lives in the consequences of it. The roof — the same roof from which David had first looked down at Batsheva, the same roof on which Avshalom had taken the concubines — was the site that linked the original wound to the long shadow it had cast over the household.

* * *

The volume ends here.

Avshalom is dead and buried under a heap of stones in the forest of Efrayim. David is back on the throne of Yerushalayim. The kingdom is technically restored. The army is back in its barracks. The court is functioning again. Yoav is once again the chief general. Tzadok and Evyatar are once again the High Priests. The routines of the palace have resumed.

But nothing is the same.

David is no longer the king he was before the rebellion. He is older, smaller, more cautious, more hesitant. He has been broken by the loss of Avshalom in a way that he was not broken by the loss of the first child of Batsheva or by the death of Amnon. The cumulative grief of the past several years has taken something out of him that will not come back. He will live for several more years, but the David of the second half of his reign is the man who came back to Yerushalayim after the rebellion, not the man who left it. The energy is gone. The boldness is gone. The willingness to lead his army into battle is gone. He will spend the remaining years writing psalms, preparing for the building of the Temple by his son, and managing the slow decline of his own body. The arc of his life has tilted toward its end.

The next volume will tell the story of that decline — the brief crisis with Sheva ben Bichri who tried to revive the rebellion in the days immediately after Avshalom's death; the wise woman of Avel of Beit Ma'akhah who delivered Sheva's head over the city wall to end the siege; and the slow restoration of order in the kingdom.

But all of that is for the next volume.

This volume ends in the courtyard of the palace, with David sitting alone on a bench in the early evening, looking up at the roof — the same roof — where so much of his life had been written. He was thinking about Avshalom. He would think about Avshalom for the rest of his life. The grief would never quite leave him. The kiss in the audience chamber, five years too late, had not healed anything. The rebellion in Chevron, four years in the planning, had not given Avshalom what he wanted. The death in the terebinth, hanging by the hair, had not given David what he needed. Three failed gestures in a single arc — and at the end of the arc, both father and son were broken in different ways, one of them dead under a pile of stones in the forest, the other one alive but emptied out, sitting on a bench in the courtyard of his own palace, looking up at the roof.

Beni Avshalom.

Beni veni Avshalom.

Mi yiten muti ani tachtekha, Avshalom beni veni.

The five repetitions of beni are the closing words of the volume. They cannot be answered. They cannot be undone. They are the words of a father who outlived his son, and they are the words that every father since has reached for whenever he has had to bury a child.
