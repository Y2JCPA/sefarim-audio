Chapter Two

Two Years and the Sheep-Shearers

Two years passed.

The Tanach gives us those two years in a single phrase: "And it came to pass after two full years." Two complete years. Twenty-four months. Eight seasons. Long enough for the events of the previous chapter to drop out of the daily conversation of the royal court. Long enough for Tamar to settle into her unmarried, hidden existence in the back rooms of Avshalom's house. Long enough for Amnon to become comfortable again, for him to stop watching for retaliation, for him to assume that David's silence had become permanent and that whatever Avshalom had felt at the moment of finding his sister in the street had cooled into the kind of grief that no longer demands an answer.

Two years.

That is exactly the wrong assumption to make about Avshalom.

The Tanach drops a single sentence in the middle of the previous chapter that no first-time reader pays much attention to, but that turns out to be the key to everything:

"And Avshalom spoke to Amnon neither good nor bad, for Avshalom hated Amnon, because he had violated his sister Tamar."

Spoke to Amnon neither good nor bad. The Hebrew is velo dibber Avshalom im Amnon le-mera ad tov. Avshalom did not speak with Amnon for evil or for good. The verse is doing something subtle. It is telling us that Avshalom did not confront Amnon, did not curse him, did not even act distant. Avshalom simply did not speak to him at all. He was perfectly polite when their paths crossed at family meals. He was perfectly civil at the public ceremonies of the palace. He returned greetings. He asked the right questions about Amnon's health. But he did not initiate conversation. He did not seek Amnon out for any reason. He maintained, for two full years, the kind of frigid surface civility that only a man who is planning a murder can maintain toward the man he is going to kill.

The Rambam, in his Hilchot De'ot 6:5, cites this very verse as the canonical Tanach example of what the Sages call sin'ah she-balev — the hatred of the heart. The Rambam quotes the verse and treats Avshalom as the warning example of what hatred-in-the-heart looks like and where it eventually leads. The Torah explicitly forbids sin'ah be-lev in Vayikra 19:17 — "You shall not hate your brother in your heart." Avshalom's hatred was the most extreme possible expression of this prohibited form of hatred. He did not say anything to Amnon. He did not warn him. He did not even act differently around him. But he was hating him with every breath, every day, for twenty-four months, while planning the death sentence that he would eventually carry out.

The two years were the planning phase.

* * *

Avshalom needed three things for his plan to work.

He needed a location — a place outside the immediate environs of Yerushalayim, a place where he could control who was present and who was not, a place where Amnon could be killed without the killing being witnessed by any official of the king's court. He needed a pretext — an event so ordinary, so socially expected, that David could not refuse permission for it and that Amnon would have no reason to be suspicious about attending. And he needed a moment — a specific instant during the event when Amnon would be off his guard, when his bodyguards would be relaxed, when the trap could be sprung in the minimum amount of time before anyone could intervene.

The location, the pretext, and the moment came together in the form of an annual sheep-shearing festival.

Avshalom called his chief steward into his private chamber three months before the festival was due to take place.

"I am going to host the sheep-shearing this year. The full festival. I want it large. I want every prince of the royal family to attend. The wine must be the best in the kingdom. The cooks must be the cooks of the royal kitchen. Every detail must be perfect."

"Yes, my lord. May I ask why this year?"

Avshalom looked at him.

"Because I have decided that this year I will be a host. I have not been a host since I returned from — since I have lived in this house. This is the year. Make the arrangements."

"Yes, my lord."

"And one more thing."

"Yes?"

"Pick eight men from my personal guard. Men who have served me for at least three years. Men whose loyalty is beyond any question. I want their names by tomorrow morning."

The steward hesitated.

"My lord — eight men of the guard at the festival is a large detail for a private estate. May I ask what they are for?"

Avshalom did not answer at first. He looked out the window of the chamber for a long moment. Then he turned back.

"Just give me the names. I will tell you what they are for when the time comes."

"Yes, my lord."

The steward bowed and left.

Sheep-shearing was one of the great communal events of the agricultural year in ancient Israel. Sheep were sheared once a year, in the late spring, after the last rains. The wool was a major source of wealth, and the harvest of the wool was a celebration — a feast that lasted for several days, with food and wine and music and the gathering of every family member who could be brought to the shepherd's land. It was the sheep-equivalent of the harvest festivals of grain and wine. Wealthy landowners — and especially royal princes — would invite extended family members to attend their sheep-shearings as a gesture of family loyalty and as a chance to display the prosperity of their flocks.

Avshalom owned sheep. He had been raising them for years on the hills around a place called Ba'al-Chatzor — a town a few hours' ride north of Yerushalayim, in the territory of Efrayim, far enough from the city that an event held there would be effectively beyond the king's daily reach.

Ba'al-Chatzor was the location.

The sheep-shearing was the pretext.

Now Avshalom needed the moment.

* * *

Two years to the day after the rape — or as close to two years as the timing of the spring shearing season would allow — Avshalom came to David's audience chamber.

He bowed.

"My father."

"Avshalom. Come in."

"My father. I have a request."

"Speak."

"Your servant has sheep-shearers at Ba'al-Chatzor. The wool harvest will begin in five days. I would be honored if my father the king would attend, along with all of the king's servants. It would be a great joy to me and to the men of my household if the king were present at the festival."

David smiled. It was the kind of request that pleased him. Sons did not invite their fathers to family festivals unless the relationship between them was good, and David had been quietly worried for two years that his relationship with Avshalom had been damaged by the silence after the Tamar incident. The invitation was a sign — or so David read it — that Avshalom had finally moved past the grief, that he was ready to be a son again, that he wanted his father to witness the prosperity of his estate. David's heart warmed.

But David also knew, practically, that he could not attend.

"My son. Thank you. The invitation is generous. But you must understand that if I come, and if I bring all of the king's servants with me, I will be a burden on you. The royal entourage is too large for a private estate. We would eat all your food and drain all your wine cellars and bankrupt your festival before the wool was even off the sheep. I cannot accept."

Avshalom was silent for a moment.

He had been expecting this answer. It was, in fact, the answer he had been counting on. Yonadav-style planning required not just having a plan but having a plan that anticipated the response. Avshalom had calculated, two months earlier, exactly how this conversation would go. He had counted on David refusing to come personally. The whole point of the invitation was to set up the secondary request that would follow.

He pressed.

"My father — at the very least, then — let my brother Amnon come with me."

David's smile faltered.

The mention of Amnon's name was the first wrong note in the conversation. David had not expected it. He had assumed Avshalom was inviting him to a casual family festival, the kind of event where any number of family members might be present. Why would Avshalom specifically request Amnon?

David's eyes narrowed slightly.

"Why should Amnon go with you?"

The question was suspicious. Avshalom heard the suspicion. He had also planned for it. He gave the answer that Yonadav, two years earlier, would have appreciated.

"Because Amnon is the firstborn of my father, and because the firstborn of my father deserves to be honored at any festival of the family. If you cannot come, then let your firstborn represent you. It would be a shame on me, in the eyes of the people of Ba'al-Chatzor, if no son of David attended my sheep-shearing. Send Amnon as your representative."

David hesitated.

The argument was clean. The argument was reasonable. The argument fit exactly within the social conventions of the time — a royal son hosting an estate festival was entitled to expect the firstborn of the family to attend on the king's behalf. There was nothing technically wrong with the request.

But something in David's stomach was uneasy.

He looked at Avshalom's face. Avshalom was looking back at him with the same calm, slightly distant courtesy that Avshalom had been showing him for the past two years. There was nothing visibly suspicious in the face. There was nothing that David could point to and say I see something wrong here. The face was the face of a polite son requesting a normal favor.

David surrendered to the politeness.

"Why should he go? Why should Amnon go with you? Are not all my sons enough? Why this insistence on Amnon specifically?"

"My father — I am asking you. As a personal favor. Please."

David looked at him for another long moment.

Then he nodded.

"Very well. Amnon will go. And the rest of his brothers as well — all the king's sons. Take all of them. If you are going to host a festival, host the entire family. And may Hashem bless your harvest."

Avshalom bowed.

"Thank you, my father."

He turned and walked out of the audience chamber.

David watched him go. The unease in David's stomach did not go away.

He thought about calling Avshalom back. He thought about saying no, on second thought, the festival can be without Amnon, I have changed my mind. But the moment passed. Avshalom was already through the door. The royal courtesy of the situation made it almost impossible for a king to retract a permission he had just given. And David told himself, without believing it, that the unease was just a father's anxiety about a long-standing family tension, and that nothing was actually wrong.

He went back to his work.

He should have called Avshalom back.

* * *

The festival at Ba'al-Chatzor began on the appointed day.

Avshalom hosted it with great care. He had spent the previous two months making sure that every detail was correct — the wine was the finest from the Shomron vineyards, the food was prepared by cooks borrowed from the royal kitchen, the guest tents were lined with embroidered linen, the musicians had been hired from a touring troupe out of Tzor. Every aspect of the festival was designed to communicate to the king's sons, when they arrived, that this was a serious event — a major social gathering at which it would have been an insult to refuse to attend, and at which everyone present would have a memorable time.

The king's sons arrived in a single party of about twenty people. Amnon was at the front of the group, riding the white mule that signified his status as firstborn. The other sons rode behind him on lesser mules. They had all dressed in their best festival robes. They had brought bodyguards — perhaps a dozen armed men in total, riding in a loose formation around the princes.

Avshalom met them at the gate of his estate.

"Brothers. Welcome. My house is your house. Come in, eat, drink, rest. The wool will not be sheared until tomorrow. Tonight is for the feast."

He embraced each brother in turn — including, with no apparent emotion, Amnon. Amnon embraced him back. Two years of silence had convinced Amnon that whatever Avshalom had once felt about the Tamar incident was now safely in the past. Amnon felt no fear. He had not felt fear about Avshalom in many months. He walked into Avshalom's house and sat down at the long table that had been prepared for the feast, and the wine began to be poured, and the music began to play, and the festival began.

Avshalom was a careful host. He did not drink much himself. He moved around the room, refilling cups, giving toasts, telling jokes. He made sure that the cup in front of Amnon was always full. He made sure that the wine in Amnon's cup was the strongest of the four wines he was serving. He made sure that no servant approached Amnon's place without being supervised personally by Avshalom — because Avshalom did not want anyone to notice that Amnon's cup was being refilled at twice the rate of the other cups around the table.

Within two hours, Amnon was visibly drunk.

Three days before the festival, Avshalom had summoned the eight men of his personal guard into the back room of his estate at Ba'al-Chatzor.

"Listen carefully. In three days, the king's sons will arrive at this estate for the festival. One of them is Amnon, the eldest. At some point during the feast — I do not yet know when, perhaps the first night, perhaps the second — I will give a signal. The signal will be the word now, spoken loudly enough for you to hear from across the room. When you hear that signal, you will draw your swords and you will strike Amnon. You will not strike anyone else. Only Amnon. Do you understand?"

The eight men were silent.

The senior of them — a man who had been with Avshalom since the years in Geshur — finally spoke.

"My lord. You are asking us to kill the eldest son of the king. Do you understand what will happen to us afterward?"

"Nothing will happen to you. I will take responsibility. I will tell every man at the feast that I gave the order. The killing will be on me, not on you. Be courageous, and be sons of valor. I am the son of the king. The order is mine. The consequences will be mine. You will be doing the will of your master."

"And after the feast?"

"After the feast, I will leave for Geshur. My grandfather will receive me. I will be safe. You will continue in this household under my steward until I return."

"And if you do not return?"

Avshalom looked at the senior guardsman.

"I will return. Trust me. Now — the swords. Wear long robes that day. The robes must reach to your knees, with wide sleeves. The swords go inside the sleeves. Nobody at the feast must see a weapon until I give the signal. Is that clear?"

"Yes, my lord."

"Good. Go."

The eight men filed out of the room.

The Tanach preserves the form of the order Avshalom gave that day in a single verse: "And Avshalom commanded his servants, saying: Watch now when Amnon's heart is merry with wine, and when I say to you: Strike Amnon — then kill him. Do not be afraid. It is I who command you. Be courageous, and be sons of valor."

When the festival began, the eight men were in their positions, wearing the robes Avshalom had described, the swords concealed in their sleeves, watching their master across the long table for the signal that he had told them to wait for.

Within two hours of the start of the feast, Amnon was visibly drunk.

Within three hours, he was beyond drunk.

He was laughing at jokes that had not been told. He was telling stories that had no point. He was gesturing extravagantly with his hands. He was the loudest man at the table. The other sons of David were also drinking, but none of them were as far gone as Amnon — because none of them had been as carefully and as deliberately gotten drunk by their host.

Avshalom watched him from across the room.

When Amnon's head finally began to nod forward, when the laugh became a slow, slurred attempt at a laugh, when the fingers loosened on the cup and a spill of wine ran down the front of his festival robe — Avshalom turned to his servants.

He gestured. A small, controlled gesture with two fingers of his right hand.

The servants — eight of them, all of whom had been in Avshalom's service for years, all of whom had been told the plan three days earlier and had been chosen specifically because they would obey without questioning — moved into position behind the table.

Avshalom raised his voice.

"Now."

* * *

The Tanach records the moment in the cleanest possible language.

"And Avshalom commanded his servants, saying: Watch now when Amnon's heart is merry with wine, and when I say to you: Strike Amnon — then kill him. Do not be afraid. It is I who command you. Be courageous, and be sons of valor."

The first verb is Watch. The servants had been watching for hours. They had been waiting for the signal. The signal had now come. They moved.

The second verb is Strike. The Hebrew is hakku. Hit him. The servants drew their short swords from beneath their tunics — Avshalom had instructed them to wear robes long enough to conceal the weapons — and they stepped forward to the place where Amnon was sitting.

Amnon, in the half-second before the swords reached him, looked up. He saw the eight servants. He saw the swords. He saw, on the other side of the room, Avshalom's face — and he understood, in the final fraction of a second of his life, what the past two years of silence had really meant.

He did not have time to speak.

The swords came down.

The other sons of David, sitting around the table, saw the moment too late to react. Some of them were as drunk as Amnon. Some of them were sober enough to understand what was happening but too far away to intervene. Most of them simply froze in shock. The killing of Amnon took perhaps four seconds from the first stroke of the first sword to the final stillness of the body slumped over the table. Wine and blood mixed on the wood. The musicians had stopped playing the moment they heard Avshalom's command.

The chamber went silent.

Then the chamber erupted.

* * *

The other sons of David — the ones who had not been killed — scrambled. They thought, in the panic of the moment, that all of them were about to be killed. They thought that this was a coup, that Avshalom was eliminating every potential rival for the succession in a single afternoon, that the next swords would come for them.

"Run!"

The voice was Adoniyahu's — David's fourth son, sitting two places down from where Amnon had been killed. He was the first of the remaining brothers to find his voice.

"Get to the mules! All of you — to the mules! Run!"

The other brothers did not need a second order. They threw themselves up from the table. They ran for the door. They mounted their mules in a chaos of dropped reins and shouted commands. They galloped out of Avshalom's estate at top speed, scattering across the hills of Efrayim, riding back toward Yerushalayim as fast as their mules could carry them.

Avshalom did not chase them.

He had no intention of chasing them. He had killed Amnon. Amnon was the only one he had ever planned to kill. The other brothers had been part of the cover for the operation — invitations to all of them had been a way of disguising the targeted killing of one of them. Now that Amnon was dead, Avshalom needed his other brothers to survive and to ride back to Yerushalayim with the news. He needed the news to reach David quickly.

He needed something else, also.

He needed to disappear.

He had known, two years ago when he started planning, that the moment Amnon was dead, Avshalom himself would have to flee. There was no way to kill the king's firstborn son and remain in the king's court. David would either order him executed, or order him imprisoned, or order him exiled. Avshalom did not intend to give David the choice. Avshalom had already arranged a place to go.

His mother Ma'akhah was the daughter of Talmai, king of Geshur — a small Aramean kingdom on the northeastern border of Israel, in the territory that today is called the Golan. Talmai was Avshalom's grandfather. Talmai was politically independent of David. Talmai had no obligation to extradite his grandson. Talmai had been making it known, through quiet back-channel messages over the past several years, that Avshalom would always be welcome in Geshur if he ever needed a place to go.

Avshalom needed it now.

He left Ba'al-Chatzor within an hour of the killing. He took a small group of trusted men with him — perhaps ten servants, all heavily armed. He rode east, then north, around the head of the Yarden, into the territory of his grandfather. He did not stop until he had crossed the border. He arrived at Talmai's court two days after the killing and was received with full honors as the grandson of the king.

He would remain in Geshur for three years.

* * *

Meanwhile, the news of the killing was racing back to Yerushalayim ahead of David's other sons.

The first version of the news that reached David was wrong.

It came in the form of a frantic messenger who had ridden ahead of the surviving princes — perhaps a servant of one of the brothers, perhaps a man from a nearby village who had heard the screaming. The messenger arrived at the palace gate, demanded an audience with the king, and delivered his report from the floor of the audience chamber.

"My lord! Avshalom has killed all the king's sons! There is not one left!"

David rose from the throne.

"What?"

"All of them, my lord. Every prince. At a feast at Ba'al-Chatzor. Avshalom commanded his servants and they struck them down at the table. The reports are still coming in. Some say twenty are dead. Some say more."

"All my sons are dead."

"Yes, my lord."

The Tanach records his reaction: he tore his royal robe — the formal Hebrew gesture of mourning — and he threw himself face-down on the ground. The servants and the other officers of the court who were present did the same. They tore their robes. They lay on the ground. The palace went into instant, terrible mourning. All my sons are dead. It was the worst news a father could possibly receive. David lay on the ground and wept for what felt like a very long time.

And then Yonadav arrived.

Yonadav — the very wise man, the man who had set the original plan in motion two years earlier by advising Amnon how to get Tamar alone — appeared in the audience chamber. He had been at the palace, and he had heard the messenger's report, and he had come immediately to correct it.

He bowed before David.

"My lord. Let not my lord suppose that they have killed all the young men, the king's sons. Only Amnon is dead."

David lifted his face from the ground.

"Only Amnon?"

"Only Amnon, my lord. By the appointment of Avshalom this has been determined from the day that Amnon forced his sister Tamar. Now therefore, let not my lord the king take the matter to his heart, to think that all the king's sons are dead. Only Amnon is dead."

"How do you know this, Yonadav?"

"My lord — I know."

David looked at his nephew for a long moment. Yonadav's face was perfectly composed. He showed no grief at the death of the man who had been his friend, his client, the man whose obsession with Tamar he had personally enabled two years earlier. He was speaking with the calm, measured tone of a court advisor delivering a routine update.

"You knew this was going to happen?"

"I suspected, my lord. I was in a position to suspect. I did not know the day."

"And you did not warn anyone."

"My lord — there was nothing to warn anyone about until the moment it happened. Suspicions are not actions. I would not have come before the king with a suspicion."

David did not answer.

The Tanach preserves Yonadav's speech almost word for word, and it is one of the most chilling speeches in all of the chapter. Read it again. By the appointment of Avshalom this has been determined from the day that Amnon forced his sister Tamar. Yonadav had known. Yonadav, who had advised Amnon to commit the original violation, had been monitoring Avshalom's plans for the entire two years. He knew that Avshalom was planning to kill Amnon. He knew, in some sense, when and how it was going to happen. He had said nothing — not to Amnon, who would have been his friend if friendship meant anything to Yonadav, and not to David, who would have been his uncle and king. He had simply watched.

And the moment the killing happened, Yonadav appeared at the palace to deliver the news in such a way as to minimize David's grief, to redirect David's attention to the surviving sons, and — implicitly — to position himself as a knowledgeable insider who could be trusted in the future. The Etzion commentary on Shmuel Bet 13:32 reads Yonadav's coldness in this scene as the final proof of his moral character. He had used Amnon. He had sacrificed Amnon. He had then used Amnon's death to advance his own standing at court. He was, from beginning to end, the very wise man who used his wisdom for nothing but his own advantage.

David listened to Yonadav's report.

He sat up slowly. He wiped his face. He looked at Yonadav.

"Only Amnon."

"Only Amnon, my lord."

David let out a long breath. The terrible mourning of the previous five minutes — the conviction that all his sons were dead — was being replaced by a different and slightly less terrible grief. Only Amnon. His firstborn. The boy he had loved most. The boy he had been unable to discipline two years earlier when the discipline had been needed. Now the boy was dead because the discipline had not been applied. The chain of consequences was unmistakable. If David had punished Amnon for the rape of Tamar, Amnon would still be alive. The sword that Natan had said would not depart from David's house — that sword had taken its first life today. And the sword had been able to take that life precisely because David himself had refused to use the sword of justice when the sword of justice was called for.

The other sons arrived in the palace courtyard about an hour later. They were dirty, exhausted, terrified. David received them. He counted them. They were all there — Kilav, Adoniyahu, Shefatyah, Yitre'am, the younger boys. Only Amnon was missing. The mourning of the household, which had been for all the sons, was now redirected to be for one son. The royal gardens filled with the sound of weeping. David tore his robe again. He sat on the ground for many days, mourning for Amnon his firstborn.

The Tanach records the next verse in a single line: "And Avshalom fled, and went to Talmai the son of Ammichud, king of Geshur. And David mourned for his son every day."

* * *

Three years.

The Tanach jumps over them in two sentences. "So Avshalom fled, and went to Geshur, and was there three years. And the soul of King David longed to go forth to Avshalom; for he was comforted concerning Amnon, seeing that he was dead."

For three years, Avshalom lived at the court of his grandfather Talmai in Geshur. He hunted. He rode. He drilled with the small military units of the Aramean garrison. He ate the food of his grandfather's table. He slept in a guest chamber that had been set aside for him. He was, in every external respect, a prince in exile — comfortable, safe, well-treated, but cut off from the place he was born and from the father he had once expected to inherit a kingdom from.

And in those three years, two transformations happened simultaneously in Yerushalayim and in Geshur, on opposite sides of the border.

In Yerushalayim, David's grief for Amnon slowly faded. The first year was bitter. The second year was sad. The third year was something more like resignation. By the end of the third year, the Tanach tells us, David was comforted concerning Amnon, seeing that he was dead. The grief had run its course. David had accepted the loss. And in the space left behind by the receding grief, a new feeling began to grow: a longing for Avshalom.

The Tanach is precise. Vatekhal David ha-melekh latzet el Avshalom. And the soul of King David longed to go forth to Avshalom. The verb vatekhal — from the root kalah, to be consumed — is the verb of intense yearning. David's soul yearned for Avshalom. The grief for Amnon had been replaced by a hunger to see his other son — the son who had killed Amnon, the son who was technically a fugitive from royal justice, the son who should have been brought back in chains to face execution. David did not want to bring Avshalom back in chains. David wanted to see his face.

In Geshur, meanwhile, the second transformation was underway. Avshalom was changing. He had arrived at his grandfather's court as a wounded prince, a young man burning with the satisfied rage of having killed the brother who had violated his sister. But three years of exile in a foreign court had begun to change him. The wound of Tamar's violation was scabbing over. The satisfaction of Amnon's death was fading. And in the empty space, Avshalom was being filled with a different kind of feeling — a feeling that he had not had before he came to Geshur, but that the slow exile and the foreign court had cultivated in him. He was being filled with ambition.

Avshalom was now the eldest surviving son of David. With Amnon dead, the order of succession had shifted. The next-in-line son, technically, was Kilav — but Kilav had died young (he is not mentioned in any of the later chapters of Shmuel Bet, and the tradition is that he died as a young man, which is why the line of succession passes over him). The next son after Kilav was Avshalom himself. Avshalom was the heir presumptive to the throne of David.

He had not realized this immediately. But by the second year of his exile, he had begun to think about it. By the third year, he was thinking about almost nothing else. He was the heir. He was in exile. The two facts were incompatible. He needed to come back. He needed to be reinstated. He needed his father — who, he was hearing through the back-channels of the court, had begun to long for him — to formally recognize him as the heir and welcome him home.

But Avshalom could not engineer this himself. He had killed his brother. He had no leverage to negotiate his own return. He needed someone inside the palace to engineer it for him.

He needed a man with influence over the king. A man who was loyal to David but who was also pragmatic enough to see that bringing Avshalom back was now in the kingdom's interest. A man who knew how to construct the kind of indirect persuasion that would let David make a decision he wanted to make without admitting that he wanted to make it.

He needed Yoav.

And in the fourth year of his exile, the message reached him in Geshur that Yoav was beginning to act.

The wise woman of Tekoa was about to visit the king.

* * *
