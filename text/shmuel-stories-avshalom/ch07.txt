Chapter Seven

The Forest of Ephrayim

David made Machanayim his base of operations.

Machanayim was an old Israelite town on the eastern bank of the Yarden — a town with a long history in the books of the patriarchs, dating back to the night when Yaakov had wrestled with the angel and named the place the camp of God. It had been one of the towns assigned to the Levi'im at the time of the conquest under Yehoshua. It had a defensive wall, a gate complex, a small palace that had served as the seat of various local governors over the centuries. It had also been, briefly and humiliatingly, the capital of Ish-Boshet during the civil war years that began this very volume of Tanach. The same town where Avner and Yoav had played their grim chess game at the pool of Giv'on was now serving as David's refuge from his own son.

The men of the eastern territories — the tribes of Reuven, Gad, and the half-tribe of Menasheh — rallied to David almost immediately. They sent messengers offering food, weapons, men. Three particular men of the region were especially generous: Shovi the son of Nachash from Rabbah of Ammon (a man who came from the very city David's army had besieged a decade earlier, and whose father had apparently been treated kindly by David at the time of the surrender), Makhir the son of Ammiel from Lo-Devar (the same Makhir who had once cared for Mefivoshet the son of Yonatan, mentioned briefly in Volume I of this series), and Barzillai the Gil'adi from Roglim. The three of them brought beds, basins, earthenware vessels, wheat, barley, flour, parched grain, beans, lentils, honey, butter, sheep, and cheese. The Tanach lists the supplies in detail because the supplies are evidence: the eastern tribes had not abandoned David. Avshalom's rebellion had captured the south and the center, but the tribes east of the Yarden remained loyal, and they were prepared to feed the king's household for as long as necessary.

David spent the first three days at Machanayim regrouping.

He took stock of his forces. He had brought with him from Yerushalayim perhaps six hundred men of his immediate guard — the Cherethites and Pelethites, plus the six hundred Pelishti mercenaries under Itai of Gat. With the volunteers from the eastern tribes, his total force grew over those three days to perhaps several thousand armed men. It was a small army by the standards of the rebellion that was now mustering against him, but it was an army of veterans — men who had been fighting under David for decades, who knew his tactics, who trusted his judgment, and who had no intention of surrendering to a younger usurper whose only military experience was the killing of his half-brother at a dinner party.

He divided the army into three commands. The first command went to Yoav, his oldest general. The second went to Avishai, Yoav's brother. The third went to Itai of Gat — the Pelishti convert who had pledged his loyalty on the slopes of the Mount of Olives and who had now been given a senior command in the army of Israel as a reward for that loyalty. The Tanach is careful to record the three commanders by name. The point is that David's army was led by three men whose loyalty was beyond any possible question: a man who had served David since the wilderness years, that man's brother, and a foreign convert whose only reason for being in David's camp at all was personal devotion to David himself.

When the army was ready, David called the three commanders together in the courtyard of his command quarters at Machanayim.

"I will go out with you. I will lead the third command myself."

Yoav looked at him.

"My lord — "

"I am still a soldier, Yoav. I have led every army I have ever sent into battle. I am not going to sit in this town watching my men die for me."

"My lord, you cannot."

"Why not?"

Yoav's voice was very quiet.

"Because you are worth ten thousand of us. If you flee, the enemy will not chase us — they will chase you. If half of us die, no one will care. But if one of you dies, the rebellion has won. Sit at the gate. Let us fight the battle for you."

David did not answer.

Avishai stepped forward.

"My lord. Yoav is right. The men know it. We have been talking about it all morning. If you ride out with us today, every man in this army will spend the battle watching the king instead of watching the enemy. We will lose because we will be afraid for you. Stay at the gate."

Itai of Gat — the Pelishti convert who had pledged his loyalty on the slopes of the Mount of Olives — also stepped forward.

"My lord. I left my own city to follow you. I did not follow you so that I could let you die in a forest. Stay at the gate."

David looked at the three of them — his oldest general, his oldest general's brother, and the foreign convert who had become his most loyal field commander.

He nodded slowly.

"I will do whatever seems good in your eyes."

He took up a position at the gate of Machanayim and watched his three armies march out.

But before they marched, he gave them one order that would shape everything that followed.

He stood up from the bench by the gate. He raised his voice so that the captains and the front ranks of the army could hear him.

"Yoav. Avishai. Itai. Hear me. Le-at li la-na'ar le-Avshalom. Deal gently with him. Deal gently for my sake with the young man, with Avshalom. Whatever else happens in the forest today — bring him back to me alive."

Yoav's face did not change. He bowed.

"I have heard the king's command."

Avishai bowed.

"I have heard the king's command."

Itai bowed.

"I have heard the king's command."

David nodded. He sat back down on the bench.

The three captains turned and walked back to the head of their commands. The men of the army had heard every word. The order was now part of the army's collective knowledge. Deal gently with the young man. Bring him back alive. Every soldier in the three commands, marching out of the gate of Machanayim that morning, was carrying the order in his ears as he marched.

* * *

The order is preserved in the Tanach in a single verse, and it is one of the most painful verses in all of Shmuel Bet.

"And the king commanded Yoav and Avishai and Itai, saying: Deal gently for my sake with the young man, with Avshalom. And all the people heard when the king gave all the captains charge concerning Avshalom."

Le-at li la-na'ar le-Avshalom. "Deal gently for my sake with the young man, with Avshalom."

There are several painful things about this verse. The first is the word na'ar. The young man. Avshalom was no young man. He was perhaps twenty-eight or thirty years old. He had three sons and a daughter. He had been planning a coup for four years. He had successfully overthrown his father and seized the throne of Israel. He was, by every objective measure, a mature adult and a dangerous enemy of the king. But David could not bring himself to call him the rebel, or the traitor, or the usurper, or even Avshalom my son in his rebellion. David called him the young man — the same word a father uses for a teenage boy. David's heart, in the moment of giving the order, had reverted to a much earlier image of his son. He was seeing the boy he had once held on his lap, not the man who had stolen the kingdom.

The second painful thing is the phrase le-at li — gently, for my sake. David was asking his three commanders to extend a personal favor to him in the middle of a war that they were fighting on his behalf. He was asking them to fight the battle but not to win it completely. He was asking them to defeat Avshalom's army but to spare Avshalom himself. He was asking them, in effect, to do something militarily impossible — to defeat a rebellion without killing the man leading the rebellion. Any general who has ever fought a civil war knows that this kind of order cannot be obeyed in any way that the giver of the order would actually want. If you defeat the rebel army and capture the rebel leader, you still have to do something with him — execute him, imprison him, or release him. If you release him, the rebellion will start again within a year. David was asking his commanders to put themselves into a tactical situation that had no good resolution.

The third painful thing is the phrase and all the people heard. The order was not given privately. David did not call Yoav into his private chamber and whisper the request. David gave the order publicly, in front of the gathered army, where every soldier could hear it and where every soldier would be expected to remember it. The Tanach is careful to record the public nature of the order because the public nature of the order will eventually matter — it will matter when an ordinary soldier finds Avshalom hanging in the branches of the terebinth and refuses to kill him because the king publicly ordered us to be gentle with him.

David was making his commanders fight a war with their hands tied behind their backs.

He did not know that he was doing it. He thought he was simply expressing the love of a father for a son. But he was, in fact, putting his commanders into an impossible situation, and Yoav — listening to the order from his place at the head of the first command — heard it for what it was and decided, in the same moment he heard it, that he would not obey it.

Yoav had been with David for too long to obey an order that he knew would destroy the kingdom. He understood, the way David apparently could not, that the rebellion would only end when Avshalom was dead. He understood that any other outcome would be temporary, would be unstable, would lead to a second rebellion within months. He understood that the kingdom needed Avshalom dead, and that the king's love for Avshalom was the greatest threat to the survival of the kingdom.

He nodded politely when the order was given.

He had no intention of obeying it.

* * *

The armies marched out from Machanayim early in the morning.

They marched west, then north, into the hill country known as the Forest of Efrayim. The name is a little strange — the forest is on the eastern side of the Yarden, in the territory of Gad and the half-tribe of Menasheh, not in the territory of Efrayim proper. The mefarshim explain the name in different ways. Some say there had once been a battle in this forest involving the men of Efrayim, and the place had taken the name from that battle. Others say the forest was simply named after a particular group of Efraimite settlers who had lived there for a generation. Whatever the etymology, the place was a thick, ancient forest of oaks and terebinths — old trees with massive trunks and high branches and a tangled understory that made it nearly impossible for an army to maintain ordered formation while moving through it.

That was the point of fighting there.

Yoav had chosen the forest deliberately. He had calculated that Avshalom's army — which by now had grown to many thousands of men, led by a young general named Amasa whom Avshalom had appointed in place of Yoav — would be a larger force than David's three commands but would be a less experienced force, full of new recruits who had been swept into the rebellion in the past few weeks. A larger but inexperienced force would do well in open country. In dense forest, where individual fighting was the rule and where ordered formations could not be maintained, the smaller and more experienced force would have the advantage. Yoav was going to use the forest to negate Avshalom's numerical advantage.

The two armies met in the forest at midday.

The battle that followed is described in the Tanach in a single sentence that captures everything: "And the people of Israel were defeated there before the servants of David, and there was a great slaughter there that day, of twenty thousand men. For the battle was scattered there over the face of all the country, and the forest devoured more people that day than the sword devoured."

The forest devoured more people that day than the sword devoured.

The line is one of the most haunting in all of Tanach. The forest itself was killing men. The dense undergrowth, the ravines, the tangled thickets, the holes in the ground, the swamps along the streams — the forest was a living trap. Avshalom's men, who did not know the terrain, were getting lost, falling into ravines, drowning in marshes, getting tangled in thickets where they could not draw their weapons. David's men, who were operating in a more disciplined formation and who had probably scouted the terrain in advance, were able to use the forest itself as a weapon. By the end of the day, twenty thousand of Avshalom's men were dead — not all of them killed by swords. Many of them killed by the forest.

Avshalom himself was on horseback. He had been riding through the battle on a mule — the prestige animal of the Davidic royal house, the mule that had been his since the days when he had been the heir presumptive and the king's son and the most beautiful young man in all of Israel. He had been giving orders, riding from one cluster of his men to another, trying to hold the line against the relentless advance of David's veteran troops. By midafternoon, he understood that the battle was lost. He turned his mule and tried to flee.

The flight took him under the branches of a great terebinth tree.

* * *

The Tanach records what happened next with extraordinary care. "And Avshalom met the servants of David. And Avshalom was riding upon his mule, and the mule went under the thick boughs of a great terebinth, and his head caught hold of the terebinth, and he was lifted up between the heaven and the earth, and the mule that was under him went away."

His head caught.

The most beautiful man in Israel — the man whose hair had been so long and so heavy that he had to cut it once a year — was undone by his own hair. The Tanach does not explicitly say that it was the hair that caught in the branches. The text only says vayechezak rosho ba-elah — and his head caught hold of the terebinth. But the mefarshim are unanimous in reading this as the moment when the famous hair of Avshalom — the three pounds of hair he cut at every annual cutting, the hair that had been one of the marks of his beauty since he was a teenager — became the instrument of his death. The hair tangled in the branches. The mule walked out from under him. He was suspended, hanging in midair from his own hair, between the heaven and the earth, his weight pulling on his neck, his hands flailing for the branches above him, unable to reach them, unable to climb up, unable to drop down.

He hung there.

The image is one of the most resonant in all of Tanach. Bein ha-shamayim u-vein ha-aretz. Between the heaven and the earth. Avshalom — the man who had tried to grasp the throne of Israel — was now hanging in a place that was neither heaven nor earth, neither the sky he had reached for nor the ground he had been born on. He was suspended in the very middle of the cosmos, stripped of his mule, stripped of his army, stripped of his crown, hanging by the long beautiful hair that had been his vanity since he was a boy.

The Talmud in Sotah 10b preserves a remarkable midrashic detail about this moment. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught that Avshalom, hanging in the branches, pulled a sword from his belt and raised it to cut his own hair — to free himself by sacrificing the very feature he had been so proud of. At the instant he raised the sword, Gehinnom opened beneath him. Avshalom looked down from the branches and saw the pit of Gehinnom yawning below him, and he stopped. He lowered the sword. He chose to keep hanging by his hair rather than cut himself free and fall into the mouth of Gehinnom. The midrash treats this as the moment of Avshalom's own final recognition — the moment when he understood, too late, what his rebellion had cost him. He had reached for a crown and grasped only his own hair. He had cut himself off from his father and found himself hanging between the heaven and the earth with nothing beneath him but the opened mouth of the next world.

The Etzion commentary draws an extended parallel between this scene and the scene at the binding of Yitzchak in Bereishit 22. The parallel is striking. In the Akedah, Avraham is willing to sacrifice his son, and a ram is caught in a thicket by its horns and offered up in the son's place. In our scene, David is not willing to sacrifice his son — he has issued the order deal gently with the young man — and the result is that the son himself is caught in a thicket by his head and offered up in the place of the ram. The binding of Yitzchak ended with the son saved by a substitute. The binding of Avshalom ends with the son becoming the substitute, because his father was unwilling to let him die and the unwillingness itself was what created the situation in which he had to die.

A soldier of David saw him hanging.

The soldier was an ordinary infantryman of one of Yoav's units. He had been chasing a small group of Avshalom's men through the trees and had lost the trail when the men scattered into a ravine. He was working his way back through the undergrowth toward his unit when he came around the trunk of a large terebinth and saw, hanging in the branches above him, the figure of a man.

He looked up.

The man's face was contorted. The man's hair — long, dark, famously beautiful — was tangled in the branches above his head. The man's body was twisting slowly in the air. The mule was standing a few paces away, grazing on a patch of low scrub, evidently unconcerned that its rider was suspended ten feet above the ground.

The soldier recognized the face.

He had seen Avshalom many times in Yerushalayim. Every man in Israel knew Avshalom's face. The face hanging from the branches was unmistakable.

The soldier did not raise his sword. He did not even step closer.

He turned and ran.

He ran back through the forest until he found the central command position. He was breathless when he reached Yoav.

"My lord. I saw Avshalom hanging in a great terebinth."

Yoav stared at the man.

"You saw him hanging? Where?"

"About half a mile north of here. By the deep ravine. His hair is caught in the branches. He is alive."

"And you did not strike him down to the ground?" Yoav's voice was rising. "I would have given you ten silver pieces and a girdle. Ten silver pieces. For a single sword stroke. Why did you not do it?"

The soldier shook his head.

"My lord. Even if I were to receive a thousand silver pieces in my hand, I would not put forth my hand against the king's son. We heard the king charge you and Avishai and Itai, saying: Beware that none touch the young man Avshalom. If I had wrought any falsehood against his life — and there is no thing hidden from the king — then you yourself would have stood aloof from me. You would have let me face the king's anger alone."

Yoav was silent for a long moment.

The soldier was right, and Yoav knew he was right. The king's order had been given publicly. The whole army had heard it. Any individual soldier who killed Avshalom would be doing so against a direct royal command, and the king — when he found out — would feel obliged to punish that soldier. The soldier could not afford the risk. Even ten silver pieces and a girdle were not enough to make me kill the king's son. The soldier had behaved exactly as David had wanted him to behave. He had let Avshalom hang.

Yoav looked at the soldier. His face went very still.

He was a man who had made his peace with hard decisions a long time ago. He had killed Avner without an order. He had killed Amasa later in life with the same kind of cold efficiency. He understood, with absolute clarity, that the kingdom needed Avshalom dead, and that the soldiers under David's command were too obedient to do what needed to be done. The job was going to fall to him.

"I am not going to wait for you to be ready," he said. "I will do this myself. Take me to him."

The soldier hesitated.

"My lord — "

"Take me to him."

The soldier took him to him.

They walked perhaps a thousand paces over rough ground, past dead bodies and abandoned weapons and the smoke of small fires that the battle had started in the dry leaves. They came to the great terebinth.

Avshalom was still hanging.

He was still alive. He was conscious. He had been hanging for perhaps an hour by this point — long enough for the tendons in his neck to begin to give way, long enough for his arms to grow numb from being held above his head, long enough for his eyes to be wide and staring with the horror of a man who knows he is going to die slowly. He was looking down. He saw Yoav approaching. He recognized him. He tried to speak, but his throat was constricted and the words came out as a low moaning sound that was not quite words.

"Yoav."

It was the only word he managed.

Yoav looked up at him.

"Avshalom."

"Yoav — cut me down."

"I am going to cut you down, Avshalom. But not the way you mean."

Yoav did not waste any more time. He pulled three short javelins from his belt — the standard short throwing-spears that Israelite officers carried in close combat. He moved into position about ten paces from the hanging man. He took aim. He threw the first javelin into Avshalom's heart. The point went in deep. Avshalom convulsed in the branches, his body jerking against the tangled hair that was holding him up. He was not yet dead. Yoav threw the second javelin. It also went into the heart. Avshalom convulsed again. Yoav threw the third. It struck the same place. The body went still.

The Tanach records the action in a single verse: "And he took three darts in his hand, and thrust them through the heart of Avshalom, while he was yet alive in the midst of the terebinth."

Be-od chai be-lev ha-elah. While he was yet alive in the midst of the terebinth. The Tanach is careful to record that Avshalom was still alive when Yoav reached him. The hanging by itself would have killed him, eventually, but it was killing him slowly, and Yoav did not have the patience to wait. The killing was direct. The killing was deliberate. The killing was Yoav's personal decision, made in defiance of David's explicit order, made because Yoav had calculated that the kingdom would not survive any other outcome.

Then Yoav called for the ten young men of his personal guard.

"Surround him. Strike him with your swords until you are sure he is dead. Then cut him down."

The ten young men did exactly what Yoav had told them to do. They surrounded the body. They struck it with their swords — not because the body was not yet dead, but because Yoav wanted the killing to be a collective killing rather than his individual killing. He was making sure that no single soldier could later be blamed by David for the death. Yoav had ordered it. The ten guardsmen had carried it out. Everyone was complicit. Nobody could be singled out. It was the kind of cold operational thinking that had been Yoav's signature for forty years.

The body was cut down from the branches.

It was thrown into a great pit in the middle of the forest. A heap of stones was raised over the pit — the kind of cairn that the men of ancient Israel raised over the bodies of executed criminals or fallen enemies whose graves they did not want anyone to honor in the future. The body of Avshalom — the most beautiful man in Israel, the prince who had been planning his coronation for four years, the heir who had stolen the hearts of the people at the gate of Yerushalayim — was buried under a pile of forest stones in an unmarked grave in the middle of the wilderness.

Yoav blew the shofar.

The blast was the agreed-upon signal for the end of the battle. The men of David's three commands began to withdraw from the forest, no longer pursuing the scattered remnants of Avshalom's army. The Tanach uses a particular Hebrew word for what Yoav did with the shofar: vayachsokh. "For Yoav held back the people." The verb chasakh — to restrain, to hold back — is the same verb used in the binding of Yitzchak when the angel tells Avraham "now I know that you have not withheld your son". The Etzion commentary catches the parallel. Avraham, in the Akedah, did not withhold his son from death, and the angel praised him for it. Yoav, in the forest, did withhold the men from continuing the slaughter — and the holding-back is described with the very same verb. The two stories are linked across a thousand years of Hebrew narrative by a single shared word, and the link is deliberate.

The battle was over.

Avshalom was dead.

The rebellion that had threatened the kingdom of Israel was finished.

David did not yet know.
