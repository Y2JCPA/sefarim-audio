Chapter One

Tamar

## Volume IV — Avshalom ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

The chapter that will tell the story of Tamar opens with a sentence so quiet that the first-time reader of Tanach might miss the warning embedded in it.

"And it came to pass after this, that Avshalom the son of David had a fair sister, whose name was Tamar; and Amnon the son of David loved her."

After this. Read those two words again. After this. After what? After the sin with Batsheva. After Natan's rebuke. After the death of the first child. After the birth of Shlomo. After the four punishments that Natan had recited in the throne room — the punishments that David had received in silence and that Hashem had said would be carried out in His own time and in His own way. The chapter is opening with a temporal marker that the narrator has placed there deliberately. After this. The story you are about to read is the consequence of the story you just finished reading. The sword that Hashem promised would not depart from David's house — the sword that Natan had said would take your wives before your eyes and give them to your neighbor — that sword is now beginning to walk through the rooms of the palace.

The first room it walks through is the room of the king's daughter.

* * *

There are several Tamars in Tanach. There is the Tamar of Bereishit 38, who was the daughter-in-law of Yehuda and who, in a story almost as morally complicated as the one we are about to read, became an ancestor of the line of David. There will be one more Tamar at the end of this volume — the daughter of Avshalom himself, named for her aunt. But the Tamar of Shmuel Bet 13 is a young woman who appears in the narrative only briefly, who is the central victim of the story, and whose fate will set in motion every event in the rest of the book of Shmuel Bet.

She was the daughter of David and Ma'akhah the daughter of Talmai king of Geshur. Ma'akhah had been one of David's wives during the Chevron years — taken, according to Sanhedrin 21a, under the law of yefat to'ar, the captive woman taken in war. The technical halachic discussion in Sanhedrin 21a is complex; the upshot, according to one major reading, is that Tamar was conceived in the period when Ma'akhah was still legally a non-Jewish captive, before her conversion was completed. By the technical reading of that halacha, Tamar would not have shared the full halachic status of David's other daughters, and her relationship to Amnon — David's firstborn from a different mother — would have fallen outside the strict prohibition against sibling marriage.

This is one reading. It is the reading the Talmud preserves to make sense of why Tamar herself, in the middle of the assault we are about to describe, will tell Amnon that he could ask the king for her in marriage. The Tosafot and Rabbeinu Yeshaya read the story slightly differently — they suggest that Tamar was Avshalom's sister from his mother's side only, not biologically David's daughter, and that her relationship to Amnon was therefore not a blood relationship at all. The mefarshim differ on the technical halachic details. They do not differ on what Amnon did to her.

She was beautiful. The Tanach makes a point of mentioning her beauty in the very first verse of the chapter — yefat to'ar, fair of form. She was a virgin, betulah, and according to the customs of the royal household she wore the long-sleeved striped tunic that was the special garment of the unmarried daughters of the king. The same word — ketonet passim — is the word the Torah uses for the famous coat of Yosef in Bereishit 37. The parallel is deliberate. Just as Yaakov's coat for Yosef would be torn and bloodied at the moment of Yosef's family tragedy, Tamar's coat will be torn and ashes will be poured on her hair at the moment of her family tragedy. The narrator is telling the reader, in the small detail of the garment, that the events of Shmuel Bet 13 are going to echo the events of Bereishit 37 — that the violation of one beloved child by jealous brothers will be paid for in the violation of one beloved daughter by a perverse half-brother.

Amnon was David's firstborn son. He was the child of Achinoam the Yizre'elite, David's first wife from the wilderness years. He was therefore the heir presumptive to the throne — the eldest of David's many sons, the one who would by the natural order of succession sit on the throne of his father after David's death. He was twenty-one or twenty-two years old when this story begins. He had the physical bearing of a man who had been raised expecting to be a king. He had the moral character of a man who had been raised expecting to take what he wanted.

He was, the verse says, in love with Tamar.

The Hebrew is vaye'ehava. The verb is the same verb the Torah uses for the love of Yaakov for Rachel, the love of Yitzchak for Rivkah, the love of Yonatan for David. It is the highest verb in the language of human relationships. It is the verb of holy attachment, of soul-bond, of the love that lasts a lifetime.

Within twenty verses, the same Hebrew root will appear in its opposite form: vayisna'eha sin'ah gedolah me'od. And he hated her with a very great hatred. And the next clause adds, with terrible precision: for the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her.

Twenty verses. One half of one chapter. The Tanach is using the speed of the transformation to show us what Amnon's "love" had actually been. It had not been love at all. It had been a fixation — a physical, possessive, self-centered desire that called itself love because Amnon did not know any better word for it. The instant Amnon got what he wanted, the desire collapsed into its opposite, and the woman who had been the object of his hunger became, in the space of a single act, the object of his disgust.

That is what real love does not do. The Tanach does not need to tell us this. The Tanach simply lays the two verses side by side and lets us draw the conclusion ourselves.

* * *

Amnon could not eat.

The first thing the chapter tells us about him, after introducing his "love" for Tamar, is that he was sick over it. He was lovesick in the literal sense — he could not sleep, he could not eat, he wandered around his quarters in a kind of fevered preoccupation that was making him visibly thin. The verse says: "And it grieved Amnon, so that he became sick for his sister Tamar; for she was a virgin, and Amnon thought it impossible to do anything to her."

The phrase "it seemed impossible" is the key. Amnon was not lovesick because he could not have her. He was lovesick because he could not figure out how to have her without anyone finding out. She was a virgin princess, kept under the protection of her brothers and the women of the palace, never alone with men outside her immediate family. The royal household had layers of supervision designed exactly to prevent the situation Amnon was contemplating. He could not approach her at a banquet. He could not catch her alone in a corridor. He could not follow her to the cisterns or the women's gardens. Every avenue of approach was blocked.

He had been trying to figure it out for weeks. He had been failing. And his failure had begun to manifest itself in physical illness.

The Yad Ramah, a medieval commentator on the Talmudic discussion in Sanhedrin 21a, draws a parallel between Amnon's sickness and the sickness of David's mother during the years David was fleeing Shaul. There is, in both cases, a kind of physical and psychological breakdown brought on by an obsession that cannot be acted upon. The difference, of course, is that the obsession of David's mother was a holy one — fear for her son's safety. The obsession of Amnon was a profane one. "He was sick" — but not the sickness of a righteous man. This is a man whose body was reflecting the corruption of his soul.

His friend Yonadav noticed.

* * *

Yonadav ben Shim'a was Amnon's cousin. He was the son of Shim'a, who was one of David's brothers (see Shmuel Aleph 16:9, where he is called Shamah — variant spelling of the same name). Yonadav and Amnon had grown up together in the royal household. They were close in age. They were both descended from David. They had the kind of friendship that develops between cousins who are also peers and who are also rivals — the kind of friendship that includes affection but also envy, loyalty but also the willingness to use each other.

The Tanach introduces Yonadav with one of the most disturbing phrases used to describe a single individual in all of Tanach: vehu ish chacham me'od. "And he was a very wise man."

The phrase is not a compliment.

The Sages, in Sanhedrin 21a, read it precisely the opposite way. "He was very wise" — wise to do evil. Yonadav was clever. Yonadav was perceptive. Yonadav was the kind of man who could read the room, understand what other people wanted, and figure out the most efficient way to get it for them. But Yonadav had no moral compass. He used his intelligence not for righteousness, not even for his own benefit, but for the pure pleasure of figuring out a problem that other people had told him was impossible. He was a fixer. He was a yetzer hara in human form. The Avot d'Rabbi Natan (9:4) preserves the tradition that Yonadav is the canonical Tanach example of how a single bad friend can destroy a person who would otherwise have remained righteous, the same way Yehoshafat would later be destroyed by his association with Achav. The Avot d'Rabbi Natan says: And we also find this with Amnon, who became a friend of Yonadav, who gave him bad advice. Yonadav had not raped Tamar. Yonadav had simply told Amnon how it could be done.

He came into Amnon's chamber on a morning when Amnon looked particularly drawn.

"You are losing weight."

Amnon did not look up.

"I am tired."

"You have been tired for weeks. You are always tired now. Amnon, son of the king, why are you, the king's son, becoming thinner morning by morning? Will you not tell me what is wrong?"

Amnon looked at his cousin for a long moment. He was tempted to lie. But he had been carrying the secret for too long, and Yonadav had a way of looking at a man that made lying seem pointless. Yonadav already half-knew. The other half could be told.

"I love Tamar," Amnon said. "The sister of my brother Avshalom."

Yonadav nodded slowly.

He did not pretend to be shocked. He did not protest the inappropriateness of the love. He did not warn Amnon that this was dangerous ground. He simply nodded — the way a fixer nods when a client has finally identified the problem the fixer was hired to solve — and then he said the words that would set the entire chain of events in motion.

"Lie down on your bed," Yonadav said. "And pretend that you are sick. When your father comes to visit you — and he will come, because he loves you — say to him: Please let my sister Tamar come, and let her give me bread to eat, and let her dress the food in my sight, that I may see it and eat it from her hand."

Amnon stared at him.

"Will it work?"

"It will work. Your father will not refuse a sick man's request for the company of his sister. Your father will especially not refuse, because the request will sound innocent. Bread from her hand. What could be more innocent than a sick brother who wants his little sister to bring him a meal? The king will hear it and his eyes will fill with tears. He will send her to you within the hour."

"And then?"

Yonadav looked at his cousin.

"And then, Amnon, you must do what you came to do. I cannot do that part for you. I am your friend, not your hands."

He stood up.

"Get into bed. Stop combing your hair. Stop bathing. Look as ill as you possibly can. The king will be here by sunset."

He walked out of the chamber.

Amnon got into bed.

* * *

David came that evening, exactly as Yonadav had predicted.

He came alone, without his attendants, the way a father comes to visit a sick child. He sat on the edge of the bed. He took Amnon's hand. He looked at his son's face — the dark circles under the eyes, the unwashed hair, the genuine thinness — and his heart went out to his firstborn.

"Amnon. What is it? What has been making you ill?"

"Father. There is one thing that I think might help."

"Anything. What do you need?"

Amnon delivered the request that Yonadav had drafted for him. "Please let my sister Tamar come, and let her dress the cakes for me in my sight, so that I may eat them from her hand."

The Hebrew word for the food Amnon requested is levivot — a special kind of small cake or pastry, traditionally translated as "fritters" or "heart-shaped cakes." The root of the word is lev, heart. The mefarshim notice the wordplay. Levivot — heart cakes. Amnon was asking for cakes whose name shared the root with the word for the very part of him that he was about to violate. The Hebrew is full of these small dark jokes. I want my sister to make me heart-cakes from her own heart's hand. And then I will take her heart from her.

David did not hear the dark joke. He heard a sick son asking for a comfort food made by a beloved sister. He nodded.

"I will send her."

He left the chamber. He sent a messenger to the women's quarters of the palace.

The messenger arrived at Tamar's chamber and bowed.

"My lady. The king your father has commanded that you go to the chamber of your brother Amnon. He is ill. He has asked that you prepare levivot for him in his presence and feed him by hand."

Tamar nodded. There was nothing strange about the request. Brothers and sisters of the royal household ate together regularly. Amnon was her half-brother. She had cooked for him before. She had no reason to suspect anything.

She put on a clean apron over her ketonet passim, and she walked across the palace to Amnon's chamber.

* * *

She entered the chamber. Amnon was lying on the bed, very still, with his eyes half-closed. He looked sick. He was a good actor — the days of pretending had given his face the right pallor, and Yonadav had coached him well on how to play the part.

"Brother. I am here. Father sent me."

Amnon's voice came out weak, the way he had been practicing.

"Tamar. Thank you. I cannot eat anything. I cannot eat what the cooks bring. I asked for levivot. Your levivot. They are the only thing I think I could eat."

"I will make them. Where is your kitchen?"

"The outer room. The flour and oil are already there. I had them brought this morning."

Tamar nodded. She set up her cooking station in the outer room of Amnon's quarters. She had brought a small bag of flour and a jar of oil. She kneaded the dough. She rolled it out. She formed the small heart-shaped cakes. She fried them in the oil. The smell of the cooking spread through the chamber. Tamar worked silently, the way she had been trained to work — not making conversation, not making noise, focusing on the task in front of her.

When the cakes were ready, she took the pan and carried it into the inner room where Amnon was lying.

She set the pan on a low table beside the bed.

"Brother. Your food is ready. Do you want me to feed you?"

Amnon's voice came up from the pillow, weak and querulous.

"Send everyone away. I cannot eat in front of an audience."

Tamar turned. She saw that there were two servants standing by the door of the chamber — servants who had brought water and towels. She gestured to them.

"You may go."

"My lady — "

"He says he cannot eat in front of others. Please. Wait outside."

The two servants bowed and left. The door closed behind them. The chamber was now empty except for Amnon on the bed and Tamar standing beside it with the pan of cakes in her hand.

She turned back to Amnon.

"They are gone. Now will you eat?"

"Bring the food into the inner chamber. Bring it close. Feed me from your hand."

Tamar carried the pan of cakes into the inner chamber. She approached the bed. She bent down to set the pan on the small bedside table.

That was when Amnon grabbed her.

* * *

The Tanach records what happened next in terrible compression.

"And he took hold of her, and said to her: Come lie with me, my sister."

"And she said to him: No, my brother, do not force me, for no such thing is done in Israel. Do not commit this vile thing. And I — where shall I take my shame? And you will be like one of the vile men in Israel. Now therefore, I beseech you, speak to the king, for he will not withhold me from you."

The speech Tamar gave is one of the longest speeches by a woman in the entire book of Shmuel Bet. The Tanach preserves it almost word for word, because the Tanach wants the reader to hear her trying to talk her brother out of what he was about to do.

She made four arguments.

The first argument was the Torah argument. No, my brother, do not force me, for no such thing is done in Israel. The Hebrew phrase lo ye'aseh khen be-Yisrael — no such thing is done in Israel — is the same phrase used in Bereishit 34, when the sons of Yaakov hear about the rape of Dinah and react with horror. The phrase is a Tanach formula for this is the kind of thing that the people of Hashem do not do. Tamar was reaching for the highest possible appeal — the appeal to the moral identity of the entire people of Israel. We are not the kind of people who do this. You are not the kind of person who does this. Stop.

The second argument was the personal argument. And I — where shall I take my shame? The Hebrew is ve-ani anah olikh et cherpati. Where shall I take my shame? Tamar was telling Amnon that her life would be destroyed by what he was about to do. She would carry the shame for the rest of her life. She would never be able to marry. She would be condemned to a half-existence in the household of her brother Avshalom, where she would live as a forgotten woman in a back room, never named by visitors, never referred to by her father, never able to bear children. She was telling Amnon — in the small handful of seconds she had — that the act he was contemplating would erase her from the world of normal women. Where shall I take my shame. It is one of the most haunting lines in all of Tanach.

The third argument was the appeal to Amnon's own future. And you will be like one of the vile men in Israel. Tamar was telling her brother that this act would also destroy him. He would no longer be the firstborn of David. He would no longer be the prince. He would become, in the eyes of every Israelite who heard the story, one of the vile men. Nevalim. The same word the Tanach uses for the most contemptible characters in its narrative. Tamar was trying to wake Amnon up to what he was about to become.

The fourth argument was the practical alternative. Now therefore, I beseech you, speak to the king, for he will not withhold me from you. Tamar was saying: Wait. There is another way. Speak to my father. Ask him for me in marriage. This is the verse the Talmud in Sanhedrin 21a uses to support the yefat to'ar tradition — Tamar would not have suggested marriage if it had been impossible under the halacha. The Abarbanel reads the verse differently, suggesting that Tamar was simply playing for time, that she knew the marriage would not actually be possible but was hoping that the delay of going to the king would allow Amnon's lust to cool. Either reading is possible. What is clear is that Tamar was offering Amnon a way out of the moment without violating her — a way out that he could have taken, that any other man in Israel would have taken, that he refused to take.

Amnon listened to her four arguments.

The Tanach records his response in a single, terrible verse.

"But he would not hearken to her voice. And being stronger than she, he forced her, and lay with her."

Vayechezak mimena — "and he was stronger than she." The two words contain the entire moral horror of the moment. Tamar fought him. The Sanhedrin 21a passage adds a midrashic detail: Tamar struggled with him. She did not surrender. She used her body to resist. The struggle was real, and it was prolonged, and at the end of it Amnon was physically stronger than she was, and his physical strength was used to overcome her physical resistance, and the act was committed without her consent and against her active opposition.

Then it was over.

* * *

The next verse is one of the most terrible single verses in the Tanach.

"Then Amnon hated her with exceedingly great hatred; for the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her. And Amnon said to her: Get up. Leave."

There it is.

The hatred came on instantly. The instant the act was over, the instant the desire was satisfied, the instant the fixation collapsed into its consummated form, Amnon looked at the woman in front of him and felt only loathing. He could not stand to look at her. He could not stand to be in the same room with her. He wanted her out of his sight before he could even think clearly about what had just happened.

The Sanhedrin 21a passage offers a midrashic explanation for the suddenness of the hatred. The Talmud says that during the struggle, Tamar had wrapped a single hair around Amnon in a particular way — one of the small details that the Talmud is fond of preserving — and that the wrapping had injured Amnon in a way that, under the laws of Devarim 23:2, would have disqualified him from entering the assembly of Hashem. According to this reading, Amnon's hatred was the hatred of a man who realized, in the seconds after the act, that his own future had been destroyed along with hers. He had not just raped his sister. He had also unmade himself.

The Ralbag offers a simpler reading. The hatred, he says, was the natural reaction of a man whose lust had been entirely about acquisition rather than relationship. Once Amnon had taken what he wanted from Tamar, she became, in his eyes, the witness to what he had done. She was the only person in the world who could testify against him. She was a threat. The hatred was not really hatred of her — it was the projection of his own self-disgust outward onto the most convenient target.

Whatever the explanation, the hatred was real, and it produced one final cruelty.

Tamar, lying on the floor of the inner chamber where Amnon had thrown her, looked up at him and made one more appeal.

"No, my brother. Sending me away is a worse evil than the other one you have already done to me."

The Hebrew is striking. Ki gedolah ha-ra'ah ha-acharonah min ha-rishonah asher asita imadi. "For the second evil is greater than the first that you have done to me."

Tamar was telling Amnon that to throw her out of his chamber after the rape was actually worse than the rape itself. By the customs of the time, a man who had violated a virgin had at least the obligation to take her as his wife — to marry her, to support her, to give her a recognized place in his household. Devarim 22:28-29 prescribes exactly this remedy. By those laws, Tamar was now Amnon's wife in everything but the formal ceremony. He could not just throw her out. To throw her out was to compound the original violation with a public abandonment that would condemn her to a life of unmarriageable shame.

Amnon did not listen to this appeal either.

He called for his servant.

"Get this thing out of my house."

The Hebrew is even worse than the English. The pronoun Amnon uses is not her — otah — but zot. This. This thing. He cannot even bring himself to refer to her with the feminine pronoun anymore. She is no longer his sister, no longer a person, no longer a woman with a name. She is a thing that needs to be removed from his presence.

The servant came in. He took Tamar by the arm. He pulled her up off the floor. He led her out of the chamber and out of the door of the house, and he closed the door behind her, and he barred it from the inside.

Tamar stood alone in the street outside Amnon's house.

She was wearing the ketonet passim — the long-sleeved striped tunic of the unmarried daughters of the king. Her face was streaked with tears. Her hair was disheveled from the struggle. She was bleeding.

She did what every woman in the ancient Near East did at the moment of supreme calamity. She bent down to the dirt of the street, and she gathered ashes from a nearby cooking-fire that had burned out, and she poured the ashes onto her own head. She tore the ketonet passim — the special garment of her status as virgin princess — from her own body, ripping it open from the neck down. Then she put her hands on top of her head, in the formal posture of public mourning, and she walked out into the streets of Yerushalayim screaming.

* * *

The Sages, reading this scene, said something extraordinary about it.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha taught: Tamar established a great fence at that time. The Mishnaic Hebrew is gader gadol gadrah Tamar. Tamar had built a great fence. People said: if such a thing could happen to the daughters of kings — what about the daughters of ordinary people? If such a thing could happen to a modest woman like Tamar, who fought back, what about women who do not even know how to fight back? The teaching, preserved in Sanhedrin 21a, is one of the most important Talmudic teachings on the dynamics of sexual violence in any ancient text. Tamar's public outcry — the ashes on the head, the torn garment, the screaming in the streets — was not the loss of dignity. It was the establishment of a precedent. By making her violation public and visible, Tamar forced the entire kingdom to look at what had happened. She refused to be the silent victim that Amnon had wanted her to be. The Sages credit her, by name, with the establishment of the laws of yichud — the prohibition against unmarried men and women being alone together in private, the foundational halachic structure that the Jewish community would build around the protection of women for the next three thousand years.

The Tanach does not editorialize about Tamar's public mourning. It simply records that she did it, and that her brother Avshalom found her in the street.

* * *

Avshalom was riding through Yerushalayim that morning. He was returning from a hunt, perhaps, or from some royal errand. He saw a woman in the street with ashes on her head and a torn garment, walking with her hands on top of her head and screaming.

He recognized her.

He pulled up his horse.

"Tamar."

She stopped screaming. She looked at him. Her face was swollen and streaked with ashes and tears. She did not speak.

"Tamar — what has happened?"

Still she could not answer. She stood in the middle of the street with her hands on top of her head, and her mouth opened and closed, and no sound came out.

Avshalom understood without her answering. The torn ketonet passim, the ashes, the disheveled hair, the public posture of supreme mourning — and the fact that she was walking out of the street where Amnon's house was located. He understood within three seconds. The understanding hit him like a physical blow.

He dismounted.

He walked over to her. He put his cloak around her shoulders. He looked at her face. He spoke quietly, so that only she could hear.

"Has Amnon, your brother, been with you?"

She nodded. She still could not speak.

"My sister. Listen to me. Be quiet for now. Do not say his name in the street. Do not say anything. Come with me."

"Avshalom — "

"Quiet. Not here."

"He told his servant to — "

"Quiet, Tamar. Not here. Not in the street. We are going to my house. Everything else later. Walk with me now."

He took her arm. He walked her, slowly, toward his own house on the other side of the city. The crowd that had been watching from doorways dispersed awkwardly as they passed. Avshalom was already thinking. He was already calculating. He had recognized, in the three seconds it had taken him to understand what had happened, that the situation was now in his hands. His father David — the king, whose daughter this was — would either react or not react. If David reacted, the matter would be handled by the king's justice. If David did not react, the matter would be handled by Avshalom himself. Either way, the immediate need was to get Tamar out of the public eye and into a safe place where the bleeding could be tended and the shame could be absorbed.

He took her to his own house.

* * *

Inside, he handed her to one of the older women of his household — a widow who had served his mother in the days of Geshur.

"Take her. Wash her. Give her clean clothes. Do not ask her any questions. Do not repeat anything she says to you. If anyone from the palace comes asking about her, she is not here."

"Yes, my lord."

The woman led Tamar into an inner room. The door closed.

Avshalom stood alone in the courtyard of his own house. He was breathing hard, though he had not run. His hands were shaking, though the day was warm. He was not crying. He was past crying. He was calculating.

He went inside and sat down at a small table in his private chamber, and he did not move for a long time.

* * *

The Tanach records the next verse with characteristic compression and devastating implication.

"So Tamar remained desolate in the house of her brother Avshalom. But when King David heard of all these things, he was very angry."

Two clauses. Two halves of a single verse. They are placed next to each other for a reason.

Tamar remained desolate in the house of her brother Avshalom. The Hebrew word for desolate is shomemah. It is a word with apocalyptic resonance — used in the Tanach for ruined cities, for empty land, for the desolation of Sodom after the fire from heaven. Tamar was shomemah. She was a desolation. She was a ruined place where life used to be. She would never marry. She would never have children. She would live the rest of her days in her brother's house, in a back room, eating the food brought to her by servants, never going out into the public spaces where people might recognize her, slowly fading from the consciousness of the kingdom that had once known her as a beautiful princess. The Tanach gives us no further information about her. The next time her name appears in the narrative will be many chapters from now, when Avshalom names his own daughter after her — a poignant act of remembrance for a sister who had ceased to be a complete person.

But when King David heard of all these things, he was very angry.

That is the second half of the verse, and it is the half that explains everything that comes next.

David was angry.

The Tanach is careful to use the verb vayichar. And it burned in David. The same verb that has been used in earlier chapters for moments of David's deepest emotional reaction — for the death of Uzza in Vol II, for the parable of the poor man's lamb in Vol III. David's anger was real. He had heard what had happened to his daughter. His anger had flared up.

And then the Tanach stops.

It does not tell us what David did with the anger. It does not tell us that David summoned Amnon. It does not tell us that David held a trial. It does not tell us that David applied any punishment. It does not tell us that David sent for his daughter to comfort her or that he visited her in Avshalom's house. It tells us only that he was angry, and then it moves on.

The silence is the entire commentary.

David did nothing.

Some traditions, preserved in the Septuagint and in some early manuscripts, add a sentence here that tries to explain David's silence: but he did not grieve the spirit of Amnon his son, because he loved him, for he was his firstborn. The sentence is not in the Masoretic text. It may be a later gloss. But it captures the explanation that the mefarshim have given for David's silence: David could not bring himself to discipline his firstborn son, because the firstborn was the heir, and because the firstborn had been David's favorite from the cradle, and because — and this is the painful part — David himself had recently sinned in a way that involved the violation of a married woman, and David did not feel that he had the moral standing to punish another man for a sexual violation when he himself had committed a worse one.

The Etzion commentary on Shmuel Bet 13 reads David's silence as the first concrete consequence of the prophecy that Natan had delivered in the previous chapter. The sword shall not depart from your house. The first stroke of the sword was the violation of Tamar. And the reason David could not respond was that David had been morally disarmed by his own sin in the chapter that preceded this one. He had no platform from which to denounce sexual violence. He could not say to Amnon what you did was an outrage without hearing the voice of Natan in his own ears saying and what about you? He could not order Amnon executed for taking a woman by force without remembering his own letter to Yoav. He could not even sit in judgment over the case, because every judgment he tried to render against his son would have been a judgment he had earlier failed to render against himself.

So David was angry, and David did nothing.

And in the silence that David's nothing created, Avshalom began to plan.
