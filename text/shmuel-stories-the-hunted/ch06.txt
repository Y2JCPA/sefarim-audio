Chapter Six: The Corner of the Cloak Shaul came to Ein Gedi with three thousand men. He had been pursuing David through the wilderness of Ziph, through the crags and the wadis and the barren hills east of Chevron. Three thousand chosen soldiers against six hundred fugitives. The mathematics should have been decisive. But the wilderness was David's terrain. He had grown up in these hills. He knew the caves, the water sources, the paths that twisted through the rock like veins through a body. He had been tending sheep in this landscape since he was a child. Shaul came to the sheep pens along the way. There was a cave — a deep one, the kind that honeycomb the cliffs above the Dead Sea. Shaul entered the cave to relieve himself. The Gemara in Berachot 62b records the detail with its characteristic specificity: Shaul "covered himself like a sukkah" — he arranged his garments around him for privacy, sitting in the entrance of the cave. He did not know that David and his men were sitting in the deepest recesses of that same cave. David's men saw the silhouette against the light. A murmur ran through the cave — the sound of six hundred men holding their breath. Avishai ben Tzeruyah — David's nephew, Yoav's brother, the fiercest of his inner circle — crouched beside David in the darkness. His hand was on his sword. "Today," Avishai whispered. "Hashem has delivered your enemy into your hand. Let me pin him to the ground with one thrust of the spear. I won't need to strike twice." David looked at Avishai. He looked at the silhouette in the cave mouth. He could hear Shaul breathing — the heavy, labored breathing of a ninety-year-old man who had been marching through the wilderness with an army. "No," David said. "He's right there. One thrust." "No." "He killed the Kohanim of Nov. Eighty-five men. Women. Children. And you say no?" David's jaw tightened. "I say no." David's men saw the silhouette against the light.

"This is the day," they whispered. "Hashem said to you: 'I will deliver your enemy into your hand, and you shall do to him as seems good to you.'" The temptation was absolute. The king who had thrown spears and massacred Kohanim and hunted David through the wilderness for months — that king was now sitting alone, defenseless, in the very cave where David was hiding. One stroke. One sword-thrust. The nightmare would be over. The throne would be David's. David rose. He crept forward in the darkness. His men held their breath. The sword of Goliath was in his hand — the weapon that had killed the Pelishti champion, the weapon that had been kept in Nov among the holy vessels, the weapon that could end a dynasty with a single swing. He reached Shaul. He cut the corner of Shaul's cloak. The knaf — the corner, the fringe, the hem. Not the man. The garment. David took the symbol of kingship instead of the king himself. He could have taken Shaul's life. He took a piece of his robe. And immediately — the text says "afterward" — David's heart struck him. "Vayach lev David oto" — his heart struck him. Not his conscience. His heart. The organ that Hashem had looked at in Beit Lechem when seven brothers passed and were rejected. The heart that made David different from every other warrior in that cave. That heart convulsed with guilt at the mere act of cutting a king's garment. The Gemara in Berachot 62b records the consequence: because David treated clothing with disrespect — even the clothing of his enemy, even the clothing of the man who was trying to kill him — he was punished in his old age. Clothes could no longer warm him. The connection seems almost absurd in its precision, but the Gemara's point is not about thermodynamics. It's about reverence. David understood, in the instant after the blade passed through the fabric, that he had crossed a line. Not the line of murder — he hadn't killed anyone. The line of respect. Shaul was still meshiach Hashem — Hashem's anointed. And the anointed's garment is not for cutting. David turned to his men. They were crouched in the darkness, weapons ready, eyes gleaming with the expectation of violence. "Chalilah li me'Hashem!" David said. His voice was fierce — the fierceness of a man restraining not his enemies but his friends. "Hashem forbid that I should do this thing to my lord, Hashem's anointed — to stretch out my hand against him. For he is the anointed of Hashem."

Meshiach Hashem. He said it twice. The man who was trying to kill him was still God's anointed, and David would not raise his hand against God's choice, even when God's choice was trying to destroy him. "With these words David restrained his men and did not permit them to rise against Shaul." Shaul left the cave. He walked out into the sunlight, unknowing, unharmed. David followed him out. "My lord the king!" Shaul turned. He saw David standing at the mouth of the cave, bowing to the ground, pressing his face into the dust. "See, my father — see the corner of your robe in my hand!" David held up the piece of fabric. "I cut the corner of your robe and did not kill you. Know and see that there is no evil or rebellion in my hand. I have not sinned against you — yet you hunt my life to take it." His voice cracked. "May Hashem judge between you and me. May Hashem avenge me of you — but my hand will not be against you." He paused. Then he quoted a proverb — an ancient saying, old enough that both of them would have known it from childhood: "From the wicked comes wickedness — but my hand will not be against you." The proverb was a mirror. David was saying: if I were wicked, I would have acted wickedly. But I did not. What does that tell you about who I am? Shaul heard. And for a moment — one of the last clear moments in a mind that was drowning in darkness — he saw the truth. "Is this your voice, my son David?" He wept. The king of Israel wept at the mouth of a cave while the man he had been hunting stood before him with a scrap of fabric and a broken voice. "You are more righteous than I," Shaul said. "For you have dealt well with me, and I have dealt ill with you. You have shown today how you have dealt well with me — for Hashem delivered me into your hand, and you did not kill me." The confession was absolute. No excuses. No rationalization. And then Shaul said the thing that sealed everything:

"Now I know that you will surely be king, and that the kingdom of Israel will be established in your hand." He knew. He had always known. From the women's song to the spear in the wall to this cave — Shaul had known that David was the one Hashem had chosen, and every act of violence had been the thrashing of a man fighting an outcome he could not change. "Swear to me by Hashem," Shaul said, "that you will not cut off my descendants after me, and that you will not destroy my name from my father's house." David swore. The covenant of the cave — echoing the covenant Yonatan had made in the field. Father and son, each in their own way, asking David for the same thing: remember us. When you are king and we are gone — remember us. David swore. And Shaul went home. And David and his men went back up to the stronghold. Because David knew — with the wisdom of a man who has been hunted long enough to understand hunters — that Shaul's tears would dry, and the darkness would return, and the spears would fly again. They did. But that is the next story. * * *
