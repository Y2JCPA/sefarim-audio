Chapter Two: The Spear in the Wall The women sang, and the world changed. "Shaul has struck his thousands — and David his ten thousands!" The song filled the streets as the army returned from the Pelishti campaign. Women came out of every city with tambourines and instruments of joy, dancing and singing. It was a celebration. It should have been a celebration. Israel had won. The giant was dead. The Pelishtim had fled to Ekron. But Shaul heard the numbers. Thousands. Ten thousands. The ratio cut into him like a blade. "They have ascribed to David ten thousands," Shaul said, "and to me they have ascribed thousands. What more can he have but the kingdom?" Vayiten Shaul et David me'habayin — Shaul eyed David from that day forward. The word ayin means eye. Shaul watched David the way a predator watches prey — not openly, not yet, but with a calculating, jealous attention that would soon become violence. The Malbim explains what happened inside Shaul's mind: the ruach ra'ah — the evil spirit from Hashem — had created a distortion in Shaul's perception. David was loyal. David had fought for Israel. David had healed Shaul's own tortured spirit with his music. But the darkness in Shaul's mind took these facts and twisted them into threats. Every act of David's faithfulness became, in Shaul's broken vision, evidence of a conspiracy. The next day, Shaul was in his house, and the evil spirit came upon him. He raved — the text says vayitnabei, which usually means "he prophesied," but here carries a darker connotation. The same word for divine ecstasy now described madness. The spirit that had once lifted Shaul among the prophets now drove him to the edge of reason. David played his harp. As he always did. As he had been brought to the palace to do — soothe the king, calm the storm, fill the empty space where the spirit of Hashem had once dwelt. Shaul's hand found the spear. The text says it plainly: "And the spear was in Shaul's hand. And Shaul cast the spear, saying: I will pin David to the wall." He threw the spear at the boy who was playing music to heal him. He threw it at the shepherd from Beit Lechem, at the giant-killer, at the man his own son loved as his own soul. He threw the spear in his own palace, at his own servant, while the harp was still vibrating.

David dodged. Twice. The text says Shaul threw the spear twice, and David escaped both times. "And Shaul was afraid of David, because Hashem was with him and had departed from Shaul." This verse is the hinge of the entire narrative. The fear was not rational — David had done nothing threatening. The fear was spiritual. Shaul could feel the presence of Hashem around David like a force field, and he could feel the absence of that same presence in himself like a hole in his chest. The man with the spear was the one who was terrified. The boy with the harp was the one who had power. Shaul removed David from his presence. He made him a commander of a thousand — an apparent promotion that was actually an exile. Send him to the front lines. Let the Pelishtim do what the spear could not. "Let not my hand be upon him," Shaul calculated. "Let the hand of the Pelishtim be upon him." David went out and came in at the head of his troops. He succeeded in everything he did. The Radak notes the irony: the more Shaul tried to destroy David, the more David prospered. The campaigns that were supposed to kill him made him famous. The dangers that were supposed to eliminate him built his reputation. The exile that was supposed to remove him from the people's hearts placed him in front of the people's eyes. Hashem was with David. And Shaul knew it. And the knowing was destroying him. * * *
