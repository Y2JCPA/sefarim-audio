Chapter Three: The Arrows and the Field The break came at the new moon feast. David's seat at the king's table was empty. Yonatan had helped him hide — they had devised a plan, a code, a way to determine once and for all whether Shaul's hatred could be turned or whether David would have to flee for his life. "Tomorrow is the new moon," David had said to Yonatan, crouching in a field outside Givah. "I should be sitting with the king at the feast. Let me hide in the field for three days. If your father misses me, tell him I asked permission to go to Beit Lechem for a family sacrifice." "And if he's angry?" Yonatan asked. "Then you'll know." Yonatan looked at his friend. The ruddy face was thinner now than it had been in the valley — months of service, months of war, months of dodging spears and navigating the labyrinth of a court where the king wanted him dead and the prince wanted him alive. "If there is still iniquity in me," David said, his voice dropping, "then kill me yourself. But do not hand me over to your father." "Chalilah!" Yonatan said. "If I knew for certain that my father intended evil against you — would I not tell you?" They made their plan. A code using arrows. After the feast, Yonatan would come to the field where David was hiding. He would bring a boy — a servant — and shoot arrows. If he told the boy, "The arrows are on this side of you" — David could return safely. But if he said, "The arrows are beyond you" — David must flee, because Hashem was sending him away. They swore again. Yonatan made David swear by their covenant — not only for David's lifetime but for his descendants. "Do not cut off your chesed from my house forever," Yonatan said, "not even when Hashem cuts off every one of David's enemies from the face of the earth." Every one of David's enemies. Yonatan was including his own father in that phrase. He was asking David: when you are king, and my father is gone, and his house lies in ruins — remember my children. Remember this covenant. Remember me.

The first day of the feast, Shaul noticed the empty seat but said nothing. Perhaps David was tamei — ritually impure. These things happen. The second day, Shaul spoke.

"Why has the son of Yishai not come to the meal, neither yesterday nor today?" The son of Yishai. Not "David." Not "the musician." Not "the commander of a thousand." The son of Yishai. The contempt was in the naming — reducing David to a lineage, a nobody from Beit Lechem. Yonatan delivered the cover story. "David asked leave of me to go to Beit Lechem. His brother commanded him to be there for a family sacrifice." Shaul erupted. "You son of a perverse, rebellious woman!" The insult was aimed not at Yonatan but through him — at his mother, at his loyalty, at everything that connected the prince to the shepherd instead of to the king. "Do I not know that you have chosen the son of Yishai to your own shame and to the shame of your mother's nakedness? For as long as the son of Yishai lives on the earth, neither you nor your kingdom will be established!" There it was. The truth laid bare. Shaul was not merely jealous. He understood the political reality with perfect clarity: as long as David lived, Yonatan would never be king. The dynasty would not pass from father to son. The throne that should have been Yonatan's birthright was being given — by Hashem, by destiny, by the oil that Shmuel had poured in Beit Lechem — to someone else. "Now send and bring him to me," Shaul said, "for he is a ben mavet — a dead man." Yonatan tried one last time. "Why should he be put to death?" he asked. "What has he done?" Shaul answered with his spear. He hurled it at his own son — the same spear, the same arm, the same madness that had targeted David in the palace. The weapon that was supposed to pin David to the wall was now aimed at the prince of Israel, in the middle of a feast, in front of the entire court. Yonatan rose from the table in fierce anger. He did not eat that second day of the new moon — not because of the spear, but because of the shame. His father had shamed David. Had called him a dead man. Had tried to kill the friend whose soul was bound to his own. The next morning, Yonatan went to the field. He brought the boy. He took his bow. He shot the arrow. "Is not the arrow beyond you?" he called to the boy. "Hurry! Be quick! Don't delay!"

The arrow was beyond. The message was clear. Yonatan sent the boy back to the city. And when the boy was gone, David rose from his hiding place behind the stone. He fell on his face and bowed three times. They kissed each other. They wept together. David wept more. "Go in peace," Yonatan said. His voice was breaking. "For we have sworn, both of us, in the name of Hashem, saying: Hashem shall be between me and you, and between my descendants and your descendants, forever." David rose. He turned south, toward the wilderness. Yonatan went back to the city. They would see each other once more — in a forest, briefly, desperately — before Har Gilboa took Yonatan forever. But this was the real farewell. The field, the arrows, the tears. * * *
