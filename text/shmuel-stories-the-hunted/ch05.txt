Chapter Five: The Cave of Adullam David went down to the cave of Adullam. He was a fugitive now — not a commander, not a musician, not a prince's friend. A man on the run, hiding in a cave in the Judean lowlands, with nothing but the sword of Goliath and the weight of eighty-five deaths on his conscience. Word spread. And people came. "Everyone who was in distress gathered to him. Everyone who was in debt. Everyone who was bitter of soul. And he became their captain." The Metzudat David explains who these people were: men who had been crushed by Shaul's reign — taxed beyond endurance, dispossessed of their land, marginalized by a court that rewarded loyalty to the king above loyalty to Hashem. They were the broken. The discarded. The bitter. Four hundred of them at first, eventually six hundred. David took them. The shepherd who had tended the youngest sheep first, who had given the softest grass to the weakest animals — now gathered the weakest men and made them into something. He trained them. He organized them. He gave them purpose. They would become the gibborim — the mighty men — the most legendary fighting force in Israelite history. But first they were refugees, hiding in a cave with a fugitive king-in-waiting, eating whatever they could scavenge and sleeping on stone. David's brothers came first. Then his father's household. They came not because they believed in his kingship — they came because Shaul's madness had made Beit Lechem dangerous for anyone named Yishai. The family of the anointed was now the family of the hunted. David sent his parents to Moav for safety. "Let my father and mother come and stay with you," he asked the king of Moav, "until I know what God will do for me." The Radak notes that David chose Moav because Ruth — David's great-grandmother — had been a Moabite. There was a family connection, thin but real, and David used it to protect the people he loved most. The prophet Gad came to the cave and said: "Do not stay in the stronghold. Go to the land of Yehudah." David obeyed. He moved to the forest of Cheret — deeper into the wilderness, deeper into the territory that would one day be his kingdom, moving through his future domain as a fugitive before he would walk it as a king. It was from this cave — and the wilderness that followed — that David composed Tehillim. The Gemara in Berachot says that a lyre hung above David's bed, and at midnight the north wind

would cause its strings to vibrate, and David would rise and study Torah until dawn. In the cave, there was no lyre above his bed. There was rock. But the psalms came anyway — poured out of the same place Chanah's prayer had come from, the place where the soul has been scraped raw and the only thing left is the cry. "Save me, O God, for the waters have come up to my soul." "From the depths I call to You, Hashem." "Hashem is my shepherd — I shall not want." The shepherd boy was writing the songbook of a nation. And he was writing it in a cave. * * *
