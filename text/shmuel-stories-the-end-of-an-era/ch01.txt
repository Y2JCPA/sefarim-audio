Chapter One: The Death of a Prophet Shmuel died. The text records it in a single verse — flat, unadorned, devastating in its brevity: "And Shmuel died, and all Israel gathered and mourned him, and they buried him at his home in Ramah." All Israel. The entire nation mourned. Not a tribe. Not a city. Everyone. From Dan to Be'er Sheva, the people who had heard his voice — at Mitzpah, at Gilgal, at the palm tree circuits he had walked in his father Elkana's footsteps — gathered and wept for the last prophet of the old order. They buried him at his home. In Ramah. The town where Chanah had raised him before she gave him to Shiloh. The town where Elkana had loaded donkeys with provisions and walked a different road each year to inspire the pilgrimage. Shmuel had come home at the end, and they buried him there, in the ground where everything had started. The Radak notes that the text places Shmuel's death at the beginning of chapter 25 — immediately before the Naval and Avigail story — because his death created a vacuum. The man who could have mediated between David and the world, who could have restrained Shaul's madness with a word, who could have guided the anointed king-in-waiting through the wilderness years — that man was gone. What followed was the chaos of a world without its prophet. David felt the loss more than anyone. Shmuel had poured the oil. Shmuel had looked at seven brothers and said no, and then looked at the shepherd boy and said yes. Shmuel had seen the heart that Hashem was looking for. And now the man who had seen was gone, and David was alone in the wilderness with six hundred men and a dead prophet's blessing and no idea when the wilderness would end. He moved south. To the wilderness of Paran. To Maon. To the territory where a man named Naval was shearing his sheep. * * *
