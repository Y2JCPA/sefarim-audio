Chapter Four: Tomorrow You and Your Sons The battle came. The Pelishtim fell upon Israel on Har Gilboa, and the men of Israel fled before them. The text records the rout with terrible economy — "they fell slain" — the Hebrew vayiplu chalalim, which sounds like the thud of bodies hitting stone. The Pelishtim pursued Shaul's sons specifically. They found them. Yonatan fell. He fell fighting. Not hiding. Not fleeing. The prince who could have stayed in the camp, who could have negotiated with the Pelishtim, who could have leveraged his friendship with David to secure his own survival — that prince drew his bow and fought beside his father until the arrows found him. The Radak notes that the text lists Yonatan first among the sons who died — not because he was the eldest (he was), but because his death was the one that mattered most to the story. The son who should have been king. The friend David loved. The man who had given away a throne with open hands and a full heart. He died on the high places of Gilboa — the same high places where David's lament would later say he was "slain upon your high places." The elevation was both geographical and spiritual. Yonatan died on the heights, and the heights would never be the same. The soul that had been bound to David's soul — the prince who had climbed a cliff with a "perhaps," who had given his robe and sword and bow to a shepherd boy, who had wept in a field with his face against his friend's face, who had said "Hashem be between me and you forever" — Yonatan fell on Har Gilboa. Avinadav fell. Malchi-shua fell. The sons of the king, one after another, like trees in a forest fire. Shaul was alone. The archers found him. They wounded him severely. The text says the archers "overtook him" and he was "greatly distressed." "Draw your sword," Shaul said to his armor-bearer, "and thrust me through with it. Otherwise these uncircumcised will come and thrust me through and abuse me." His armor-bearer would not. He was too afraid.

Shaul took the sword. His own sword — the weapon of the king who had been anointed with a flask of oil at a crossroads outside a city, who had prophesied among the prophets, who had hidden among the baggage, who had saved Yavesh Gilad, who had thrown spears at the boy who played the harp, who had massacred the Kohanim of Nov, who had hunted the man Hashem loved through the wilderness for years, who had wept at the mouth of a cave and said "you are more righteous than I." Shaul fell on his sword. The armor-bearer saw that Shaul was dead, and he too fell on his sword. And so Shaul died — and his three sons, and his armor-bearer, and all his men — on that day, together. Shmuel had said: "Tomorrow, you and your sons will be with me." Tomorrow had come.

The men of Israel who were on the other side of the valley and across the Yarden saw that Israel had fled and that Shaul and his sons were dead. They abandoned their cities and fled. The Pelishtim came and occupied them. The next day, the Pelishtim came to strip the dead. They found Shaul and his three sons fallen on Har Gilboa. They cut off Shaul's head. They stripped his armor. They sent messengers throughout the land of the Pelishtim to carry the news to their idol temples and their people. They put Shaul's armor in the temple of Ashtarot. They fastened his body to the wall of Beit Shan. The echo was devastating. The Pelishtim had once captured the Aron and placed it in the temple of Dagon. Now they captured the body of the king and placed it in the temple of their goddess. The nation that had taken God's dwelling-place had now taken God's anointed. The pattern repeated, but this time there was no plague, no Dagon falling on its face, no cows walking home on the road to Beit Shemesh. This time, the disgrace stood. Until Yavesh Gilad remembered. * * *
