Chapter Two: The Wise Woman of Carmel Naval was very wealthy. Three thousand sheep, a thousand goats, and a household full of servants in Maon, with his business in Carmel. He was a descendant of Kalev — noble lineage, ignoble character. The text describes him with unusual directness: "The man was harsh and evil in his dealings. He was a Calebite." His wife was his opposite in every way. Avigail — beautiful, intelligent, and counted by the Gemara in Megillah among the seven prophetesses of Israel. The Gemara adds that she was one of the four most beautiful women in history, alongside Sarah, Rachav, and Esther. David's men had been protecting Naval's shepherds in the wilderness for months — a wall around them, the text says, by night and by day. Not a single sheep was stolen. Not a single man was harmed. The protection was real, extensive, and unpaid. Now it was sheep-shearing time — a season of abundance and feasting. David sent ten young men to Naval with a polite request. "Say to him: 'Peace to you, peace to your house, peace to all that is yours. I have heard that you have shearers. Your shepherds were with us — we did not harm them, and nothing of theirs was missing all the time they were in Carmel. Ask your young men and they will tell you. Let my men find favor in your eyes, for we have come on a festive day. Please give whatever you can to your servants and to your son David.'" The request was courteous. The tone was humble. David called himself Naval's son — a gesture of deference from a man who had been anointed king. Naval's response was a slap in the face. "Who is David? Who is the son of Yishai? There are many servants these days who break away from their masters. Shall I take my bread and my water and my meat that I have slaughtered for my shearers, and give it to men who come from I don't know where?" "Who is David?" The same contemptuous phrasing Shaul had used — "the son of Yishai." As if the name itself were an insult. As if the man who had killed Goliath and saved Israel was a runaway slave unworthy of bread. David's reaction was immediate and volcanic. "Every man strap on his sword." Four hundred men rode out with David. Two hundred stayed with the baggage. David swore an oath: "May God do thus and more to me if I leave alive by morning a single male of all who belong to Naval."

He was going to kill them all. Every man in Naval's household. Over an insult and a refusal of food.

One of Naval's servants — a young man who understood the danger better than his master — ran to Avigail. "David sent messengers from the wilderness to greet our master, and he drove them away. But those men were very good to us. We were not harmed. Nothing was missing. They were a wall around us, day and night, all the time we were with them tending sheep." He paused. His face was tight with fear. "Now know and consider what you will do. For evil has been determined against our master and against all his household. And he is such a worthless man that no one can speak to him." Ish beliyaal. The same phrase used for Eli's sons. The same phrase used for the men who mocked Shaul at his coronation. Naval was a man beyond the reach of reason. Avigail moved fast. She didn't tell her husband — the text says this explicitly, because telling him would have been useless. She loaded two hundred loaves of bread, two skins of wine, five prepared sheep, five measures of roasted grain, a hundred clusters of raisins, and two hundred cakes of figs onto donkeys. She sent the servants ahead. She mounted her own donkey and rode out to intercept David. She met him coming down the mountain — four hundred armed men, swords drawn, fury on their faces, riding toward the massacre of an entire household. Avigail dismounted. She fell on her face before David and bowed to the ground. "Upon me, my lord, let this iniquity be. Please let your maidservant speak in your ears, and hear the words of your maidservant." Then she spoke. And what she said was one of the greatest speeches in all of Tanach — a masterpiece of rhetoric, theology, and political prophecy delivered by a woman on a donkey to a man with a sword. "Let my lord pay no attention to this worthless man, Naval. For as his name is, so is he — Naval is his name, and folly is with him." She acknowledged the insult. She didn't defend her husband. She called him exactly what he was.

"And now, my lord, as Hashem lives and as your soul lives — it is Hashem who has held you back from bloodguilt and from saving yourself by your own hand." She told David: Hashem sent me. Not to protect Naval — to protect you. You are about to stain your hands with innocent blood, and when you are king — and you will be king — this stain will follow you. The Gemara in Megillah records what happened next in coded language. Avigail showed David a blood-stained cloth and asked him a question about ritual purity — a question about menstrual blood, which must be examined in daylight. David said: "Do we examine blood at night?" And Avigail answered: "Do we judge capital cases at night?" The legal argument was devastating. David was acting as judge, jury, and executioner in the middle of the night. Even a king cannot condemn a man to death without a trial by day. Avigail used halachah to stop a massacre. David tried to argue: "Naval is a rebel against the crown." Avigail answered: "Shaul is still alive. Your coin has not yet been issued. You are not yet the king." The logic was airtight. David could not claim royal authority he did not yet possess. He could not execute a man for treason against a throne he had not yet occupied. And then Avigail prophesied. "Hashem will certainly make for my lord a sure house — for my lord fights the battles of Hashem, and evil has not been found in you all your days." A sure house. A bayit ne'eman. The same phrase that would later be used of David's dynasty by the prophet Natan. Avigail was seeing David's future — the throne, the dynasty, the house that would endure — and she was warning him not to destroy it before it was built. "Let not this be a stumbling block for you," she said, "or a grief of heart to my lord — to have shed blood without cause, or for my lord to have saved himself by his own hand." The Gemara explains: "this" would not be David's stumbling block. But another incident would — the incident with Batsheva, years later. Avigail saw it coming. She was a prophetess, and she could see the shadow of a future sin falling across the present moment. "And when Hashem has done for my lord all the good He has promised — remember your maidservant." The Gemara quotes a folk saying in response to this: "While the woman talks, she holds the spindle." Even in the middle of saving David's soul, Avigail was spinning her own future. She knew Naval would not live long. She knew David was the future king. And she planted the seed.

"Only fear Hashem," she said, her voice steady, her eyes holding his. "For the life of my lord will be bound in the bundle of life with Hashem your God. And the lives of your enemies — He will sling them out, as from the hollow of a sling." As from a sling. The image was pointed — David, the slinger of stones, who had killed a giant with a shepherd's weapon. Avigail was telling him: Hashem is the one who slings. Your enemies will fall by His hand, not yours. Put down the sword. Let the sling of Heaven do its work. "And when Hashem has done for my lord all the good He has promised — and has appointed you as ruler over Israel — this will not be a stumbling block for you, or a grief of heart to my lord, to have shed blood without cause." A stumbling block. The Hebrew word — pukah — appears only here in all of Tanach. The Gemara reads it as prophecy: "this" will not be your stumbling block. But another will. Another woman, another night, another man's wife. Avigail saw the shadow of Batsheva falling across the future and warned David without naming the sin. David heard her. Every word. And the rage that had been driving him down the mountain — the fury of a hunted man insulted by a fool — drained out of him like water from a cracked vessel. "Blessed is Hashem, the God of Israel, who sent you this day to meet me," David said. "And blessed is your wisdom. And blessed are you, who have kept me this day from bloodguilt and from saving myself by my own hand." He took the provisions. He sent her home in peace. Naval died ten days later. When he heard what Avigail had done — the gifts, the provisions, the bowing before David — his heart turned to stone within him, and after ten days, Hashem struck him and he died. David married Avigail. The woman who had saved him from himself became his wife. The prophetess who had seen his dynasty before it existed joined her life to his. * * *
