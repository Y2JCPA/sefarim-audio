Chapter Three: The Witch of Endor The Pelishtim gathered their armies at Shunem. Shaul gathered all Israel and camped at Gilboa. And when Shaul saw the Pelishti camp, he was terrified. "His heart trembled greatly." This was not the Shaul of Yavesh Gilad, who had rallied a nation with cut oxen and the spirit of God burning inside him. This was not the Shaul who had fought the Pelishtim at Michmash with three thousand men. This was a hollowed-out man — the spirit of Hashem gone, the evil spirit in its place, the prophet who anointed him dead, the son he loved bound to the man he hunted, and the kingdom slipping from his fingers like sand. He inquired of Hashem. Hashem did not answer him — not through dreams, not through the Urim, not through prophets. Every door was shut. The God who had once spoken to him — who had sent His spirit rushing upon him among the prophets — was silent. The silence was not punishment in the narrow sense. It was consequence. The man who had rejected the word of Hashem was now rejected by the word of Hashem. The mechanism was symmetrical and absolute. Shaul turned to the darkness. "Find me a woman who is a baalat ov," he told his servants. "A medium. So that I may go to her and inquire through her." His servants found one. In Endor. A woman who practiced the necromancy that Shaul himself had outlawed — he had expelled all the mediums and sorcerers from the land in the early years of his reign, when the spirit of Hashem was still with him and the law of the Torah still mattered. Now he was going to one in disguise. Shaul changed his clothes. He took two men — the Gemara identifies them as Avner and Amasa — and went to the woman at night. At night. In disguise. The king of Israel, creeping through the darkness to consult the very witchcraft he had banned. "Divine for me through the ov," he said. "Bring up for me the one I shall name to you." The woman was suspicious. "You know what Shaul has done — how he has cut off the mediums and the sorcerers from the land. Why are you laying a trap for my life, to cause my death?" "As Hashem lives," Shaul swore, "no punishment shall come upon you for this thing."

He swore by Hashem. By the God who had stopped answering him. By the Name he had spent his reign dishonoring. The irony was thick enough to choke on. "Whom shall I bring up for you?" the woman asked. "Bring up Shmuel."

The woman saw Shmuel ascending and she screamed. The Vayikra Rabbah explains her scream: normally, a spirit summoned by necromancy rises feet-first, inverted, entering the world of the living upside down. But Shmuel rose head-first — upright, dignified, ascending the way a man ascends from a mikveh. This only happened when the spirit was summoned on behalf of a king. The woman saw the upright figure and understood instantly: the man in the disguise was Shaul. "Why have you deceived me?" she cried. "You are Shaul!" "Do not be afraid," Shaul said. "What do you see?" "I see a godlike being rising from the earth." "What is his form?" "An old man coming up, and he is wrapped in a robe." A me'il. The same word for the robe that Chanah had made for the boy Shmuel every year — the small coat she brought to Shiloh when she came for the festival. The prophet was recognizable even in death by the garment his mother had made him. Shaul knew it was Shmuel. He bowed his face to the ground and prostrated himself. The Radak records the dispute among the Geonim about what happened in that room. Rav Saadya Gaon and Rav Hai Gaon both said that the woman's sorcery had no real power — it was Hashem who allowed Shmuel to appear, as a hora'at sha'ah, a temporary divine ruling. Hashem wanted Shaul to receive the prophecy from Shmuel specifically, even through the channel of impurity, because no other channel remained open. The Ralbag offers a different reading: usually the baalat ov's power was false — she manipulated her clients' imaginations into believing they saw something. But this time, something real happened, and that is why she screamed. Her own power had never produced anything genuine before. Either way, the figure that stood before Shaul was Shmuel — or something that spoke with Shmuel's voice and Shmuel's authority and delivered Shmuel's judgment.

And Shmuel spoke.

"Why have you disturbed me, to bring me up?" The voice of the dead prophet — the boy from Shiloh, the voice in the night, the man who had never let a word fall to the ground — spoke from beyond the grave with the same clarity he had carried in life. "I am in great distress," Shaul said. His voice was stripped bare. No royal pretense. No commander's authority. Just a man who had lost everything and was grasping at shadows. "The Pelishtim are fighting against me. God has turned away from me and does not answer me anymore — not through prophets and not through dreams. So I called you, to tell me what to do." "Why do you ask me," Shmuel answered, "when Hashem has turned away from you and become your adversary?" Adversary. The word was a blade. The God who had once been Shaul's strength was now positioned against him. "Hashem has done for Himself as He spoke through me," Shmuel continued. "He has torn the kingdom from your hand and given it to your fellow — to David." The Radak notes that Shmuel identified David by name — something the living Shmuel had carefully avoided doing in Shaul's presence. In death, there was no longer any reason for discretion. "Because you did not listen to the voice of Hashem, and did not execute His burning anger against Amalek — therefore Hashem has done this thing to you this day." And then the final sentence. The sentence that made Shaul collapse to the ground. "Tomorrow, you and your sons will be with me." With me. In death. In Gan Eden — because the Gemara in Chagigah records that "with me" meant Shaul would be in Shmuel's compartment in the World to Come. Despite everything. Despite the sins, the failures, the spears, the murders at Nov. Shaul's place in eternity was beside the prophet who had anointed him. The Gemara's reading transforms the sentence from a death warrant into a mercy. You will die tomorrow. But you will not be lost. You will be with me. "And Hashem will also deliver the camp of Israel into the hand of the Pelishtim." Shaul fell to the ground. Full length. His strength left him. He had not eaten all day and all night, and the words of Shmuel took whatever remained.

The woman came to him. She saw the king of Israel lying face down on her floor, broken, and she did what every Jewish woman in the story had done when confronted with a man in pieces — she fed him. She slaughtered a calf. She baked matzot. She set food before Shaul and his men. He ate. He rose. He went out into the night. Toward Gilboa. Toward his sons. Toward tomorrow. * * *
