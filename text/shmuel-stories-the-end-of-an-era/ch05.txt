Chapter Five: The Fall of Gilboa The men of Yavesh Gilad heard what the Pelishtim had done to Shaul. They remembered. They remembered that twenty years earlier — or thirty, or however long the count had been since the beginning of a kingdom that now lay in ruins — a young farmer had heard their cry. They had been besieged by Nachash the Ammonite, facing the mutilation of every man's right eye, and a newly crowned king had cut oxen in pieces and rallied a nation and marched through the night to save them. Shaul's first act as king had been to save Yavesh Gilad. Now Yavesh Gilad would perform his last honor. All the valiant men rose. They walked all night — the Radak says it was approximately eight hours of marching through hostile territory, in the dark, with the Pelishtim in control of the land. They reached the wall of Beit Shan. They took down the body of Shaul and the bodies of his sons. They carried them back to Yavesh — the bodies of the king who had saved their city, borne on the shoulders of the men whose city he had saved. They burned them. The burning of bodies is unusual in Israelite practice — the Radak explains that this was necessary because the Pelishtim had mutilated the corpses so badly that a conventional burial would have been a further disgrace. The burning was an act of mercy, not disrespect. They took the bones and buried them under the tamarisk tree in Yavesh. And they fasted seven days. The Midrash expands on this scene with a detail that ties the entire sefer into a single thread. When the men of Yavesh Gilad heard about the mutilation of Shaul's body, one of the elders — an old man, a survivor of the original siege — stood up and said to the assembled warriors: "Do you remember? When Nachash came with his army and demanded our right eyes — who saved us? Who cut the oxen and rallied the nation? We owe him our eyes. We owe him our lives. Now they have fastened his body to a wall, and we sit here?" No one argued. They marched that night. Seven days. The same number of days that Shaul had been told to wait at Gilgal — the seven days he had not been able to endure, the seven days that had begun the cracking of his

kingdom. The men of Yavesh Gilad gave Shaul in death the patience he had lacked in life.

The tamarisk tree. The Gemara notes this detail — an eshel, the same word used for the tree that Avraham planted in Be'er Sheva to provide shade and hospitality to travelers. Shaul's bones rested beneath a tree of chesed, and the men who buried him there were repaying the chesed Shaul had shown them at the beginning of his reign. The circle was complete. Shaul's story — from the lost donkeys to the lost kingdom, from the anointing at the crossroads to the sword on Gilboa, from "Am I not from the smallest tribe?" to the silence of a God who would not answer — was finished. He had been chosen and anointed and crowned and celebrated. He had been warned and tested and found wanting. He had been gentle when he should have been fierce, and fierce when he should have been gentle. He had spared the king of Amalek and slaughtered the Kohanim of Nov. He had hidden among the baggage and thrown spears in the palace. He had wept at the mouth of a cave and hunted the man Hashem loved through every wilderness in the land. And at the end, on a mountain, with his sons dead around him and the archers closing in, he had done the one thing left to do — he had chosen his own death over the humiliation of capture by the uncircumcised. Shmuel had said: "Tomorrow, you and your sons will be with me." The Gemara's promise held. Shaul was with Shmuel — in the compartment reserved for the righteous, in the place where flawed men who served Hashem imperfectly but genuinely are received. Because Shaul's story was not the story of a villain. It was the story of a tragedy. A man who was not quite equal to the crown he was given. A man whose virtues became his vices and whose humility became paralysis and whose compassion became disobedience. The Midrash says that when David heard the news — when a messenger came running from the battlefield with torn clothes and earth on his head, just like the messenger who had come to Eli at Shiloh — David did not celebrate. He did not claim the throne. He did not say: at last. He tore his clothes. He wept. He fasted until evening. And then he composed a lamentation that would be sung in Yehudah for generations — the kinah that opens the second book of Shmuel, the elegy that speaks for every man who has ever lost someone complicated and beloved: "The beauty of Israel is slain upon your high places! How the mighty have fallen!"

"Shaul and Yonatan — beloved and pleasant in their lives, and in their death they were not parted. They were swifter than eagles. They were stronger than lions." "How the mighty have fallen in the midst of battle! Yonatan is slain upon your high places." And then the line that breaks open the heart of the sefer and spills everything: "I am distressed for you, my brother Yonatan. You were very pleasant to me. Your love to me was more wonderful than the love of women." The love of Yonatan. The soul that was bound. The prince who gave away his throne. The friend who wept in the field. The man who climbed a cliff with a "perhaps" and believed in David before David believed in himself. Gone. On Gilboa. With his father. With his brothers. In the compartment of the righteous, where Shmuel waited. "How the mighty have fallen, and the weapons of war perished." Sefer Shmuel Aleph was over. The kingdom was broken. The prophet was dead. The king was dead. The prince was dead. But the shepherd was alive. And the oil that Shmuel had poured on his head in Beit Lechem — from a horn, not a flask — was still burning. David would rise. But that is Shmuel Bet. * * *
