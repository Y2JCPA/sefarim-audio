Chapter Three: The Diplomat The message Yiftach sent to the king of Ammon was the last thing anyone expected from a man whose reputation was built on raids and bloodshed. It was not a threat. It was not a challenge. It was a legal brief. "What is between me and you," Yiftach's messenger said to the king, "that you have come against me to fight in my land?" The king of Ammon answered with a territorial claim as old as the Exodus: "Because Israel took away my land when they came up from Mitzrayim — from the Arnon to the Yabbok to the Yarden. Now restore them peacefully." It was a reasonable-sounding argument. The Ammonites claimed that the territory Gilad now occupied had originally been theirs, seized by the Israelites during the conquest. Give it back, and there would be peace. Yiftach could have ignored the claim and fought. He had the men. He had the mandate. But the outcast from Tov — the man everyone dismissed as a rough warrior with empty men at his back — did something that revealed a mind far more sophisticated than anyone had given him credit for. He built a three-part legal argument that would have held up in any court in the ancient world.

"Thus says Yiftach," the messenger began, standing before the Ammonite king in his audience hall. The messenger was a young man named Elyav, steady-voiced, chosen because he could remember long speeches word for word. "Israel did not take the land of Moav or the land of the children of Ammon." Elyav paused. The king's counselors shifted. This was not the response they expected. "When Israel came up from Mitzrayim, they walked through the wilderness to the Yam Suf and came to Kadesh. Israel sent messengers to the king of Edom, saying, 'Let me pass

through your land.' But the king of Edom would not listen. They also sent to the king of Moav, and he would not consent. So Israel stayed in Kadesh." The historical recitation was precise. Elyav delivered it from memory — every name, every place, every diplomatic exchange — and the Ammonite court listened with growing discomfort, because the account was accurate and accuracy is dangerous to a false claim. "Then they went through the wilderness and went around the land of Edom and the land of Moav, and came to the east side of the land of Moav, and camped on the other side of the Arnon. They did not come within the border of Moav, for the Arnon was the border of Moav." Here was the first argument: Israel had respected borders. When Edom said no, Israel went around. When Moav said no, Israel went around. The land wasn't stolen. It was avoided. "And Israel sent messengers to Sichon, king of the Emorites, king of Cheshbon, and Israel said to him, 'Let us pass through your land to our place.' But Sichon did not trust Israel to pass through his border. Sichon gathered all his people and encamped at Yahatz and fought against Israel." "And Hashem, the God of Israel, delivered Sichon and all his people into the hand of Israel, and they struck them. So Israel possessed all the land of the Emorites, the inhabitants of that land." Here was the second argument, and it was devastating: the land in question was never Ammonite territory at the time of the conquest. It belonged to Sichon, king of the Emorites. Sichon had taken it from Moav before Israel arrived. Israel took it from Sichon. The Ammonites were three transfers removed from any claim. The Malbim notes the brilliance of this argument: Yiftach was establishing what later jurisprudence would call "chain of title." The Ammonites couldn't claim land that had changed hands multiple times before Israel ever touched it. If they had a grievance, it was with Sichon's ghost, not with Israel. The king's chief counselor leaned forward. "Your master twists history," he said to Elyav. "The land was ours before Sichon took it. It was ours before there were Emorites on it. The original title belongs to Ammon." Elyav didn't blink. He had been instructed by Yiftach to anticipate exactly this objection.

"My lord Yiftach says this," Elyav replied. "Will you not possess what Chemosh your god gives you? So whatever Hashem our God has dispossessed before us — we will possess." The theological argument. The boldest of the three. Yiftach was meeting the Ammonite king on his own terms: you believe Chemosh gives you territory? Fine. We believe Hashem gives us territory. Our God won this land for us. Take it up with Him. The Radak notes that Yiftach knew perfectly well that Chemosh was the god of Moav, not Ammon. But he used the name deliberately — either because the Ammonites also venerated Chemosh, or because he was lumping the trans-Yarden nations together to make a theological point that transcended the specifics of any one deity. The point was: don't lecture us about divine right. We invented divine right. Elyav continued. Yiftach's third argument shifted from theology to prescription. "And now — are you better than Balak ben Tzippor, king of Moav? Did he ever quarrel with Israel? Did he ever fight against them? While Israel dwelt in Cheshbon and its towns, and in Aroer and its towns, and in all the cities along the Arnon, for three hundred years — why did you not recover them during that time?" Three hundred years. The prescriptive argument. If the land was yours, you had three centuries to say so. You said nothing. Your silence was consent. The claim is extinguished by the passage of time. "I have not sinned against you," Elyav concluded, his voice ringing in the Ammonite hall, "but you are doing me wrong by making war against me. Let Hashem, the Judge, judge today between the children of Israel and the children of Ammon." The Ammonite king listened. He was not moved. The text records this with devastating brevity: "But the king of the children of Ammon did not listen to the words of Yiftach." Diplomacy had failed. Not because the argument was weak — it was perhaps the strongest diplomatic argument in all of Tanach, a model of legal reasoning that commentators would study for three thousand years. It failed because the Ammonite king didn't want justice. He wanted land. Yiftach received the non-answer and understood. He had done what a responsible leader does: he had exhausted the path of peace before opening the road to war. The Ralbag

draws the toelet: a leader must always attempt diplomacy before violence, even when diplomacy seems futile, because the attempt itself is a mitzvah and because it establishes moral standing before God and man. Yiftach had his moral standing. Now he would go to war. ∗ ∗ ∗
