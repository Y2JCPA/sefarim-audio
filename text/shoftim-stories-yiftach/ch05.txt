Chapter Five: The Victory The battle was swift. But before the battle, there was the night. Yiftach's army camped on the high ground above the Ammonite positions. The men were quiet — the particular quiet of soldiers on the night before a fight, when every sound is louder than it should be and sleep is a country no one can reach. Nadav found Yiftach standing at the edge of the camp, staring south. "The men are ready," Nadav said. "How ready?" "Some of them haven't slept. Some of them are praying. A few of the younger ones are sharpening swords that are already sharp because they need something to do with their hands." Nadav paused. "They're ready." "The Ammonite camp?" "Fires are spread across the valley. Wide perimeter. They think they have time." "They don't." Yiftach turned to Nadav. "We hit them at dawn. Three columns. I take the center. You take the left flank through the wadi. Tzuri takes the right along the ridge. We converge at the garrison town. No one stops until they break." "And if they don't break?" Yiftach looked at him. "They'll break." "How do you know?" "Because Hashem told me they would." Nadav studied his commander's face. He had followed Yiftach for years — through raids and ambushes and the slow, patient work of building an army from broken men. He had never heard Yiftach speak about God with such certainty. The diplomatic message to the Ammonite king had invoked Hashem's name, but that was rhetoric. This was different. This was a man who believed, in that moment, that the God of Israel was standing beside him.

"Dawn, then," Nadav said. "Dawn."

Yiftach struck the Ammonites with a force that combined the tactical genius of his wilderness years with the divine assistance that the spirit upon him guaranteed. He led the charge himself — the outcasts of Tov at the front, the men of Gilad behind them, driving into the Ammonite lines with the focused violence of a hammer strike. The Ammonites had expected a frontal assault. They got three. Nadav's column burst from the wadi on the left while the main body hit the center, and before the Ammonite commanders could redeploy, Tzuri's men were pouring down the ridge on the right. The camp collapsed inward. Twenty cities fell. From Aroer to the approach of Minnit — a broad arc of territory recaptured, garrison after garrison broken, the Ammonite army collapsing under the weight of a campaign that moved faster than their commanders could respond to. Nadav fought beside Yiftach through the final assault. "They're running," Nadav said, breathing hard, his sword red to the wrist. The Ammonite line had broken. Men were streaming east, throwing down weapons, abandoning positions they had held for eighteen years. "Don't let them regroup," Yiftach said. "Push them to Avel Keramim. Finish it there." "How far?" "As far as it takes." The pursuit went to Avel Keramim — the Meadow of the Vineyards — and there the Ammonite army was destroyed. The text records it in a single phrase: a very great slaughter. The Ammonites were subdued before the children of Israel. Eighteen years of oppression ended in a single campaign. The fields of Gilad, the towns of Menasheh, the ford crossings of the Yarden — all of it, recovered. The tribute would stop. The garrisons would empty. The Ammonite soldiers who had walked through Israelite markets helping themselves to whatever they pleased would not walk through them again. Yiftach had won.

The men around him cheered. Nadav clasped his arm. Tzuri, bloodied and grinning, pushed through the crowd to embrace him. The soldiers shouted his name — "Yiftach! Yiftach!" — the way soldiers shout the name of a general who has just given them back their land and their pride and their future. Yiftach didn't cheer. He was thinking about the vow. He was thinking about the road home. About the doors of his house in Mitzpah. About what — about who — might come out to greet him. The army celebrated around him, and Yiftach stood in the middle of the celebration like a man standing in the middle of a fire, burning from the inside, because he had made a promise to God and God had kept His end, and now Yiftach would have to keep his. He began the march home. Every step was heavier than the last. ∗ ∗ ∗
