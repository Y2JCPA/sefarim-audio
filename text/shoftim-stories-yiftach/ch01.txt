Chapter One: The Outcast They threw him out on a Tuesday. He remembered the day because it was market day in Gilad, and the streets were full of people who watched him leave. They didn't look away. That would have been mercy. They looked. They stared. A few of the older women covered their mouths with their hands, and a merchant near the gate stopped mid-sentence and turned to watch the young man walk past with a sack over his shoulder and nothing else. Yiftach didn't look back. His half-brothers stood at the threshold of their father's house — their father's house, not his anymore, never really his — and watched him go. The eldest, a thick-necked man named Avidan, had done the talking. Avidan always did the talking. "You will not inherit in our father's house," Avidan had said that morning, his voice carrying the flat finality of a man who has rehearsed his speech. "You are the son of another woman." Another woman. Not "our father's second wife." Not "a woman from the village." Another woman. The word they used — zonah — hung in the air between them like a blade. What does zonah mean? The Radak offers two readings. The first is the plain one: a harlot, a woman who sold her body, a woman whose son carried the stain of that transaction in his blood. The second is gentler: an innkeeper, a woman who kept a public house, a woman whose profession placed her in contact with travelers and strangers and earned her a reputation she may not have deserved. Yiftach's mother was dead. She could not defend herself. The word was whatever the brothers wanted it to be, and the brothers wanted it to be the worst thing it could be, because the worst reading justified the expulsion and the expulsion protected the inheritance. This was about land. It was always about land. Gilad ben Menasheh had been a man of substance — fields, flocks, a house large enough for multiple wives and the sons they bore him. When he died, the inheritance passed

to his sons. But how many sons? The legitimate ones — born to his proper wife — said the answer was clear. They were the heirs. Yiftach was the son of another woman, a woman whose status disqualified her offspring from the family table. "I have as much right to this house as any of you," Yiftach said. He was standing in the courtyard. His brothers had surrounded him — not threatening, exactly, but the geometry of five men standing in a circle around one carries its own threat. "I am Gilad's son." "You are Gilad's mistake," Avidan said. Yiftach looked at his brother. Avidan was soft — had always been soft. Fed on the best portions, raised in comfort, never tested. Yiftach had spent his childhood on the edges of the family, tolerated but not embraced, included at meals but excluded from the conversations that mattered. He had learned to fight because no one protected him. He had learned to lead because no one led him. "You're making an enemy," Yiftach said quietly. "You understand that." "You're making a threat?" "I'm making an observation." Avidan stepped closer. "Take your sack. Take whatever is in it. Walk out through the gate. If you come back, we'll make sure the elders hear about your mother's profession in detail. Every detail. There won't be a family in Gilad willing to give you bread." Yiftach picked up his sack. He looked at his brothers one more time — five faces, all bearing some version of his father's features, all closed to him. He walked out through the gate. The market-day crowd watched him go. Nobody stopped him. Nobody offered a word. The son of the zonah was leaving, and that was simply how things were.

He went to the land of Tov. Tov was east — deep in the trans-Yarden wilderness, beyond the settled villages, in the rough hill country where the law was whatever the strongest man said it was. It was the kind of place that attracted the kind of men who had nowhere else to go. The text calls them anashim reikam — empty men. The Ralbag explains: they were empty of possessions, not necessarily empty of character. Landless. Dispossessed. Some

were criminals. Some were debtors who had lost everything. Some were men like Yiftach himself — thrown out by families who found them inconvenient. They gathered around him the way iron filings gather around a magnet. Within a year, he had fifty men. Within two years, a hundred. They raided. They fought. They protected villages on the frontier that the settled tribes ignored, and in exchange, those villages fed them and sheltered them and didn't ask questions about where the new livestock came from. Yiftach was good at war. He was better than good — he was gifted, with the tactical instinct of a man who had been calculating odds since childhood. He could read terrain the way a scribe reads a scroll. He could look at a group of men and know within minutes who would fight, who would run, and who would get someone killed. A man named Tzuri joined him in the second year. Tzuri was from Menasheh — a farmer who had lost his land to Ammonite raiders and had nothing left but a scar across his cheek and a hatred that burned like a coal. "I heard about you," Tzuri said, standing at the edge of Yiftach's camp. "They say you're the son of Gilad." "They say a lot of things about me." "They say you're the best fighter in Gilad." "That's the one thing they say that's true." Yiftach looked at him. "Can you use a sword?" Tzuri held up his scarred hands. "I can learn." "Then sit down. Eat something. Training starts at dawn." This was how Yiftach built his army — one broken man at a time. He gave them what no one else would give them: a place. A purpose. A leader who didn't care where they came from or what they'd done, only what they could do now. The brothers in Gilad heard about him. They heard about the raids and the growing band of fighters and the reputation spreading through the frontier. They said nothing. Yiftach was someone else's problem now. He would not be someone else's problem for long.

Years passed in Tov. Five years. Then eight. Then ten.

Yiftach's reputation grew the way a fire grows in dry brush — slowly at first, then with a speed that surprised even him. His men raided Ammonite supply lines. They intercepted tribute caravans heading east. They appeared out of the hills like ghosts, struck hard, and vanished before anyone could organize a pursuit. The villages on the frontier began to rely on him. Not openly — no one wanted to be publicly associated with the son of a zonah and his band of reikam. But quietly, through intermediaries, the farmers and herders of the border region sent grain and livestock and information, and in return, Yiftach's men kept the Ammonite raiders at a distance. One evening, a woman from a border village came to the camp. She was old, stooped, walking with a stick. She had traveled two days to reach Tov. "My grandson," she said. "The Ammonites took him. Three days ago. He's twelve years old." Yiftach looked at the old woman. He looked at Tzuri, who shrugged. "Where did they take him?" Yiftach asked. "The garrison at Na'aran. They're holding him for ransom. We can't pay." "How many soldiers at the garrison?" "Forty. Maybe fifty." Yiftach turned to his men. "Nadav. Take fifteen men. Leave at midnight." The old woman stared at him. "You'll do this? For a boy you don't know?" "I was a boy no one helped once," Yiftach said. "Sit down. Eat something. Your grandson will be home before the week is out." Nadav left at midnight with fifteen men. The grandson was home in four days. The garrison at Na'aran had thirty-eight soldiers. It had zero when Nadav was finished. Stories like this built Yiftach's legend. Not the legend of a hero — the legend of a man who did the things that needed doing, for reasons that were partly noble and partly personal and entirely his own. He wasn't fighting for Israel. He wasn't fighting for God. He was fighting because fighting was the only language the world had taught him, and he was fluent. But somewhere beneath the raids and the tactics and the growing army, something else was building too. A knowledge of the land. A feel for its politics and its pressure points. A deepening awareness that the Ammonite problem was not going to be solved by raids — that

sooner or later, the confrontation would come to open war, and when it did, the men of Gilad would need someone who knew how to fight. They would need him. He could wait. ∗ ∗ ∗
