Chapter Seven: Between the Pillars They made him dance. The temple of Dagon was full. The five lords of the Pelishtim sat in their places of honor, wreathed in garlands, flushed with wine and triumph. Bronze lamps burned in every alcove. The smell of roasted meat and incense filled the air. Musicians played in the corners — Pelishti instruments, drums and lyres and something that wailed like a wounded animal. Three thousand men and women packed the roof — common people who had come from every Pelishti city to celebrate the great festival of their god. The sound of their voices was a roar that never stopped, a continuous wave of celebration that crashed against the temple walls and echoed back. And they had asked for entertainment. "Call for Shimshon," the first lord said, wiping grease from his beard. "Let him make sport for us." "Yes!" The crowd picked it up. "Shimshon! Bring Shimshon!" The word in Hebrew is sachak — to play, to amuse, to make laughter. The strongest man who had ever lived, the scourge of their nation for twenty years, the Nazir of God who had torn a lion apart with his hands and killed a thousand men with a jawbone and carried the gates of their city on his back — they wanted him to dance. To stumble. To grope blindly for walls that weren't there while they laughed and threw scraps and told each other how their god Dagon had defeated the God of Israel. This was the deepest form of the degradation. Not the chains. Not the grinding. Not even the blindness. This: being brought out like a trained animal to perform for the people who had destroyed him. Every stumble was proof that Dagon was stronger than Hashem. Every laugh was a prayer to their god, paid for with the dignity of God's own Nazir. They sent a boy to lead him from the prison. A young boy — a na'ar — who took the blind man's hand and guided him through the streets of Gaza and into the temple. The boy was not cruel. He walked slowly. He warned Shimshon of steps. "There's a step here," the boy said. "Two more. Now the floor is flat."

Shimshon followed the boy's voice. His feet felt the stone change — from rough street to polished temple floor. The roar of the crowd grew louder. He could feel the vibration of three thousand people through the soles of his feet. He could smell the incense and the wine and the sweat of a packed room. "Where are we?" Shimshon asked. "In the great hall. The lords are on the platform. Everyone is watching." "And the pillars — the ones that hold up the roof. Where are they?" The boy paused. "Ahead. In the center." "Take me to them. I want to lean on them. I'm tired." The boy guided his hands to the pillars. Two pillars. The Malbim explains the architecture: the temple of Dagon was built on a design common in Pelishti cities — a large central hall whose roof was supported by two main pillars set close enough together that a man could reach them both if he stretched out his arms. The three thousand people on the roof were directly above the hall, their weight borne by these two pillars and the structure they supported. Shimshon felt the stone under his palms. Cool. Smooth. Massive. The left pillar under his left hand. The right pillar under his right. The weight of three thousand people pressing down through the stone and into the earth beneath his feet. He stood there. The crowd roared above him. They sang songs to Dagon. They drank. They celebrated the capture of their enemy and praised their god for delivering him into their hands. "Our god has given into our hands Shimshon our enemy," they sang. "The destroyer of our land, who multiplied our dead." They were right about one thing. He had multiplied their dead. He had burned their fields and slaughtered their soldiers and carried away their city gates and humiliated them for twenty years. And now he stood blind and broken between two pillars, and they believed the story was over. It was not over.

Shimshon prayed.

"Lord God, remember me. Strengthen me, please, just this once, O God. Let me be avenged of the Pelishtim for one of my two eyes." The prayer was everything. It was all of Shimshon compressed into a single breath. Remember me. As if to say: I know You left. I know the Shechinah departed. I know I told Delilah the secret and the hair was cut and the covenant was broken and I have been grinding in darkness ever since. But remember me. Not the fallen version. The version You consecrated in the womb. The version whose mother was visited by an angel. The version who tore the lion and killed the thousand and drank water from the rock You split open. Remember that man. Because that man is still here, inside this blind and broken body, and he has one thing left to give You. Strengthen me just this once. Once. He wasn't asking for the strength to come back permanently. He wasn't asking to be restored. He was asking for a single moment — one burst of the old power, one last visitation of the spirit that used to ring around him like a bell in Machaneh Dan. Just once. Just enough. For one of my two eyes. The Malbim reads this as: let me at least be repaid for the suffering of one eye. As if to say: I know I don't deserve full justice. I followed my eyes. The punishment was earned. But one eye — give me vengeance for one eye, and I will count the other as my own debt. Some read the prayer as selfish — personal vengeance, not national salvation. The Gemara records a tradition that questions whether Shimshon's final act was l'shem shamayim — for the sake of Heaven. But the same Gemara also records that Shimshon judged Israel "like their Father in Heaven." The two readings coexist because the two truths coexist. The prayer was personal and national. The vengeance was for his eyes and for his people. Shimshon was never purely one thing. That was his tragedy and his greatness. He braced his arms against the pillars. "Let me die with the Pelishtim," he said. He pushed.

The pillars moved. For a single breath, nothing happened. The stone resisted. The weight of the roof and the three thousand people and the entire temple of Dagon pressed down against the force of

one blind man's arms. For that one breath, the outcome hung in the balance — the crushing weight of Pelishti power against the last flicker of divine strength in a shattered Nazir's body. Shimshon felt the stone shift under his palms. A fraction of an inch. Then another. The pillars were tilting outward — slowly at first, then faster, the way a tree falls: the first movement almost imperceptible, and then gravity takes over, and there is no stopping it. Someone screamed. High up, on the roof, a woman who felt the floor tilt beneath her feet screamed, and the scream was swallowed by a sound so deep and vast that it wasn't a sound at all — it was the world breaking. The pillars gave. The roof beams cracked. Not one at a time — all at once, a chain reaction that moved faster than thought. The ceiling split open. Stone and timber and plaster rained down into the great hall. The five lords were still sitting on their platform when the first beam struck — they didn't even have time to stand. The three thousand on the roof dropped through the collapsing floor in a cascade of bodies and rubble, falling into the darkness below, the dust rising in a column so thick it blotted out the sun. The walls buckled. The doorways collapsed. The great stone idol of Dagon — its fish body and human face watching over the hall with dead painted eyes — toppled forward off its pedestal and shattered on the floor, its head separating from its body the way the Tanach would one day record a different Dagon falling before the Aron in the time of Shmuel. The sound lasted for what felt like an eternity. Then it stopped. And the silence that followed was the loudest sound any survivor would ever hear. Shimshon fell with them. He died with them. The man who had lived between two worlds — Israelite and Pelishti, holy and flawed, consecrated and compromised — died between two pillars, with the dust of Dagon's temple settling on his body like a shroud. "And the dead which he slew at his death were more than those he slew in his life." More than the thirty men of Ashkelon. More than the thousand at Lechi. More than all the Pelishtim he had killed in twenty years of war. The final act — the act of a blind, broken man with nothing left but a prayer and two hands — was greater than everything that came before it. The Gemara in Sotah draws the line that connects the beginning to the end: "Shimshon was named for the sun. Just as the sun illuminates from one end of the world to the other, so Shimshon protected Israel from one end of the land to the other." And just as the sun sets —

descending below the horizon in a blaze of light that is somehow more beautiful than the noon — Shimshon's final moment was his most luminous. He died in darkness, but the darkness was full of light. ∗ ∗ ∗
