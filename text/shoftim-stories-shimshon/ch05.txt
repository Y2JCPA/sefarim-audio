Chapter Five: The Jawbone The Pelishtim came for him. They didn't come to Etam. They came to Yehudah. They camped in Lechi — a flat area in the territory of Yehudah, miles from where Shimshon was hiding — and spread out across the hills like a stain. The men of Yehudah were terrified. Not of the Pelishtim — they had long ago stopped being terrified of the Pelishtim, the way a man stops being terrified of the weather. You don't fear rain. You just get wet. No, they were terrified of what the Pelishtim would do to them because of what Shimshon had done to the Pelishtim. "Why have you come up against us?" the men of Yehudah asked. "To bind Shimshon," the Pelishtim said. "To do to him as he has done to us." Three thousand men of Yehudah went down to the rock of Etam. Three thousand Israelites. Going to hand over one Israelite to the enemy. Because it was easier than fighting. Because forty years of submission had taught them that the safest course was always surrender. Because the man who was fighting their war was, to them, not a liberator but a liability. They found Shimshon in the cleft of the rock. He was sitting. Waiting. He had known they would come. "Don't you know that the Pelishtim rule over us?" they said. "What have you done to us?" The words were the most devastating indictment in the entire story. Not because they were angry. Because they were resigned. Don't you know? As if Pelishti rule were a law of nature, like gravity. As if the idea of resisting it were not brave but insane. What have you done to us? — as though Shimshon's war against the oppressor was an act of aggression against his own people. One of the men — an elder, gray-bearded, his voice carrying the weariness of four decades under Pelishti rule — stepped forward.

"We have families," he said. "Children. We have fields and flocks and houses. You burn their grain and they come looking for blood, and whose blood do you think they find? Not yours. You hide in a cave. They come to our villages. They come to our doors." "They've been coming to your doors for forty years," Shimshon said. "And we've been alive for forty years. Alive, Shimshon. Our children are alive. Can you say the same for the people you've tried to help?" The silence that followed was the silence of a truth that stung because it was partly right. The woman of Timnah — dead. Her father — dead. Every person who had been close to Shimshon's war had been consumed by it. The elder wasn't wrong. He was just small. He had shrunk his definition of life to fit inside the space the Pelishtim allowed, and he called that shrinking wisdom. Rashi reads this as the spiritual low point of the generation. Three thousand men of Yehudah — the royal tribe, the tribe that would produce David — coming to a cave to tie up the one man in all of Israel who was still willing to fight. The nation had not merely accepted the chains. It had come to love them. It would not merely tolerate the oppression. It would punish anyone who disturbed it. This was worse than what the Pelishtim did. The Pelishtim were the enemy. That was their role. But when your own people come to bind you — when the chains are fastened not by foreign hands but by the hands of your brothers — that is a darkness no amount of strength can fight. Shimshon looked at his brothers from Yehudah. Three thousand of them. Armed. Afraid. Asking him to please stop making trouble. "Swear to me," he said, "that you won't kill me yourselves." "We will not," they said. "We will only bind you and give you to them." Even this was negotiated. They wouldn't kill their own brother. They would just hand him to the people who would. They bound him with two new ropes and brought him up from the rock.

When he came to Lechi, the Pelishtim saw him and roared. The sound of it — the collective shout of an army that has been hunting a man and has finally cornered him — rolled across the open ground like thunder.

And then the spirit of Hashem came upon him. Vatitzlach. The same word as the lion. The same rushing, crashing power. The ropes on his arms became like flax burned by fire — they didn't break so much as dissolve, melting away from his skin as though they had never been there. The bindings fell from his hands. He looked at the ground. A jawbone lay in the dirt — the jawbone of a donkey, fresh, still moist, not yet dried and brittle. He picked it up. A jawbone. Not a sword. Not a spear. The most worthless, contemptible thing lying on the ground — the refuse of a dead donkey, an object so unclean that no Israelite would normally touch it. The Malbim notes the divine irony: the weapon that would break the Pelishti army was the jaw of an ass. The most powerful military force between Mitzrayim and Mesopotamia would fall to a piece of garbage in the hands of a consecrated man. He swung. The first Pelishti soldier lunged at him with a spear. Shimshon sidestepped and brought the jawbone down on the man's helmet. The helmet crumpled. The soldier dropped. A second man came from the right. A third from behind. They were trained warriors — armored, disciplined, fighting in coordinated pairs the way the Pelishti military taught. Against any normal opponent, they would have overwhelmed him in seconds. Against Shimshon, they were straw. He moved through them the way water moves through a channel — not with the careful precision of a trained warrior but with the overwhelming, unstoppable force of something that cannot be resisted. The jawbone rose and fell and rose and fell. Men who came close enough to strike found themselves on the ground before their arms completed the swing. Men who tried to form a line found the line broken before it set. Men who tried to run found that the blind panic of their retreat tangled them with the men behind them, and by the time they sorted themselves out, the man with the jawbone was already there. The ground was littered with bodies. The living stumbled over the dead. The cries of the wounded filled the air — and above it all, the sound of the jawbone striking, again and again and again, rhythmic and relentless, like a hammer on an anvil. A thousand men. "With the jawbone of a donkey, a heap, two heaps. With the jawbone of a donkey I have struck a thousand men."

He sang it — composed it on the spot, in the aftermath of slaughter, standing among the dead with the jawbone still in his hand and the blood running down his arm. It was raw and brutal and triumphant, a war song from a man who had just done something impossible and knew it, and the melody — if there was one — was lost the moment the last word left his lips. He threw the jawbone down. He named the place Ramat Lechi — the Lifting of the Jawbone. A young soldier from the men of Yehudah — one of the three thousand who had come to bind him — had stayed to watch from a distance. He had hidden behind a ridge when the ropes melted and the killing began, and he had watched all of it, every swing of the jawbone, every body falling, the impossible one-man destruction of a Pelishti regiment. He came out from behind the ridge now, stepping carefully over the dead, his face the color of chalk. "How?" he whispered. "How is this possible?" Shimshon looked at him. The jawbone was at his feet. His arm was covered in blood to the shoulder. His breath was coming in ragged gasps. "You came to tie me up," Shimshon said. "Three thousand of you. Armed. To hand me to the enemy." The young man said nothing. "And now you want to know how this is possible." Shimshon laughed — a raw, broken sound. "It's possible because Hashem hasn't given up on Israel, even though Israel has given up on itself." The young man looked at the battlefield. He looked at the jawbone. He looked at the man who had done all of this alone, because no one in Israel would stand with him. "I'm sorry," he said. "Don't be sorry. Be different." And then the strength left him. Not the supernatural strength. That would stay as long as the hair stayed. But the human strength — the water in his body, the energy in his muscles, the simple physical capacity to remain standing after killing a thousand men in the heat of the day with nothing but a bone in his hand.

He was dying of thirst. "You have given this great deliverance by the hand of Your servant," he prayed. "And now I shall die of thirst and fall into the hands of the uncircumcised?" It was his first prayer in the text. His first direct address to God. And it was a complaint — a raw, desperate, almost accusatory plea from a man who had just won a miraculous victory and was about to die because there was no water. Hashem answered. He split the hollow place — the maktesh, a depression in the rock — and water came out. Clear, cold water, bubbling up from stone that had been dry since creation, filling the hollow place and spilling over the edge. Shimshon fell to his knees. He cupped the water in his hands and drank. He drank until his hands were shaking too hard to hold the water, and then he put his face into the pool and drank more. The young soldier from Yehudah was still there, watching. "Is that — did you just — " "Hashem did it," Shimshon said, water running down his chin. "I asked. He answered. That's how it works." He looked at the young man. "That's how it's always worked. Your people just forgot." Shimshon named the spring En HaKoreh — the Spring of the Caller — because he had called and been answered. Rashi connects this to Moshe at the rock. The same God who brought water from stone in the wilderness brought water from stone at Lechi. The miracle was not diminished by three hundred years. The God of Moshe was the God of Shimshon. The water tasted the same. He judged Israel twenty years. The text places this statement here — in the middle of the story, not at the end. The Radak explains: this means he judged Israel for twenty years during the period of Pelishti domination. The years of judgment were not separate from the years of conflict. Shimshon was simultaneously fighting the Pelishtim and governing Israel. Judge and warrior. Leader and loner. A man whose twenty years of service happened inside the forty years of oppression, which means the oppression continued even while he was judging, which means he never fully won.

The twenty years were real. The deliverance was partial. And the fall was still coming. ∗ ∗ ∗
