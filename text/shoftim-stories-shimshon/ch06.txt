Chapter Six: Delilah It began — as it always began with Shimshon — with a woman. Before Delilah, there was Gaza. Shimshon went to Gaza — deep into Pelishti territory, the southernmost of their five cities, a walled stronghold on the coastal road to Mitzrayim. He went openly, without disguise, without an army, without any of the caution that a man hunted by the five lords of the Pelishtim should have exercised. He went because he chose to. Because the spirit that moved him was not a spirit of caution. Because the man named Sun did not skulk in shadows. The Pelishtim learned he was there. They surrounded the city. They posted guards at the gates. "Wait until the morning light," they told each other, "and then we'll kill him." Shimshon lay down until midnight. Then he rose. He walked to the city gates — the massive, iron-reinforced double gates that kept entire armies out — and he took hold of the doors. Both doors. And the two posts. He pulled them from the ground, bar and all, and put them on his shoulders and carried them to the top of the hill that faces Chevron. Carried the gates of Gaza. On his shoulders. Up a hill. Forty miles from the coast. The image defied comprehension. It was not a battle or a strategy or a provocation. It was a statement — a declaration written in iron and wood and carried on the back of one man: your walls cannot hold me. Your gates cannot contain me. Your entire city, with its soldiers and its locks and its carefully laid ambush, is a joke to the God of Israel. The Pelishtim woke in the morning to find their city open. Gateless. Naked to the world. The most fortified city on the coast, stripped of its most fundamental defense by a single man while they slept. They were not amused. And then came Delilah. Her name was Delilah, and she lived in Nachal Sorek, the Valley of the Vine, a lush lowland between the Israelite hills and the Pelishti plain. The text doesn't say she was a

Pelishtit. Rashi is careful about this — she may have been an Israelite living in contested territory, or a woman of mixed heritage, or something else entirely. What the text says is that Shimshon loved her. Not "saw" — the word used for the woman of Timnah. Loved. The word carried a weight that the earlier word did not. This was not infatuation. This was the real thing, or felt like it, which in matters of the heart amounts to the same danger. The lords of the Pelishtim — all five of them, the rulers of Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, Ekron, and Gat — came to Delilah. "Entice him," they said. "Find out where his great strength lies, and by what means we can overpower him and bind him and humble him. Each of us will give you eleven hundred pieces of silver." Eleven hundred pieces from each lord. Fifty-five hundred pieces of silver total. A fortune. Enough to live in luxury for the rest of her life. Enough to buy a house, land, servants, security. Enough to make a woman who lived in the valley between two peoples — belonging fully to neither — into someone who would never have to depend on anyone's protection again. Whatever Delilah's reasons, the money was real, and the men who offered it were not the kind of men who accepted failure.

The first time, she asked him directly. They were alone in her house, in the Valley of the Vine, the lamplight casting long shadows on the walls. She leaned against him. Her voice was soft. "Tell me, please, where does your great strength lie? And how could you be bound and humbled?" Shimshon looked at her. He ran his hand through his hair — the hair that had never been cut, the hair that held everything. He smiled. He had been asked this question before — not in these exact words, but the same question, wearing different clothes. The woman of Timnah had used tears. Delilah used directness. Different technique. Same goal. He didn't see it. Or he saw it and didn't care. Or he saw it and thought he was clever enough to play the game and win. All three of these were true, and none of them excused what happened next.

"If they bind me with seven fresh bowstrings that have not been dried," he said, "I'll become weak like any other man." A lie. An obvious lie, delivered with the casual confidence of a man who believed he was in control. Delilah waited until he slept. She bound him with the bowstrings — she must have had them ready, which meant the lords had prepared them in advance, which meant this was not a spontaneous question but a planned extraction. Men were hiding in the inner room of the house — Pelishti soldiers, waiting in the shadows, barely breathing. "Shimshon! The Pelishtim are upon you!" He woke. He snapped the bowstrings like thread in a flame. The soldiers in the inner room didn't move. They pressed themselves against the walls and prayed to Dagon that the man who had just broken seven cords like cotton wouldn't come looking for them. The strength was still there. "You lied to me," Delilah said the next morning. Her voice carried a wound that may or may not have been real. "You mocked me. Now tell me truly — how could you be bound?" "New ropes," he said. "Ones that have never been used. Bind me with those and I'll be weak." Another lie. Another binding while he slept. Another ambush. Another morning waking to the sound of her voice and the scramble of soldiers in the inner room. "Shimshon! The Pelishtim are upon you!" He tore the ropes from his arms like thread. Delilah's frustration was building. Or her fear — the lords had paid in advance, and they expected results. A woman who takes eleven hundred pieces of silver from each of five warlords and delivers nothing is not a woman with a bright future. The third time, she tried something different. She wove his hair — his actual hair, the seven locks of the Nazir — into the loom while he slept. Closer to the truth now. The hair was involved, though the method was wrong. When she woke him with the familiar cry, he pulled the loom from the wall and walked away with the pin still tangled in his hair, trailing thread behind him like a man who had walked through a spider's web. Three lies. Three ambushes. Three escapes. And Delilah didn't stop.

This is the part of the story that troubles every reader. Three times, Shimshon watched Delilah try to hand him to the Pelishtim. Three times, he woke to soldiers in the room and the sound of her voice crying "The Pelishtim are upon you!" Three times, the evidence that she was betraying him was as clear as the sun he was named for. And he stayed. The Radak addresses this with painful honesty: Shimshon's desire overrode his judgment. The same pattern that began in Timnah — "she is right in my eyes" — had deepened into something that reason could not penetrate. He knew what she was doing. On some level, he must have known. But the knowing couldn't compete with the wanting, and the wanting won. The Gemara's verdict rings over the entire scene: "He went after his eyes." The eyes that saw the woman of Timnah. The eyes that saw Delilah in the Valley of the Vine. The eyes that would soon see nothing at all. "How can you say 'I love you,'" Delilah said, "when your heart is not with me? Three times you have mocked me and have not told me where your great strength lies." Day after day. The same words. The same pressure. The same tears — were they real tears? Calculated tears? Did it matter? The effect was the same. She pressed him. She compressed him. She squeezed. The Hebrew is identical to the word used for the wife of Timnah: vatetzikeihu — she narrowed him, she made his world so small that the only way out was to give her what she wanted. "His soul was vexed unto death." Vatiktzar nafsho lamut. His soul was shortened to the point of death. Not his body. His soul. Something inside him — the thing that resisted, the thing that held the secret, the thing that kept the covenant — was being worn down to nothing. Day after day after day, the same question, the same pressure, the same tears, until the wall between what he knew he must not say and what he could no longer keep silent became so thin that it was no wall at all. He told her everything. "A razor has never come upon my head, for I am a nazir of God from my mother's womb. If I am shaved, my strength will leave me, and I shall become weak and be like any other man."

Everything. The hair. The vow. The womb. The God. He laid it all before her the way a man lays down a weapon — not because he wants to surrender but because he is too exhausted to hold it anymore. Delilah knew. She could hear it — the difference between this answer and the three that came before. The Malbim says she recognized the truth because "he told her all his heart." The other answers had been clever. This one was naked. She sent for the lords. "Come up this time, for he has told me all his heart." They came. They brought the silver. Shimshon slept on her lap. She called a man. He shaved seven locks from his head. "She began to humble him, and his strength went from him." "Shimshon! The Pelishtim are upon you!" He awoke. He shook himself — the same way he had shaken himself every time before, the instinctive motion of a man who knows his strength and expects it to be there. "And he did not know that Hashem had departed from him." This is the most devastating sentence in the story. Perhaps in all of Shoftim. Perhaps in all of Tanach. He did not know. The strength was gone. The Shechinah was gone. The covenant that had been sealed in his mother's womb and carried in his uncut hair for his entire life — gone. And he didn't feel it leave. He reached for the power and found empty air, the way a man reaches for a wall in the dark and finds that the wall has moved, and in the space between the reaching and the finding, everything changes. The Pelishtim seized him. They gouged out his eyes. Measure for measure. He had followed his eyes to Timnah, to Gaza, to the Valley of Sorek. His eyes had led him to every woman, every mistake, every compromise. And now the eyes were gone — taken by the same hands he had been fighting for twenty years, in the house of the woman he had loved more than his vow. They brought him down to Gaza. They bound him with bronze chains — nechushtayim, a dual form that suggests chains on both hands, or both hands and feet, or a

double weight of metal. They set him to grinding at the mill, walking in endless circles in the prison house, blind and shorn and broken, pushing the stone that ground the grain. The Gemara in Sotah adds a detail the text only hints at: "Grinding — they brought women before him." Even in the prison, even in the darkness, the Pelishtim used women as instruments of degradation. The man who had been undone by desire was tormented by desire even in his chains. The measure-for-measure was relentless. It pursued him into the dark. The prison was underground — or it felt underground. Without eyes, there was no way to know if it was day or night, summer or winter, morning or evening. The world had contracted to the radius of a chain and the circumference of a millstone. Step. Push. Step. Push. The stone ground the grain. The grain became flour. The flour became bread for Pelishti tables. The Nazir of God was feeding his enemies from a treadmill. He had time to think. That was the cruelest part of the punishment — not the labor, not the chains, not even the blindness, but the time. Endless, unstructured, formless time in which a man with nothing to do but push a stone could replay every decision that had brought him here. The woman of Timnah. The riddle. The foxes. Delilah's voice saying "how can you say you love me?" The moment he told her everything. The moment he woke and reached for the strength and found it gone. He thought about his mother. The woman the angel had visited twice. The woman who had understood everything the first time. The woman who had kept the secret of the hair — the secret he had given away for the price of a night's peace from a woman's tears. He thought about the angel ascending in the flame. About his father's terror and his mother's calm logic: "If Hashem had wanted to kill us, He would not have shown us all these things." If Hashem had wanted him dead, he would be dead. He was not dead. He was grinding in the dark. And his hair was growing. He could feel it. Not the individual hairs — not at first — but the weight of it. The slow accumulation. Each day, a fraction of a fraction heavier. Each day, the stubble on his scalp a little longer, a little thicker. It was the most patient thing in the world, the growing of hair. It couldn't be rushed. It couldn't be willed. It simply happened, the way dawn happens — invisibly, incrementally, and then all at once. And with the hair, something else returned. Not the strength — not yet. Something quieter. The memory of what the strength was for. In the darkness, stripped of everything —

sight, freedom, dignity, the hair, the women, the wars — Shimshon was left alone with the one thing that had always been the source and that he had always taken for granted: the God who had consecrated him before birth. The teshuvah happened the way the hair grew. Slowly. Without announcement. In the dark. No one saw it. No one knew it was happening. Not the guards who brought his food. Not the Pelishtim who came to watch him grind and laugh at the fallen giant. Not even Shimshon himself could have pointed to the moment when the turning began. But it began. The way seeds begin in the ground — invisible, underground, in silence. The man named Sun lived in permanent darkness. But the text adds one more line — a line that holds the entire ending of the story in its palm, the way a seed holds a tree. "And the hair of his head began to grow after it was shaved." Began to grow. The same word — vatachal — that described the spirit first stirring in Machaneh Dan. The beginning. The stirring. The first movement of something that had been still. The hair was growing. And with it, silently, invisibly, in the grinding darkness of a Pelishti prison, something else was growing too. ∗ ∗ ∗
