Chapter Eight: Legacy His brothers came to bury him. They came down from the hill country of Dan — from Tzorah and Eshtaol and the villages between them — and they traveled to Gaza, into the heart of Pelishti territory, to the ruins of the temple where Shimshon lay under the rubble. They came because he was their brother, and because even after everything — the foreign women, the solitary wars, the stubborn refusal to be anything other than what he was — he belonged to them. They carried him home. The road from Gaza to Tzorah was long, and they walked it slowly, bearing their brother's body between them the way pallbearers have always walked — in silence, with the weight distributed evenly, and the dust rising around their feet, and the sun — Shimshon's sun — tracking across the sky above them. They buried him between Tzorah and Eshtaol, in the burial place of Manoach his father. Between the two towns where the spirit of Hashem had first begun to stir. Full circle. The bell that had rung around a boy in Machaneh Dan was silent now, and the boy was in the ground, and the ground was the same ground where his mother had stood when the angel appeared to her in the field. One of the brothers — the text doesn't name them, doesn't say how many there were — stood over the grave in the silence that follows a burial, when the last stone has been placed and the prayers have been said and there is nothing left to do but stand there. "He was alone," the brother said. "His whole life. He fought alone." An older man — perhaps a cousin, perhaps a neighbor from Tzorah who had known Shimshon as a boy — answered quietly. "He was a nazir. Set apart. That's what the word means." "Set apart." The brother looked at the hills around them — the hills of Dan, the tiny tribal territory that had never been large enough or strong enough to hold its own. "Set apart for what?"

"For us." The brother was quiet for a long time. Then he turned and walked back toward the village, and the others followed, and the grave was left alone between the two towns, and the wind moved through the dry grass the way wind moves through all the empty spaces left by the dead. "And he judged Israel twenty years." The text says it again. The second time. The Radak explains: the first mention, at the end of chapter 15, marked the twenty years of judgment during the Pelishti oppression. This second mention, after his death, teaches that even the years after his capture — the years of blindness and grinding, the years when he could no longer fight or judge — were still counted. The service was not erased by the fall. The twenty years stood.

What do you do with a man like Shimshon? The commentators struggled with this question for two thousand years, and they arrived at answers that contradicted each other and were all true at once. The Gemara in Sotah calls him the man who "judged Israel like their Father in Heaven" — a comparison so exalted that it borders on blasphemy. Like God Himself. What did this mean? That Shimshon, like God, needed no army and no advisors. He judged alone. He fought alone. He operated by his own authority, answerable to no one, the way God operates in the world — without consensus, without committee, without the approval of the nations. "Dan shall judge his people as one." As one man. As One. But the same page of Gemara also says: "He went after his eyes, therefore the Pelishtim gouged out his eyes. His legs carried him to Gaza, therefore his legs were bound in chains." Measure for measure. Every failure tracked and repaid with devastating precision. The God who judges the world judged His own champion by the same standard, and the standard was exact. How could both be true? Because Shimshon was not a simple story. He was not a moral fable where the good man triumphs and the bad man falls. He was something harder: a man of extraordinary sanctity and extraordinary weakness, bound together in the same body, powered by the same passions. The strength that tore the lion was the desire that loved Delilah. The spirit that rang like a bell was the same spirit that drove him to Timnah. You could not separate the fire from

the fuel. The thing that made him great was the thing that destroyed him. This is why the Gemara places Shimshon in remarkable company. When Rosh Hashanah lists the leaders who judged Israel, Shimshon's name appears alongside Moshe, Aharon, and Shmuel. Not because he reached their level — he didn't. But because the principle was the same: God sends each generation the leader it needs, and the leader He sends carries divine authority regardless of personal imperfection. The generation got the judge it deserved. And the judge got the burden he could carry — barely, imperfectly, with scars — but he carried it. The Ralbag draws the lesson that threads through all of Sefer Shoftim: the righteous must guard themselves against the very forces that drive their greatness. The passion that makes a man capable of extraordinary things is the same passion that, unchecked, will undo him. Shimshon's story is not a warning against strength. It is a warning against the illusion that strength alone is enough. Ehud needed cunning. Devorah needed wisdom. Gideon needed faith. Shimshon needed restraint — the one thing he never had, the one muscle that was always weak, the one battle he always lost. He could tear a lion apart with his bare hands but couldn't hold his tongue in the presence of a woman's tears. He could carry the gates of Gaza on his shoulders but couldn't carry the weight of his own desire without buckling. And yet — the ending redeems. Between the pillars, blind and broken, with his hair grown back and his hands on the stone, Shimshon returned. Not to the man he had been. To something truer. The prayer — "remember me, strengthen me just this once" — was the prayer of a man who had lost everything and found, at the very bottom, the one thing that could not be taken: the God who had consecrated him before birth. Teshuvah. Return. The word doesn't mean "repentance" the way English speakers think of repentance — groveling, guilt, self-punishment. It means turning around. Facing the direction you were always supposed to face. Shimshon, in the darkness, turned. The hair that grew in the prison was the physical sign of an invisible process: the covenant, broken and mended, broken and mended, until the final mending was strong enough to bring down a temple. "Blessed above women shall Yael be," Devorah had sung, two generations earlier. For Shimshon, there was no such song. No celebration. No feast on a hillside above the Kishon. Just brothers carrying a body home in the dust, and a grave between two towns, and a line in the text that said he judged Israel twenty years, and the silence that follows the death of a

man who was everything and not enough and, in the final moment, exactly enough. The land did not rest forty years. The Pelishtim would continue. Shmuel would come, and Shaul, and David, and the long arc of the struggle would bend on toward its resolution. Shimshon had begun the deliverance — just as the angel had said. He had begun it. Others would finish it. But in the temple of Dagon, in the darkness, between two pillars, with the weight of three thousand enemies pressing down from above, a blind man named Sun had pushed with both hands and brought the house down. And the dead which he slew at his death were more than those he slew in his life. That was the last thing the text said about Shimshon. It was enough. ∗ ∗ ∗
