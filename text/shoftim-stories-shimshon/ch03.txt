Chapter Three: The Lion and the Riddle They were on the road to Timnah when the lion came. Shimshon had separated from his parents — walked ahead or fallen behind, the text doesn't specify, only that he was alone when it happened. The vineyards of Timnah spread out on both sides of the road, the grapevines heavy with late-summer fruit, the air sweet with the smell of nearly ripe grapes that the Nazir could not touch. The young lion came roaring toward him. Not a cub. A kefir — a young lion in its full strength, the most dangerous age, when the power is at its peak and the caution hasn't yet developed. It came from among the vines, and it came fast, and Shimshon was alone, with no weapon, on a road between vineyards, and the lion's mouth was open and its roar was the sound of death arriving. "And the spirit of Hashem came mightily upon him." Vatitzlach — the word means something between "rushed" and "succeeded." The spirit didn't settle gently. It crashed into him like a wave. And Shimshon — with no sword, no spear, no tool of any kind — took the lion and tore it apart with his hands. "As one tears a young goat," the text says. The comparison is deliberate. A young goat. Something a strong farmer might do as a matter of routine. The text wants you to understand that this act — this impossible, superhuman act — felt, to Shimshon, like nothing. The spirit made the extraordinary feel ordinary. That was its gift and its danger. He told no one. Not his mother. Not his father. Not the woman he was going to Timnah to see. The strength was a secret, the way the Nazir's hair was a secret. Some things are not meant to be shared with the world, because the world will find a way to use them against you.

Time passed. The negotiations were completed. The wedding was arranged.

On his way back to Timnah for the feast, Shimshon turned off the road to see the lion's carcass. Days or weeks had passed — enough time for the flesh to dry and the bones to bleach in the sun. The Radak explains: enough time had passed that the carcass was no longer ritually impure in the way a fresh corpse would be. It was a skeleton now, an empty shell. But it wasn't empty. A swarm of bees had colonized the lion's ribcage. Honey filled the hollow spaces between the bones. Golden, fragrant, alive. Shimshon reached in and scooped the honey out with his hands. He ate as he walked. He gave some to his parents when he caught up with them, and they ate too, and he did not tell them where it came from. The image lodged in his mind. Something sweet, born from something terrible. Life emerging from death. Honey in the carcass of a predator. It was the kind of thing a man carries with him, turning it over in his thoughts, feeling the edges of a meaning he can't quite articulate. By the time they reached Timnah, it had become a riddle.

The wedding feast lasted seven days. This was Pelishti custom — a mishteh, a drinking feast, with wine and food and entertainment, and Shimshon sat at the center of it, a Nazir at a drinking party, an Israelite among Pelishtim, a man consecrated to God at a table with people who worshipped Dagon. The hall was loud with music and laughter. Shimshon's wife sat beside him, her face decorated in the Pelishti style, her eyes darting between her new husband and the men her people had sent to watch him. She was afraid of Shimshon — not physically, though his size was intimidating — but the way a person is afraid of something they don't understand. He had come from the Israelite hills, and he had torn a lion apart with his bare hands, and he sat among her people with a calm that was either confidence or madness, and she couldn't tell which. The Pelishtim provided thirty companions — merei'im, the text calls them, though Rashi notes the word more accurately means "guards." They were not friends. They were watchers, assigned by the Pelishtim to keep an eye on this dangerously strong Israelite who had walked into their territory and claimed one of their women. Thirty young men,

well-built, armed, who smiled at Shimshon with the careful smiles of men who have been told to be friendly but who carry knives under their robes. Shimshon looked at his thirty companions. He smiled back. But his smile was different from theirs. His carried something else in it — the flicker of a man who knows something no one else in the room knows. "Let me put a riddle to you," he said. "If you can solve it within the seven days of the feast, I'll give you thirty linen garments and thirty changes of clothing. But if you can't solve it, you give the same to me." "Tell us your riddle," they said. "Let's hear it." "Out of the eater came something to eat. Out of the strong came something sweet." They stared at him. They looked at each other. They had no idea. Three days passed. Then four. The companions couldn't solve it. By the fourth day they were furious, and their fury turned — as fury always does when men feel humiliated — toward the nearest available target. They went to Shimshon's wife. "Entice your husband," they said. "Get him to tell you the riddle. If you don't — we'll burn you and your father's house with fire. Did you invite us here to impoverish us?" The threat was real. These were not men who made idle threats. And the wife of Shimshon — this unnamed woman, caught between a husband she barely knew and a community she had lived in her whole life — did the only thing she could see to do. She wept. "You hate me," she said to Shimshon. "You don't love me. You've put a riddle to my people and you haven't told me the answer." "I haven't even told my father and mother," Shimshon said. "Why would I tell you?" But she didn't stop. She wept before him for the remaining days of the feast. Day after day. The same tears. The same accusation. "You don't love me." The words were a tool — precise, effective, wielded with the desperation of a woman who believed her life depended on extracting this information. On the seventh day, he told her.

The text says: "Because she pressed him." The Hebrew — hetzikatu — means she made things narrow for him, she compressed him, she squeezed until there was no room left to breathe. It was the first time a woman's tears broke Shimshon's resolve. It would not be the last. She told her people. And before the sun set on the seventh day, the men of the city came to Shimshon with their answer. "What is sweeter than honey? And what is stronger than a lion?" Shimshon looked at them. He looked at his wife. He understood exactly what had happened. "If you hadn't plowed with my heifer," he said, "you would not have found out my riddle." The spirit of Hashem came upon him. He went down to Ashkelon — twenty miles away, a major Pelishti city — and struck down thirty men and took their garments and gave them to the men who had solved the riddle. He returned home to his father's house, burning with a rage that was only partly his own. And his wife was given to his companion. The man who had been his best man at the wedding took the bride while Shimshon was gone. He didn't know it yet. But the pretext that Hashem had been seeking — the spark that would light the fire between Shimshon and the Pelishtim — had been struck. ∗ ∗ ∗
