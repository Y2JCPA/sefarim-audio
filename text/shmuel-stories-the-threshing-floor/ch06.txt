Chapter Six

The Threshing Floor

David came down from the roof of the palace.

He had seen the angel. He had seen where the angel was standing when Hashem had said rav. He knew, now, what he was being asked to do. The threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi was the place where the wrath of Hashem had been turned aside, and the place where wrath was turned aside had to be the place where a thanksgiving altar was built. He had understood the instruction without being told it explicitly. Any king standing on the roof of his palace and watching the angel of death stop short of his city at a specific piece of ground would have understood the instruction.

And then — as if to confirm what David had already figured out — Gad the prophet came to him a second time.

"My lord."

"Gad. I know what you are going to say."

"I am sure you do. I have come to say it anyway, because Hashem commanded me to say it. Go up, and build an altar to Hashem on the threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi."

"I saw the angel stop there. I was already on my way."

"Yes, my lord. But the instruction had to be given by a prophet for the record. Now I have given it. You can proceed."

David nodded. He went into the inner chamber, changed into his traveling clothes, and ordered his horse. A small escort of Gibborim — the old men who were still around, the survivors of the wilderness years — assembled at the palace gate. David rode out of the city with the escort and headed north toward the hill where the threshing floor stood.

* * *

The hill was called Moriah. Most people in the kingdom did not know this, but David, because he was a student of the Torah, knew it. Moriah. The name appears in the Torah only once, at the beginning of Bereshit 22, in the verse where Hashem tells Avraham to take Yitzchak up to the eretz ha-Moriah — "the land of Moriah" — and to offer him as a sacrifice on one of the mountains that Hashem would show him. That mountain had been, by tradition, on this very hill. The place where Avraham had built the altar and bound Yitzchak and raised the knife and heard the voice of the angel from heaven telling him not to lay his hand on the boy — that very place — was somewhere on the hillside that David was now riding toward.

David had never been certain of the location before. The tradition had always said it was somewhere near Yerushalayim, but the exact spot had not been preserved in any text David had access to. Now, suddenly, the spot had been identified for him by the angel itself. The angel had stopped at the threshing floor of Aravnah, and the threshing floor of Aravnah was on Mount Moriah, and that meant the threshing floor of Aravnah was where the Akeidah had taken place.

The mefarshim are unanimous on this identification. The Rambam, in Hilchot Beit HaBechirah 2:2, states it as a settled tradition: "It is universally accepted that the place on which David and Shlomo built the altar, on the threshing floor of Aravnah, is the very place where Avraham built the altar on which he bound Yitzchak. It is the same place where Noach built an altar when he came out of the ark. It is the same place where Kayin and Hevel brought their offerings. It is the same place where Adam, the first man, offered a sacrifice — and it is the place from which Adam himself was formed. For the sages said: man was created from the place where he would find atonement."

Read that again. The tradition is staggering in its cosmic scope.

The threshing floor of Aravnah — an ordinary piece of farmland owned by a Yevusi farmer on a hill outside the walls of Yerushalayim — was, according to the Rambam (who was preserving the consensus tradition of the sages), the same location as five earlier events in the history of the world:

1. The creation of Adam. The first human being was formed from the dust of that specific spot. Bereshit Rabbah 14:8 preserves the teaching: "The Holy One gathered dust from the place of atonement and fashioned man out of it, so that man would be able to find atonement in the place from which he was drawn."

2. The altar of Adam. After Adam's expulsion from Gan Eden, he built an altar and brought an offering on the same spot from which he had been drawn.

3. The offerings of Kayin and Hevel. The two sons of Adam brought their offerings — Kayin's grain and Hevel's first-born lamb — at that same altar. The first murder of human history followed, because Hevel's offering was accepted and Kayin's was not.

4. The altar of Noach. After the Flood, when Noach came out of the ark, he built an altar and offered sacrifices on that same spot. Bereshit 8:20 describes the altar, and the tradition identifies its location with the Temple Mount.

5. The Akeidah of Yitzchak. When Avraham took Yitzchak up to the land of Moriah and built an altar and bound his son and raised the knife, the location was the same spot. The tradition reads eretz ha-Moriah as a direct reference to this specific hill outside Yerushalayim.

And now, at the end of Shmuel Bet, David was being told to build an altar on the same spot. The altar he was about to build would be the sixth altar on a site that had already seen five. And the Beit HaMikdash that Shlomo would later build on the same spot would make it permanent. This was not just a convenient piece of farmland. This was the place — the place of atonement, the place where the human relationship with Hashem could be mended, the place where every previous generation of human beings had come when they needed to turn back toward the Divine.

David was riding toward that place now, as an old king at the end of his reign, to build the sixth altar.

* * *

Aravnah the Yevusi was working on his threshing floor when he saw the royal party approaching.

He had been threshing wheat. The harvest was still going on. He had a team of oxen pulling a heavy wooden sledge in circles across the grain, separating the kernels from the chaff. He was an old man himself — a Yevusi who had been living in the area since before David conquered Yerushalayim, one of the small number of Yevusim who had not been displaced when David's forces had taken the city. He was a farmer, not a soldier, and the conquest had not disturbed his land. He had kept working his threshing floor through the reigns of two kings now.

He saw the approaching party. He recognized the king at the head of the escort. He stopped the oxen and walked forward to meet them, bowing his head low.

"My lord king."

David dismounted. He walked up to Aravnah slowly. He was tired — the emotional weight of the last three days had worn him out — and he was in no hurry to begin the negotiation. He stood in front of Aravnah for a long moment before he spoke.

"Aravnah."

"My lord."

"I need to buy your threshing floor from you."

Aravnah was startled. He had expected many things, but not this.

"My lord — why do you need to buy my threshing floor?"

"Because I need to build an altar on it. Hashem has commanded me through Gad the prophet to build an altar here. The angel of the plague stopped here this morning. This ground is sacred. I need to own it before I can sanctify it."

Aravnah understood immediately. He did not argue. He did not ask questions. He had seen something strange in the sky over his threshing floor earlier that morning — something that had made him back away in fear — and the news of the plague had reached him in the last few hours. He had put the pieces together. He was not surprised that the king had come.

"My lord. Take it."

"I said I need to buy it."

"My lord. Take it for free. All of it. The threshing floor itself — it is yours. The oxen pulling the sledge — yours, to be used as the burnt offering. The wooden sledge — yours, to be used as the firewood for the offering. I will give all of it to you. Let the king take whatever is good in his eyes and offer it up. May Hashem your God accept you."

David shook his head slowly.

"No, Aravnah. I cannot take it as a gift."

"My lord — I am offering it freely. It would be my honor."

"I know you are. And I thank you for the offer. But I cannot take it. If I take the threshing floor as a gift, and I take the oxen as a gift, and I take the sledge as a gift — then when I offer the sacrifice on the altar, it will be your sacrifice, not mine. The whole point of building this altar is that I am the one who sinned and I am the one who needs to atone. If the sacrifice costs me nothing, it is not my sacrifice. It is your sacrifice that I am presenting to Hashem. That is not what Hashem asked for. Hashem asked me to build the altar, and that means Hashem asked me to pay for the altar. Do you understand?"

Aravnah was silent for a moment.

"I understand. But my lord — "

"I will tell you what I will give you. I will not negotiate. I will simply tell you the price, and you will accept it, and the transaction will be done. Fifty shekels of silver for the threshing floor and the oxen. That is the price the Torah would recognize as a fair price for a piece of farmland of this size with working animals and tools included. It is a full market price. It is not a token payment. It is what the land is worth. Take the silver. Give me the deed of transfer. The transaction is complete."

"My lord — fifty shekels is too much. The land is worth less than that."

"It is not too much. I am the king, and I am paying a fair price because the Torah does not allow me to pay less. Take the silver."

"My lord — "

"Aravnah. Take the silver."

Aravnah took the silver.

* * *

A note on the prices.

Shmuel Bet 24:24 records the price as fifty shekels of silver for the threshing floor and the oxen. Divrei HaYamim Aleph 21:25 records the price as six hundred shekels of gold — for "the place," which the Chronicler apparently understands as a larger area than just the threshing floor itself. The discrepancy has been the subject of extensive rabbinic discussion.

The Gemara in Zevachim 116b gives a beautiful and unexpected resolution. The Gemara asks the same question the modern reader asks — how can the two verses give such different prices for what appears to be the same purchase? — and it answers with a single sentence that transforms the whole meaning of the transaction. "He collected fifty shekels from each tribe, and together they are six hundred."

Read that again.

David did not pay the fifty shekels out of his own treasury. He collected fifty shekels of silver from each of the twelve tribes of Israel. Fifty times twelve is six hundred. The two verses are not describing two different transactions. They are describing the same transaction from two different accounting angles: Shmuel Bet gives the per-tribe figure (fifty shekels was the contribution of each tribe) and Divrei HaYamim gives the total figure (six hundred shekels was the aggregate contribution of all twelve tribes combined).

Why does this matter? It matters because it means that the threshing floor — the future site of the Beit HaMikdash — was not purchased by David as a private royal acquisition. It was purchased by all of Israel, as a collective act of the twelve tribes. The silver that bought the Temple Mount came out of twelve tribal treasuries simultaneously. Every tribe had a financial stake in the purchase. Every tribe had a share in the ground. And therefore the Beit HaMikdash that Shlomo would eventually build on that ground would belong to all of Israel and not merely to the tribe of Yehuda or the tribe of Binyamin (in whose territory the Temple Mount technically sat). The Rambam picks up this principle in Hilchot Beit HaBechirah and treats it as one of the reasons the Temple Mount is the property of the entire Jewish people in perpetuity: the original acquisition was paid for by all the tribes together.

(The Chronicler's version of the price — six hundred shekels of gold rather than silver — introduces its own difficulty, which the mefarshim resolve in various ways. Some read it as the gold equivalent of the silver after a later conversion. Some, like Rashi on Divrei HaYamim, follow the Gemara's reading and simply note that the aggregate sum can be expressed either in silver terms or in the higher-denomination gold terms depending on the accounting convention being used. Others, like the Metzudat David, suggest that David first paid the fifty shekels of silver for the immediate threshing floor and the oxen used in the initial sacrifice, and then later went back and paid an additional six hundred shekels of gold for the surrounding hillside as the eventual site of the full Temple complex — a two-transaction reading that is compatible with the Gemara's per-tribe reading if we assume the per-tribe collection was for the full six hundred gold figure. All these readings are preserved in the classical tradition. The Gemara's own reading — fifty per tribe, twelve tribes, six hundred total — is the primary one.)

Rabbinic tradition also notes the parallel between David's purchase at the threshing floor and Avraham's purchase of the Me'arat HaMachpelah in Bereshit 23. In both cases, an Israelite patriarch purchases a piece of ground from a local non-Israelite landowner. In both cases, the seller initially offers the land as a gift. In both cases, the buyer insists on paying the full market price. In both cases, the Tanach preserves the exact price. In both cases, the piece of ground becomes permanent — Me'arat HaMachpelah as the burial place of the patriarchs, and the threshing floor as the site of the Temple. The parallel is not accidental. The Tanach is telling us that the two purchases are bookends: one acquires the land of the burial of the forefathers, and one acquires the land of the worship of their descendants. Both are essential. Both are remembered. Both were acquired at full market price, so that the ground could truly belong to Israel in perpetuity.

* * *

David built the altar on the threshing floor that same day.

The Tanach does not tell us what the altar looked like. The mefarshim assume it was a temporary altar of uncut stones, built in the manner prescribed by Shemot 20:22 — stones gathered from the field, piled in a low square, without any iron tool touching them. The altar did not need to be beautiful. It needed to be functional. David was going to offer sacrifices on it within a few hours, and the altar only had to be sturdy enough to hold the burning wood and the carcasses of the oxen that Aravnah had been using to thresh the wheat a few hours earlier.

David himself helped build it. This is the detail the mefarshim preserve: he did not delegate the construction to his servants. He picked up the stones himself. He arranged them himself. He was an old man — his hands were thin, his back was sore — but he wanted to be the one who built the altar, because the altar was the physical expression of his own repentance, and he was not willing to delegate his repentance to someone else. The Gibborim in the escort offered to help. He let them carry stones from the edges of the threshing floor, but he insisted on placing each stone himself in the position where it belonged. The altar rose slowly under his hands. By late afternoon, it was finished.

One of the Gibborim — a very old man, one of the few survivors of the cave of Adullam — came over and stood beside David as the king was placing the last stones.

"My lord."

"Yes."

"May I say something to you?"

"Say it."

"I have been with you for a very long time. I was in the cave when you took us in. I have fought beside you in every major campaign of the reign. I have seen you at your best and I have seen you at your worst. And I want to tell you that what you are doing right now — placing the stones of the altar with your own hands on a hilltop outside Yerushalayim at the end of your life, after a plague that you caused — this is the image of you that I am going to carry for the rest of my life. This is the David I am going to remember. Not the king in the palace. Not the commander in the field. The old man on the hilltop, placing the stones himself because the altar has to be built by the hands of the one who sinned. That is who you are, my lord. That is who you have always been. You are the king who places his own stones."

David stopped for a moment. He looked at the old Gibbor. He did not know what to say. After a long pause, he said:

"Thank you."

"You do not need to thank me. I needed to say it. I have been wanting to say it for years. This seemed like the right moment."

"It is the right moment."

The old Gibbor stepped back. David placed the last stone. The altar was finished.

* * *

Aravnah brought the oxen. David slaughtered them. The wooden sledge that Aravnah had been using for threshing was broken up and placed on the altar as firewood. The carcasses of the oxen were placed on the firewood. David took a torch and lit the altar.

The fire caught slowly. The wood was dry but not fully seasoned, and it took several minutes to catch properly. Once it caught, the flames rose high. The smoke carried upward toward the sky. David knelt in front of the altar and began to pray.

The Tanach does not preserve the specific words of his prayer. It says only: "And David built there an altar to Hashem, and offered burnt offerings and peace offerings. And Hashem was entreated for the land, and the plague was stayed from Israel."

Va-ye'atzar ha-magefah me-al Yisrael.

The plague was stayed. The angel with the drawn sword, which had been standing between earth and heaven above the threshing floor for most of the morning, lowered the sword and disappeared. The wave of death that had been moving across the countryside stopped. The seventy thousand who had died were the final count. No more would die. The kingdom had been rescued from the remainder of the punishment by the king's own sacrifice on the altar he had built with his own hands on the ground he had purchased with his own silver.

David stayed by the altar for a long time after the sacrifice was complete. The fire burned down. The ashes cooled. The sun began to set behind the western hills. The Gibborim of the escort stood at a respectful distance, waiting for him to rise. He did not rise for a long time.

* * *

The parallel in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 21:26 preserves a detail that Shmuel Bet 24 does not preserve: when David offered the sacrifice on the new altar, fire came down from heaven and consumed the offering. Va-ya'anehu ba-esh min ha-shamayim al mizbach ha-olah. "And Hashem answered him with fire from heaven on the altar of burnt offering."

This is the detail that confirmed for David that the site was the right site. A sacrifice consumed by heavenly fire is the sign of special divine approval — the same sign that had appeared when Eliyahu would later build an altar on Mount Carmel and call fire down from heaven (Melakhim Aleph 18), and the same sign that had appeared when the Mishkan was inaugurated in the wilderness (Vayikra 9:24). Fire from heaven consuming an offering means that Hashem has accepted the sacrifice directly, without the mediation of human priests performing the consumption by ordinary flame. David saw the fire come down. He understood that the threshing floor of Aravnah had been chosen by Hashem as the permanent place of atonement — that the altar he had built that day was going to become the altar of the Beit HaMikdash that his son would build.

Divrei HaYamim Aleph 22:1 preserves David's own declaration of this understanding. After the fire came down and the plague was stayed, David stood up from his kneeling position, turned to Gad the prophet who had come up to the threshing floor during the afternoon, and said:

"Gad."

"My lord?"

"This is the place."

"My lord?"

"This is the place. The place where the Beit HaMikdash will stand. The place where Shlomo will build the House of Hashem. I am telling you so that you will write it down and the record will show that I said it here, on this day, on this threshing floor, while the smoke of the sacrifice was still in the air."

"Say it again, my lord. So I can remember the exact words."

David said it slowly, deliberately, so that the old prophet could memorize the phrasing for the record.

"Zeh hu Beit Hashem ha-Elohim, ve-zeh mizbe'ach le-olah le-Yisrael."

"This is the House of Hashem the God, and this is the altar of burnt offering for Israel."

Gad nodded.

"I have it. I will write it down."

* * *

Read that sentence slowly. It is the closing sentence of the whole arc.

This is the House of Hashem the God.

The altar on the threshing floor was a temporary altar. But the place of the altar was permanent. David was looking at a patch of ground on a hillside outside Yerushalayim and saying: this is the place where the Beit HaMikdash is going to stand. This is the place my son Shlomo will build. This is the place where the Aron will come to rest. This is the place where the daily sacrifices will be offered for the rest of the history of Israel. This is the place the Mashiach will come to at the end of days. He was seeing, in the raw ground of a Yevusi farmer's threshing floor, the future Temple that he himself would never see. And he was identifying it — naming it — declaring it — with a single short sentence that would become the theological foundation of everything that came after.

Zeh hu Beit Hashem ha-Elohim.

This is the House of Hashem the God.

The whole of Shmuel Bet has been moving toward this sentence. The bringing up of the Aron to Yerushalayim in chapter 6 was pointing toward a House in which the Aron would eventually rest. The prophecy of Natan in chapter 7 — "Your son who shall come from your body, he shall build a house for My name" — was pointing toward a House that David's son would build. The sin with Batsheva and the disqualification of David from building the House himself was pointing toward a House that would have to wait for the next generation. The Avshalom rebellion and the restoration were pointing toward a House that had to be built on a stable foundation. The Gibborim and the register of the Thirty were pointing toward a House whose servants had already been in place for decades. The three-year famine and the Ritzpah episode were pointing toward a House whose holiness would be preserved by faithful witness. The song of deliverance and the last words of David were pointing toward a House whose builder was the sweet singer of Israel. And now, in this final moment, all of those threads converged: David standing on the threshing floor, looking at the smoke of his sacrifice rising into the sky, the fire from heaven still visible in his memory, and saying the one sentence the whole book has been waiting for.

This is the House of Hashem the God, and this is the altar of burnt offering for Israel.

* * *

## The end of the volume and of the series

David did not build the Temple. That was for his son Shlomo. But David bought the ground. David built the first altar. David made the first offering. David declared the site. And David spent the rest of his reign — the years that would be covered in the book of Melakhim Aleph — preparing the materials that Shlomo would need. He collected the gold and the silver and the cedar wood and the stones. He organized the courses of the kohanim who would serve in the Temple. He composed the psalms that would be sung in its courts. He prepared everything that could be prepared while he was still alive, so that when he died and Shlomo took the throne, the Temple could be built with the minimum possible delay.

Imagine David that evening, returning from the threshing floor to the palace, sending for his son Shlomo. Shlomo was perhaps fifteen or sixteen years old at this point — not yet king, not yet the sage the world would remember, but already being groomed by his father for the work that was coming. David was in the upper chamber. Shlomo came in.

"Father. You sent for me."

"Sit down, Shlomo. I have something to tell you."

"Yes, father."

"I went up to the threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi today. On the hill to the north of the city. I built an altar there and offered a sacrifice. Fire came down from heaven and consumed the offering. The plague was stayed."

"I heard the news as I was coming to the palace. The whole city is talking about it."

"Good. I want you to remember what I am about to say. I want you to remember it word for word. Do you understand?"

"Yes, father."

"That hill — the hill where the threshing floor stands — is the place where the Beit HaMikdash is going to be built. Not by me. By you. I am not the one who will build it. I have too much blood on my hands. Natan the prophet told me years ago, when I first wanted to build it — he told me that a man of war could not be the one to build the House of peace. I accepted the ruling. I have accepted it ever since. The Temple is for you, not for me. But the place of the Temple has now been shown to me. I have bought it. I have built the first altar on it. I have made the first offering on it. I have heard the fire come down from heaven. And I am now telling you, so that when the time comes and you are king, you will know exactly where to build."

"Father — how will I know the exact spot?"

"I will show you. Tomorrow morning, if you are willing, I will take you up to the threshing floor and walk you around it. I will mark the corners with stones. I will show you where the altar should go. I will show you which direction the Holy of Holies should face. I will walk every step of it with you, so that when I die and you become king, you will not have to guess at anything. You will know the ground. You will know the dimensions. You will know the orientation. Everything. I am not going to leave this to chance, Shlomo. The Temple is too important."

"Yes, father."

"And one more thing."

"Yes?"

"I am going to spend the rest of my life collecting the materials for the Temple. Gold. Silver. Cedar wood from Levanon. Cut stones from the quarries in the north. Bronze. Iron for the tools that will not be used in the final construction but that will be needed to prepare the materials beforehand. Skilled craftsmen — I will start sending messages to the courts of neighboring kings and asking them for the best craftsmen they can spare. I will build up the stockpiles so that when you take the throne, you can begin construction in the first year of your reign. I do not want you to waste time after I am gone. The Temple has waited long enough."

"Father — "

"Yes?"

"I am not ready."

"I know you are not. That is why I am telling you this now. I am going to spend the next few years getting you ready. I am going to teach you what I have learned about ruling. I am going to introduce you to the old counselors who have been with me for decades. I am going to walk you through the administration of the kingdom until you know it as well as I do. You will be ready when the time comes. Do not worry about the readiness. Worry only about the willingness. Are you willing to build the Temple, Shlomo? That is the only question I need you to answer tonight."

Shlomo looked at his father for a long moment.

"Yes, father. I am willing."

"Then that is enough for tonight. Go to sleep. Tomorrow morning we will go up to the threshing floor together."

Shlomo stood up, bowed, and left the chamber. David sat alone in the upper chamber for a long time after his son had gone, looking out the window toward the hill where the threshing floor was, thinking about the House that would eventually stand there — the House he himself would never enter.

* * *

The book of Melakhim Aleph is the book of Shlomo. This series, Sipurei Shmuel Bet, is not going to cover it. Shlomo's wisdom, Shlomo's Temple, Shlomo's hundreds of wives and concubines, Shlomo's decline at the end of his life, the splitting of the kingdom after his death — all of these belong to a different volume in the broader Sipurei Tanach series. We end here, with David on the threshing floor, the fire still smoldering on the altar, the plague stopped, the ground bought and sanctified.

And we end here because the book of Shmuel Bet ends here.

The next verse after David's declaration — Shmuel Bet 24:25 — is the final verse of the entire book: "And David built there an altar to Hashem, and offered burnt offerings and peace offerings. So Hashem was entreated for the land, and the plague was stayed from Israel." That is the last sentence of Shmuel Bet. The book closes with the plague stayed, the altar built, the land entreated. It does not continue. It does not go on to describe Shlomo's coronation or the building of the Temple. It leaves the reader at the threshing floor, with the smoke of the sacrifice in the air, and lets the silence that follows carry the reader forward into whatever comes next.

* * *

## A last look at David

We began this series seven volumes ago with David weeping on a hilltop for Shaul and Yonatan. We are ending it with David on his knees in front of an altar on the hillside that would become the Temple Mount. The arc of the book is the arc of a man — a flawed, brilliant, passionate, sometimes cruel, sometimes breathtakingly generous man — who was given the task of founding a kingdom and who spent his whole adult life trying to be worthy of the task even as he repeatedly failed at specific moments within it.

He failed with Batsheva. He failed with Uriah. He failed with Amnon and Tamar. He failed with Avshalom. He failed in the census. He failed in small ways and in large ways throughout the book. The Tanach does not hide any of the failures. It preserves them all, in the same voice and with the same clarity with which it preserves the moments of triumph. This is why David is remembered. Not because he was flawless, but because he was honest about not being flawless. He confessed the sins out loud, in front of the prophet or in front of the angel or in front of Hashem directly, without trying to hide or shift the blame or invoke mitigating circumstances. Chatati me'od — I have sinned greatly. Le-khah levaddekha chatati — against You alone have I sinned. Hineh anokhi chatati — behold, I am the one who sinned. Every one of these phrases is David's own voice. Every one of them is preserved in the Tanach without softening. David's honesty about his own failures is what made him worthy, despite the failures, of being the founder of the dynasty from which the Mashiach would come.

And he was, more than anything else, a man who loved Hashem. The song of deliverance in chapter 22 is the song of a man who understood his whole life as a conversation with the God who had been with him since he was a shepherd boy. The last words in chapter 23:1–7 are the closing statement of a man who knew that the covenant with his house was eternal and that the dynasty was secure and that the House would be built by his son on a specific piece of ground. The prayer on the roof in chapter 24 is the prayer of a man who would rather die himself than see the sheep of his flock cut down for a sin he had committed. The building of the altar on the threshing floor is the final act of a man who wanted his last visible deed to be the consecration of the place where Israel would worship for generations to come.

David was a king. David was a sinner. David was a psalmist. David was a father. David was a warrior. David was a penitent. David was a lover of Hashem. The book of Shmuel Bet preserves all of these Davids in the same twenty-four chapters, without preferring one to another, because the Tanach wants us to see the whole man.

And the whole man built an altar on a threshing floor on a hill outside Yerushalayim, on the last pages of the last chapter of the book, on the ground from which Adam had been formed and at which Adam and Kayin and Hevel and Noach and Avraham had all worshiped before him, and he said: This is the House of Hashem the God, and this is the altar of burnt offering for Israel.

That is where we leave him.
