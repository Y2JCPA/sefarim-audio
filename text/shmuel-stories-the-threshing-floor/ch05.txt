Chapter Five

Three Days of Plague

Gad the prophet came to the palace early in the morning.

He had received the word of Hashem during the night. He had not slept after receiving it. He had sat in his own small house on the edge of Yerushalayim, going over the words of the message, trying to find a way to soften them and finding no way. The message was what it was. He would have to deliver it exactly as it had been given to him. There was no escape from it for David, and there was no escape from it for Gad either. The prophet, when given an unwelcome message for a king, does not have the option of editing the message. He has to deliver it as received, and then he has to stand there while the king hears it, and then he has to carry back whatever answer the king gives.

Gad had been with David for a long time. He had first come to the band at the cave of Adullam, when David was still a fugitive and Gad had been a young prophet trained at one of the prophetic schools that had been scattered across the country after the death of Shmuel. He had been with the band through the wilderness years. He had advised David on the movement of the camp from Adullam to Ziklag, and from Ziklag to Chevron after Shaul's death. He had been with the court in Chevron during the seven-year reign there, and he had followed David to Yerushalayim when the capital moved. He had been a background presence for the whole of the forty-year reign — sometimes consulted on military matters, sometimes on questions of ritual, sometimes simply as a man David trusted to tell him the truth about what Hashem was saying.

He had never been given a task like this one.

He walked up the palace steps in the gray light of early morning. The guards at the door knew him and let him in without asking his business. He went directly to the chamber where David received early-morning visitors. David was already there, sitting on a low chair by the window. He had not slept either. He was still wearing the clothes he had been wearing the night before. The wax tablets of the census were no longer on the table — they had been removed to the archive room — but David's gaze kept drifting to the spot where they had been.

"Gad."

"My lord."

"You have a word from Hashem."

"Yes, my lord. How did you know?"

"I was expecting you. I confessed the sin last night. I asked for Hashem to forgive it. I knew someone would come to me this morning with the answer. You are the one who was sent."

"Yes."

"Speak."

Gad stood very still in the middle of the room. He had practiced the words on the way over. He wanted to deliver them cleanly, without any hesitation that might suggest he was trying to soften the message.

"Thus says Hashem. I offer you three things. Choose one of them for yourself, that I may do it to you."

David did not move.

"Three things?"

"Yes, my lord. Three. I am commanded to present them to you. You must choose one. That is the procedure Hashem has set for this."

"Tell me the three."

"First: seven years of famine in your land."

"Seven years."

"Yes, my lord. Seven years of famine."

"Longer than the famine for the Givonim. Double and more."

"Yes, my lord. Seven years."

David said nothing for a long moment.

"And the second?"

"Second: three months of fleeing before your enemies while they pursue you."

"Three months of flight."

"Yes, my lord."

"Like the Avshalom rebellion, but without the forest of Efrayim at the end. Three months of running without a restoration to come. Do I understand correctly?"

"I am not given the details, my lord. I am given only the three options. The second option is three months of fleeing before enemies. You will have to understand the shape of it as you live through it, if that is the one you choose."

"And the third?"

"Third: three days of plague in your land."

"Three days."

"Yes, my lord. Three days."

David was silent again. He was working the three options through in his head. Gad waited. The mefarshim fill in the analysis David was doing in those long silent seconds.

Seven years of famine. A long, slow punishment that would affect primarily the poor — the farmers, the day laborers, the people who did not have stored food reserves. The wealthy would survive a seven-year famine by liquidating assets and buying imported grain from Egypt. The king himself, in his palace, would never go hungry. The burden would fall on the weakest members of the kingdom. Children would starve. Old people would die of malnutrition. Families would lose their lands and become tenant farmers. The long-term damage to the social fabric of the country would be enormous.

Three months of fleeing before enemies. A mid-range punishment that would affect primarily the fighting men — the soldiers, the officers, the captains of the levies. The king himself would be vulnerable: he was an old man, he had already been forced to stop going out to battle after Avishai had had to save him from Yishbi-benov, and three months of running from enemies was probably going to kill him. The Gibborim would die in large numbers defending him during the flight. Some of the kingdom's most loyal servants would be killed. And at the end of three months, the kingdom might well have lost significant territory — the border regions would be overrun by whichever enemies were doing the pursuing, and the losses might be permanent.

Three days of plague. A short, sharp, catastrophic punishment that would affect everyone equally — king, Gibborim, wealthy, poor, old, young, strong, weak, men, women, children. A plague does not discriminate. It would move through the country in a wave over seventy-two hours and leave thousands or tens of thousands of dead in its wake. But it would also be quick. It would be over in three days. The country would still be intact at the end of it. The social fabric would not be torn. The borders would not be lost. The kingdom would still be the kingdom. And the king himself would be at risk of the plague along with everyone else — the plague would not spare the palace.

David thought about it for a long time.

Then he said:

"Tzar li me'od. Nipelah na be-yad Hashem, ki rabim rachamav, u-ve-yad adam al epolah."

"I am in great distress. Let us fall, I beseech, into the hand of Hashem, for His mercies are great, but do not let me fall into the hand of man."

Gad nodded slowly.

"My lord. The third option, then."

"The third option. The plague. Three days."

"Why, my lord?"

"Because of what I just said. If I pick the famine, the people will starve for seven years while the enemies of Israel watch and make jokes about how Hashem abandoned us, and the children of farmers will die while the children of the palace will eat. If I pick the flight, we will be delivered into the hands of human enemies — Pelishtim, Aramim, Moavim, whoever Hashem chooses to send after us — and human enemies have no mercy. They will torture the men they capture. They will rape the women. They will kill the children without thought. A man who falls into human hands is at the mercy of the worst impulses of his captors. But if I pick the plague, we will be directly in the hand of Hashem — in the hand of the one whose attribute is mercy, whose judgments are always tempered by compassion, who might yet relent and cut the plague short before the three days are over. Hashem may strike, but Hashem may also show mercy in the middle of the striking. A human enemy will not. Therefore I choose to fall into the hand of Hashem rather than the hand of man."

Gad nodded again. He understood.

"I will bring the answer back."

"Gad."

"My lord?"

"Tell Hashem — if it is not inappropriate to add this — that I am sorry. Tell Him I know I brought this on the nation by myself, and that the plague should really fall on me and on my house rather than on the people. Tell Him — well, no. Do not tell Him that. It would sound like I was trying to bargain. I am not trying to bargain. I am trying to accept the decision. Bring only the answer He asked for. The third option. The plague."

"Yes, my lord."

Gad bowed and left the chamber. He walked back through the city streets, past the morning crowds who did not know what had just been decided, to his small house on the edge of Yerushalayim. He reported the answer to Hashem in his own private chamber, the way prophets reported answers when the answer was private. The message had been delivered. The king had chosen. Now the plague would come.

* * *

The plague came that same morning.

The Tanach records it in a single sentence: "So Hashem sent a pestilence upon Israel from the morning even until the appointed time, and there died of the people from Dan even to Be'er Sheva seventy thousand men."

Seventy thousand.

Read that number. Let it sit for a moment. The nation had been counted at one million three hundred thousand fighting men a few months earlier. Seventy thousand of them were about to die in three days. That is roughly one in every eighteen adult men in the kingdom. Every extended family would know someone who died. Most households would lose a relative. Whole villages in the harder-hit regions would lose a significant fraction of their adult male population in a single seventy-two-hour period.

The Tanach does not describe the plague in clinical detail. It does not name the disease. It does not track the symptoms. It says only that it came in the morning and continued until the "appointed time" — which the mefarshim variously explain as the middle of the third day, the time of the afternoon sacrifice on the third day, or the time when the angel arrived at the threshing floor of Aravnah (which we will come to in a moment). The point of the absence of clinical detail is that the plague is being presented not as a medical phenomenon but as a theological one. The cause is divine judgment. The agent is the angel of Hashem. The mechanism is not the concern of the Tanach. The concern of the Tanach is who is dying, and why, and when it will stop.

People were dying everywhere. They were dying in the hill country of Yehuda. They were dying in the coastal plain. They were dying in the northern tribes up by Dan. They were dying in the Gilead territory across the Yarden. The plague was moving across the whole kingdom at the same time — not as a contagion spreading from a single point but as a direct affliction coming from the hand of the angel who had been sent to execute the judgment. The sick did not necessarily have sick neighbors. A man could be healthy in the morning and dead by afternoon. A child could be playing in the courtyard in the early light and lying still by noon. The plague was everywhere and nowhere at once. The angel of death was moving.

David sat in the palace. He knew what was happening. Messengers were bringing reports back to him every few hours. The reports were all bad. He had not eaten since the morning. He had not changed his clothes. He had not spoken to anyone except the messengers and the chief of the household who was keeping the reports coming. He was an old man who had just watched himself cause a catastrophe that would have been entirely avoidable if he had simply listened to Yoav nine months ago, and he could not stop thinking about it.

His advisors came to him. They told him what they were seeing. They told him how many had died so far in each region. The numbers were climbing. By the end of the first day the estimated dead was over twenty thousand. By the end of the second day it was over fifty thousand. The third day began with the total approaching seventy thousand, and the plague was still moving, and the angel was still walking, and there was no sign that it was stopping.

* * *

On the third day, at some point in the late morning, David went up to the roof of the palace.

The mefarshim tell us he had gone up to pray. He could no longer sit in the chamber listening to the reports. He needed to be somewhere where he could see what was happening. The roof of the palace was the highest point in the city. From the roof he could see the hills around Yerushalayim. He could see the valleys where the wheat and the barley were being harvested. He could see the flocks grazing on the terraces. He could see, in the middle distance, the dark shapes of funeral processions moving from the villages toward the burial grounds — families carrying their dead to the family tombs in the hills outside the city walls. The processions were everywhere. They were on every path. The plague had been running for two full days and the burials had been unable to keep up; bodies were being buried as fast as they could be carried, but there were more bodies than there were hands to carry them, and the processions were continuous.

And then David looked up.

The Tanach preserves what he saw: "And David lifted up his eyes, and saw the angel of Hashem standing between the earth and the heaven, having a drawn sword in his hand stretched out over Yerushalayim."

An angel. Standing between earth and heaven. A drawn sword in his hand. The sword stretched out over Yerushalayim itself.

The angel had been working across the countryside for two days, moving from village to village, taking lives. Now the angel was at the edge of the city — standing in the air between earth and heaven, with the sword drawn and stretched out, about to enter Yerushalayim itself. The plague, which had so far been concentrated in the countryside, was about to come into the capital. The palace would not be spared. The Aron at the tent of David would not be spared. The priests and the Gibborim and the royal children and the ordinary residents of Yerushalayim — all of them were about to be exposed to the same wave of death that had been moving across the countryside.

David fell to his knees on the roof.

He cried out. The Tanach preserves his cry with extraordinary directness. It is one of the most moving moments in all of Shmuel Bet:

"Hineh anokhi chatati ve-anokhi he'eviti, ve-eleh ha-tzon meh asu? Tehi na yadekha bi u-ve-veit avi."

"Behold — I am the one who sinned, I am the one who acted wickedly. But these sheep — what have they done? Let Your hand, I pray, be against me and against my father's house."

Read that again.

I am the one who sinned. I am the one who acted wickedly.

But these sheep — what have they done?

Let Your hand be against me, and against my father's house.

David is on the roof. He is an old man. The angel of death is standing in the air above the city with a drawn sword. Seventy thousand men have already died in the countryside. And David, seeing the angel, is offering himself and his family as the substitute for the people. He is saying: do not kill the people. They did nothing. They are sheep — innocent sheep who have been caught up in a judgment that was meant for their shepherd. Kill me instead. Kill my whole family. Put the punishment where it belongs. I am the one who committed the sin. The people only did what I told them to do. They are not the guilty ones.

This is the verse the mefarshim return to whenever they discuss what David was at his best. David at his worst was the king who arranged Uriah's death and took his wife. David at his best was the king who stood on a roof and offered his own life and his whole house in exchange for the lives of the people he had sinned against. Both Davids are real. Both are in the Tanach. But the David on the roof of the palace, with the angel above him and the drawn sword over the city, is the David whose voice the Jewish tradition has loved across the centuries — the David of "against You alone have I sinned" in Tehillim 51, the David of the honest confession, the David who would rather die himself than see the sheep of his flock cut down for a sin they did not commit.

Eleh ha-tzon — meh asu?

These sheep — what have they done?

It is the question that every responsible leader should ask when disaster comes to the people under their care. It is the question that David was willing to ask aloud on the roof of his palace, in front of the angel of death, with full understanding that the answer might be: take you instead.

* * *

The angel stopped.

The Tanach preserves the moment with unusual literary power: "And when the angel stretched out his hand toward Yerushalayim to destroy it, Hashem relented from the destruction, and said to the angel who was destroying the people: It is enough; now restrain your hand. And the angel of Hashem was by the threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi."

Rav, atah heref yadekha.

"It is enough. Restrain your hand."

The angel stopped. The sword that had been stretched out over the city was lowered. The plague did not enter Yerushalayim. The seventy thousand who had died were the full count; no more would be added.

And the angel was standing — at the moment of being told to stop — at the threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi.

This is the detail that matters. The angel, in the course of moving across the country with the drawn sword, had arrived at a specific physical location at the exact moment when Hashem said stop. That location was the threshing floor of a Yevusi farmer named Aravnah, on a hill just outside the walls of Yerushalayim, on the north side of the city. It was an ordinary piece of farmland owned by an ordinary foreign resident of the area who had been farming the land for years. It was not a sacred site. It was not a shrine. It was a place where grain was threshed in the harvest season. And it was the place where the angel of death had been standing when the angel of death was told to stop.

The Tanach preserves this detail for a reason. The place where the angel stopped was the place where Hashem's mercy had been activated. The place where Hashem's mercy had been activated was, by that fact alone, made sacred. It was the place where the wrath of Hashem had been turned aside by Hashem's own decision to say enough. Any ground on which such a thing happens is ground that has been singled out by the Divine. The threshing floor of Aravnah was now a place in which something theological had happened. And David, standing on the roof of the palace and looking out at the angel, could see where the angel was standing, and he understood immediately what the Tanach was telling him: the place the angel stopped is the place you are supposed to sanctify.

The next chapter is about what David did when he understood this. It is about the purchase of the threshing floor, the building of the altar, the sacrifice that completed the atonement, and the extraordinary tradition preserved by the Rambam in Hilchot Beit HaBechirah that identifies the threshing floor of Aravnah with the same site where Adam had built the first altar, where Kayin and Hevel had brought the first offerings, where Noach had built an altar after the Flood, and where Avraham had bound Yitzchak at the Akeidah.

It is the final chapter of this volume and the final chapter of the series.
