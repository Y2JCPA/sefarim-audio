Chapter Three

The Song and the Last Words

Between the two appendices that frame the closing of the book of Shmuel Bet sits a small island of poetry.

Chapter 22 of Shmuel Bet is a long song — fifty-one verses long, one of the longest poetic passages in all of Tanach — that David composed at some point after Hashem had delivered him from his enemies. The song is nearly identical to Tehillim 18, where it appears a second time with minor variations. It is a thanksgiving hymn, a psalm of deliverance, a magnificent piece of Hebrew poetry that the tradition attributes to David's mature years when he was looking back on the whole arc of his life. Chapter 23 begins with another poetic fragment — seven verses long — that the Tanach introduces with the formula "And these are the last words of David." The fragment is a short, dense, oracular poem attributed to David at or near the very end of his life.

These two poetic passages — the long song of chapter 22 and the short "last words" of 23:1–7 — sit between the story of the three-year famine (which we told in the first two chapters of this volume) and the story of the Gibborim register (which we told throughout Volume VI) and the story of the census and the threshing floor (which we will come to in the next three chapters). They are, in terms of the structural architecture of the book, the poetic heart of the appendix section. They are the Tanach's way of pausing the narrative action of Shmuel Bet and letting David speak — for once, in his own voice, in poetry rather than in dialogue — about what the whole journey has meant.

This chapter is about those two poems.

It is a quieter chapter than the others. There will not be as much dialogue in it. The mefarshim treat these chapters as an opportunity to reflect on David's self-understanding at the end of his life, and the best way to honor the chapters is to follow the mefarshim in slowing down and listening to what David is actually saying. We will look at the long song first. Then we will look at the last words. We will try to understand what David was telling us about himself and about his reign and about the covenant with Hashem that he believed had made his whole life possible.

But before we begin, let us imagine the scene of the composition. David is in the upper chamber of the palace. It is evening. He is sitting by the window, looking out at the hills. A scribe is sitting on a low stool with a wax tablet on his knees. David has just finished dictating the first verse.

"Read it back to me."

The scribe reads.

"Hashem sal'i u-metzudati u-mfalti li. Elohei tzuri echeseh bo, magini ve-keren yish'i, misgabi u-menusi, moshi'i me-chamas toshi'eni."

"Hashem is my rock, and my stronghold, and my deliverer. The God of my rock, in Him will I take refuge; my shield, and the horn of my salvation, my high tower, and my refuge; my savior — You save me from violence."

"Good. Write the next line."

The scribe dips his stylus.

"My lord — may I ask a question before you continue?"

"Ask."

"Why are you composing this now? You have had a peaceful year. The famine is over. There are no enemies on the horizon. Why are you writing a song about deliverance at a moment when there is nothing to be delivered from?"

David looked at the scribe for a long moment.

"Because the song is not about a specific deliverance. The song is about the whole arc. I am old. I am going to die soon — maybe this year, maybe next year, maybe in five years, but soon. And before I die, I want to put on record what I have come to understand about the shape of my life. I have been thinking about it for some time. I want to write it as poetry rather than as narrative, because poetry carries the meaning better. A man who writes a narrative of his own life gets caught up in the details. A man who writes a song about his own life can speak in images and leave the details to the reader."

"So the song is — the whole arc of the reign?"

"The whole arc. From the cave of Adullam to the throne in Yerushalayim. From the young shepherd to the old king. From the first night I slept on a stone floor with a rolled-up cloak as a pillow, to the nights I have spent in the palace thinking about all the nights I slept on stone floors. The song is the summary. Everything Hashem has done for me — every rescue, every deliverance, every moment of grace that I did not deserve and could not have earned. I want it all to be there. And I want it in poetry because poetry is how songs become prayer, and prayer is what I am trying to write. This is the song the people are going to sing in the Temple that my son will build. I am writing the words now so that they will be there when the building goes up."

The scribe nodded slowly.

"Shall I continue?"

"Continue."

And so we will proceed — the way the scribe did, taking down the verses one at a time.

* * *

## The Song of Deliverance

The song begins with a superscription: "And David spoke unto Hashem the words of this song in the day that Hashem delivered him out of the hand of all his enemies, and out of the hand of Shaul."

The superscription is strange. It says David sang the song in the day that Hashem delivered him out of the hand of all his enemies, and out of the hand of Shaul. But "the day" does not mean a specific twenty-four-hour period. The mefarshim are clear that the song was not composed in a single day at a particular moment. It is a song that summarizes a lifetime of deliverance. The "day" is the entire arc of David's adult life from the moment he was anointed by Shmuel in his father's courtyard in Beit Lechem to the moment, many decades later, when he finally sat on the throne in Yerushalayim with no major enemies remaining against him. The phrase "in the day that Hashem delivered him" is a Tanach idiom for "in the general period of life during which the deliverance was accomplished." David composed the song in his middle-to-late years, looking backward over everything that had happened.

And the song begins with the four words that have become one of the most famous openings in all of Tehillim:

Hashem sal'i u-metzudati u-mfalti li.

"Hashem is my rock, and my stronghold, and my deliverer."

The three images are military. A sela is a high rocky crag where a man could take cover from enemies who were trying to catch him in the open valleys. A metzudah is a fortress — a man-made stronghold built on a height, with walls and towers. A mefalet is a deliverer, a rescuer, someone who reaches down and pulls a drowning man out of the water. David is saying that Hashem has been all three of these things for him. The natural rock he hid behind during the cave years. The fortress he built on Mount Tzion when he conquered Yerushalayim. The hand that reached down and pulled him out of the many moments of danger when he could not save himself.

The song continues in the same register for dozens of verses. David piles up images of divine deliverance: Hashem is his shield, his horn of salvation, his high tower, his refuge. David praises Hashem for hearing his cry from the depths of his distress. He describes in cosmic terms what happened when Hashem came to rescue him: "Then the earth shook and trembled, the foundations of heaven moved and were shaken, because He was wroth. There went up a smoke out of His nostrils, and fire out of His mouth devoured." The imagery is the imagery of a theophany — a descent of the Divine in fire and cloud and earthquake to rescue a servant in trouble. David is saying that the deliverance he experienced in his life was not a mechanical thing; it was not the ordinary operation of luck or of his own military skill. It was Hashem stepping into his life in visible, palpable ways that shook the ground under his feet.

And then the song turns to the moral foundation of the deliverance:

"Hashem rewarded me according to my righteousness; according to the cleanness of my hands He recompensed me. For I have kept the ways of Hashem, and have not wickedly departed from my God. For all His judgments were before me, and as for His statutes, I did not depart from them."

This is the verse that has given readers pause for centuries. David — the David of the Batsheva crisis, the David who arranged for Uriah to be killed, the David whose son Amnon raped his daughter Tamar and whose other son Avshalom killed Amnon and then rebelled against David himself — is saying that Hashem rewarded him according to his righteousness and the cleanness of his hands? How can the author of this song be the same man whose sin fills the middle chapters of this very book?

The mefarshim handle the difficulty in several ways. Some, like Rashi, suggest that David composed the song before the Batsheva incident — in the early or middle years of his reign when his conscience was not yet burdened by the great sin. Others, like the Radak, suggest that David did teshuvah so completely after the Batsheva incident that he was restored to the status of a righteous man in the divine court, and the song reflects his restored standing rather than his pre-sin standing. Still others, like the Malbim, read the song as a careful articulation of what David had learned across his whole life: that the relationship with Hashem depended on David's consistent turning toward the Divine, on his willingness to confess and do teshuvah when he failed, and on his commitment to the framework of Torah even when he fell short of its ideals. The "righteousness" and "cleanness of hands" that David praises are not perfect innocence; they are the ongoing commitment to the Torah path despite specific failures.

Whichever reading we adopt, the song is making a theological claim. David is saying that the whole arc of his life, from the cave of Adullam to the palace in Yerushalayim, has been a story of Hashem being present in the life of a man who was trying — imperfectly, but sincerely — to walk in the ways of Torah. David is not claiming to have been flawless. He is claiming that the flaws were set against a background of overall faithfulness, and that the overall faithfulness was what Hashem had responded to.

The song ends — in its fifty-first and final verse — with a line that stretches forward into the future:

"He is a tower of deliverance for His king, and showeth loving-kindness to His anointed, to David and to his seed, for evermore."

Le-David ule-zar'o ad olam.

"To David and to his seed, forever."

The "seed" is the Davidic dynasty. The song is closing by invoking the promise made to David through Natan the prophet in chapter 7 of Shmuel Bet — the promise that David's descendants would sit on the throne of Israel forever, and that the ultimate redemption of the people would come through a descendant of the house of David. The song is David's way of acknowledging that his own personal deliverance is only the first stage of a much larger deliverance that will extend through his descendants into the messianic future. He is not singing only about himself. He is singing about the whole dynasty that Hashem has promised, and about the Mashiach who will come from his line at the end of days.

This is why the song is placed here, at the closing of Shmuel Bet, rather than in the middle of the book at the moment of a specific deliverance. It is placed at the end because it is summary. It is David stepping back from his whole life and summarizing it as a single coherent arc of divine deliverance that stretches from his youth to the end of time. The song is not a response to a single event. It is a response to the pattern of a whole existence.

## The Last Words of David

Seven verses after the song ends, the Tanach pivots again. Chapter 23 opens with a new formula:

"And these are the last words of David: The utterance of David the son of Yishai, and the utterance of the man raised on high, the anointed of the God of Ya'akov, and the sweet singer of Israel..."

Ne'im zemirot Yisrael.

"The sweet singer of Israel."

This phrase has become one of the most beloved epithets for David in the entire Jewish tradition. It is how the rabbinic literature refers to him whenever his poetry or his music is being discussed. It is the phrase that justifies the tradition's attribution of the book of Tehillim to David as its primary author. It is the Tanach's own designation of David as the paradigmatic singer of Israel — not just a king who happened to write some songs, but the definitive poet of the covenant between Hashem and the Jewish people.

And the phrase appears here — in the opening line of his "last words" — because the Tanach wants us to understand that when we read David's psalms, we are reading the voice of a man who was, among his many other roles, the designated musician of the national soul. His songs were not a hobby. They were not a minor skill. They were the deepest expression of who he was. The young shepherd who had played the lyre in his father's field to calm Shaul's troubled spirit had grown into the aging king who was still writing poetry on his deathbed, and the Tanach wants us to hold those two images together — the boy with the lyre and the old man dictating his last song — as the two ends of a single continuous musical life.

The last words themselves are dense and oracular. They are not a simple poem. They are a compressed theological statement about the kind of king David believed he had been and the kind of king his descendants ought to be.

"One who rules over men must be righteous, ruling in the fear of God. And he shall be as the light of the morning, when the sun rises, a morning without clouds, when through clear shining after rain the tender grass springs out of the earth."

Moshel ba-adam tzaddik, moshel yirat Elohim.

"One who rules over men, a righteous one, ruling in the fear of God."

This is David's theological summary of the nature of kingship. A king is not a man who rules by raw power. A king is not a man who rules by birthright alone. A king is a man who rules tzaddik — righteously — and yirat Elohim — in the fear of God. The two phrases are the essential qualifications. Righteousness means integrity in the relationship with other human beings: justice, compassion, honesty, fairness in judgment. Fear of God means integrity in the relationship with the Divine: humility, awareness of accountability, recognition that the king is himself a servant of a higher King. David is saying that without these two things, a king is not really a king; he is a usurper, a man who is occupying the throne but not fulfilling the office.

The image that follows is striking: the righteous king is like the light of the morning, when the sun rises, a morning without clouds, when through clear shining after rain the tender grass springs out of the earth. A good king brings light to his people. A good king brings the rain that causes the grass to grow. A good king is a force of nourishment and clarity in the life of his country — a force as welcome as the first dawn after a long dark night, as life-giving as the first gentle rain after a long drought. David is not describing himself in this image. He is describing an ideal. He is telling his successors (and perhaps, implicitly, the Mashiach who will eventually come from his line) what they should aspire to be.

And then he turns to his own relationship with this ideal:

"For is not my house so with God? For He has made with me an everlasting covenant, ordered in all things and sure; for all my salvation and all my desire — will He not make it grow?"

Ki lo-khen beiti im El.

"For is not my house so with God?"

The verse is usually read as a rhetorical question. David is asking whether his own house — his own dynasty — is the kind of house that has an everlasting covenant with God. The answer is implicitly yes: the covenant of chapter 7 of Shmuel Bet, the promise to David that his descendants would sit on the throne forever, is the basis for David's hope. The covenant is arukhah ba-kol u-shemurah — "ordered in all things and sure" — meaning that it has been laid out in full, that it does not depend on future conditions, that it is a completed promise that will unfold in its own time. David is saying: whatever happens to me personally, whatever my personal failings have been, the covenant with my house stands. The dynasty will continue. The Mashiach will come. The house of David is secured by the word of Hashem that will not be broken.

The honesty of this statement — the David who could have bragged about his military victories or his famous deeds, but who instead simply acknowledges the covenant that holds the whole thing in place — is one of the most moving things about the "last words." David is saying that the whole meaning of his life is not what he did but what Hashem promised. The deliverance was from Hashem. The deliverance was for David. The covenant was the reason. The covenant would continue.

And the final image of the "last words" is darker:

"But the ungodly are all of them as thorns thrust away, because they cannot be taken with the hand; but the man that touches them must be armed with iron and the shaft of a spear — and they shall be utterly burned with fire in the same place."

David ends his oracle with an image of the enemies of the kingdom being thrown aside like thorns, because thorns cannot be picked up with a bare hand — the man who deals with them must be armed with iron and a spear, and the thorns will eventually be burned where they lie. The image is not a threat. It is a recognition. David is telling his successors that the enemies of the kingdom will always be there, that they cannot be handled gently, that they will require iron and the spear to be cleared away, and that the final end of the enemies will be fire. The kingdom will continue, but it will continue through continuous struggle. It will not be a kingdom at rest. It will be a kingdom that has to keep removing the thorns generation after generation.

The last word of the poem — the last word of the "last words of David" — is ba-shavet, "in the place where they sit." The thorns will be burned where they are. The enemies of the kingdom will be destroyed not by being chased across the earth but by being burned in the specific places where they have taken root. The kingdom will not exhaust itself in pursuit. It will simply keep turning over the ground.

## A moment of reflection

Before we return to the narrative and begin the story of the census in the next chapter, it is worth sitting for a moment with what David has just said in these two poems.

David is an old man. He has been through everything. He has been a shepherd boy, a harp player in the court of a mad king, a fugitive in caves, a commander of a band of outlaws, a mercenary general in the service of a Pelishti lord, a regional king in Chevron, the king of all Israel in Yerushalayim, a conqueror, a builder of a capital, the subject of an eternal covenant, the adulterer with Batsheva, the murderer of Uriah, the penitent of "against You alone have I sinned," the father of the murdered Amnon, the father of the rebel Avshalom, the fleeing king who crossed the Yarden and returned, the old king who could no longer go out to battle, and now — in these two poems — the aging poet who is trying to make sense of the whole thing in his own voice one last time.

And what he says is this.

He says the whole thing was a story of divine deliverance. He says Hashem was the rock and the fortress and the deliverer. He says the deliverance was not an accident. He says there was an underlying righteousness to his life, even with the specific failures, and that the righteousness was what Hashem had responded to. He says the kind of king he had tried to be was the kind of king who rules in the fear of God, and the image he used for that kind of king was the image of the morning sun after rain. He says the covenant with his house is eternal. He says his dynasty will continue. He says the enemies will be burned where they sit.

That is what David thought about himself at the end of his life.

We are allowed to disagree with him. We are allowed to say that he was being generous to himself, that the Batsheva crisis and the death of Uriah were not just "failures" against a background of righteousness but something more serious than that, that the tragedies of his sons were the natural consequences of his own parental failures, that the kingdom he passed on to his son Shlomo was more fragile than his song makes it sound. The mefarshim have said all of these things in various forms. There has never been a single settled rabbinic reading of David's self-assessment in these poems.

But we are also allowed to accept David's reading on its own terms. We are allowed to say that a man at the end of his life has the right to summarize his life in his own words, and that his summary does not have to be an objective report — it can be a statement of what the life meant to him, from the inside, looking back. David, at the end, understood his life as a story of divine deliverance. He understood himself as a flawed but essentially faithful servant of Hashem. He understood his dynasty as eternal. He understood his poetry as the offering he had made to the tradition he belonged to. The "sweet singer of Israel" was, at the end, a man who sang himself to his grave in the voice of gratitude.

## The pivot to the census

And then — immediately after the "last words" — the Tanach pivots again.

Chapter 23 continues with the register of the Gibborim (which we covered in all of Volume VI), and then chapter 24 opens with a sentence that is, in its own way, as devastating as any sentence in the book:

"And again the anger of Hashem was kindled against Israel, and He moved David against them, saying: Go, number Israel and Yehuda."

The anger of Hashem was kindled again. The "again" refers back to the anger that had caused the three-year famine in chapter 21. The kingdom had just been delivered from one divine judgment. The kingdom is now about to face another. The poetry of chapters 22 and 23:1–7 has given us a moment of peace — a moment in which David is an old man summarizing his life in songs of deliverance — and then the narrative picks up where it left off in chapter 21 and plunges the kingdom into a new crisis.

This is why the mefarshim read the "last words" as a framing device rather than as the literal last things David ever said. They are the last words of David's song. They are the end of his poetic self-reflection. But they are not the last things he did or said in the book. After the song and the last words, there is one more major story to tell — the story of the census and the threshing floor — and that story is the climax of the whole book of Shmuel. The poetry sits in the middle of the appendix because the Tanach wants us to hear David's voice once before the final crisis hits. We hear him summarize his life. We hear him speak about the covenant. And then the narrative begins again, and the final crisis begins, and the book moves toward its climax at the threshing floor of Aravnah the Yevusi.

The next chapter is where the final crisis begins.
