Chapter Four

The Census

The opening verse of Shmuel Bet 24 is one of the strangest verses in the entire book.

"And again the anger of Hashem was kindled against Israel, and He moved David against them, saying: Go, number Israel and Yehuda."

Read it carefully. The verse says that Hashem was angry at Israel and that Hashem moved David — vayaset David — to number the people. The Hebrew verb vayaset literally means "and He incited." Hashem incited David to conduct a census. And the census will then turn out to be a sin for which seventy thousand people will die in a plague.

There is something very difficult happening here. Hashem is said to have been angry at Israel, and then Hashem is said to have moved the king to commit a sinful act, and the sinful act becomes the occasion for the punishment that Hashem's anger had already been preparing. The whole sequence reads as if the sin was engineered by Hashem for the purpose of providing a pretext for the punishment. This is the kind of verse that makes careful readers stop and re-read and wonder whether they are reading it correctly.

And the difficulty becomes more acute when we compare Shmuel Bet 24:1 with its parallel in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 21:1:

"And Satan stood up against Israel, and incited David to number Israel."

Satan.

In the Divrei HaYamim version, the one who incited David was not Hashem but Satan — the accusing angel who appears in the early chapters of Iyov and a few other places in the Tanach as a heavenly adversary whose role is to test and to accuse. The Divrei HaYamim version reads the incident as a classic yetzer hara story: Satan came to David, suggested the census, David yielded to the suggestion, the sin was committed, the plague followed. Hashem is not the initiator of the sin in Divrei HaYamim. Hashem is the one whose anger is aroused by the sin after Satan has engineered it.

The two versions are sharply different. One says Hashem incited the king. The other says Satan incited the king. The mefarshim have worked hard for centuries to reconcile the two, and their attempts fall into several categories.

The simplest approach — taken by the Radak and others — is to say that the two versions are describing the same event from two different angles. In Shmuel Bet, the Tanach is describing the event from the perspective of the ultimate source: everything that happens, including the work of Satan, is ultimately permitted by Hashem, and therefore the Tanach in Shmuel Bet simply attributes the inciting to Hashem as the First Cause. In Divrei HaYamim, the Tanach is describing the event from the perspective of the proximate source: the immediate inciter was Satan, the heavenly adversary whose job is to tempt kings and test their judgment. Both descriptions are true. Shmuel Bet is telling us what was happening at the level of the divine plan. Divrei HaYamim is telling us what was happening at the level of the actual experience of the king. The two versions are complementary rather than contradictory.

The Rambam (in the Guide for the Perplexed, III:22) takes a related but slightly different approach. He reads ha-satan in Divrei HaYamim not as a personal adversarial angel but as a general principle of adversity and testing in the world. The "Satan" that incited David was the ordinary temptation that comes to any king whose kingdom is successful and whose armies are victorious: the temptation to count what he has achieved, to measure the size of his power, to take pride in the numerical scale of his accomplishments. This is a temptation that any successful ruler would experience. The Tanach in Divrei HaYamim personifies the temptation as "Satan." The Tanach in Shmuel Bet says that the temptation arose because Hashem's anger was kindled against Israel and Hashem wanted an occasion to correct the people. Both descriptions are pointing at the same underlying phenomenon: a king's susceptibility to pride, activated at a particular moment for a particular purpose.

A third approach — taken by the Abarbanel and picked up by several modern Orthodox commentators — is to distinguish between the desire to take the census (which Hashem may have planted) and the manner in which David took it (which was David's own sinful choice). Hashem, in this reading, wanted a census to happen; Hashem's anger at Israel required some kind of national accounting. But the way David conducted the census was David's own failure. He forgot the half-shekel rule from Shemot 30:12. He counted the people as if they belonged to him rather than to Hashem. He failed to use the indirect counting methods that would have protected the people from the plague. The sin was not the census itself — Hashem had apparently wanted one — but David's mishandling of it. And that is why the punishment came: not because David did something Hashem had not wanted, but because David did something Hashem had wanted in a way that Hashem had not wanted.

All three approaches are preserved in the classical tradition. None of them is definitive. The difficulty of the opening verse is a difficulty the mefarshim treat as a feature, not a bug — an invitation to think carefully about the relationship between divine initiative and human responsibility.

* * *

But whatever the source of the incitement, David gave the order.

The scene is preserved in the Tanach with a particular detail that has become the center of the rabbinic discussion of this chapter. David called for Yoav.

"Yoav."

"My lord."

"I want you to take a census of the fighting men of Israel and Yehuda. Go through all the tribes, from Dan in the north to Be'er Sheva in the south, and count every man who is capable of drawing the sword. Report the numbers to me."

Yoav stared at him.

"My lord — why?"

"I want to know how many men are under arms in the kingdom."

"We already know. We have rosters. We have the tribal levies. We have the standing army. We have the reserves. Every commander keeps his own count. The numbers are available to you whenever you want them. I can bring you the aggregate tomorrow morning if that is what you need."

"I want a fresh count."

"A fresh count of what? The numbers have not changed significantly since the last time we checked. The kingdom is not in a state of mobilization. There is no war on the horizon. There is no specific need for a new census right now. My lord — I am asking you this as someone who has been counting your soldiers for decades. Why do you want to redo the work?"

David did not answer immediately.

Yoav pressed on.

"My lord — forgive me for speaking directly, but I have to say this. A census is not a neutral act. A census asserts ownership. A census says these are mine. Hashem has warned us in the Torah about counting Israel: the half-shekel in Shemot is the way the people have to be counted, and the half-shekel is specifically so that no plague should come upon them when you number them. The very command to count them already acknowledges that counting is dangerous. The Torah treats the census as a kind of risk that has to be hedged against. And you want to do this without any specific reason, just to know a number? My lord — I am begging you. Do not do this. Let me bring you the aggregate from the existing rosters. The numbers will not be exact but they will be close enough, and we will not be taking the risk."

"Yoav — I am the king. I want the count. Go and take it."

"My lord — "

"Yoav."

"My lord."

"I am the king. The order is given. Take the count."

Yoav was silent for a long moment. Then he bowed.

"As my lord commands."

He left the room. As he left, the Tanach preserves a detail that has become famous in the rabbinic reading of the chapter: Yoav said something quietly as he walked out. The Hebrew of Shmuel Bet 24:3 records it as Yoav's formal address to the king, but the Divrei HaYamim parallel preserves it in a stronger form: "Why then does my lord require this thing? Why should he be a cause of guilt to Israel?"

Yoav was saying: you are about to cause the entire nation to bear guilt. You are the king, and you are the one ordering the count, but the consequences of the count will fall on the people, not on you alone. Why are you bringing guilt on the nation? What justification could you possibly have?

David gave no justification. He had no justification. The Tanach does not record one. He had wanted the count, and he had given the order, and Yoav was now required to carry it out, and whatever the theological source of the incitement — Hashem in Shmuel Bet, Satan in Divrei HaYamim, the ordinary royal pride of the Rambam's reading, or the Abarbanel's fine distinction between divinely-willed counting and humanly-chosen method — the fact on the ground was that David had ordered a census, and Yoav was going to take it.

* * *

The census took nine months and twenty days. The Tanach preserves this figure with unusual precision: "and when they had gone through all the land, they came to Yerushalayim at the end of nine months and twenty days." Yoav and his officers walked the entire kingdom, tribe by tribe, village by village, counting the men of military age in each place. They began in Gilead on the east side of the Yarden. They moved northward through Dan and the territory of Naftali and the coastal plain. They worked their way down through the tribal territories of the center and the south. They ended — after nearly ten months of walking — back in Yerushalayim where they had started.

While Yoav was walking, David was in the palace. He was thinking about what he had done. He was, the mefarshim tell us, beginning to have doubts. A king who has ordered something in a moment of imperial confidence is a king who, in the quiet of the months that follow, may begin to wonder whether the moment of confidence was actually an illusion — whether he had been doing something he should not have been doing, whether the advisor who had tried to stop him had been right. Yoav had been right. David was starting to realize it. But the census was already in motion, and David could not stop it without admitting that he had been wrong, and for nine months he did not admit it.

On a few occasions during those nine months, Yoav sent reports back to Yerushalayim. He would send a runner with a wax tablet listing the counts from a particular region. David would read the tablet. He would frown. He would put the tablet aside. The numbers were large. They were bigger than David had expected. The men of Israel and Yehuda were numerous. The fighting force of the kingdom was substantial. A king looking at those numbers should have felt pride — the kind of pride that had been the underlying motivation for the whole census — but David did not feel pride anymore. He felt a growing unease. The numbers on the tablets were beginning to feel like evidence against him. This is what you wanted to know? This is what was so important that you had to bring guilt on the whole nation to find out? He put the tablets in a drawer and tried not to think about them.

Yoav, the Gemara in Berachot tells us, deliberately refused to count two tribes — Levi and Binyamin. He skipped them. He reported back to David at the end of the census that those two tribes had not been counted. The Tanach preserves the detail explicitly in the parallel in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 21:6: "But Levi and Binyamin he did not count among them, for the king's word was abominable to Yoav."

Why these two tribes? The mefarshim explain. Levi was the priestly tribe, and the Torah in Bamidbar 1:49 explicitly exempts the Levites from ordinary military censuses. Yoav was on solid ground in skipping Levi. Binyamin was a more difficult case. The mefarshim suggest that Yoav skipped Binyamin because Binyamin was the tribe of Shaul, and a census of the tribe of Shaul at this particular moment would have been especially politically sensitive; or because Binyamin was small and Yoav wanted to protect it; or because Yoav was already expecting a plague and wanted to minimize the number of tribes that would be exposed to it; or because he was simply running out of time and energy at the end of the ten-month project and cut the last tribe off the list.

Whatever the reason, the two tribes were skipped. This is the Tanach's way of telling us that Yoav — the rough, pragmatic, morally complicated general who had killed Avner and Avshalom and Amasa — was on this one issue the voice of restraint. Yoav, of all people, was the one who protested the census. Yoav, of all people, was the one who refused to complete it. The Tanach preserves the moral irony deliberately. The king who should have known better did not know better. The general who had been a source of David's frustration for forty years was the one who tried to protect him from his own mistake.

* * *

The numbers came back on the day Yoav returned to Yerushalayim.

The Tanach records them: eight hundred thousand valiant men that drew the sword in Israel, and five hundred thousand men in Yehuda. One million three hundred thousand fighting men in the kingdom.

(The parallel in Divrei HaYamim gives slightly different numbers — one million one hundred thousand in Israel and four hundred seventy thousand in Yehuda, totaling one million five hundred seventy thousand. The discrepancy between the two versions is another of the classic text-critical difficulties of these two books, and the mefarshim discuss several reasons for the difference. The simplest reading is that the two texts preserve different counting conventions or include different categories of men in their totals.)

Yoav came to the palace. He stood in front of David and handed him the final wax tablet with the totals.

"My lord. The count is complete. One million three hundred thousand men. Eight hundred thousand in Israel, five hundred thousand in Yehuda. I did not count Levi or Binyamin."

"Why not?"

"Levi is exempt by Torah command. Binyamin I refused to count. I was hoping you would not pursue the question."

"Yoav."

"My lord?"

"I should have listened to you. Nine months ago, when you tried to warn me. I should have listened."

Yoav did not say anything.

"I was being prideful. That is the only honest thing I can say about it. I wanted to know the size of my kingdom. I wanted the number for its own sake. There was no military need. There was no administrative requirement. I wanted the count because I wanted to feel the weight of what I had built over the course of my reign. And I have the count now, and the weight of it does not feel like what I thought it would feel like. The numbers are large. They should make me feel strong. Instead I feel sick."

"My lord — "

"You were right, Yoav. You were right to warn me and I was wrong to ignore you. I have brought guilt on the nation. I do not know yet what the consequence is going to be. But I know there is going to be a consequence. The Torah is clear that you cannot count the people without the half-shekel ransom and without expecting a plague to follow. I skipped the ransom. I did not think it applied to me. I thought the rule in Shemot was for Moshe in the wilderness and did not extend to my kingdom. I was wrong. The rule extends. And I did not collect the half-shekels. And there is going to be a plague."

Yoav was silent.

"Take the tablets away. Put them somewhere. I do not want to look at them anymore."

Yoav gathered the wax tablets from the table and left the room. He took them to the chief of the archives and had them placed in a wooden box in the back of the archive room. The numbers would be preserved in the official records of the kingdom, because the Tanach would later preserve them, but David would never look at them again.

* * *

That night, David did not sleep.

He sat in the upper chamber of the palace. He was an old man. His hair was white. His hands were thin. He had been king for nearly forty years. He had ordered a census that he had no good reason to order. He had ignored a warning from his chief general that he should have heeded. He had forgotten a Torah command that he should have remembered. He had brought guilt on his people. And he did not yet know what the punishment was going to be.

He thought about the Batsheva incident. The parallel was unmistakable. In both cases, he had given in to a moment of imperial confidence and had done something that a lower-ranking man would have been held fully accountable for. In both cases, a prophet (or a general) had warned him or rebuked him. In both cases, the consequences had fallen not only on him personally but on his whole house and on his whole kingdom. In both cases, innocent people were going to suffer for what he had done alone.

The difference, this time, was that he had seen the shape of the sin before the prophet arrived. He knew what he had done. He did not need Natan to tell him you are the man. He was already saying it to himself.

The Tanach preserves David's interior monologue in a single verse: "And David's heart smote him after that he had numbered the people." Va-yakh lev David oto acharei ken safar et ha-am. "And David's heart struck him after he had counted the people." The Hebrew is striking. His heart struck him. His own conscience acted like a blow to the body. He felt it physically — the sick recognition that he had done a wrong that could not be undone.

And then, still sitting alone in the upper chamber, he prayed. The Tanach preserves his prayer with unusual directness: "And David said to Hashem: I have sinned greatly in what I have done. And now, Hashem, put away, I beseech You, the iniquity of Your servant, for I have done very foolishly."

Chatati me'od asher asiti. Ve-atah Hashem, ha'aver na et avon avdekha ki niskalti me'od.

Read that prayer carefully.

I have sinned greatly in what I have done.

There is no excuse. There is no attempt to shift the blame to Yoav, or to Satan, or to the advisors who had not stopped him. There is no claim that he had meant well. There is no appeal to mitigating circumstances. There is only the raw admission: chatati me'od — I have sinned greatly. And then the request: put away the iniquity of Your servant. And then the diagnosis: ki niskalti me'od — for I have done very foolishly.

The word niskalti is important. It does not mean I have been wicked. It does not mean I have rebelled. It means I have been foolish. David is saying that his sin was not a sin of willful rebellion but a sin of stupidity — of failing to think clearly, of letting his pride override his judgment, of doing something he should have known better than to do. It is the honest self-diagnosis of an old man who has just watched himself make a mistake he would have told anyone else not to make. I was foolish. I knew better and I did it anyway.

This is the kind of prayer that the Gemara later quotes as a model of what a real confession looks like. David is not performing repentance. He is simply saying what is true. The confession is made in the middle of the night, in an empty chamber, with no audience except Hashem and the wax tablets of the census still sitting in the archive room down the hall. There is no one to perform for. There is only the old king and the recognition of what he has done.

And he is asking Hashem to remove the iniquity. He is not asking to avoid the consequences. He is asking for forgiveness. The consequences, he already knows, are going to come. The punishment is going to arrive by some form and through some agent. But the forgiveness is what he needs for his own soul. He is not a man trying to escape a plague. He is a man trying to be in right relationship with the God he has wronged. The plague will do whatever the plague is going to do. The forgiveness is the thing that matters.

* * *

The Tanach then pivots, immediately, to the next scene.

"And when David rose up in the morning, the word of Hashem came unto the prophet Gad, David's seer, saying: Go and speak unto David: Thus says Hashem..."

Gad the prophet is being sent.

The name should be familiar. Gad had been with David since the wilderness years, from the time of the cave of Adullam. He had been the prophet attached to the band of six hundred during the years of flight from Shaul. He had advised David on strategic matters throughout the reign. He had been a background figure in most of our volumes — mentioned occasionally, never in the foreground. But now, at the end of David's life, Gad is being given the most difficult task a prophet can be given. He is being sent to the king to deliver a message from Hashem that will require David to choose among three terrible options.

The next chapter of this volume is about the three choices, and about which one David picked, and about the consequence.
