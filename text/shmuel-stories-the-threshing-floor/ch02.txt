Chapter Two

Seven Sons and Ritzpah bat Ayah

The Givoni elders came back on the third day.

They were brought into the same courtyard where they had stood three days earlier. David was waiting. He had cleared his schedule. He had been told by the chief of the household that the Givonim had come prepared with an answer, and he wanted to hear the answer without any distractions.

He stood up when they entered. This was itself unusual — a king does not normally stand for a delegation of elders from a subordinate population — but David wanted to show respect. He wanted the Givonim to feel that they had his full attention and his full acknowledgment of the wrong that had been done to them.

"My fathers. Welcome back. Please speak."

The senior elder stepped forward. He was older than the other elders — perhaps seventy, with white hair and a slight stoop. He had been alive, barely, when Nov had been destroyed. He had lost most of his own family in the collapse of the community.

"My lord the king. We have conferred among ourselves. The decision was not easy. But we have come to an answer. Before we tell you the answer, we want you to know that we did not choose this lightly. We argued for three days. Some of our elders wanted to ask for silver. Some wanted land. Some wanted the king to rebuild a specific village that had been destroyed. All of these were reasonable. But in the end, we could not accept any of them. None of them felt like a real answer to what had been done to us. We would have taken the silver and gone home, and the matter would not have been closed. The matter would still have been hanging in the air."

"Speak, my father. Tell me what you want."

The elder took a breath.

"Ein lanu kesef ve-zahav im Shaul ve-im beito, ve-ein lanu ish le-hamit be-Yisrael."

"We have no claim for silver and gold against Shaul and against his house, and we have no standing to put any man in Israel to death."

David listened carefully. The formulation was strange. The elder was saying that the Givonim did not want money and did not have the legal authority to execute an Israelite on their own. Both of these statements were technically true. Money would not repair the harm; the Givonim did not have the standing of a tribe of Israel and could not carry out a capital sentence under ordinary law. But why was the elder laying out these two negative statements? Where was he going with them?

The elder continued.

"Ha-ish asher killanu va-asher dimah lanu nishmadnu me-hityatzev be-khol gevul Yisrael — yutan lanu shiv'ah anashim mi-banav, ve-hoka'anum la-Hashem be-Giv'at Shaul bechir Hashem."

"The man who consumed us and who plotted against us, so that we should have been destroyed from holding any ground within the borders of Israel — let there be given to us seven men from his sons, and we will hang them before Hashem in Giv'at Shaul, the chosen of Hashem."

* * *

The courtyard went silent.

David did not move. He had not been expecting this. He had been expecting silver, or land, or a public monument, or a rebuilt village, or the restoration of the Givonim to their ancestral role in the service of the Mishkan. Any of those requests would have been acceptable, and he would have granted any of them immediately. He had not been expecting the Givonim to ask for blood.

And yet the request was technically within the framework of the ancient law of blood-vengeance. The Torah recognizes that unavenged blood cries out from the ground. The Torah recognizes that the only way to cleanse the land of spilled blood is through the blood of the one who spilled it (or, in the case of manslaughter, through the exile of the killer to a city of refuge). The Torah recognizes that the go'el ha-dam, the blood-avenger, has a legitimate role in the process of justice. The Givonim were, in effect, invoking their rights as blood-avengers against Shaul — and because Shaul was dead, they were extending their claim to his male descendants, on the principle that the house of a dead killer inherits the killer's liability.

But it was still shocking. The Torah also has strong protections against children being put to death for the sins of their fathers (lo yumtu avot al banim ve-vanim lo yumtu al avot — "fathers shall not be put to death for children, nor children for fathers"). The general rule is that each person dies for his own sin. The Givonim's request seemed to violate this rule. They were asking for seven of Shaul's descendants — not because the descendants had personally harmed the Givonim, but because the descendants were the surviving representatives of Shaul's house, and the blood-debt of Shaul was being collected from the house that had inherited his line.

The mefarshim have wrestled with this for centuries. Several of them — including the Gemara in Yevamot 79a — argue that David's acceptance of the Givonim's demand was theologically justified precisely because the ordinary rule against children dying for fathers' sins can be set aside in extraordinary circumstances, when a major kiddush Hashem (sanctification of the divine name) requires it. The Gemara in Yevamot reads the incident as one of those extraordinary circumstances. David, the Gemara says, reasoned as follows: if I refuse the Givonim's demand and the famine continues, the entire kingdom will starve and thousands of innocent Israelites will die. If I grant the demand, seven members of Shaul's house will die. Seven deaths now, for many more deaths averted. The arithmetic alone would justify the choice. But beyond the arithmetic, there is also the principle that kiddush Hashem trumps the ordinary protections of law: when the nations of the world see that even the Israelite king enforces a four-hundred-year-old oath to a group of converts at the cost of his own predecessor's descendants, the nations will understand that the word of Israel is an iron thing, and many of them — the Gemara says, more on this later — will be moved by the demonstration to convert to Judaism themselves.

David reasoned this out, the mefarshim tell us, in the moment between the Givoni elder's request and his own reply. He did not have much time to think. The elder was still standing in front of him, waiting for an answer. The other elders were watching. The guards at the edge of the courtyard were watching. David had to decide whether to grant the request or to refuse it, and the decision had to be made in the next breath.

He made the decision.

"I grant it."

He said it quietly. The word he used was ani eten — "I will give." I will give you seven men from the sons of Shaul, and you will hang them before Hashem as you have asked. The phrase is preserved in the Tanach as the formal legal answer of the king to the demand of a petitioner. The petitioner asks. The king grants or refuses. In this case, David granted.

The senior elder bowed.

"My lord. We thank you."

"Do not thank me. I am doing what the Urim has told me to do. I am not giving you the seven men because I agree with your choice. I am giving them because the land requires it and the oath of Yehoshua requires it. If I had any other way to end the famine, I would use the other way. I do not have another way. Go back to your people. Prepare the place in Giv'at Shaul where the hangings will take place. I will send the seven men to you within the week. I will need to determine which seven. There are restrictions I have to honor first. Do not move ahead of my messengers."

"Yes, my lord."

The Givonim left the courtyard. David sat back down on the low chair. He was alone for the next several hours, turning over in his mind the terrible task he had just accepted.

* * *

The first restriction was the house of Yonatan.

David had sworn an oath to Yonatan, many years earlier, in the wilderness years. The oath is preserved in Shmuel Aleph 20. When David and Yonatan had met secretly in the field outside Shaul's palace, with Yonatan shooting arrows past a servant boy as a coded signal, the two of them had sworn a covenant with each other in which each promised that if one of them died, the other would show kindness to his descendants forever. The specific words Yonatan had spoken were: "And you shall show me the kindness of Hashem while I am alive, that I may not die; and do not cut off your kindness from my house forever." David had sworn back. The oath had been sworn between the two of them in the name of Hashem.

The relevant descendant of Yonatan who was still alive was Mefiboshet — the lame son of Yonatan, whom we had encountered in Volumes I and IV of this series. Mefiboshet had been five years old when his father had died on Mount Gilboa. His nurse, in her panic, had dropped him while running away, and his legs had never healed. He had grown up in hiding and had been found by David many years later and brought to the royal table at Yerushalayim as a permanent honored guest. He was the only male descendant of Yonatan still alive.

And he was also a potential target for the Givonim's demand.

David could not allow this. The oath to Yonatan took absolute precedence. Even if the Urim ve-Tumim had told him to give Mefiboshet to the Givonim, he would have refused. An oath sworn in the name of Hashem to a living person in whom the oath would eventually come to rest — that oath cannot be broken even under divine command. This is the halachic principle the mefarshim extract from David's decision to spare Mefiboshet. David could give the Givonim seven men from the house of Shaul, but Mefiboshet was not one of them. The oath with Yonatan ruled him out absolutely.

The Tanach puts it this way in Shmuel Bet 21:7: "And the king spared Mefiboshet the son of Yonatan the son of Shaul, because of the oath of Hashem that was between them — between David and Yonatan the son of Shaul." The verse is extraordinary in its compression. It preserves the fact of the sparing, the identity of the spared person, and the legal ground for the sparing, all in a single sentence. The reader who does not know the backstory of Shmuel Aleph 20 will miss the full weight of the verse. But for the reader who has read Shmuel Aleph, the verse is charged with the memory of Yonatan — the friend David had lost on Mount Gilboa, the man whose body he had seen on the walls of Beit She'an after the Pelishtim had hung it there, the man whose bones had been recovered by the men of Yavesh Gilad and buried in their own territory in the eastern side of the Yarden. Yonatan had been dead for decades. But the oath David had sworn to him was still binding. And the oath extended to Mefiboshet.

* * *

The other restriction was more mechanical. David had to choose which seven. There were more than seven male descendants of Shaul still alive — sons of Shaul's concubines, grandsons through the various lines, a handful of more distant male relatives. The Gemara in Yevamot 79a discusses the question of how David chose. The answer preserved in Rav Huna's name is stark: David had the descendants of Shaul pass one by one in front of the Aron, and the Aron itself indicated which seven would be selected. The selection was not made by human judgment. It was made by a direct oracular sign from the Aron — a flash of light, a movement, some visible indication that singled out a particular man.

The method was used because David did not want to take the selection on his own authority. He did not want to be accused, later, of having used the Givonim's demand as an excuse to eliminate particular political rivals from the house of Shaul. By having the Aron choose, he was making the selection theologically transparent: the men who are going to die are the men whom Hashem has designated, not the men I would have chosen if I had been choosing on my own. The Aron selected seven men. David accepted the selection.

Two of the seven were the sons of Ritzpah bat Ayah, one of Shaul's concubines. Their names were Armoni and Mefiboshet. Yes — Mefiboshet. This was a different Mefiboshet from the son of Yonatan. The name was common in the house of Shaul, apparently; there was the Mefiboshet who was the son of Yonatan and who was spared, and there was another Mefiboshet who was a son of Ritzpah and who was selected. The Tanach preserves both names without confusing them, but it is a point of potential confusion for readers.

The other five were described as the five sons of Michal the daughter of Shaul whom she bore to Adriel the son of Barzillai the Mecholati. This formulation is puzzling on its face. Michal was David's first wife. Michal did not have any children with Adriel. Adriel had been married to Merav, Shaul's older daughter — not Michal. The parallel in Shmuel Aleph 18:19 is explicit: "it came to pass at the time when Merav Shaul's daughter should have been given to David, that she was given unto Adriel the Mecholati to wife." Merav, not Michal, had married Adriel. How, then, does Shmuel Bet 21:8 say that Michal bore these five sons to Adriel?

The mefarshim (Rashi, following the Gemara in Sanhedrin 19b) resolve the difficulty by explaining that Merav — the biological mother — had died when the five sons were young, and Michal — Merav's younger sister — had raised them as her own. The Tanach therefore attributes the sons to Michal on the principle that one who raises another's child is credited as if they had borne the child themselves. It is a beautiful halachic principle and the mefarshim find it in multiple places in the Tanach. Michal had no biological children of her own — the Tanach notes elsewhere that Michal had no child until the day of her death — and she raised her sister's orphaned boys instead. Her name ended up attached to them in the official records of the house of Shaul because she had been their mother in every practical sense.

All five of these sons of Merav-raised-by-Michal were selected by the Aron. Together with the two sons of Ritzpah (Armoni and the other Mefiboshet), they made up the seven men who were to be handed over to the Givonim.

* * *

The hangings took place at Giv'at Shaul — Shaul's ancestral town in Binyamin, in the hills a few miles north of Yerushalayim. The Givonim carried them out. The Tanach is spare in its description. It says only: "And the seven fell together, and they were put to death in the days of the harvest, in the first days, at the beginning of the barley harvest."

The beginning of the barley harvest. In the agricultural calendar of the land of Israel, the barley harvest begins in late Nisan — mid-spring, shortly after Pesach. The harvest runs for several weeks. It is a time of plenty. It is a time when the fields are full of workers and the threshing floors are piled high and the countryside is full of activity. The Givonim had deliberately asked for the hangings to take place at the beginning of the harvest season because they wanted the public impact of the hangings to be as great as possible. Every farmer coming to his field for the first day of reaping would see the seven bodies hanging on the hill of Giv'at Shaul, and the story of what had been done would spread across the country in a matter of days. The Givonim wanted the whole country to know that their grievance had finally been answered.

Imagine the morning of the hangings.

The seven men — two sons of Ritzpah, five sons of Merav-raised-by-Michal — were brought out of the royal prison before dawn. They had been held there since the day the Aron had selected them a week earlier. They knew what was coming. They had been allowed to speak briefly with family members during the week. They had made their peace as best they could.

The youngest of the five sons of Merav, a boy of perhaps nineteen, walked beside Armoni, the elder son of Ritzpah. They were cousins. They had grown up together in the royal compound at Giv'at Shaul during the years when Shaul was still alive.

"Armoni."

"Yes."

"I am afraid."

"I know. So am I."

"Will the Givonim make it quick?"

"I hope so. I do not know. They are not soldiers. They may not know how to do this well. We will have to help them if we can."

"Help them?"

"By being still. By not struggling. By making it easy for them to do the thing correctly the first time. If we fight, they will make mistakes, and the mistakes will make it worse for us. Stand still when they put the rope around you. Look at the sky. Think about your mother. Be done quickly."

The younger cousin nodded. He did not say anything else.

They reached the hill at Giv'at Shaul. The Givonim were waiting. Seven wooden frames had been set up in a row along the crest of the hill, facing east so that the bodies would be visible from the road that ran below. The Givoni elders were there. A small crowd of Israelite witnesses had gathered at the foot of the hill — farmers from the nearby villages who had heard that something was going to happen and had come to see.

The senior Givoni elder stepped forward.

"Sons of Shaul. You are being handed over by David the king of Israel for the blood-debt of your father's house. Do you understand why?"

Armoni answered for the seven.

"We understand."

"Do you have any last words?"

"Yes. I want to say that we did not do what our grandfather Shaul did to you. We were not even born when Nov was destroyed. But we understand that the debt of a father falls on the house of the father, and we accept that we are paying it on his behalf. We do not hold a grievance against you. We do not hold a grievance against David. We hold a grievance against our grandfather, if we hold a grievance at all. Do what you need to do."

The elder nodded.

"Your words will be remembered. The Givonim are not a cruel people. We did not want this. But we needed an answer to the blood. You are the answer. I am sorry it fell on you."

"Do it quickly, my father. That is the only kindness we ask."

"I will."

The Givonim led the seven men to the seven frames. The hangings were carried out in silence. The Tanach is spare in its description: "And the seven fell together." The Gemara reads this phrase — va-yiplu shivatam yachad, "the seven fell together" — as indicating that the hangings were all completed in a single moment, so that no one of the seven had to watch any of the others die first. The Givonim, rough as they were, granted the kindness Armoni had asked for. They did it quickly. They did it together. The seven fell at the same instant.

Seven bodies. Hanging on wooden frames on a hill in Binyamin. The first day of the barley harvest.

And then Ritzpah bat Ayah did what no one had expected.

* * *

Ritzpah was the mother of two of the seven men. Armoni and Mefiboshet the son of Ritzpah — these had been her only children. She had been a concubine of Shaul in his later years. After Shaul's death she had been taken briefly by Avner as part of the dispute between Avner and Ish-boshet (a story we told in Volume I), and she had lived in obscurity since then. She had no other family. She had no political protector. She was, by the standards of the kingdom, an old woman with no place of any importance in the social order.

But her two sons had just been hanged.

The Tanach records what happened next in a sentence that has become one of the most quietly devastating sentences in all of Shmuel Bet:

Va-tikach Ritzpah vat Ayah et ha-sak, va-tatehu lah el ha-tzur, mi-techilat katzir ad nitakh mayim aleihem min ha-shamayim, ve-lo natnah of ha-shamayim lanu'ach aleihem yomam, ve-et chayat ha-sadeh laylah.

"And Ritzpah the daughter of Ayah took sackcloth, and spread it out for herself upon the rock, from the beginning of the harvest until water was poured upon them from the heavens; and she did not let the birds of the air rest on them by day, nor the beasts of the field by night."

Read that carefully.

From the beginning of the harvest. The barley harvest begins in mid-spring. The rains of the fall season begin, in a normal year, in the month of Tishrei — late October, early November. Six months. Ritzpah bat Ayah sat on a rock at the foot of the hill of Giv'at Shaul, under a piece of sackcloth stretched out on four sticks for shade, guarding the bodies of her two sons and the five sons of her stepdaughter Michal, for six continuous months. She did not let the birds land on the bodies during the day. She did not let the wild animals approach the bodies at night. She stayed at her post, alone, a grieving mother defending the honor of her dead children against the scavengers of the countryside, from the first days of spring through the hot months of summer and into the beginning of the rains in the fall.

Six months.

Think about what that means physically. Think about the exposure to sun and wind during the summer. Think about the flies. Think about the smell of the decomposing bodies. Think about the lack of shelter beyond the piece of sackcloth. Think about the water she had to carry in every day, the food she had to find or beg for, the loneliness of sitting for six months on a rock in the shadow of seven hanging bodies. Think about the nights — the packs of jackals coming down from the hills at dusk, the owls hunting over the fields, the hyenas that would have been drawn by the smell. Think about the terror of sitting alone in the dark with a stick or a stone in her hand, driving the animals away from the bodies of her sons over and over again, night after night, for something like one hundred and eighty nights in a row.

She did it.

She did it not because anyone was paying her. She did it not because anyone had ordered her to. She did it because the boys were her children, and because in the tradition of Israel the bodies of the dead are sacred — the met mitzvah, the unattended corpse, is owed burial and dignity — and because no one else was going to do it if she did not. The Givonim had carried out the hangings and then had gone back to their villages. The royal household had allowed the bodies to hang as the Givonim had demanded. No one in the kingdom was going to intervene. Ritzpah was alone. And Ritzpah stayed.

Imagine an afternoon in the middle of the vigil. Perhaps in the month of Tammuz, the hottest month of the summer, when the sun over the hills of Binyamin was brutal and the air was full of flies. A young woman from a nearby village — a farmer's wife who had come out to gather firewood — walked past the rock where Ritzpah was sitting under her piece of sackcloth. She stopped.

"Woman."

Ritzpah looked up slowly. She was very tired. Her hands were covered in small cuts from the stones and sticks she had been using to drive the birds away.

"Yes."

"Are you the one they call Ritzpah?"

"I am."

"I have heard the stories in our village. They say you have been here since the beginning of the barley harvest."

"I have."

"How long ago was that?"

"I do not count the days anymore. It was the first days of Nisan. I think we are in Tammuz now. You would know better than I would."

"We are in Tammuz. It has been almost four months."

"Four months. Thank you. I had lost track."

The young woman looked up at the seven bodies on their frames. She looked back down at Ritzpah. She was holding a small waterskin.

"Here. Drink some water. I do not have much, but I have more than you do."

"Thank you, my daughter. I will not take all of it. A few swallows."

Ritzpah drank. The young woman watched her. When Ritzpah handed the skin back, the young woman refilled her cupped hand with water and poured it over Ritzpah's cut fingers to clean them.

"Why are you doing this?"

"Because they are my sons. Two of them, and five of my sister-wife's sons whom I helped to raise. The Givonim hanged them as payment for what Shaul did to the Givonim. I was not consulted. I had no voice in the decision. The king had no other way to end the famine, so he made the trade, and the boys were the trade. I am not angry at the king. I am not angry at the Givonim. I am not angry at anyone. I am only here because the boys need someone to keep the birds and the animals from touching them. The Torah says a hanged man has to come down the same day. My boys were not allowed to come down the same day. They are going to hang here until the rains come, because that is what the king has decided and what the Givonim have demanded. So someone has to be here. Someone has to keep the scavengers off. I am the mother. I am the one who is here."

The young woman was silent for a long moment.

"Will you let me come back tomorrow? I can bring you water and bread. I can sit with you for a few hours so that you can sleep during the day."

"I do not want to sleep during the day. If I sleep during the day, I will not be able to stay awake at night, and the nights are the most dangerous. I stay awake at night because that is when the wild animals come."

"Then I will come at night. I will keep watch with you. One night a week, my daughter. Let me do that much."

Ritzpah looked up at the young woman. Her eyes were wet.

"Yes. Come tomorrow night. Bring a stick. Bring a lamp if you can. The jackals are afraid of lamps."

"I will come."

The young woman walked back toward her village. She would return. And across the six months of the vigil, she would tell other women in other villages, and the women would come too — not in any organized way, not as a formal support network, but as a slow accumulation of individual acts of kindness from women who had heard about the mother on the rock and wanted to help her without knowing how. Some brought food. Some brought water. Some came at night to share the watch. Some came in the early morning to bring clean sackcloth when Ritzpah's own was stained with blood from the birds that had managed to get close to the bodies before she could drive them off. None of them stayed for long. None of them took over the vigil. But they made the vigil possible. Ritzpah could not have done it alone for six months without water and food being brought to her by strangers. The Tanach does not mention these women because the Tanach is focused on Ritzpah herself. But the mefarshim are clear that the vigil could not have happened without the small, unnamed acts of support from the women of the nearby villages, and that those women are part of the meaning of the whole story. Ritzpah did the hard work. But the women of Binyamin kept her alive while she did it.

* * *

The halachic aspect of the six-month vigil is itself extraordinary. The Torah commands, in Devarim 21:22–23, that a body that has been hanged on a tree must be taken down the same day. The verse is explicit: "And if a man has committed a sin worthy of death, and he be put to death, and you hang him on a tree — his body shall not remain all night upon the tree, but you shall in any wise bury him that day, for he that is hanged is a reproach unto God; that your land be not defiled." The principle is clear. A hanged body is not to be left on the tree overnight, let alone for six months. The bodies of the seven sons of Shaul should, by the strict letter of the Torah, have been taken down on the same day they were hanged.

Why, then, did David allow them to remain hanging for six months?

The Gemara in Yevamot 79a addresses this exact question. The Gemara's answer is that this was an extraordinary case in which the ordinary halachah was suspended for the sake of a greater kiddush Hashem. The famine had been caused by a four-hundred-year-old oath being broken; the restoration of the famine required a public demonstration of the seriousness with which Israel took its oaths to converts. If the bodies had been taken down on the same day as the hanging, the public impact would have been minimal. The story would have traveled slowly through the countryside. Many people would never have heard about it. But with the bodies hanging visibly on the hill of Giv'at Shaul for six continuous months, the story became the most talked-about event in the entire land. Every farmer, every traveler, every visiting merchant, every ambassador from a neighboring kingdom, heard the story of the seven sons of Shaul and the grieving mother on the rock. The story moved across the region. It reached the neighboring Canaanite peoples. It reached the Pelishtim. It reached all the way to distant nations.

And — the Gemara says, in a line attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadok — when the nations of the world saw what Israel had done to appease the converts, one hundred and fifty thousand non-Jews decided to convert to Judaism themselves. They saw that the Israelite king had been willing to hand over seven descendants of his predecessor's house to a wronged group of converts, and had been willing to let the bodies hang for six months in public testimony, and had been willing to do all of this in order to honor a four-hundred-year-old oath. The nations of the world looked at this and said: this is a people whose word can be trusted; this is a people whose covenants mean something; this is a people we want to belong to. And one hundred and fifty thousand of them came to the gates of Yerushalayim and asked to convert.

This is why the halachah was set aside. The ordinary rule about taking the hanged body down the same day protects the individual honor of the hanged person. The extraordinary decision to leave the bodies hanging for six months served a much larger kiddush Hashem — a public sanctification of the name of Hashem that brought one hundred and fifty thousand souls into the covenant. The arithmetic of souls justified the suspension of the ordinary rule.

And the woman on the rock made the whole thing possible. Without Ritzpah's vigil, the bodies would have been destroyed by the birds and the animals within days. The public display would have become a public humiliation. The story would have been one of kingdom cruelty rather than kingdom fidelity. It was Ritzpah's six months of defending the bodies from scavengers that allowed the story to become what it became. She did not know this when she started. She was not acting from any calculation about kiddush Hashem. She was acting from a mother's grief and a mother's loyalty. But her grief and her loyalty turned out to be the lever that moved the moral weight of the whole story.

* * *

At some point, probably late in the summer, someone told David about Ritzpah.

We do not know who told him. The Tanach does not say. But the Tanach does record his reaction. "And it was told David what Ritzpah the daughter of Ayah, the concubine of Shaul, had done." The next verse describes what David did in response: he went to the men of Yavesh Gilad, retrieved the bones of Shaul and Yonatan from the grave where they had been buried in Yavesh Gilad after being rescued from the walls of Beit She'an, brought them back across the Yarden, and gave them a proper burial in the tomb of Shaul's father Kish at Tzela in the territory of Binyamin.

Pause on this for a moment.

Shaul had been dead for decades. His bones had been buried in Yavesh Gilad since shortly after his death on Mount Gilboa — because the men of Yavesh Gilad had remembered how Shaul had rescued their city in his very first military campaign as king (Shmuel Aleph 11) and had risked their lives to steal his body from the wall of Beit She'an where the Pelishtim had hung it. The bones had been in Yavesh Gilad all this time. David had never bothered to retrieve them. There had been opportunity many times over the decades — after the battle of Gilboa, after David was crowned in Chevron, after David was crowned over all Israel in Yerushalayim, after the kingdom was consolidated, after the Aron was brought to Yerushalayim, after every year of every decade of the long reign. At no point had David gone to Yavesh Gilad for the bones. The bones had been left where the men of Yavesh Gilad had buried them. The ancestral tomb of Shaul's family at Tzela in Binyamin — where Shaul should have been buried — had stood empty all this time.

Why? The mefarshim suggest several reasons. Some say David was politically cautious about doing anything that might seem to honor the rival house of Shaul early in his reign, when the northern tribes were still uncertain about him. Others say David was simply distracted by the wars and by the Aron and by the palace and by the Batsheva crisis and by Avshalom. Others say — and this is the reading the Gemara in Yevamot 79a takes — that David had forgotten. Not forgotten emotionally, but forgotten the specific obligation. The obligation to honor the remains of a king of Israel, especially a king who had been anointed by Shmuel, is a very serious one. David had not attended to it. The famine, in part, was Hashem's way of reminding him.

And it took Ritzpah's vigil to jog David's memory. When David heard that an elderly concubine of Shaul was sitting on a rock for six months guarding the bodies of her sons and stepsons from scavengers, something in David broke. He thought about how long the bones of Shaul and Yonatan had been sitting in a foreign grave in Yavesh Gilad while he, David, had been sitting in a palace in Yerushalayim doing nothing about it. He thought about the oath he had sworn to Yonatan — the same oath that had just made him spare Mefiboshet. He thought about the song he had composed decades earlier on Mount Gilboa, the kinah for Shaul and Yonatan — "how are the mighty fallen, and the weapons of war perished" — and about how that song had been the full extent of his formal mourning for his father-in-law and his best friend. He had written the song and had never done anything else. The bones had been left in Yavesh Gilad.

The old concubine on the rock was teaching him what a proper mourning looked like.

David gave the order. He sent a large delegation to Yavesh Gilad. They crossed the Yarden. They went to the town and asked for the remains. The men of Yavesh Gilad — who had been guarding the bones for decades — released them to the delegation. The bones of Shaul and Yonatan were placed in a cedar-wood coffin and carried back across the Yarden and through the central hill country to the ancestral tomb at Tzela in Binyamin.

A detail preserved in the Yalkut Shimoni and picked up by several mefarshim: David ordered the coffin to be carried not directly to Tzela but through the territories of every tribe of Israel first, so that each tribe could come out and mourn over Shaul and fulfill the mitzvah of honoring a fallen king. The tribes did so. The men and the sons and the daughters and the wives of each tribe came out and acted with kindness toward Shaul and his sons. It was the national funeral that Shaul had never received. It was thirty years late. But it was finally happening.

Imagine David walking behind the coffin through the hill country. He is in sackcloth. He has not shaved since the procession began. He walks beside Evyatar the High Priest, who is carrying a small bundle of the bones of Yonatan wrapped in a linen cloth.

"Evyatar."

"My lord."

"I have been thinking about Yonatan the whole way from Yavesh Gilad. I have not thought about him this much in thirty years. I cannot stop remembering him. The way he used to laugh. The way he used to shoot a bow. The day he came to me in the wilderness at Choresh and told me not to be afraid, because his father's hand would never find me and I would be king over Israel and he would stand next to me as second to the throne. Do you remember me telling you about that day?"

"I remember."

"He said it so confidently. He said it as if it were already true, as if the kingdom had already passed from his father to me and from me to him as my second. And then he was killed on Mount Gilboa a few years later and the kingdom did pass to me but he was not alive to stand next to me. I have never gotten over it, Evyatar. I have lived with that loss for thirty years and I have never gotten over it. And I have been carrying his bones in my heart all this time without carrying his actual bones to a proper grave, because I forgot. I forgot the simplest obligation any friend has to any friend — to bury him properly when he dies. How could I forget something like that?"

"You did not forget, my lord. You delayed."

"Is there a difference?"

"Yes, there is a difference. A man who forgets is a man whose heart is unmoved. A man who delays is a man whose heart is so full that he cannot yet face what he would feel if he completed the task. You were not unmoved. You were overwhelmed. You could not face Yonatan's bones because facing them meant facing the full weight of losing him, and the full weight was too much. So you delayed. Ritzpah finally showed you what to do. That is the only difference. The feeling was there all along. You just needed the example."

David walked in silence for a few paces.

"Do you think Yonatan forgives me?"

"I cannot speak for Yonatan. But I can tell you what the tradition says. The righteous dead do not hold grudges against the righteous living. They wait patiently. When the living finally do the thing that the dead have been waiting for, the dead accept the act without reproach. Yonatan has been waiting. You are doing the thing now. The delay does not matter to him anymore. What matters is that you are finally here, walking behind his bones, with sackcloth on your body and tears on your face, bringing him home to Tzela. That is all he was ever going to ask for."

"Thank you, Evyatar."

"My lord — may I say one more thing?"

"Say it."

"When we arrive at Tzela, I want you to walk up to Ritzpah bat Ayah before the burial begins and thank her properly. Not a formal thanks. A personal thanks. She is the reason this procession exists. Without her vigil on the rock, you would still be sitting in the palace with Yonatan's bones in Yavesh Gilad and Shaul's bones in Yavesh Gilad and your conscience eating you alive. She did the thing that made you able to do the thing. I want you to tell her that. Tell her to her face, in front of the other mourners, so that the whole kingdom will know that the king acknowledged what the old concubine of a dead king did for the house of Israel."

"I will."

"Thank you, my lord."

David walked the rest of the way to Tzela with Yonatan's bones in a bundle beside him, held by the High Priest of the kingdom, with the sound of the procession behind him and the sound of the countryside around him, and with the face of his friend in his memory as it had been on the day of the arrows in the field outside Shaul's palace almost forty years before.

* * *

The seven sons of Shaul who had been hanged at Giv'at Shaul were taken down from their frames when the first rains of Tishrei began to fall. Ritzpah had stayed on her rock until that moment. Until water was poured upon them from the heavens. The arrival of the rains was the sign that the famine was ending — that Hashem had been entreated for the land, that the oath to the Givonim had been fulfilled, and that the sons could be buried. The bodies were taken down. They were placed in the same procession that was carrying the bones of Shaul and Yonatan. Together, all nine sets of remains — the two from Yavesh Gilad (Shaul and Yonatan) and the seven from Giv'at Shaul — were carried to the tomb at Tzela and buried there together in the ancestral grave.

Ritzpah came along with the procession. The Tanach does not say so explicitly, but the mefarshim assume she did. She had sat on the rock for six months; she was not going to let the bodies of her sons be buried without her. She walked behind the coffins to Tzela, and she stood at the graveside as the bones were placed in the earth, and then — the Tanach never mentions her again — she went home, or perhaps she stayed in Tzela, or perhaps she died shortly afterward of the exhaustion of her six-month vigil. We do not know. The Tanach closes her story with the burial, and the silence after the burial is the Tanach's respectful acknowledgment that an old woman who has sat on a rock for six months guarding the bodies of her children deserves, at the end, to step off the page and be left to her rest.

The last verse of the section reads: "And they performed all that the king commanded. And after that, God was entreated for the land."

Va-ye'ater Elohim la-aretz.

God was entreated for the land. The land was at peace. The famine ended. The rains came. The next year's crops would grow. The debt of the Givonim had been paid. The debt of Shaul's unburied bones had been paid. The oath of Yehoshua had been kept. The oath of David to Yonatan had been kept. Ritzpah had been honored without ever being spoken to. And the kingdom, for the first time in three years, had enough to eat.

* * *

The story is one of the hardest in all of Shmuel Bet.

It is hard because seven men die who did not personally commit the sin for which they are dying. It is hard because the bodies hang for six months in violation of the ordinary halachah. It is hard because the Givonim ask for blood instead of silver and because David grants their request. It is hard because a mother has to spend six months on a rock fighting off scavengers to protect her children's dead bodies. It is hard because the whole thing reads like a throwback to a more primitive kind of justice than the later Jewish tradition would be comfortable with.

And yet — and this is what the mefarshim keep coming back to — the story is preserved in the Tanach because of what it teaches about three things.

First, the inviolability of oaths. An oath sworn in the name of Hashem cannot be broken, even four hundred years later, even by a king who had nothing to do with the original swearing. Shaul broke the oath by destroying Nov. David was obligated to pay the cost of the broken oath even though he had not been the one who broke it. The cost was terrible. But the alternative — a kingdom that did not honor its oaths — would have been worse.

Second, the power of private faithfulness. Ritzpah was an unimportant woman. She was an old concubine of a dead king. She had no political standing. She had no economic resources. She had nothing in the world except her two dead sons on a hill and her sackcloth and her stone. And what she did on that rock for six months shamed David into finishing the mourning for Shaul that he had been putting off for three decades. The small act of a powerless woman moved the king to the act he had never gotten around to. The Tanach preserves the woman's name — Ritzpah the daughter of Ayah — with full honor, specifically because the mefarshim want us to understand that the faithfulness of a nobody can change the history of a kingdom.

Third, the public sanctification of the name of Hashem. The six-month hanging, as hard as it is to read, produced one of the great conversion events in the history of ancient Israel. One hundred and fifty thousand souls were moved by what the Israelite king had done to enter the covenant of Hashem themselves. The arithmetic of souls — the seven who died balanced against the one hundred and fifty thousand who came in — is the mathematical expression of why the Gemara thought the whole episode was, on balance, a kiddush Hashem of the highest order. The converts walked into the gates of Yerushalayim because the story of Ritzpah on the rock had reached them. Ritzpah did not know that her private grief would become the instrument of public conversion. But the Gemara knew, and preserved the connection, and the tradition has held it ever since.

* * *

The Tanach does not tell us what Ritzpah said to David if they ever met after the burial.

We do not know if she was summoned to the palace to be thanked. We do not know if David ever spoke to her directly. We do not know if she received any compensation or any honor from the kingdom, beyond the honor of having her name preserved in the Tanach. The last time the Tanach mentions her is at the moment of her vigil. After that, she is gone.

But we can imagine a meeting. A brief one. Perhaps on the day of the burial, before the procession left the graveside at Tzela, David walked over to the elderly woman who had been standing in silence at the edge of the mourners.

"Ritzpah bat Ayah."

"My lord."

"I am sorry for what I did to your sons. I had no other way. The Urim had told me what had to be done."

"My lord. I understand. I am not here to accuse you."

"I know you are not. I am here to thank you. What you did on the rock — nobody in the kingdom could have done what you did. I am a king, and I have an army, and I have wealth and power and everything a man could need, and I could not have done what you did. I could not have sat on a rock for six months defending the bodies of my children against the scavengers of the countryside. I know, because I have been failing to do the equivalent thing for Shaul and Yonatan for three decades. You did in six months what I had been refusing to do for thirty years. You shamed me into finishing the mourning. I needed to be shamed. I needed somebody to show me what loyalty to the dead looks like when you do not have any of the resources I have. Thank you. I am in your debt for as long as I am king."

Ritzpah did not answer for a long moment. She was a very old woman and she was very tired. Then she said, quietly:

"My lord. Do not be in my debt. Just remember my boys. That is all I want. Remember that they were kings' sons and that they died for a crime they did not commit and that their mother sat on a rock for six months so that their bodies would not be dishonored. Remember that. Write it down somewhere, if you can, so that the people who read the record of your reign will see it. That is the only thanks I ask for."

"I will write it down."

"Thank you, my lord."

She turned and walked slowly down the path from Tzela toward whatever village she was returning to. The Tanach does not follow her. David watched her until she disappeared around the curve of the hill, and then he turned back to the graveside, and the procession of mourners dispersed, and the story of Ritzpah the daughter of Ayah was over.

Except that she got her wish. David wrote it down. Or someone in his court did. And three thousand years later the story is still here, and her name is still preserved in the Tanach with full honor, and every time a Jew reads Shmuel Bet chapter 21, Ritzpah's six months on the rock are being remembered one more time.

That is what she asked for. That is what she received.
