Chapter One

The Three-Year Famine

## Volume VII — The Threshing Floor ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

This is the last volume.

The book of Shmuel Bet has twenty-four chapters. We have worked our way through most of them. Volume I took us from the death of Shaul at Mount Gilboa to the crowning of David in Chevron. Volume II brought the Aron up to Yerushalayim and saw David dance before it in a linen ephod. Volume III traced the terrible arc of the sin with Batsheva and the death of Uriah the Chittite. Volume IV followed the rebellion of Avshalom from the rape of Tamar to the forest of Efrayim where Yoav threw three darts into the heart of David's third son. Volume V told the story of the restoration — Sheva ben Bichri at Avel Beit Ma'akhah, the wise woman who threw down a head over the wall, the long return across the Yarden. Volume VI slowed the clock down and looked sideways at the Gibborim, the thirty-seven men who had held the kingdom up and whose names were preserved in the honor roll at the back of the book.

And now we come to the two chapters that the Tanach has saved for last.

Most modern readers, if they even read Shmuel Bet chapters 21 and 24, read them as afterthoughts. They are often described as "appendices" — pieces of narrative material that did not quite fit into the main forward flow of the book and that were tucked in at the end almost as a kind of textual attic. Readers skim through them on their way to the book of Melakhim. They are not the most famous chapters. They do not contain the most quoted verses. They are not the chapters that children are taught in school.

But the mefarshim have never read them that way.

The mefarshim have always seen chapters 21 and 24 as the theological frame of the whole book. They come at the end for a reason. They are placed there because they answer the question that the rest of Shmuel Bet has been quietly asking: what is the land of Israel for, and what is the king of Israel for, and what is the kingdom of David pointing toward? The answer is given in these two chapters. The land of Israel is for dwelling in the presence of Hashem. The king of Israel is a servant of that presence. And the kingdom of David is pointing toward the Beit HaMikdash — the Temple that David himself will not build but that his son Shlomo will build on a specific piece of ground that David will purchase at full price at the end of his life.

The two chapters are parallel. Both open with a national disaster — a famine in chapter 21, a plague in chapter 24 — that turns out to be a punishment for a specific sin. Both trace the process by which David, as king, negotiates an expiation for the sin. Both end with a moment of restoration: Hashem being entreated for the land in chapter 21, and the plague being stayed in chapter 24. And both culminate in a physical location that becomes sacred because of what happened on it — the rock on which Ritzpah spread her sackcloth in the first story, and the threshing floor on which David built an altar in the second.

The second location — Aravnah's threshing floor — becomes the site of the Beit HaMikdash. The first location — Ritzpah's rock — does not become a Temple, but it becomes something almost as important: a landmark in the collective memory of Israel, a place where a grieving mother taught the kingdom what loyalty to the dead looks like, and where her teaching moved David to finally give the bones of Shaul and Yonatan a proper burial after decades of neglect.

The two chapters together form a theological arc: from famine to threshing floor, from sin to altar, from a field of bones to a house of prayer. This is the arc we are going to follow in Volume VII. We will take the two appendices in order, with one chapter devoted to the poetry between them (the song of David in chapter 22 and the last words of David in 23:1–7), and we will end — at the very end of the volume, and at the very end of the whole series — with the verse that will become the closing sentence of David's whole life: "This is the House of Hashem the God, and this is the altar of burnt offering for Israel."

We begin with the famine.

* * *

The verse opens with an unusual formula.

Va-yehi ra'av bimei David shalosh shanim, shanah acharei shanah.

"And there was a famine in the days of David for three years, year after year."

The phrase shanah acharei shanah — year after year — is the Tanach's way of telling us that this was not an ordinary drought. A single bad year was a hardship. Two bad years in a row was a crisis. Three consecutive bad years was a catastrophe. By the end of the third year, the grain stores were empty, the flocks were dying, the children were malnourished, the farmers were starting to sell their land, and the mood of the country was turning dark. The people were beginning to ask the question that they always asked in a sustained crisis: what did we do to deserve this?

And David, the king, was asking the same question.

He did not ask it lightly. He had been king for many decades by this point. He had seen droughts before. He had seen localized famines in specific regions. He had seen bad harvests followed by good harvests. The ordinary rhythm of agricultural life in the land of Israel included bad years; the country was dry, the rainfall was unreliable, and no farmer expected every year to be abundant. But three consecutive years of failed harvests across the whole country was different. It was not an ordinary bad-weather pattern. It was something theological. It was a sign that something had gone wrong in the relationship between the people and Hashem, and that the land itself was reflecting the wrongness back at the people by refusing to produce.

David went to inquire.

The verse puts it simply: va-yevakesh David et penei Hashem. "And David sought the face of Hashem." The mefarshim explain that he went to the High Priest, probably Evyatar, and asked him to consult the Urim ve-Tumim — the oracular breastplate of the High Priest, which in the early period of the kingdom was still used for direct communication with Hashem on matters of national importance. The High Priest would put on the breastplate, stand before the Aron, and ask the question. The answer would come through the pattern of the lighted letters on the twelve stones of the breastplate, which the High Priest would then read back to the king.

David went to the High Priest in the early morning. He was not a young man anymore. The weight of the long reign was on his face. The Batsheva crisis had happened years earlier. The Avshalom rebellion had happened and been put down. The men who had been young with him at the cave of Adullam were mostly dead. He was an old king in a dying country, and the famine had been dragging on for three years, and he wanted an answer.

"Evyatar."

"My lord the king."

"Put on the Urim ve-Tumim. I need to ask Hashem why this famine has not ended."

Evyatar went into the inner chamber. He took the breastplate from its place on the table in front of the Aron. He put it on over the white linen robe he wore for oracular consultations. He came back out into the outer chamber where David was waiting. He stood in front of the king and closed his eyes.

"Ask."

David asked the question.

"Why has there been a famine in Israel for three years, year after year?"

Evyatar stood in silence. The twelve stones on the breastplate began to glow in a pattern. He read the pattern. He opened his eyes.

"The answer, my lord."

"Speak."

"El Shaul ve-el beit ha-damim, al asher hemit et ha-Givonim."

"It is for Shaul and for his bloody house, because he put the Givonim to death."

David said nothing for a long moment.

"Say it again."

"For Shaul and for his bloody house, because he put the Givonim to death."

"Shaul."

"Yes, my lord."

"Shaul who has been dead for decades?"

"Yes, my lord."

"And the Givonim?"

"The Givonim, my lord. The ones who came to Yehoshua in the time of the conquest of the land. The ones who pretended to be travelers from a far country and tricked us into making a covenant with them. The ones we vowed, in the name of Hashem, to protect in perpetuity. Those Givonim."

"What did Shaul do to them?"

"That is what I cannot see in the Urim. The answer says only that he put them to death. It does not say when, or where, or how. But the famine is for that."

David was quiet again. He was looking past Evyatar, past the inner chamber, past the wall of the tent of the Aron, toward something only he could see. He was, the mefarshim tell us, running back through his memory. Trying to place the event. Trying to figure out when Shaul had acted against the Givonim and why he had never heard about it before.

Then he figured it out.

"It was at Nov."

"My lord?"

"It was when Shaul destroyed the city of Nov. Do you remember? The Kohanim. The eighty-five priests. Your own father Achimelech. Shaul had them all killed because Do'eg the Edomite reported that they had given me bread and the sword of Goliath."

"I remember. I was the only survivor. I came to you afterward."

"Yes. And when he destroyed Nov, he destroyed the city where the Mishkan had been standing. And the Givonim — the Givonim were wood-choppers and water-drawers for the Mishkan. They had been assigned that role by Yehoshua, and they had been doing it for four hundred years. Their livelihood depended on the service of the Mishkan at Nov. And when Shaul destroyed Nov, he did not just kill the priests. He destroyed the entire economic base of the Givonim. They lost their jobs. They lost their food supply. They lost their homes, probably — they had been living in the precinct of the Mishkan for generations. Many of them must have starved, or been killed in the fighting, or been scattered to the hills. Shaul did not personally raise a sword against them. But he killed them by taking away their livelihood. El beit ha-damim, al asher hemit et ha-Givonim. That is what the Urim means. That is Shaul's bloody house."

Evyatar nodded slowly.

"I had not thought of it that way. But you are right, my lord. I remember the Givonim from before my father's death. They were the wood-choppers and water-drawers of the Mishkan at Nov. After Nov was destroyed, I never saw them again. I assumed they had scattered. I did not know they had been killed."

"They were killed. Indirectly. By the collapse of their livelihood. The Tanach does not always distinguish between direct killing and indirect killing — a man who takes away your food has killed you as surely as a man who puts a spear through your ribs, even if the death takes longer. The Gemara in Bava Kamma will, centuries from now, explain this exact distinction. Gorem shel gorem. The one who causes the cause. Shaul caused the destruction of Nov, which caused the destruction of the Givonim's livelihood, which caused the deaths of the Givonim themselves. He is liable for all of it. And the famine is the national punishment for the unavenged blood."

Evyatar took off the breastplate and placed it back on the table in front of the Aron.

"What are you going to do, my lord?"

David was already moving toward the door.

"I am going to find the Givonim. Whatever is left of them. And I am going to ask them what they need from the house of Shaul in order to forgive the debt. And I am going to give it to them. Whatever they ask. Whatever the price. The famine cannot continue. The children are dying. I will pay whatever Shaul should have paid if he had lived."

* * *

To understand the rest of this chapter, we have to go back a long way — all the way back to the book of Yehoshua.

The Givonim were not originally a tribe of Israel. They were not even a group of people with a distinctive name. They were one of the local Canaanite peoples who lived in the central hill country at the time of Yehoshua's conquest — specifically in four cities (Giv'on itself, Kfirah, Be'erot, and Kiryat Ye'arim) all clustered in what would later become the tribal territory of Binyamin. When Yehoshua crossed the Yarden and began the conquest of the land, the Givonim were in a panic. They had heard what had happened to Yericho and what had happened to Ai. They knew that the war of the conquest was a total war — that the Torah had commanded Yehoshua to leave no Canaanite population alive in the land — and they knew that unless they could somehow escape the general fate of the Canaanite cities, they were going to be wiped out.

So they devised a trick.

The trick is preserved in Yehoshua chapter 9. A delegation of Givonim put on old clothes, loaded their donkeys with torn leather bags and patched wineskins, and packed dry and crumbled bread into their traveling provisions. They made themselves look like men who had been on a long journey across a great distance. Then they went to Yehoshua at his camp at Gilgal and told him that they had come from a far country to make a treaty of peace. They said they had heard of the great deeds of Hashem — the crossing of the Yam Suf, the defeat of Sichon and Og — and they wanted to make a covenant of friendship and submission with the people of Israel.

Yehoshua was skeptical at first. He asked them where they had come from. They were vague. He asked them about their dry bread. They pointed to the dry bread as evidence that it had been fresh when they left home and had grown stale on the journey. They pointed to the patched wineskins as evidence that the skins had been new when they left home and had split on the long road. They were very convincing.

Yehoshua did not consult the Urim ve-Tumim. This is the detail that the mefarshim single out as his central mistake. He made the decision about the treaty on his own, without asking Hashem. The Tanach records it baldly: "And the men took of their provisions, but they did not ask counsel at the mouth of Hashem." Yehoshua, relying on his own judgment, decided that the Givonim were telling the truth. He made a covenant of peace with them. He swore in the name of Hashem that he and his people would not harm the Givonim. The leaders of the tribes countersigned the oath.

Three days later, the trick was discovered. Israelite scouts reported back that the "travelers from a far country" were actually living in four cities only a day or two's walk from Gilgal. The whole thing had been a deception. The Givonim were not from a distant land; they were from the central hill country that Yehoshua was about to invade next. They had tricked him into protecting them by pretending to be travelers when they were actually the next targets on his list.

The Israelite leaders were furious. They wanted to break the treaty and kill the Givonim anyway. Yehoshua refused. The oath had been sworn in the name of Hashem. Even though the Givonim had obtained it through deception, the oath was still an oath; breaking it would be a chilul Hashem — a desecration of the Divine name that would make Israel look like a people whose word could not be trusted. Yehoshua insisted that the oath would be kept. The Givonim would not be killed. But they would not go unpunished either. They would be assigned a permanent role in the service of the Israelite community: they would be chotvei etzim ve-sho'avei mayim — wood-choppers and water-drawers — for the whole congregation and for the altar of Hashem.

This was the arrangement that held for the next four hundred years. The Givonim lived in their four cities in Binyamin. They provided wood and water for the Mishkan and for the community. They were not quite Israelites, but they were not quite foreigners either — they were a legally defined class of semi-converted residents, with their own rights and responsibilities, protected by the ancient oath Yehoshua had sworn in the name of Hashem. They chopped wood. They drew water. They served at the Mishkan, and later at the sanctuary at Nov when the Mishkan moved to Nov after Shiloh fell. Their lives were organized around the service of the Mishkan. Their food and their housing and their small economy all flowed from the service. Without the Mishkan, they had nothing.

And then Shaul destroyed Nov.

* * *

The destruction of Nov is one of the darkest episodes in Shmuel Aleph, and we told the story in passing in Volume I of this series. Shaul was in the grip of his pursuit of David. Do'eg the Edomite, who had been at Nov when David passed through and had seen the High Priest Achimelech give David bread and the sword of Goliath, reported the incident to Shaul. Shaul concluded that Achimelech was part of a conspiracy against him. He summoned Achimelech and eighty-five other kohanim of Nov to his court. He ordered them executed. The regular Israelite guards refused to carry out the order — killing eighty-five kohanim was a line they would not cross. So Shaul turned to Do'eg, who had no such scruples, and Do'eg killed the eighty-five men in a single afternoon. Shaul then sent soldiers to the city of Nov itself and had the entire population — men, women, children, oxen, donkeys, sheep — put to the sword. Only one man survived: Evyatar, the son of Achimelech, who escaped and fled to David in the wilderness.

The destruction of Nov killed the kohanim. It killed the civilian residents. It ended the functioning of the Mishkan at that site. And — as David had just realized in the conversation with Evyatar — it also destroyed the livelihood of the Givonim who had been serving as wood-choppers and water-drawers for the Mishkan at Nov for many decades. The Givonim were not listed among the dead because the Tanach's account focuses on the priests and the civilians. But they were casualties of the same event. When the Mishkan was destroyed, their entire way of life was destroyed. Many of them died in the days that followed, from starvation and exposure and the general chaos of a population whose economic base had been suddenly removed. Others fled into the hills and survived as scattered refugees, never regaining a collective existence.

The Gemara in Bava Kamma 119a discusses this exact kind of harm — the harm that comes not from a direct act of killing but from destroying someone's means of livelihood. The principle is that if a person's survival depends on a particular resource, and you deliberately destroy that resource, you are responsible for the person's death even though you never touched them physically. Shaul had not personally killed any Givoni in the destruction of Nov. He had destroyed the Mishkan at Nov. The destruction of the Mishkan had destroyed the Givonim. Therefore Shaul was responsible for the deaths of the Givonim — indirectly, but fully.

The famine in the days of David was the national punishment for this unavenged blood. The Torah is clear, in Bamidbar 35:33, that the land cannot be cleansed of the blood that is shed on it except through the blood of the one who shed it. Blood that has been spilled but not avenged pollutes the land. The land then refuses to yield its produce. The crops fail. The rains stop. The famine sets in. And the famine continues until someone finds the unavenged blood and deals with it. This was what the Urim ve-Tumim had just told David. The blood of the Givonim was crying out from the ground. The land was refusing to give its produce until the blood was answered.

And David, the king, was the only person who could answer it.

* * *

David sent messengers to find what was left of the Givonim. The messengers rode out from Yerushalayim and began asking in the villages of Binyamin. Where are the descendants of the men who used to be the wood-choppers and water-drawers at the Mishkan at Nov? Where did their children go after Nov was destroyed? Are there any of them left?

After some weeks, the messengers came back with a small group — perhaps a few dozen men, the surviving elders of a scattered community. They had been living in obscurity in the hills around Giv'on itself, trying to rebuild some kind of existence after the catastrophe of Nov. They had never approached any Israelite king for compensation. They had assumed that no king would care. They were stunned when a delegation from David's court arrived in their village with an invitation to come to Yerushalayim and meet with the king.

The head of the delegation had been a young officer named Yishai, distantly related to David's father. He had delivered the invitation with formal courtesy. The senior Givoni elder had questioned him carefully before agreeing to come.

"Young man. Why does the king want to see us?"

"I do not know, my father. I was only told to bring you."

"The king has not summoned the Givonim since the reign of Shaul. He has ignored us for decades. Why now?"

"I do not know. The king has been consulting the Urim ve-Tumim about the famine. Perhaps the consultation has produced an answer that concerns your people."

"Does he mean us harm?"

"If he meant you harm, my father, he would not have sent a delegation of courtiers to invite you. He would have sent soldiers to arrest you. You will be safe in Yerushalayim. I swear it on my own life."

The elder had conferred with the other Givoni elders. They had agreed, reluctantly, to make the journey. The delegation had escorted them south through the hills of Binyamin to the city of David, and had brought them, on the morning of the third day, into the courtyard of the palace.

The meeting took place in the courtyard of the palace.

David sat on a simple chair — not his throne, but a lower seat of the kind he used for informal audiences. The Givoni elders stood in front of him. They were old men, most of them. Their clothes were worn. They looked at the king with a mixture of suspicion and hope.

David spoke first.

"My fathers. I have called you here because I need to speak with you about a debt that Israel owes to your people."

The Givoni elders looked at each other. The senior elder stepped forward.

"My lord the king. With respect. Israel has not owed us anything for four hundred years. We were tricked into the Mishkan as wood-choppers and water-drawers. We have served faithfully. When Nov was destroyed, we lost everything. We have not complained. We do not expect anything from the king. We are here because we were summoned, but we do not have a claim to bring before you."

"You do have a claim. The Urim ve-Tumim has just told me so. The famine in the land is because of the blood of the Givonim that Shaul spilled when he destroyed Nov. Shaul destroyed the Mishkan and thereby destroyed your livelihood, and when your livelihood was destroyed, many of you died. The blood of those deaths is on Shaul's house. The land will not yield its produce until the blood is answered. I am asking you what payment the house of Shaul owes you. Whatever you ask, I will give. The house of Shaul is not able to give it to you themselves, because Shaul is dead and most of his sons are dead, but I am the king, and I am the one responsible for paying debts that former kings left unpaid. Tell me what you need."

The senior elder looked at David for a long moment.

"My lord — we did not come here to ask for payment."

"I know. But I am asking. What do you need?"

The elder turned and conferred with the other elders behind him. They spoke in low voices for several minutes. Then the senior elder turned back to David.

"My lord. We will tell you what we think. But we need to speak privately among ourselves before we give you an answer. This is a matter that requires thought. May we come back to you in three days?"

"Three days."

"Yes, my lord."

"Granted. Come back in three days. Ask for me by name. The servants of the palace will bring you directly to me."

The Givoni elders bowed and left the courtyard.

David sat alone on the low chair for a while after they had gone. He was thinking about the ancient oath of Yehoshua. He was thinking about the fact that a group of men four hundred years earlier had sworn something in the name of Hashem that was still, today, binding on him as the king. The oath had never expired. The oath had never been renegotiated. The oath was as valid today as it had been on the day Yehoshua and the elders of Israel had signed it at Gilgal. That was the nature of an oath sworn in the name of Hashem. It did not fade with time. It did not weaken across the generations. It bound the descendants as fully as it bound the signatories. And any Israelite king who came to power had inherited the oath as part of the terms of his office, whether he knew it or not.

Shaul had broken the oath. Shaul had treated the Givonim as if they were expendable — as if their lives and their livelihoods did not matter because they were not full Israelites. Shaul had forgotten, in his pursuit of David, that the oath of Yehoshua was still in force and that destroying Nov meant destroying the protection that Yehoshua had sworn the Givonim would have forever.

And David — who had been watching Shaul's mistakes from the outside for all the years he had been a fugitive — had been given the task of cleaning up what Shaul had left undone. This was the cost of inheriting a kingdom. You inherited not only the crown and the palace and the army. You inherited the unpaid debts. You inherited the broken oaths. You inherited the consequences of decisions that had been made by people who were now dead. And if you wanted the land to produce its crops again, you had to pay the debts even though they were not yours.

He would hear the answer in three days. Whatever the Givonim asked for, he was prepared to give.

He did not yet know what they were going to ask.

If he had known, he would have been in less of a hurry.
