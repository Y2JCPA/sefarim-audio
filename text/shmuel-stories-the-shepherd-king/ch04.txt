Chapter Four: Forty Days The Pelishtim gathered their armies for war. They encamped at Socho, in the territory of Yehudah, and spread across the ridge above the Emek HaElah — the Valley of the Terebinth. Shaul and the men of Israel gathered on the opposite ridge. Between them lay the valley — a narrow, rocky depression with a dry streambed running through its center. From the Pelishti camp, a man walked out. He was enormous. The text says six cubits and a span — roughly nine feet tall, though the Radak notes variant readings. He wore a bronze helmet. A coat of mail that weighed five thousand shekels of bronze — roughly 125 pounds of armor on his body alone. Bronze greaves on his legs. A javelin of bronze slung between his shoulders. The shaft of his spear was like a weaver's beam, and the iron head of the spear weighed six hundred shekels. A shield-bearer walked before him, carrying a shield so large it covered both of them. His name was Goliath. He was from Gat. The Gemara in Sotah asks: What does the word habeinayim mean — "the man of the between"? Rav says it means he was muvneh — built, constructed, perfect in his physical form, without any blemish. Shmuel (the Amora) says it means he was the middle brother — beinoni — among his siblings. The academy of Rabbi Shila says it was because he was built like a building — ke'vinyan. Rabbi Yochanan gives the most dramatic reading: he was the son of a hundred fathers and one mother — a product of promiscuity so excessive that his very name carried the stain of it. The Gemara also records that Goliath boasted of his own accomplishments: he was the one who had captured the Aron from Israel. He was the one who had led the Pelishti army in every campaign for decades. And yet, for all his service, he had never been elevated above the rank of a common soldier. His bitterness at this lack of recognition fueled his arrogance. The Gemara in Sotah further identifies him as the son of Orpah — Ruth's sister-in-law, the woman who had kissed Naomi goodbye and turned back to Moav while Ruth clung to her. The Gemara says that as a reward for the four tears Orpah shed at the parting, she was given four mighty sons — and Goliath was one of them. Ruth's great-grandson would be David. The two sisters' descendants would meet in the valley, and one would fall. Goliath stood between the armies and shouted.

"Why do you come out to set your battle in array? Am I not a Pelishti, and you servants of Shaul? Choose a man from among you, and let him come down to me! If he is able to fight with me and kill me — we will be your servants. But if I prevail and kill him — you will be our servants and serve us." He paused. His voice carried across the valley like the crack of a siege engine. "I defy the ranks of Israel this day! Give me a man, that we may fight together!" The Yalkut Shimoni records that Goliath timed his challenges deliberately — morning and evening — to disrupt Israel's recitation of the Shema. The holiest words in the Jewish liturgy — "Hear, O Israel, Hashem our God, Hashem is One" — were being drowned out by the booming voice of a Pelishti giant who mocked the God those words proclaimed. Shaul and all Israel heard, and they were dismayed and greatly afraid. Forty days. Morning and evening. Eighty challenges. Eighty times the giant walked out, eighty times the army of Israel shrank back, eighty times no one answered. The king who had been anointed to save Israel from the Pelishtim could not bring himself to face the champion of the Pelishtim. The man who had stood a head taller than everyone in Israel was now dwarfed by a man who stood three heads taller than him. And in the fields of Beit Lechem, a shepherd boy was tending his father's sheep, unaware that his forty-day countdown had begun. * * *
