Chapter Six: The Valley of Elah The giant saw the boy and laughed. David was walking toward him across the valley floor — a red-haired youth with a staff and a sling and a shepherd's bag, moving through the stones and scrub grass of the dry streambed with the easy stride of someone who had spent his life on uneven ground. No helmet. No armor. No sword. "Am I a dog," Goliath roared, "that you come to me with sticks?" He cursed David by his gods. He cursed him by Dagon, whose head Hashem had severed in Ashdod. He cursed him by every idol in the Pelishti pantheon, as though the accumulated weight of pagan blasphemy could stop what was walking toward him. "Come to me," Goliath said, "and I will give your flesh to the birds of the sky and the beasts of the field!" Goliath squinted. The figure approaching him across the valley floor was... small. No armor. No helmet. No sword visible. A boy. With a stick and what appeared to be a leather sling. The insult was worse than any weapon. The Pelishtim had sent their greatest champion to demand a fight with Israel's best warrior. And Israel had sent a child. A ruddy-faced, smooth-skinned child with a shepherd's bag. Goliath felt the rage building. Forty days he had stood in this valley. Forty days of shouting and waiting and watching an entire army cower. And this was the response? This? David stopped. He stood in the valley — the same valley where two armies waited, the same valley where forty days of fear had frozen an entire nation — and he answered. "You come to me with a sword and a spear and a javelin." His voice was not loud. It did not need to be. The valley carried it. "But I come to you in the name of Hashem Tzvaot — the God of the armies of Israel — whom you have defied." The words rang off the hillsides. The soldiers on both ridges heard them. Shaul heard them. The Pelishti commanders heard them. "This day, Hashem will deliver you into my hand. I will strike you and take your head from you. And I will give the carcasses of the Pelishti camp this day to the birds of the sky and the wild beasts of the earth — so that all the earth will know that there is a God in Israel."

He was not finished. "And all this assembly will know that Hashem does not save with sword and spear. For the battle is Hashem's, and He will give you into our hands." Ki l'Hashem hamilchamah. The battle is Hashem's. Five words that contained everything — everything Shmuel had taught at Mitzpah, everything the Mitzpah thunder had demonstrated, everything Chanah had known when she stood at the doorpost and poured out her soul. The battle does not belong to the strong. It does not belong to the armed. It does not belong to the tall. It belongs to Hashem. Goliath moved forward. The ground shook under his weight. His armor clanked. His shield-bearer ran ahead, and the giant's shadow fell across the valley floor like a stain. David ran. He ran toward the giant. Not away — toward. The text says vayimaher vayaratz — he hurried and he ran. No hesitation. No calculation. The shepherd's legs that had chased lions now carried him toward the largest man he had ever seen. He reached into the bag. He took a stone. He placed it in the sling. He slung. The stone flew. It crossed the space between the boy and the giant in the time it takes to blink. It struck Goliath in the forehead — the one unarmored point on his massive body, the strip of skin between the helmet's rim and the browline — and sank into the bone. Goliath fell. Forward. Face down. The way Dagon had fallen before the Aron. The way every idol falls when it meets the God of Israel. The giant who had defied the living God for forty days fell on his face in the dust of the Valley of Elah, and the sound of his fall carried to both ridges like the closing of a door. David ran to where the giant lay. He had no sword. He drew Goliath's own sword from its sheath — the weapon that had been carried to intimidate Israel — and he cut off the giant's head. The Pelishtim saw their champion dead and they fled. The men of Israel and Yehudah arose with a shout — the shout they had been unable to produce for forty days — and they pursued the Pelishtim all the way to the gates of Ekron. The same Ekron that had screamed when the Aron arrived. The same Ekron that had begged to be rid of the God of Israel.

The Pelishtim fell on the road from Sha'arayim to Gat and Ekron. The valley that had been a place of terror for forty days became a place of deliverance in a single afternoon. David stood in the valley with Goliath's head in his hand. He was seventeen. Maybe younger. He was covered in dust and the blood of a giant. He was holding a head that weighed more than the stones in his shepherd's bag. And the spirit of Hashem that had rushed upon him in Beit Lechem — from that day forward — was burning inside him like the sun going forth in its might. Shaul watched from the ridge. He turned to Avner, his general. "Whose son is this youth?" The question was strange — Shaul knew David. David had played the harp in his chambers. David had been his armor-bearer. And yet the text records Shaul asking the question as though he had never seen the boy before. The Malbim explains: Shaul was not asking who David was. He was asking who David was becoming. The boy who had entered the palace as a musician was now standing in a valley with a giant's head in his hand, and the distance between the harp-player and the giant-killer was the distance between a servant and a king. The Radak observes that this was the moment the relationship between Shaul and David shifted. Until now, David had been a servant — beloved, talented, soothing, but a servant. Now he was something else. A giant-killer. A national hero. The man who had done what the king could not. In the days that followed, as the army returned from the pursuit, the women of Israel came out from the cities to meet them. They sang and danced with tambourines and instruments of joy. And their song — the song that would haunt Shaul for the rest of his life — went like this: "Shaul has struck his thousands — and David his ten thousands." The Malbim notes that this song was not merely a numerical comparison. It was a coronation. In the mouths of the women of Israel, David had already surpassed the king. Ten thousands to thousands. The ratio was the verdict, and the verdict was public, and there was no appeal. From that day forward, Shaul watched David with a different eye. Shaul felt it. The same instinct that had made him hide among the baggage — the instinct that recognized destiny before the mind could name it — was telling him that the boy in the valley was not a servant. "Whose son is this youth?" he asked again.

And David answered: "I am the son of your servant Yishai of Beit Lechem." The son of Yishai. From Beit Lechem. Where Shmuel had gone with a horn of oil and a heifer for sacrifice. Where seven brothers had been passed over and an eighth had been summoned from the sheep. Shaul heard the words. He did not yet understand them. But somewhere in the ruins of the spirit that had once been his, in the place where the ruach ra'ah now dwelt, a cold recognition was forming. The shepherd was coming. And the king's days were numbered. * * *
