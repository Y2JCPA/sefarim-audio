Chapter Five: Five Stones Yishai sent David to the valley with provisions for his brothers. "Take these ten loaves of bread for your brothers," Yishai said. "And these ten cheeses for the commander of their thousand. See how your brothers are doing and bring back their pledge." David rose early, left the sheep with a keeper, loaded the provisions, and went as Yishai had commanded. He arrived at the camp as the army was going out to the battle line, shouting the war cry. Israel and the Pelishtim drew up in opposing lines. David left the provisions with the supply keeper and ran to the front line to find his brothers. He was talking to them — asking about the battle, about the camp, about the food — when the ground began to shake with footsteps. Goliath walked out. David heard the challenge for the first time. He watched the soldiers around him — grown men, warriors, men who had fought the Pelishtim before — turn pale and step backward. "Have you seen this man who comes up?" the soldiers whispered to each other. "He comes to defy Israel. The king will give great riches to the man who kills him. He'll give him his daughter. He'll make his father's house free in Israel." David turned to the soldiers near him. "What will be done for the man who kills this Pelishti and removes the disgrace from Israel? For who is this uncircumcised Pelishti that he should defy the ranks of the living God?" The soldiers repeated what they'd said. The wealth. The daughter. The freedom. But David wasn't asking about rewards. He was asking a different question entirely: who is this Pelishti that he defies the living God? The emphasis was not on the prize for killing him. It was on the outrage that he was still alive. Eliav heard. David's oldest brother — the one Shmuel had looked at first, the one who looked like a king — turned on his younger brother with a fury that had been building for months. Since the anointing. Since the oil. Since the day the prophet had passed over all seven brothers and poured the horn on the head of the boy who had been out with the sheep. "Why have you come down?" Eliav snapped. "And with whom have you left those few sheep in the wilderness? I know your arrogance and the evil of your heart. You came down to see the battle!"

The accusation dripped with contempt. Those few sheep. As though David's entire life was negligible. As though tending sheep — the job Moshe and Yaakov and Rachel had done — was beneath the dignity of a conversation about war. David's answer was quiet and devastating. "What have I done now? Was it not just a question?" He turned away from his brother. He asked the same question to other soldiers. The word spread through the camp: there's a boy from Beit Lechem who isn't afraid. There's a shepherd asking why no one will fight the giant. The word reached Shaul.

"Let no man's heart fail because of him," David said, standing before the king. "Your servant will go and fight this Pelishti." Shaul leaned forward on his throne. He studied the boy standing before him — the shepherd, the musician, the youngest son of Yishai. The boy who had calmed his raging spirit with the sound of a harp was now volunteering to fight a nine-foot warrior in single combat. "You cannot go against this Pelishti," Shaul said. He tried to keep his voice gentle. The boy meant well. "You are a na'ar — a youth. He has been a man of war from his youth." "My lord," David said. "May I speak?" "Speak." David spoke. And what he said revealed not arrogance but a faith forged in solitude, in the silence of the pastures, in the nights when the sheep were his responsibility and God was his only witness. "Your servant was tending his father's sheep. A lion came — a real lion, not a metaphor — and seized a lamb from the flock." The court was silent. David's voice was steady, but something burned beneath the steadiness — the memory of a moment when a boy and a predator stood face to face in the wilderness with no audience and no rescue. "I went out after him. I struck him. I rescued the lamb from his mouth. When he rose against me, I seized him by his beard — " David paused, let the image land — "and I struck him, and I killed him." A murmur ran through the attendants. Seized a lion by its beard.

"Your servant killed both the lion and the bear. And this uncircumcised Pelishti will be like one of them — for he has defied the ranks of the living God." Shaul looked at the boy. The same boy who played the harp in his chambers when the dark spirit came. The same boy whose music brought peace to his tortured mind. Now standing before him, volunteering to face a nine-foot warrior in single combat. "You cannot go against this Pelishti to fight him," Shaul said. "You are a youth. He has been a man of war from his youth." David spoke. And what he said revealed not arrogance but a faith forged in solitude, in the silence of the pastures, in the nights when the sheep were his responsibility and God was his only witness. "Your servant was tending his father's sheep. A lion came, and a bear, and took a lamb from the flock. I went out after him and struck him and rescued it from his mouth. When he rose against me, I seized him by his beard and struck him and killed him." He paused. Let the image settle. A boy. A lion. A bear. Alone. "Your servant killed both the lion and the bear. And this uncircumcised Pelishti will be like one of them — for he has defied the ranks of the living God." Then the words that carried the weight of everything David believed: "Hashem, who delivered me from the paw of the lion and from the paw of the bear — He will deliver me from the hand of this Pelishti." Shaul looked at David for a long moment. He saw the harp-player. He saw the shepherd. He saw something else — the same thing Hashem had seen in Beit Lechem when seven brothers passed and were rejected. The thing that doesn't show in the height of the stature or the handsomeness of the face. The heart. "Go," Shaul said. "And Hashem be with you." He dressed David in his own armor. His helmet. His coat of mail. His sword. The king's equipment on the shepherd's body. David tried to walk. He couldn't. The armor was too heavy, too unfamiliar, too much of someone else's war. "I cannot go in these," David said. "I have not tested them." He took them off. He picked up his staff. He went to the streambed in the valley and chose five smooth stones and put them in the shepherd's bag at his side.

Five stones. The Midrash gives reasons for the number. One for each of the Avot — Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov. One for Aharon, whose descendants Chofni and Pinchas had been killed by this same Pelishti army. And one for God Himself, whom Goliath had blasphemed. He took his sling in his hand. He walked toward the giant. * * *
