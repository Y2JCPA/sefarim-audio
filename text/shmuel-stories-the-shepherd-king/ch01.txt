Chapter One: The Horn of Oil "Until when will you mourn for Shaul?" The voice of Hashem found Shmuel in Ramah, where the old prophet had been sitting for weeks — or perhaps months — wrapped in the grief that had settled over him like a garment he could not remove. He had wept for Shaul. He had wept for the kingdom that should have endured forever. He had wept for the oil he had poured on a young man's head at a crossroads outside a city, when the world was new and the king was humble and everything seemed possible. Now that king was broken, and Shmuel could not stop mourning. "Until when?" Hashem said again. "I have rejected him from being king over Israel." The rebuke was gentle but firm. Mourning has its place. But mourning that paralyzes a prophet — mourning that keeps him sitting in Ramah when there is work to be done — is mourning that has become indulgence. "Fill your horn with oil," Hashem said. "And go. I am sending you to Yishai of Beit Lechem. For I have seen among his sons a king for Me." A king for Me. Not for them — for Me. The people had asked for a king "like all the nations." Hashem was now choosing a king according to His own criteria. Not height. Not military bearing. Not the confident posture of a man who looks like a leader. Something else. Something only Hashem could see. Shmuel's response surprised even him. "How can I go?" he said. "If Shaul hears of it, he will kill me." The Radak pauses at this verse and asks the question every reader asks: since when does a prophet of Hashem refuse a divine command because of physical danger? Shmuel had stood before the entire nation at Mitzpah. He had hewed Agag in pieces. He had confronted kings and delivered verdicts that changed history. And now he was afraid of Shaul? The Abarbanel offers a startling reading: Shmuel's fear was not genuine. It was an excuse. The old prophet did not want to anoint a replacement for the king he still loved. The question "How can I go?" was not about logistics — it was about the heart. How can I do this? How can I pour oil on another man's head when the first man I anointed is still alive, still sitting on the throne, still wearing the crown I placed there? Hashem answered not with a rebuke but with a solution.

"Take a heifer with you. Say: 'I have come to sacrifice to Hashem.' Invite Yishai to the sacrifice. And I will show you what to do. You will anoint for Me the one I tell you." A cover story. Hashem — the God of truth — gave His prophet a cover story. Because sometimes the truth must travel in disguise until it is ready to reveal itself. Shmuel filled his horn with oil. He took the heifer. He walked south, toward Beit Lechem. And behind him, in Givah, a king named Shaul sat on his throne and felt the spirit of Hashem slipping away from him like water through open fingers. * * *
