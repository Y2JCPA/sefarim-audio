PELE YOETZ — EATING AND DRINKING
==================================================

Eating and Drinking - Eating and drinking can be divided into three categories: a commandment, permissible, and forbidden. A person can make all their meals into meals of a commandment by following the conditions truly spoken by the sages, from whom we derive our way of life. First, one must intend that their eating is to be healthy and strong for serving God, blessed be His name, and to fulfill the saying "a kind person benefits himself," meaning to clarify and bring to light the sparks of holiness found in everything one eats and drinks. It is important to pray for one's food before eating, which is a significant obligation as explained in the Zohar. (I, the humble one, have arranged a prayer to express these intentions verbally, as speaking helps remember in the heart, and it also brings holiness to the eating, especially when one's intention is for heaven, and I have included it in my prayer book with divine assistance.)

Anyone who reads the holy book "Reishit Chochmah" will see that the intentions of eating are compared to the offerings. Happy is the person who can build their spiritual ascents through their eating, as long as they direct their heart to heaven and do things properly and lawfully. "He who has clean hands will grow stronger," so be careful with the ritual washing of hands as prescribed by the sages. One should be filled with reverence when blessing the Creator who provides food and other pleasures, and it is crucial to recite the blessings properly, word by word, especially when mentioning God's name, with trembling, for one is blessing the exalted King, the Lord of Hosts. If one is not careful, God forbid, it is considered blasphemy. Therefore, one must be cautious with all blessings, particularly those over enjoyment, and even more so with the Grace After Meals, which is a Torah commandment. It should not be rushed because it is long, but recited properly and lawfully. This is logical, as even in worldly matters, people are careful with blessings, all the more so when blessing the King of Glory, awesome and revered, who in His great kindness allows us to bless Him and mention His holy and awe-inspiring name, and it is considered a commandment for us, granting us reward and blessing for the blessing. Therefore, one must be careful to say it properly. A good practice is to say all blessings aloud, as the voice awakens intention, and one will know for sure if they have blessed, avoiding doubt, and enabling others to respond "Amen" and learn from them.

One must also be careful to follow the sages' instruction to dip the piece of bread in salt three times, and not to neglect this when possible, for all their words are of utmost importance. One should sit respectfully at the table, for it is as if eating at the king's table. It is known from the story of Rabbi Yisrael Najara during the time of the Ari, who would sing at his table with a pleasant voice, attracting ministering angels as if at a wedding feast. However, his arms were exposed due to the heat, and a heavenly voice declared, "Flee from this man who diminishes the honor of heaven by not sitting respectfully at the table before God," and immediately all the angels left. The Ari saw this great vision from his home and revealed it to Rabbi Yisrael Najara, who was greatly alarmed, dressed himself properly, and resumed singing as before, and the angels returned as at first. Therefore, one who fears the word of God will honor it.

One must eat with proper manners, not like gluttons and greedy people, who are said in the holy Zohar to be from the side of Esau, who said, "Pour me some of that red stuff," and is despised by God and man. One should eat to satisfy their soul, not to fill their belly with excessive eating, for there lies the evil's rest. Overindulgence builds a foundation for the evil inclination (yetzer hara), as the sages wrote that eating beyond satisfaction strengthens the negative forces and the evil inclination, especially when indulging in excessive feasts and frivolous gatherings, which greatly empower the negative forces. Just as a commandment elevates and adds strength to the heavenly assembly when done publicly, so too does a transgression done publicly empower the negative forces. The ethical sages have written that overeating causes several harms: it violates the prohibition of wastefulness, wastes time for eating and digestion, and if the stomach is upset, it wastes three times. If one becomes ill and dies, they are responsible for their life. Therefore, one who guards their soul will avoid excessive eating and drinking, eating only when hungry and not until completely full, just a little less. The Raavad wrote that refraining from eating something delicious for atonement is considered as if one fasted and is like a sacrificial altar of atonement. This is wise, for one eats to satisfy their soul and it is as if they fasted. One who eats for the sake of heaven will not pursue what is tasty but harmful to the body, but rather what is beneficial, even if less tasty.

One must be especially cautious with excessive drinking, as it is like a consuming fire, and many have been destroyed by it. Many have drunk, and it has brought ruin and destruction to their borders, bad for them and the world. The sages have strongly condemned it, especially for a scholar, as it is more painful if they drink and behave improperly among the common people, for it desecrates God's name. Happy is the wise person who conducts themselves with justice: "Put a knife to your throat if you are given to appetite." One must be very careful to avoid any hint of forbidden matters related to eating, for the body grows from forbidden food, and the soul is guilty, going from bad to worse, as its impurity is in its skirts, and it cannot attain the paths of life to fear and love the revered and awesome God and serve Him wholeheartedly. Therefore, one must be diligent and ensure that the slaughterers are skilled, knowledgeable, and God-fearing, and instruct their household to check thoroughly for forbidden insects. One should not eat fruit that has grown with worms until it is cut and checked, and be cautious of flies, fleas, and small creeping creatures that fall into food during the summer, even unknowingly. One must be knowledgeable in the laws of wine and food touched by non-Jews and other hidden matters. It is good and pleasant to practice abstinence and avoid a hundred permissible things to not enter one forbidden gate, for all of man's labor is for his mouth, and one who guards their mouth and tongue guards their soul from troubles.

One must be careful not to be strict with their household over anything, especially regarding food, even if the dish is burnt or not to their liking, and not treat mistakes as intentional or coercion as willingness. One should not be angry with their wife, servants, or household members, for a person is judged from heaven according to their measure, and it is written, "If I have despised the cause of my servant or my maid when they contended with me, what then shall I do when God rises up? And when He visits, what shall I answer Him?" Reflect on this and overlook your measures, for more than your wife and servants owe you respect and service, you owe respect and service to your Creator.

One must ensure to say words of Torah at the table, not to pass without a verse or Mishnah. When in the company of others, if capable, they should speak words of wisdom to benefit themselves and others. If not capable or the situation does not allow, at least they should learn some Mishnah they know by heart, such as "All Israel" or "Rabbi Chananya," and the more, the better. Since the holy Zohar praises the remembrance of the Temple at the table and the sorrow for its loss, I have made it a practice to say this teaching at the table to fulfill the obligation of Torah words: It is taught: Rabbi Yose said, "Once I was walking on the road and entered one of the ruins of Jerusalem to pray..." and "Rabbi Elazar says: The night is divided into three watches..." Yehuda ben Tema says... and on Shabbat, I am accustomed to saying the teaching of Yehuda ben Tema and the saying of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yose, "Anyone who delights in the Sabbath is given an inheritance without boundaries..." Rabbi Chananya ben Akashya... And after the intentions of the heart are the actions; in times of sadness, sadness, and in times of joy, joy.

A person whom God has blessed with abundance, how fortunate is their lot if they are careful to give a portion of their meal to a poor person who lacks, for a person's gift will make room for them in this world and the next. It is good to raise an orphan and orphaness in one's home, for this righteousness endures forever with every meal. And for one whose means are limited, they should intend to fulfill this by providing for their sons and daughters, as this is also considered doing charity at all times. On Shabbat and holidays, it is fitting for everyone to sing at their table, raising their voice to honor their Creator with the grace He has given them.

The final washing of hands is obligatory both at home and when traveling. Many have neglected and abolished this practice, especially travelers. One must be more careful with this than with the initial washing, for one who transgresses the words of the sages is liable to death, and according to the effort is the reward.
