PELE YOETZ — LOVE OF TORAH SCHOLARS AND THOSE WHO FEAR GOD
==================================================

Love for Torah Scholars and Those Who Fear God

Loving those who study Torah and those who revere God stems from a love for the Divine. Just as someone who is a loyal friend loves the children and household of their friend, so too, when someone has a deep love for their beloved and seeks their well-being with all their heart and soul, they also love anyone who brings joy and comfort to their beloved. Furthermore, reason dictates that we should love a person of admirable character, someone complete in virtues and beliefs, or anyone who aspires to such completeness, even if they have not yet achieved it. For all these reasons, it is fitting to awaken love for Torah scholars and those who fear God, so they are cherished as the apple of one's eye.

The reward for this love is great, as our sages have said: "Whoever loves the sages will have children who are sages." The essence of love is in the heart, but it should also be expressed through actions. If God has blessed someone with wealth, they should support Torah scholars with all their heart and resources, actively engaging in deeds. If they lack financial means, they should still serve them with their physical presence, doing whatever they can to ensure that the scholars can focus on their study and service without distraction. It is crucial to avoid causing any distress to a Torah scholar or giving them any reason to be upset. As the sage advised, "Warm yourself by the fire of the sages, but be careful not to get burned by their embers."

If the obligation to love Torah scholars is significant even when they are not relatives, it is even more so when the scholar is a family member. In such cases, there is a greater duty for siblings and relatives to support them, ensuring they can continue their dedication to Torah and service. This brings honor and pride to the family, as it brings joy to the scholar's parents and allows the family to share in the scholar's achievements, thus performing kindness for both the living and the deceased.

Therefore, blessed is the person who acts in this way and holds onto this path. "It is a tree of life for those who grasp it, and those who support it are fortunate." However, one should not feel superior or self-satisfied for providing for a Torah scholar, as this is simply fulfilling God's mission. In fact, the scholar benefits the benefactor more than the benefactor helps the scholar, as the benefactor earns a share in the world to come through their support.

Additionally, Torah scholars should honor their wealthy supporters, who provide for them. Even if the scholars do not need their support, as was the case with Rabbi and Rabbi Akiva, they still showed respect to the wealthy. How much more so should scholars honor those they rely on, showing gratitude and respect, and making an effort to bring them comfort. Scholars should also guide their benefactors with kindness and love, offering gentle and loving rebuke when necessary, sharing in their troubles, and praying for their success. In all their teachings and good deeds, they should also have their benefactors in mind. In this way, a wise person will continue to learn and grow, finding favor and good understanding in the eyes of God and people.
