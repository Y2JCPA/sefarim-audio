PELE YOETZ — LOVE OF FRIENDS
==================================================

Love of Friends - It is well known how great the value of loving friends is, to the extent that our sages said, "Love your neighbor as yourself" is a great principle in the Torah. They explained this to mean, "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow." The greater the good, the more a person's evil inclination (yetzer hara) tries to overpower him daily. Because of our sins, due to baseless hatred, our city was destroyed, our Temple was ruined, and we were exiled from our land. Even now, the adversary dances among us to cause us harm, as people turn to their own gain and do not care about their friend's downfall. Some even rejoice in others' misfortune, and for a small benefit, people swallow each other alive. Woe to the eyes that see this, for our eyes long for salvation, but there is no savior as long as this trait exists among us. Therefore, we must strengthen ourselves to awaken love.

The details of loving friends have been explained at length in the "Book of the Covenant," and I will summarize briefly. One must fulfill "Turn away from evil and do good" in speech and action. In speech, "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow." Be careful not to speak to or about your friend in any way you would not want spoken to or about you. This includes gossip, hurtful words, and much more. Similarly, in action, do not harm your friend or cause them any damage, anything you would not want done to you. One must delve deeply into this, as the evil inclination is full of deceptions. Especially be cautious where financial loss is concerned, as a person is prone to justify things for themselves and pursue their own gain even at the expense of their friend. It is no wonder they said, "A person is recognized by their wallet." Fortunate is the one who fears God and loves Him with all their resources, not chasing after gain.

One must also fulfill "do good" in speech and action: in speech, speak good and appropriate words to bring joy to others, such as good news, good advice, and removing obstacles from a friend's path. Inform them if you know of any harm that might come to them, and comfort them in their distress. In action, perform acts of kindness with your body and resources. Just as you wish others would do for you and have compassion on you, you should do to others to fulfill the commandment of loving your neighbor as yourself. Fortunate is the person who does this and holds onto it. According to the effort is the reward, for according to the effort, one brings satisfaction to their Creator. Just as a father has compassion for his children and a brother is born for adversity, so should one help their friend, for we are all children of one man, we are brothers. Just as one would care for any part of their body, so should one care for every person of Israel, for we are all as one person, like one body.

A person naturally loves themselves, their child, and their sibling because nature compels them. But regarding a friend, where one thinks there is a loss or physical effort involved, self-love prevails. Moreover, because this is a great commandment, a person's evil inclination tries to sway them from their Creator's will to lose much good. One must overcome their inclination, care for the honor of their Creator, and awaken love. Fortunate are the people of Israel, who exert themselves, endure much toil, and spend greatly for the sake of commandments and good deeds. But alas, there are great commandments that are neglected because they are not considered as such. If it was said, "Run to a minor commandment as to a major one, for you do not know the reward for commandments," how much more so for such a great commandment that includes loving kindness, love of friends, and ways of peace, which should be pursued as one pursues life.

Since the evil inclination is close and shows forty-nine reasons to justify the unclean and bring benefit to oneself, and since there are many matters in this where one who fears the word of God is confused and does not know which path is right, the advised counsel is to fulfill the teaching, "Do not rely on your own understanding." This is a great principle in the entire Torah, especially in matters of money. "He who walks with the wise will become wise and will attain paths of life." The essence of the commandment of love is in the heart, not to rejoice in a friend's downfall, God forbid, but to rejoice in their good and feel sorrow in their distress to the point of praying for them.

The way to acquire this love is by considering the teaching that even when the wicked are in distress, the Divine Presence is distressed. What does the tongue say? And when a person is at ease, the Divine Presence is also at ease. Therefore, one who loves God should rejoice in the joy of the Holy One and the Divine Presence and feel sorrow in their distress. Moreover, when a person is at ease and has wealth and possessions, they will surely increase commandments and good deeds, bringing satisfaction to our Creator. But when a person is in distress, their heart is not with them, and they fall short in serving God, and poverty does not leave room for good traits, thus transgressing the will of their Creator and causing bitterness to their parent.

From all that has been said, a wise person will understand that one should greatly rejoice when seeing people doing the will of their Creator and feel deep sorrow for those who transgress. Even if one falls short in serving the Creator, they should not wish for their friend to be like them, for the distress of many is the comfort of fools. What is this like? A noblewoman with ten sons, and the king commanded them to feed and support her and provide for all her needs. One day, one of the king's sons broke the law and did not give his portion to his mother. Though he will be punished, the son who loves his mother and is distressed by his failure will ask if at least his brothers fulfilled their portion, for this would be half a comfort. If told that all betrayed like him, his distress will be great, and the mother will weep and lament for being left hungry, thirsty, and lacking everything. The analogy is clear.

Moreover, one should rejoice in a friend's good because as goodness increases, so do commandments and good deeds, and as these increase, the goodness reaches them too, so that their debts do not outweigh the world's merits, and they are punished for all the evil they cause. But when goodness increases through a friend tipping the world to merit, on a good day, they too will be in good.

King Solomon, peace be upon him, said, "Hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offenses." There are reasons for hatred to arise between a person and their brother, but all is vanity and striving after wind. Some hate because the other sinned against them physically, financially, or in honor. For this, the Torah commands, "Do not take revenge or bear a grudge." The reason is explained: the person is compelled in their actions and is a messenger of the Merciful, for God said to curse, and their bad fortune caused them to be the vehicle for obligation. Therefore, there is no place to hate one's friend, for they are like an ax in the hand of the woodcutter. What can a living man complain about, a man for his sins?

Some hate out of jealousy, thinking their livelihood is diminished by their friend, but this is only from those of little faith, as will be explained under "Faith" with divine assistance. Some hate because they see their friend not walking a good path. For this, the living should take to heart that they too have shortcomings and are still according to their level, for everything is according to the person. The scales and balances of justice are in God's hand. If their friend's inclination overpowered them and they transgressed the will of their Creator, and God showed them kindness and guided them on the right path, they should give thanks to God and be careful in the minutiae of commandments as a hair's breadth according to their knowledge and understanding and the help of the Almighty they received, and not despise and hate their brother in their heart. It is only permissible to hate a wicked person who, after being rebuked many times, still stands in rebellion and angers our Father in heaven, causing distress to the heavens. But for all other people, it is forbidden to hate. The sage greatly condemned hatred, saying, "Hatred of people removes a person from the world."

The great safeguard against hatred is fulfilling the teaching, "Judge every person favorably." Even if your eyes see the curse in the wrongdoer, do not blame them too much and hate them, for a person sees their way as right, and what can they do? A person does what is right in their own eyes. We have no choice but to pour out our soul before God, that He may correct us with good counsel from Him and guide us in paths of righteousness for His name's sake. If a person says, "But the Holy One, blessed be He, punishes a person for following their own heart and what is right in their eyes because they should have learned and did not," you should say to them, "This is for one who is strict, but one who overlooks their measures, all their transgressions are overlooked." And with the measure a person measures, they are measured. But a person should not hate their friend for not walking in the ways of the good, for if so, they should hate themselves more,
