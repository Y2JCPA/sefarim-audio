PELE YOETZ — MOURNING FOR THE DESTRUCTION OF THE TEMPLE
==================================================

Mourning for the Destruction of the Temple

Mourning for the destruction of the Temple is something that involves action, especially during the period known as "between the straits" (the three weeks leading up to the Ninth of Av). During this time, both day and night, one should place ashes on the spot where the tefillin (phylacteries) are placed and sit by the mezuzah (doorpost scroll) to recite the order of Tikkun Chatzot (midnight lamentations). However, the actions themselves are not the main point. What truly matters is having eyes that weep bitterly and a heart that is broken and crushed, like a mourner who groans with deep sorrow over the pain of the heavens. This depends on one's understanding, the depth of one's love, and the purity of one's soul.

It is true that we are very diminished and do not fully comprehend what we have lost, what we have caused with our many sins, and what the exile of the Divine Presence (Shechinah) entails, nor the extent of its suffering and the suffering of the higher and lower realms. For this, all who mourn should grieve. Nevertheless, each person is only obligated according to their capacity. One should imagine as if their mother is dressed in sackcloth, wearing black, and crying out bitterly, like a dove moaning. As the saying goes, "My head hurts, my arm hurts." "I raised and exalted children, and they have rebelled against me." Such thoughts should penetrate one's heart, causing it to become bitter, and one should pour out their soul with wailing, hoping to merit seeing the comfort of Zion and the rebuilding of our Temple and glory, speedily in our days, Amen.

From this, you can understand how wrong and bitter the actions of some people are. They gather together to celebrate with frivolous joy on Saturday nights and other nights, especially winter nights, indulging in meaningless merriment, eating, drinking, playing music, and singing inappropriate songs and songs of idol worship. Woe to them, alas for them, for without a doubt, their joy will turn to sorrow, either in this world or the next, unless they take it to heart to repent. Besides the many prohibitions they violate by sitting with scoffers and speaking obscenely through inappropriate songs, our sages have said that an ear that hears such music should be uprooted. Moreover, it is said, "A foolish son is a grief to his father and bitterness to the one who bore him." The Holy One, blessed be He, says, "Woe, cruel children, a voice is heard in Ramah, lamentation and bitter weeping, Rachel weeping for her children," while she is like a virgin dressed in sackcloth. "The Lord will roar from on high and raise His voice from His holy dwelling, roaring mightily over His habitation." The angels of peace will weep bitterly, and all the heavenly hosts, the righteous and pious, and our righteous Messiah will cry and lament, all because of the destruction of the Temple, which was destroyed due to our sins. Meanwhile, we sit and sing, rejoicing in frivolous celebrations. This is nothing but a sign of a wicked heart and blindness.

Whoever is fearful and soft-hearted, upon hearing these words, should take them to heart and refrain from such frivolous celebrations and unnecessary gatherings. Instead, one should only rejoice in celebrations that are a mitzvah (commandment), and in this way, Israel will rejoice in its Maker.

Regarding the general commandment of mourning, when someone loses a relative they are obligated to mourn, they should observe the laws of mourning as established by our sages, as explained in the Shulchan Aruch (Code of Jewish Law). Many common people are unaware and fail to observe these laws due to ignorance. They study Torah and wear leather sandals, seeking to distract themselves from mourning. If they were wise, they would understand that when divine judgment is upon them, they must be especially careful to observe everything properly and accept the heavenly decree. They should weep for three days, lamenting their sins, and during the seven days, they should engage in eulogies, recounting the virtues of the deceased and anything good found in them. They should study the laws of mourning or matters that prevent distraction from mourning, as even idle talk is forbidden to avoid distraction. By fulfilling the words of the sages, they are assured not to add to their sorrow.

It is fitting for everyone, should misfortune occur, heaven forbid, to seek books on the laws of mourning. If they do not understand Hebrew, they should find translations, such as the Shulchan Aruch in another language or the book "Me'am Loez" on the portion of Vayechi, and similar works. This is the essence of a person, to seek to know what to do according to the law, either from scholars or books, and not to go against the will of their Creator. In this way, joy will dwell in their abode.

Especially for one's father and mother, it is appropriate to show extra mourning for the entire twelve months, not attending any celebrations or meals, nor visiting anyone on Sabbaths or festivals. Instead, they should increase their study for the peace and elevation of their souls, avoiding any leisure trips. They should always consider: perhaps they are in distress, so how can I be at ease? One should not say, "What will my mourning add or change for them? Can I bring them back this way?" Not so, for it is taught in the Aggadah, as brought by the Ramban in the book "Torat HaAdam," that Moses, our teacher, peace be upon him, said to Joshua, "Joshua, wear black for me and cover yourself in black, and be careful to honor this poor soul." This implies that showing mourning for their departure is an honor for the soul. However, one should not follow the custom in some places of wearing regular clothes on the Sabbath, even during the seven days of mourning, according to the words of the holy Ari, upon whose teachings we rely. A righteous person should hold to the holy path, for all his ways are just, and his words are life.
