Chapter Two: The Man in the Winepress

Gideon was threshing wheat in a winepress, and the absurdity of it was not lost on him.

A threshing floor is wide and flat and open to the wind. You throw the grain into the air and let the breeze carry the chaff away. That’s how it works. That’s how it has always worked, since the first farmer learned that wind is the cheapest sorter in the world.

A winepress is a pit. Carved into rock, enclosed, airless. You press grapes in a winepress. You do not thresh wheat in a winepress. The grain falls straight down because there is no wind to separate it, and you end up picking chaff out of your flour for hours.

But Gideon was threshing wheat in a winepress because if he threshed it on a proper threshing floor — out in the open, visible from the road — the Midianites would see the dust and come and take everything.

So he crouched in a hole in the ground, tossing handfuls of grain into still air, sweating, muttering, doing badly what should have been done easily, because this was what life had become.

His father’s name was Yoash, from the clan of Avi’ezer, from the tribe of Menashe. They lived in Ophrah — a small village in the hill country, unremarkable except for the large oak tree in Yoash’s yard and the altar to Baal that stood beside it.

Yes. The altar to Baal.

Yoash was not a wicked man. The Radak is careful about this. Yoash kept the altar because the community expected it, because the surrounding Canaanite culture pressed in from every side, because seven years of divine silence — seven years of praying to Hashem and watching the Midianites come anyway — had eroded something. Not faith exactly. More like patience. The altar was not conviction. It was exhaustion.

Beside the altar stood an Asherah — a wooden pole or tree dedicated to the Canaanite goddess. The paired worship — Baal for rain, Asherah for fertility — was everywhere in Israel by now. In fields, in courtyards, at crossroads. The God of Sinai had not been replaced. He had been supplemented. Which was worse.

Gideon hated the altar. He said nothing about it — not yet — but his silence was the silence of a man holding something back, and what he was holding back was rage.

The angel came.

He sat down under the oak tree in Yoash’s yard — quietly, the way a traveler sits down to rest in the shade. The Ralbag explains that the angel appeared in the form of a human being, an ordinary man, and that Gideon did not initially recognize him as anything more.

"Hashem is with you, mighty man of valor," the angel said.

Gideon looked up from the winepress. His face was streaked with sweat and chaff. His hands were raw. He looked at this stranger sitting comfortably under the tree and felt something turn over inside him — not anger exactly, but the cousin of anger. Incredulity.

"My lord," Gideon said. "If Hashem is with us, why has all this happened to us? Where are all His wonders that our fathers told us about? They said, ‘Did not Hashem bring us up from Mitzrayim?’ But now Hashem has abandoned us. He has given us into the hand of Midian."

The Gemara in Sanhedrin discusses this response, and the discussion reveals something extraordinary. Gideon was not complaining for himself. He was arguing on behalf of his people — the way Avraham argued for Sodom, the way Moshe argued after the Golden Calf. "Where are all His wonders?" was not the whine of a faithless man. It was the challenge of a man who believed deeply enough to demand an answer.

Rashi on Shoftim 6:13 cites the tradition that Gideon said to the angel: "Last night was Pesach, and my father read the Hallel to me, and I heard: ‘When Israel went out of Mitzrayim.’ If Hashem did those miracles for our fathers, where are the miracles for us?"

This detail changes everything. Gideon was not ignorant of the tradition. He had just heard it. The night before this encounter — Pesach night, the night of memory, the night every Jewish family relives the Exodus — his father had recited the story. And Gideon had listened. And the distance between the story and his reality — between a God who splits seas and a man threshing wheat in a pit to hide from looters — was unbearable.

The angel’s response was unexpected.

"Go in this strength of yours," Hashem said. "And you shall save Israel from the hand of Midian. Have I not sent you?"

This strength of yours. The Metzudat David explains: the strength Gideon had just demonstrated — the strength of advocating for Israel, of demanding justice on behalf of the oppressed, of refusing to accept the suffering as normal. That was the qualification. Not military training. Not family prestige. Not physical power. The strength of a man who looked at the suffering of his people and said: this is wrong, and where is God?

Gideon stared at the angel. "My lord, how can I save Israel? My clan is the poorest in Menashe. And I am the youngest in my father’s house."

The humility was genuine. The Radak points out that Gideon was not being coy. He was stating facts. The Avi’ezer clan was not powerful. Gideon was not a firstborn. He had no army, no resources, no political standing. He was the youngest son of a marginal family in a defeated tribe, crouching in a hole in the ground.

"I will be with you," Hashem said. "And you will strike Midian as one man."

As one man. The Malbim unpacks this: not "you will defeat their army." You will strike the entire Midianite force as though it were a single opponent. The victory would be so total, so complete, that the vast multitude would collapse as if it had no more substance than one man falling.

Gideon asked for a sign. He prepared an offering — a young goat, matzot, and broth — and brought it to the angel under the oak tree. The angel told him to place the meat and the matzot on a rock and pour the broth over them.

Then the angel touched the offering with the tip of his staff, and fire rose from the rock and consumed everything. The meat. The matzot. The broth. All of it — consumed in an instant by a fire that came from stone.

And the angel vanished.

Gideon stood alone in the empty yard, staring at the scorched rock, with the smell of the offering still hanging in the air and the world rearranging itself around him.

"Alas, Hashem God!" he cried. "For I have seen the angel of Hashem face to face!"

The terror was real. The tradition held that seeing a divine messenger meant death. But Hashem spoke to him — this time directly, without the intermediary of the angelic form:

"Shalom lecha. Peace to you. Do not fear. You shall not die."

Gideon built an altar on that spot. He called it Hashem Shalom — "God is Peace." The name was not a theological statement. It was the gasp of a man who had just survived a divine encounter and was trying to name what he felt. The terror had been real. But so had the peace that followed it. And the peace was stronger.

The altar called Hashem Shalom stood in Ophrah, in the same yard where the altar to Baal stood.

Two altars. One yard.

That would not last.
