Chapter Four: The Fleece on the Threshing Floor

The spirit of Hashem clothed Gideon.

The Hebrew word is lavshah — "clothed," as in a garment. Not "came upon" or "descended." The Ruach HaKodesh wrapped itself around him like a robe. Rashi explains: the spirit clothed him the way a person puts on a coat. It was that intimate, that encompassing. He was inside the spirit and the spirit was inside him, and for the first time in his life, the youngest son of the poorest clan in Menashe felt the full weight of what it meant to be chosen.

He blew the shofar.

But before he blew it, there was a conversation.

His cousin Azaryah — a man ten years older, a farmer with a bad knee and a sharp tongue — found him at the edge of the village, holding the ram’s horn, staring at the hills.

"You’re serious," Azaryah said. It was not a question.

"I’m serious."

"You want to blow that horn and call every man in Avi’ezer to war. Against the Midianites. The Midianites who come like locusts. The ones we’ve been hiding from for seven years."

"Yes."

Azaryah leaned on his walking stick. "Gideon. Three days ago, half this village wanted to kill you for smashing the altar. Now you want them to follow you into battle?"

"Hashem told me to."

"Hashem told you." Azaryah studied his cousin’s face. He was looking for madness, for delusion, for the wild-eyed certainty of a man who has mistaken his own thoughts for divine instruction. He didn’t find it. What he found was something harder to dismiss: calm. The unshakeable calm of a man standing on ground he trusts.

"The fire came from the rock, Azaryah. You saw the scorch marks. You saw the altar."

"I saw scorch marks. I didn’t see fire from Heaven."

"Then trust me. Or trust our fathers. Or trust the God who brought us out of Mitzrayim. Trust something. Because I’m blowing this shofar whether you come or not."

Azaryah was quiet for a long time. A fly buzzed between them. Somewhere in the valley, a Midianite drum was beating.

"If I die out there," Azaryah said finally, "my wife will never forgive you."

"If we stay here, there’s nothing left for your wife to forgive."

Azaryah nodded. He didn’t smile. But he nodded. "Blow your horn."

The sound went out from Ophrah across the hills. Avi’ezer answered first — his own clan, the people who had tried to kill him days ago for destroying Baal’s altar. Now they followed him. The reversal was total. The man they wanted dead was now the man they followed to war. Because the truth about Baal had sunk in, and the truth about Hashem was rising.

Messengers went to Menashe, and Menashe gathered. Then to Asher, Zevulun, and Naftali. The tribes came. Not all of them — as in Devorah’s time, some stayed home. But enough came. Thirty-two thousand men.

And then Gideon wavered.

He stood alone on the threshing floor at sunset, with an army camped below him and the Midianite horde filling the valley to the east like a dark sea, and he prayed.

"If You will save Israel by my hand, as You have said — behold, I am placing a fleece of wool on the threshing floor. If dew is on the fleece alone, and all the ground is dry, then I will know that You will save Israel by my hand, as You have said."

Morning came. Gideon went to the threshing floor before dawn. He squeezed the fleece and wrung out dew — a bowl full of water. The ground around it was dry.

It was a miracle. It should have been enough.

It was not enough.

"Do not let Your anger burn against me," Gideon said. "Let me test just once more with the fleece. Let it now be dry only upon the fleece, and upon all the ground let there be dew."

This second test was, the Ralbag explains, actually harder to perform as a miracle than the first. Dew falls on everything — it is the nature of dew to be universal. A fleece naturally absorbs moisture. To keep a fleece dry while everything around it is wet requires a more pointed intervention, a more specific overturning of natural law. Gideon was not asking for less. He was asking for more.

And Hashem did it.

Morning came again. The ground was soaked. The fleece was dry.

Two miracles. Two tests. Two signs that the God who had spoken from the burning rock under the oak tree was not finished speaking, was not leaving, was not abandoning His people to the locusts and the looters. The conversation that began in the winepress was still going.

The commentators debate whether Gideon’s request for signs reflected weakness of faith or the proper caution of a leader about to send men to their deaths. The Malbim leans toward the latter: Gideon was not testing God for himself. He needed certainty because the lives of thirty-two thousand men depended on it. A general who leads an army into battle on the basis of a feeling he cannot verify is not faithful. He is reckless.

But there is a cost to needing signs. The Radak notes that this back-and-forth — the repeated asking, the need for confirmation — is what would eventually lead Hashem to reduce Gideon’s army so drastically. You asked for signs because you were not certain? Then I will make the victory so miraculous that no sign will be needed afterward. You will know because the thing itself will be so impossible that no explanation except God will suffice.

Gideon came down from the threshing floor. The fleece was dry. The ground was wet. And somewhere in the camp, thirty-two thousand men were sharpening whatever they had — swords, spears, farming tools, sharpened sticks — and waiting for orders.

They did not yet know that most of them were about to be sent home.
