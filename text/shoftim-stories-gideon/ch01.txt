Chapter One: The Locusts

They came with the east wind.

Every spring, when the barley was just beginning to head and the wheat stood green in the fields, the dust rose on the eastern horizon and the Midianites poured across the Yarden like a plague of living flesh. Not an army — armies march in formation, armies have banners and discipline and supply lines. This was something else. This was a migration. Men, women, children, tens of thousands of them, driving their herds before them, spreading across the Yizrael Valley in a tide of tents and livestock and hunger.

And behind them — always behind them, trailing in columns that darkened the roads for miles — came the Amalekites and the Bnei Kedem, the people of the east.

A farmer named Achituv, from the tribe of Menashe, stood on the ridge above his fields and watched them come. He had watched them come every year for seven years. He knew the pattern. They would arrive just as the crops were ready for harvest. They would set up their tents on his land. Their animals would devour what was left in the fields, and their men would take what the animals missed. When there was nothing left — no grain, no oil, no sheep, no ox, no donkey — they would move on, leaving the land stripped bare, as if a fire had passed through.

Achituv's neighbor, a man named Pedatzur, had stopped planting two years ago.

"Why?" Pedatzur had said, his voice flat. "Tell me why I should break my back plowing and seeding and weeding when they'll take it all anyway. I might as well plant stones."

Achituv had no answer. He planted anyway. Because his father had planted, and his father's father, and because the land was an inheritance from Hashem and a man does not abandon his inheritance even when the inheritance feels like a curse.

But it was getting harder.

The Midrash Tanchuma describes what the Midianite incursions did to Israel in terms that go beyond the language of the text. It was not merely theft. It was obliteration. The invaders came b’mispar — in numbers beyond counting — like locusts. The comparison was not poetic. It was precise. Locusts do not leave anything. They consume the stalk and the leaf and the husk. They eat until there is nothing, and then they move on to the next field. The Midianites did the same.

And like locusts, they were impossible to fight. You cannot swing a sword at a swarm.

Devorah's victory over Sisera had brought forty years of peace. An entire generation had grown up without fear. But that generation had done what prosperous generations always do — they had forgotten. The God who drowned Pharaoh's chariots, who sent stars to fight at the Kishon, who split the sea and fed His people in the wilderness — that God became a memory, then a story, then a formality, then an afterthought.

The Malbim explains that the backsliding after Devorah was particularly severe because it came on the heels of such an extraordinary miracle. When people witness divine intervention and still drift away, the drift carries a different weight. It is not the drift of ignorance. It is the drift of ingratitude. And ingratitude, in the economy of Heaven, costs more than ignorance.

Rashi on Shoftim 6:1 states it plainly: they did what was evil in the eyes of Hashem, and Hashem gave them into the hand of Midian for seven years.

Seven years. Not twenty, like Sisera. The punishment was shorter but in some ways more devastating, because the Midianite oppression struck at the most fundamental thing a farmer possesses: the ability to eat what he grows.

The people fled to the mountains.

This detail from the text is almost unbearable: "Because of Midian, the children of Israel made themselves the dens which are in the mountains, and the caves, and the strongholds." A people who had conquered this land under Yehoshua, who had stood at Sinai and heard the voice of God, were now living in holes in the ground. Hiding in caves. Crouching in mountain dens like animals, watching from the heights as the invaders moved through their fields.

In a village in the territory of Menashe, near a place called Ophrah, a woman named Channah — not the Channah of Shiloh but a different Channah, a farmer's wife — gathered her three children against the back wall of a shallow cave and covered them with a blanket.

"Stay quiet," she whispered. "Don't come out until I tell you."

Her oldest, a girl of eleven, looked at her mother with eyes that had seen too much for eleven years.

"Where are you going?"

"To see if they've passed."

"Imma —"

"Stay. Quiet."

Channah crawled to the mouth of the cave and looked down into the valley. The Midianite tents stretched across the fields like a second crop — a crop of goatskin and tent poles and stolen livestock. She could hear the bleating of sheep. Her sheep, probably. Or what used to be her sheep.

Somewhere below, in the ruins of her daily life, her husband Yoash was trying to salvage whatever the invaders had left. Which would be nothing. It was always nothing.

The caves had names now. That was how settled the arrangement had become. The deep one near the spring was called "Goren's cave" after the family that had claimed it first. The narrow one with the overhang was "the oven" because it trapped heat. The shallow one where Channah crouched was "the shelf" because it was barely deep enough to lie down in. Seven years ago, these caves had been places children explored on summer afternoons, daring each other to go deeper. Now they were homes.

A man could lose his dignity in a cave. The ceiling was too low to stand. The ground was too hard to sleep well. The smoke from cooking fires had nowhere to go. Children who should have been running through fields of barley were learning to sit still in the dark, to whisper, to make themselves small. The Midianites had not merely stolen Israel's grain. They had stolen Israel's posture. A people meant to walk upright before their God were learning to crawl.

And then the prophet came.

The text does not name him. The Radak notes that he was a prophet sent specifically for this moment — not to deliver the people, but to prepare them for deliverance. Before the rescue came the rebuke. Before the sword came the mirror.

The unnamed prophet stood in the gathering places of Israel — such as they were, hidden in the hills, away from the Midianite patrols — and spoke.

"Thus says Hashem, the God of Israel: I brought you up from Mitzrayim. I brought you forth from the house of bondage. I delivered you from the hand of the Egyptians and from the hand of all who oppressed you. I drove them out before you and gave you their land."

The people listened. They knew this story. Every Pesach, every Shabbat, every time a father told his son about the past, these words echoed.

"And I said to you: I am Hashem your God. Do not fear the gods of the Amorites in whose land you dwell."

A pause. The prophet's voice dropped.

"But you did not listen to My voice."

That was all. No promise of rescue. No announcement of a deliverer. Just the diagnosis.

The silence that followed was worse than the rebuke.

Then a man in the back — a farmer from the valley, gaunt and sunburned — spoke up. "So this is our fault? We brought this on ourselves?"

The prophet looked at him. He did not answer. The question answered itself.

"My children eat roots," the farmer said. His voice shook. "My wife hasn’t slept through the night in three years because she listens for hoofbeats. And you’re telling me this is because we didn’t listen?"

An older man beside him put a hand on his arm. "He’s telling us why. Not to punish us. To show us the way back."

"The way back to what? More prayers that go unanswered?"

"The prayers aren’t unanswered," the older man said quietly. "He sent the prophet, didn’t he?"

The farmer had no response to that. He sat down heavily on a stone and covered his face with his hands.

The prophet left. The people sat in the gathering place, in the dim light of a cave or a hidden clearing, and absorbed what he had said. Some wept. Some argued. Some shrugged and went back to their caves, because after seven years of having everything stolen, a speech about God — however true — felt abstract.

But the seed was planted. The cry was building. Like the cry that had risen before Devorah, slowly, house by house, village by village, a whisper that would become a roar.

Vayitz’aku Bnei Yisrael el Hashem.

And in a village called Ophrah, in the territory of Menashe, a young man was threshing wheat in a winepress, hiding from the Midianites, and he did not yet know that an angel was about to sit down under the oak tree in his father’s yard and change everything.
