Chapter Six: Torches and Trumpets

He divided the three hundred into three companies.

Each man received three things: a shofar, an empty clay pitcher, and a torch. The torch went inside the pitcher, its flame hidden by the clay. When Gideon gave the signal, each man would break the pitcher, reveal the torch, blow the shofar, and shout.

No swords. No spears. No shields. Three hundred men going into battle carrying musical instruments and pottery.

The Malbim explains the strategy — and it is, in purely military terms, one of the most elegant ambushes in ancient warfare. By surrounding the camp with three companies, each man holding a torch, the Midianites would wake to see three hundred fires ringing their position and hear three hundred shofarot blasting simultaneously from every direction. In the darkness and confusion, they would not be able to count their attackers. Three hundred torches would look like three hundred companies. Three hundred shofarot would sound like an army of thirty thousand.

It was psychological warfare. And it was devastatingly effective precisely because it relied on the enemy’s own fear to do the killing.

"Watch me," Gideon told his men. "Do as I do. When I come to the edge of the camp, whatever I do, you do the same. When I blow the shofar — I and all who are with me — then you blow your shofarot on every side of the camp. And call out: ‘A sword for Hashem and for Gideon!’"

A sword for Hashem and for Gideon. The order mattered. Hashem first. Then Gideon. Because the three hundred men on the hillside needed to remember — every one of them, every moment — whose battle this was.

The middle watch.

The darkest part of the night, when sleep is deepest and the guards are groggiest. Gideon and his company reached the edge of the Midianite camp. They could hear the animals shifting, the murmur of sleeping men, the faint crackle of dying campfires. The camp was vast — a city of tents stretching into the darkness in every direction.

Gideon looked to the left. He looked to the right. Somewhere in the dark, the other two companies were in position. He could not see them. He could not signal them. He had to trust that they were there, just as they had to trust that he was here.

He raised the shofar to his lips.

He blew.

The sound split the night like lightning splits a sky. And from the darkness on every side, a hundred shofarot answered. And then two hundred more. Three hundred blasts shattering the silence of the valley, echoing off the hills, rolling across the Midianite camp like the voice of an army that had no business being there.

Gideon smashed his pitcher. The torch blazed to life in his hand, fire erupting from broken clay. Around the camp, three hundred pitchers shattered. Three hundred torches flared. A ring of fire appeared from nowhere, encircling the Midianite host.

"A SWORD FOR HASHEM AND FOR GIDEON!"

The shout came from three hundred throats, from every direction at once, and the camp exploded.

Men jolted from sleep into a world of noise and fire. Shofarot blasting. Torches burning. Voices screaming from every side. The Midianite soldiers grabbed their weapons and struck at whatever was nearest — which, in the dark and the chaos, was each other.

"Hashem set the sword of each man against his fellow throughout the entire camp," the text records. The Radak explains: in the pitch-black confusion, with torches ringing them and shofarot deafening them, the Midianites could not tell friend from enemy. They fought each other. The army that had terrorized Israel for seven years tore itself apart in a single night, without the three hundred ever drawing a sword.

The rout was total. The Midianites fled toward the Yarden — toward Beit Shittah, toward Tzererah, toward the border of Avel Mecholah and Tabbat. They ran in every direction, stumbling over their own dead, slashing at shadows, driven by a terror that no human army could have produced.

In the camp, the scene was chaos beyond description. Tents collapsed. Animals stampeded. Men who had been sleeping peacefully an hour ago were now running barefoot through the dark, striking at anything that moved. The Midianite commanders tried to rally their forces, but their shouts were swallowed by the blare of three hundred shofarot still sounding from the hills, and their orders were obeyed by men too panicked to tell a command from a scream.

The camels — thousands of them, the wealth and the transport of the Midianite host — bolted. They crashed through tent lines and trampled sleeping men and scattered into the night, carrying provisions and weapons into the darkness where no one would find them. By morning, the great Midianite encampment that had covered the valley like a living carpet was a wasteland of broken tents and scattered gear and bodies.

And Gideon’s three hundred — still holding their torches and their shofarot, still shouting into the darkness — pursued.

Gideon sent messengers throughout the hill country of Ephraim. "Come down against Midian! Seize the fords of the Yarden ahead of them!"

The men of Ephraim came. They captured the fords. They cut off the Midianite retreat and seized two of the Midianite princes — Orev and Ze’ev. They killed Orev at the rock that would forever bear his name, and Ze’ev at the winepress of Ze’ev. They brought the heads to Gideon.

And then Ephraim turned on him.

"What is this thing you have done to us, that you did not call us when you went to fight Midian?" Their anger was real. Hot. The kind of tribal pride that could curdle a victory into a civil war.

Gideon looked at the leaders of Ephraim. These were powerful men, from the most prestigious tribe in the north. Men who did not tolerate being overlooked.

His response was a masterpiece of diplomacy.

"What have I done now compared to you? Is not the gleaning of Ephraim’s grapes better than the vintage of Avi’ezer?"

The meaning was layered. The main harvest of Gideon’s clan — the initial rout, the shattering of pitchers in the dark — was nothing compared to what Ephraim had accomplished at the fords. Your mopping up, your gleaning, was worth more than my whole harvest. Your aftermath was greater than my battle.

The Radak observes that Gideon was not lying. Ephraim had captured the two princes. They had sealed the victory. Without Ephraim at the fords, the Midianites would have escaped across the Yarden and regrouped. But Gideon presented this truth in a way that soothed wounded pride without diminishing his own men’s achievement.

"God delivered the princes of Midian, Orev and Ze’ev, into your hand," Gideon said. "And what was I able to do compared to you?"

Ephraim’s anger cooled. Their spirit softened. They had been given credit. They had been given honor. And the crisis passed.

The Malbim draws the contrast with what would happen a generation later, when Yiftach faced the same complaint from Ephraim and answered with a sword instead of a proverb. That exchange ended with forty-two thousand dead Ephraimites at the fords of the Yarden. Gideon’s humility saved lives that Yiftach’s pride would later destroy.
