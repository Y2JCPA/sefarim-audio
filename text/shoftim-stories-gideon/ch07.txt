Chapter Seven: The Aftermath

The pursuit continued across the Yarden.

Three hundred men who had not eaten, who had not slept, who had been running and shouting and fighting since the middle of the night, crossed the river and kept going. The text says they were ayefim v’rodfim — exhausted but pursuing. Those two words contain a universe. They were spent. Empty. Running on nothing but adrenaline and faith. And they did not stop.

They came to Sukkot, a city on the eastern side of the Yarden. Gideon asked for bread.

"Give bread, please, to the people who follow me, for they are exhausted. I am pursuing Zevach and Tzalmuna, kings of Midian."

The elders of Sukkot refused.

"Are Zevach and Tzalmuna already in your hand, that we should give bread to your army?"

The meaning was clear: we’re not wasting our food on a man who hasn’t won yet. Come back when you’ve actually caught them, and we’ll talk. There were seventy-seven elders in Sukkot, and not one of them — not a single one — stood up and said: these men are fighting for all of us. These men are bleeding for our freedom. The least we can do is give them bread.

Gideon’s men stood in the street of Sukkot, swaying with fatigue, and watched the elders turn their backs. Some of the soldiers had been running since midnight. Their feet were blistered. Their throats were raw from shouting. They had just accomplished something that would be remembered for three thousand years, and the people they had fought to liberate would not give them a piece of bread.

It was cowardice dressed as pragmatism. The elders of Sukkot were hedging. If Gideon failed — if the Midianite kings escaped and regrouped — then anyone who had helped the Israelite pursuit would face retribution. Better to wait. Better to see who wins before choosing a side.

The Radak compares this to Reuven in Devorah’s song — the tribe that sat among the sheepfolds, listening to the piping of flocks, waiting to see which way the wind blew. Some failures are not acts of evil. They are acts of calculation. And calculation, when the moment demands faith, is its own form of betrayal.

Gideon looked at the elders of Sukkot, and something hardened in his face.

"When Hashem delivers Zevach and Tzalmuna into my hand," he said quietly, "I will thresh your flesh with thorns of the wilderness and with briers."

He moved on. He came to Penuel and made the same request. The men of Penuel gave the same refusal. Gideon gave a similar warning.

Then he and his three hundred continued east, into the desert, after the kings.

He caught them at Karkor, with the remnant of their army — fifteen thousand men, all that remained of the 135,000 who had crossed the Yarden at the beginning of the campaign. Gideon struck the camp by surprise, just as he had at Ein Charod, and the camp scattered. He captured Zevach and Tzalmuna alive.

The two kings were brought before him, bound, their royal garments torn and muddied. They were large men — the text would later note that they had the bearing of kings, the appearance of princes. Even in captivity, something in them carried authority.

Gideon looked at them. He asked a question that had nothing to do with the war.

"The men you killed at Tavor," he said. "What did they look like?"

Zevach and Tzalmuna exchanged a glance. Whatever they had expected from their captor, this was not it.

"They looked like you," Tzalmuna said. "Each one had the bearing of a king’s son."

"They were my brothers," Gideon said. His voice was level, but something beneath it was not. "Sons of my mother. As Hashem lives — if you had let them live, I would not kill you."

The Radak explains that this was not merely personal vengeance. Gideon was establishing a principle: the Midianite kings had murdered Israelites not in battle but in cold blood, on Har Tavor. They had killed men who were not soldiers but brothers, kin, members of Gideon’s own family. Justice required an accounting.

Gideon turned to his eldest son, Yeter, a boy still young enough that his voice had not fully deepened. "Rise. Kill them."

The boy reached for his sword. His hand was shaking. He drew it halfway and stopped. The weight of the blade, or the weight of the moment, or both — something held him. He could not do it. He was a boy, and they were kings, and the distance between the two was too great for his arm to cross.

Zevach looked at Gideon. "Come, you do it yourself," he said. "For as the man is, so is his strength."

Even at the end, the king of Midian understood the principle: a boy’s sword does not carry a man’s authority. If this was to be done, it should be done by the one who had earned the right to do it.

Gideon rose and killed them both.

On the way home, he kept his word. He returned to Sukkot with the two captured kings’ ornaments and made the elders face the consequence of their refusal. He returned to Penuel and tore down their tower.

These were not the acts of a gentle man. They were the acts of a man who understood that indifference in the hour of crisis is not neutral. The elders of Sukkot and Penuel had refused to feed exhausted men pursuing the enemy of Israel. They had weighed their comfort against the liberation of their people and chosen comfort. Gideon’s punishment was harsh, and the text does not pretend otherwise. But the harshness was proportional to the stakes.

After the victory, the men of Israel came to Gideon with an offer.

"Rule over us — you, and your son, and your son’s son. For you have saved us from the hand of Midian."

They were offering him a dynasty. A kingdom. Everything a man in the ancient world could want: permanent power, hereditary authority, the guarantee that his name would endure through generations.

Gideon’s answer has reverberated through every subsequent discussion of Jewish kingship.

"I will not rule over you. And my son will not rule over you. Hashem will rule over you."

The Radak explains that Gideon was not being humble for the sake of humility. He was articulating a theological principle: Israel’s king is Hashem. Human leaders serve. They do not reign. The moment a man accepts a crown, he places himself between the people and God, and that space belongs to no one.

It was the right answer. Perhaps the greatest answer any leader in Sefer Shoftim ever gave.

And then Gideon made a request that would undo everything.

"Let me make one request of you: every man give me the earrings from his spoil."

The Midianites had been wealthy. Their earrings were gold. The Israelites threw them willingly onto a spread garment, and the total came to seventeen hundred gold shekels — an enormous sum, not counting the ornaments, the crescents, and the purple garments of the Midianite kings.

Gideon took the gold and made an ephod.

An ephod. The priestly garment. The instrument of divine inquiry. Whether Gideon intended it as a legitimate religious object or as a commemorative monument, the text does not say. The Radak suggests Gideon’s intention was for the sake of Heaven — he wanted a way to consult Hashem, to seek divine guidance outside of the Mishkan at Shiloh. Perhaps he thought that the man who had received prophecy directly from an angel, who had spoken with Hashem at the winepress and on the threshing floor, could establish a secondary channel for divine communication. Perhaps he thought that what had begun with such purity could remain pure.

He was wrong.

What the text says is what happened next:

"And all Israel went astray after it there. And it became a snare to Gideon and to his house."

The Radak says Gideon’s intention was for the sake of Heaven — he wanted a way to consult Hashem, to seek divine guidance. But the road to idolatry is paved with good intentions. The people began to worship the ephod itself. They treated it as a sacred object, traveled to Ophrah to bow before it, offered incense to it. The instrument became the idol. The means became the end.

The man who had torn down his father’s altar to Baal, who had burned the Asherah and sacrificed the seven-year-old bull on its wood, who had thundered "Hashem will rule over you" to the adoring crowd — that man created a golden object that led his own people back into the very sin he had fought to destroy.

The Tanach does not look away from this. It never looks away. The same text that records Gideon’s courage at the winepress and his brilliance at Ein Charod records the ephod. The same narrative that celebrates his humility before the men of Israel notes, with devastating understatement, that the golden object "became a snare to Gideon and to his house."

And the land had rest forty years in the days of Gideon.

Forty years. A generation of peace, bought by three hundred men with torches and trumpets, earned by a man who threshed wheat in a winepress and ended up commanding the attention of Heaven.

Gideon died in a good old age and was buried in the tomb of Yoash his father, in Ophrah of the Avi’ezer clan. He had seventy sons — "for he had many wives," the text notes — and a concubine in Shechem who bore him a son named Avimelech.

Avimelech. The name means "my father is king."

The man who refused a crown named his son "my father is king."

Perhaps this was a statement of theology — my father serves the true King. Perhaps it was something darker — the shadow of ambition that even the most righteous refusal cannot entirely extinguish. The text does not say. But the name would prove prophetic in the worst possible way, because Avimelech — the son of the concubine, the boy from Shechem — would one day claim the throne his father rejected, and he would claim it over the bodies of his own brothers.

But that is another story.

This story ends where it began — in the small, unremarkable landscape of a man’s daily life. A winepress. A threshing floor. An oak tree. A pitcher of clay and a torch.

Gideon was not born great. He was born small — the youngest son of the weakest clan in a defeated tribe, hiding in a hole in the ground, threshing wheat where grapes should be pressed. Everything about his origin story says: this man is nobody.

And the angel said: "Hashem is with you, mighty man of valor."

The valor was not in his muscles or his sword arm. It was in his question. Where are the wonders? Where is the God who split the sea? Where is the God our fathers told us about?

The question was the qualification. The demand for God’s presence was the proof of God’s presence. Because only a man who believed could ask with that kind of anguish, and only a man who loved his people could ask on their behalf.

Go in this strength of yours.

Three hundred men. Torches and trumpets. A barley cake that flattened a tent. And a God who delights in choosing the smallest and the least and the most improbable, and using them to shatter empires.

The land had rest forty years.

And then, as it always does in Sefer Shoftim, the cycle turned again.
