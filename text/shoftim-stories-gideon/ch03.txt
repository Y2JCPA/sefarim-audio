Chapter Three: The Altar at Midnight

That same night, Hashem spoke to Gideon again.

"Take your father’s bull — the second bull, the one that is seven years old. Tear down the altar of Baal that belongs to your father. Cut down the Asherah that stands beside it. Build an altar to Hashem your God on the top of this stronghold, in the proper arrangement. Take the second bull and offer it as a burnt offering, using the wood of the Asherah that you shall cut down."

Gideon sat in the darkness of his room and absorbed the command. The first mission Hashem gave him was not to fight the Midianites. It was to fight the idolatry in his own father’s yard.

The Malbim explains the sequence: before Israel could be saved from external enemies, it had to be freed from its internal corruption. The altar to Baal in Yoash’s courtyard was not incidental. It was the disease. The Midianites were the symptom. Treating symptoms without addressing the disease is not healing. It is postponement.

The bull was seven years old. Rashi notes the significance: the bull had been alive for exactly as long as the Midianite oppression. Seven years of suffering, seven years of the bull growing beside the altar of Baal. The bull and the oppression were born together. Destroying one while sacrificing the other was a statement: this ends now. All of it. The idolatry and the suffering it caused.

Gideon took ten of his servants. He went at night.

The text is honest about this: "Because he feared his father’s household and the men of the city, he could not do it by day, so he did it by night." He was afraid. The man whom the angel had called "mighty man of valor" was afraid of his neighbors. The Radak does not condemn him for this. Fear and courage are not opposites. They are partners. Courage is what fear looks like when it decides to act anyway.

They worked quickly. The Asherah came down first — hacked at the base, toppled, the wood dragged to the hilltop. Then the altar of Baal — stone by stone, dismantled, the pieces scattered. In its place, Gideon’s servants built a new altar, rough stones stacked in the darkness, arranged according to the law. The seven-year-old bull was slaughtered, dressed, and placed on the wood of the Asherah.

Fire. The offering burned. The wood of the idol fed the flames of the sacrifice to Hashem. There is a poetry in this that the text does not need to explain: the thing that had led them astray was now being consumed in the service of the God they had abandoned.

By morning, it was done.

"Who did this?"

The men of the city woke to find their altar destroyed, their Asherah cut down, and the remains of a sacrifice smoldering on a new altar on the hilltop. Fury swept through Ophrah like a brushfire.

"Who did this?"

They investigated. They asked questions. They found answers. The answers pointed to Gideon.

"Bring out your son," they said to Yoash. "He must die. He tore down the altar of Baal. He cut down the Asherah."

A crowd gathered. Torches. Angry voices. The mob energy that turns neighbors into executioners. Gideon’s life hung on whatever his father said next.

Yoash stepped out to face the crowd. And what he said was one of the most remarkable things any father has said in the history of Scripture.

"Will you fight Baal’s battle? Will you save him? Whoever fights for Baal will be put to death by morning! If he is a god, let him fight for himself, since someone has torn down his altar."

The logic was devastating. If Baal is a god, he doesn’t need a mob to defend him. If he can’t defend his own altar, what kind of god is he? The very fact that you need to avenge him proves he’s powerless.

The Metzudat David explains that Yoash’s argument was not merely clever. It was prophetic without being prophecy. It articulated the central truth that seven years of Midianite oppression should have taught: the gods of Canaan cannot save. They cannot protect their own altars. They certainly cannot protect you.

The crowd hesitated. The logic held. No one could argue with it without admitting that Baal was too weak to defend himself.

They dispersed. And from that day, Gideon received a new name: Yerubaal — "Let Baal contend with him." The name was originally a taunt, meant as a dare. Let Baal fight his own battles. Let the god of the smashed altar take his own revenge.

Baal never did.

The Radak observes that the name stuck because the contention never materialized. Day after day, Gideon walked through Ophrah alive and unharmed. The altar of Baal lay in ruins. The Asherah was ashes. And the man who had destroyed them thrived. Every day that passed without Baal’s vengeance was another argument for the God of Israel.
