Chapter Three: The Child of Shiloh She named him Shmuel. "Because I asked him of Hashem," she told Elkana, holding the infant in her arms. The name was a statement — not just a label but a declaration of origin. This child did not come from the natural order of things. This child was petitioned, argued for, wept over, demanded. He was the answer to a prayer that had shaken the doorposts of the Mishkan. The Midrash adds a dimension to the name. Every day, a divine voice would resound throughout the world saying: "A righteous man is destined to arise, and his name will be Shmuel." Women who bore sons would name them Shmuel — hopefully, expectantly, wondering if their child was the one. But when the people saw those boys grow, they would say: "This is not Shmuel." When Chanah's son was born and they saw his deeds, they said: "It seems that this one is the expected righteous man." The child was marked before he drew his first breath. Chanah did not go up to Shiloh that year. Or the next. She stayed home in Ramah, nursing the boy, holding him close, counting the days. "I will not go up," she told Elkana, "until the child is weaned. Then I will bring him, and he will appear before Hashem and dwell there forever." Forever. The word landed heavily in the room. Elkana looked at his wife — this woman who had prayed with a ferocity that made a Kohen Gadol mistake her for a drunkard — and he understood what she was saying. She was not coming to Shiloh for a visit. She was coming to leave her child there. The child she had begged for, fought for, threatened Heaven for. She was going to hand him over. "Do what seems good in your eyes," Elkana said. "Stay until you have weaned him. Only may Hashem fulfill His word." The Radak explains Elkana's response: he supported her completely. There was no argument, no resistance, no attempt to renegotiate the vow. Chanah had promised Hashem that if she received a son, she would dedicate him to service in the Mishkan. Elkana honored that promise as though it were his own. But the weaning years — two years, perhaps three — were a kind of mercy. A window of time in which Chanah could hold the child who would not be hers to hold for much longer. She must have memorized him. The weight of him in her arms. The sound of his breathing at night. The way his fingers curled around hers. Every mother memorizes these things without

trying. Chanah would have memorized them with the desperate attention of a woman who knew the clock was ticking.

The day came. She packed three bulls, an ephah of flour, and a jug of wine. She dressed the boy. She loaded the donkeys. And she walked the road to Shiloh — one of Elkana's roads, through the hill country, past the villages where families still remembered the year they had joined the caravan and returned changed. This time, Chanah was the one who would return changed. She was walking toward a doorpost where she had once poured out her soul in anguish. She was returning to pour out something more costly. They reached Shiloh. They slaughtered the bull. They brought the child to Eli. Chanah stood before the old Kohen — the same man who had accused her of drunkenness, the same man who had blessed her, the same man whose words had carried the weight of prophecy. "My lord," she said. "As your soul lives, my lord, I am the woman who stood here beside you, praying to Hashem. For this child I prayed, and Hashem granted me my request which I asked of Him." She looked at the boy. He was small. Two or three years old — old enough to walk, old enough to speak simple sentences, young enough that the world was still enormous and every face was a wonder. "And I have lent him to Hashem," she said. "All the days that he lives, he is lent to Hashem." She placed the child's hand into Eli's hand. The Metzudat David explains the word hish'iltihu — "I have lent him." Not given. Lent. The child still belonged to Hashem, who had given him, and now Chanah was returning the loan. But the language of lending implies something else too — that Chanah still had a claim. That a thread of connection would remain. That the loan was ongoing, that she was still the lender, still the mother, still the one who had asked. It was the most generous act of faith since Avraham walked up the mountain with Yitzchak.

But before she left — before she walked back down the road to Ramah with empty arms and a full heart — something happened that the Gemara records with extraordinary dramatic detail. The boy had been watching the preparations for the korbanot. He saw the Kohanim preparing to slaughter the bull, and he saw them calling for a Kohen to perform the shechitah. The child spoke. "Why are you looking for a Kohen to slaughter?" he said. His voice was small but clear — the voice of a child who had been listening to his mother discuss Torah since before he could walk. "Shechitah is valid when performed by a non-Kohen." The words fell into the courtyard like a stone into still water. The Kohanim stopped what they were doing. The pilgrims turned to look. A child — a toddler — had just issued a halachic ruling in the Mishkan of Shiloh. And the ruling was correct. Eli turned. His face was dark with fury. Not because the child was wrong — he was right, and Eli knew it. But because a student, no matter how gifted, no matter how correct, does not issue halachic rulings in the presence of his teacher. This was the law: moreh halachah bifnei rabo — one who teaches law before his master — is liable to death. "Bring this child to me," Eli said. They brought Shmuel. The boy stood before the Kohen Gadol, tiny, bewildered, not yet understanding what he had done. "This child has ruled in the presence of his teacher," Eli said. His voice was formal. Judicial. The voice of a man pronouncing judgment. "The law is clear." The courtyard went silent. The pilgrims stared. The Kohanim looked at each other. And Chanah — who had just handed over her son, who had walked away from the most precious thing in her life — heard the words and went cold. She ran to Eli. She fell before him. "My lord! As your soul lives, my lord — for this child I prayed! I stood right here, in this place, and Hashem heard my prayer. I asked for one thing — one thing — and you would take him from me now?" The Gemara records the scene with the vividness of a courtroom drama. Chanah pleaded. She argued. She invoked the prayer, the blessing, the years of waiting. She threw herself between the law and her child with the same ferocity that had once thrown itself between her barrenness and Heaven.

Eli relented. He looked at the mother, at the child, at the boy's bright eyes that had seen the truth of the halachah without being taught. "Go," he said to Chanah. "May Hashem give you children in place of this one whom you have lent to Hashem." It was the second blessing. The first had given her Shmuel. This one would give her more — the text records that Chanah bore three more sons and two daughters after Shmuel. But first, before leaving, Chanah sang.

Shirat Chanah. The Song of Chanah. "My heart exults in Hashem. My horn is raised through Hashem. My mouth is opened wide against my enemies, for I rejoice in Your salvation." The song was not about a baby. Not directly. It was about the architecture of the universe — the way God inverts the expected order, raises the low and brings down the high, kills and gives life, impoverishes and enriches, brings down to Sheol and raises up. "The barren woman has borne seven," she sang, "and the one with many children languishes." Seven. Chanah would ultimately bear six children — five after Shmuel. The Gemara explains that when Chanah bore a child, Peninnah buried one. When Chanah bore two, Peninnah buried two. By the time Chanah had borne five, Peninnah had lost ten. Peninnah came to Chanah weeping and begged her: "I know what I did. Please pray that my remaining children be spared." And Chanah prayed for her rival. The woman who had tormented her for years, who had twisted the knife of barrenness every festival — Chanah prayed for her. The sixth child was counted as though it were Chanah's because her prayer preserved Peninnah's last two. This was the woman who had threatened God with the sotah ritual. This was also the woman who prayed for the person who had caused her the most pain. The song soared. It spoke of kings before there were kings in Israel — "He will give strength to His king and raise the horn of His anointed." The Radak and other commentators note this as a prophecy: Chanah saw the monarchy before it existed. Shmuel, the child she had just left in Shiloh, would one day anoint both Shaul and David. The anointed one she sang about would be placed on his throne by the hands of the boy standing in the courtyard, bewildered, watching his mother sing and cry and leave. Chanah went home to Ramah.

Shmuel stayed in Shiloh. And every year, his mother made him a small coat — a me'il katan — and brought it to him when she came up for the festival. Every year, a new coat. Because children grow, and a mother who cannot hold her child can still clothe him. * * *
