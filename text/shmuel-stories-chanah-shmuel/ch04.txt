Chapter Four: The Sons of Eli The child grew in Shiloh. The Mishkan grew rotten. The text juxtaposes them deliberately — alternating between Shmuel's rise and the corruption of Eli's sons with a rhythm that feels almost musical. Light, dark. Light, dark. The child serving Hashem. The sons desecrating His house. The future being prepared. The present being destroyed. "And the sons of Eli were bnei beliyaal — worthless men. They did not know Hashem." The phrase is extraordinary. They did not know Hashem. These were Kohanim. They served in the Mishkan. They wore the priestly garments. They handled the sacred vessels and offered the sacrifices and stood in the place where the Shechinah was supposed to dwell. And the text says they did not know Hashem. Not that they didn't believe. Not that they were atheists. That they didn't know — the way a person can live in a house for twenty years and never notice the sky.

The corruption began with meat. When an Israelite brought a korban shelamim — a peace offering — the procedure was specific. The animal was slaughtered, its blood was dashed on the altar, and certain portions were burned for Hashem. The breast and the right thigh went to the Kohanim. The rest was eaten by the one who brought the offering, shared with family and friends in a holy meal. The system was sacred. It was also precise. The Kohanim received their portions only after Hashem received His. The order mattered. Chofni and Pinchas reversed it. They sent their servants to the cooking area while the meat was still in the pot. The servant would approach with a large three-pronged fork and plunge it into the cauldron. "Whatever the fork brings up," the servant said, "the Kohen takes." A pilgrim — a farmer from the lowlands of Yehudah, a man who had walked two days to bring his korban — stared at the servant. "But the fat hasn't been burned yet. It belongs to Hashem. Let them burn the fat first, and then take whatever you want." The servant shook his head. "No. Give it now. If you don't — he'll take it by force."

The farmer's face darkened. He looked at the three-pronged fork, at the servant's hard expression, at the meat in the pot that was supposed to be offered to God before anyone else touched it. "This isn't right," he said quietly. "Take it up with the Kohen," the servant said. And took the meat. The sin was not theft — though it was also theft. The sin was inversion. Before Hashem could receive His portion, the sons of Eli took theirs. The order of the universe — God first, humanity second — was reversed at the very altar where that order was supposed to be enacted. The Metzudat David explains: the servants acted on the orders of Chofni and Pinchas. The text attributes the sin to the sons even though the servants' hands held the fork, because the corruption flowed downhill from leadership. When the head is diseased, the body follows. Pilgrims who came to Shiloh expecting to encounter holiness encountered a shakedown instead. They came with their lambs and their flour and their hope, and they left with the taste of injustice. Some stopped coming altogether. The roads to Shiloh emptied — not because of indifference this time, not because of the slow forgetting that had plagued the generation before. Because the house of God had been turned into the house of men who served themselves.

And then there were the women. The text records: "And Eli was very old, and he heard all that his sons did to all Israel, and how they lay with the women who assembled at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." The Gemara in Shabbat stops here and delivers a ruling that would redefine how the story was read forever after. "Rabbi Yonatan said: Whoever says the sons of Eli sinned with the women is in error." In error. The Gemara insists that the literal reading — that Chofni and Pinchas committed adultery at the entrance to the Mishkan — is wrong. What actually happened? The women who came to Shiloh after childbirth needed to bring their korbanot — the bird offerings required for purification — before they could return home to their husbands. Chofni and Pinchas delayed these offerings. They processed them slowly, negligently, without the urgency that the women's situation demanded. The women waited. Days passed. They could not go home, could not return to their families, because the Kohanim who were supposed to complete the purification process couldn't be bothered to do their job. "Because they delayed their bird-offerings so that the women did not return to their husbands," the Gemara says, "the text treats it as though they had lain with them."

The equation is devastating. Preventing a woman from returning to her husband is treated by the text as equivalent to adultery. The violence was not physical. It was structural. Bureaucratic. The casual cruelty of men in authority who didn't care how their negligence affected the lives of those who depended on them. But this is not the only view. The Gemara itself acknowledges a dissenting opinion. Rav says that only Pinchas did not sin — he is included in the condemnation because he failed to rebuke his brother Chofni. And the passage in Yoma puts it even more starkly: "Why was Shiloh destroyed? Because there were two things in it — sexual immorality and disgrace of sacrifices." The commentators don't resolve the tension. Both readings exist. Both are Torah. And both point to the same conclusion: whether the sons of Eli committed the literal acts described in the text or the metaphorical equivalents that the Gemara identifies, the result was the same. The Mishkan in Shiloh was being hollowed out from the inside.

Eli knew. He was old — the text emphasizes this — and his eyes were dimming, and perhaps his authority was dimming too. He heard what his sons were doing. Everyone in Shiloh heard. It was not a secret. He rebuked them. The text records the rebuke. "Why do you do such things?" he said. "For I hear evil reports about you from all the people." The Alshich notes something in the structure of the rebuke: Eli used the word shmu'ah — "report." He was responding to what he had heard, not what he had seen. He was reacting to rumor, not investigation. A stronger leader would have gone to the courtyard, watched with his own eyes, and confronted his sons with evidence. Eli confronted them with gossip. "No, my sons," he said. "For it is not a good report that I hear. You make the people of Hashem transgress." Three times in two verses he uses the word "hear." I hear. I hear. I hear. The repetition is not emphasis — it is confession. All Eli did was hear. He heard the complaints. He heard the whispers. He heard the pain of the pilgrims who left Shiloh feeling robbed. But hearing is not acting. "If a man sins against another man, God will judge him. But if a man sins against Hashem — who will pray for him?"

The warning was theological and it was correct. But the sons did not listen. The text says it plainly: "They did not heed their father's voice, for Hashem desired to kill them." The Radak pauses at this verse. Was their free will removed? Did Hashem seal their fate before they had a chance to repent? No, the Radak explains. Hashem did not remove their choice. He removed the opportunities that might have prompted choice. A different father — a stronger father — might have done more than rebuke with words. He might have removed them from their positions. He might have barred them from the altar. He might have risked the pain of a son's hatred to save that son's soul. Eli didn't do this. And because Eli didn't do this, the path to repentance narrowed until it closed. Hashem desired to kill them — not because He pre-ordained their destruction, but because the only person who could have saved them had already failed.

An ish Elokim — a man of God — came to Eli. The text doesn't name him. He appeared, delivered his message, and vanished from the narrative like a blade drawn and sheathed. "Thus says Hashem: Did I not reveal Myself to the house of your father when they were in Egypt, in the house of Pharaoh? And did I not choose him out of all the tribes of Israel to be My Kohen?" The genealogy was a prelude to a verdict. Hashem was recounting what He had given to Eli's family — the priesthood, the altar, the incense, the ephod, the privilege of standing in His presence — before announcing what He would take away. "Why do you kick at My sacrifice and My offering? And you honor your sons more than Me, to fatten yourselves with the best of every offering of My people Israel." You honor your sons more than Me. This was the indictment. Not that Eli failed to notice. Not that Eli failed to rebuke. That Eli chose his sons over Hashem. That when the choice was clear — your children's comfort or God's honor — Eli chose his children. Every parent understands this temptation. It is the most human of all failings. And the Torah records it without softening, because the Torah does not believe that human tenderness is an excuse for divine betrayal. The sentence was absolute. "Behold, days are coming when I will cut off your arm and the arm of your father's house. There will not be an old man in your house. And both your sons, Chofni and Pinchas — in one day they will both die." One day. The two of them. Together.

The man of God departed. Eli sat in the silence he left behind, and the silence was the silence of a man who has just been told that everything he built will be demolished, and who knows — in the honest, unbearable part of his heart — that the demolition is deserved. And somewhere in the Mishkan, the child Shmuel was growing. "And the child Shmuel grew before Hashem." The text repeats this phrase like a refrain — inserting Shmuel into the narrative of corruption the way a single candle is placed in a dark room. He grew. While the sons of Eli rotted the Mishkan from the inside, Shmuel grew. While pilgrims fled and the roads emptied, Shmuel grew. While Eli sat on his chair and heard and heard and heard, Shmuel grew. The replacement was already being prepared. He was wearing a small linen ephod, serving in the Mishkan, sleeping in the chamber near the Aron HaKodesh. He was a child among failing adults, and he was growing toward something that none of them could see. Not yet. * * *
