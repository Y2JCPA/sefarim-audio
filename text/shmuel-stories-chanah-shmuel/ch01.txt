Chapter One: The Road to Shiloh He took a different road every year. This was deliberate. Elkana ben Yerocham, of the hill country of Ephraim, made the pilgrimage to Shiloh three times a year — for Pesach, for Shavuot, for Sukkot — and each time he walked a different route. Not because he was lost. Not because the terrain was difficult. Because he wanted to be seen. "Why the long way?" his neighbor Tzofar asked one morning, adjusting the pack straps on his donkey. "The northern road is two days shorter." "We took the northern road last Pesach," Elkana said. "Exactly. So we know it works." "And the villages on the northern road already know about the festival pilgrimage. The villages on the eastern road don't. They've forgotten." Elkana turned to look at the line of families forming behind him — wives, children, donkeys loaded with provisions and korbanot. It was a modest procession, but it was growing. Each year it grew. "You want them to see us," Tzofar said. "I want them to ask." And they did ask. The Midrash Shmuel records what happened every time Elkana's caravan passed through a town that had stopped making the pilgrimage. The people would come out of their houses and watch the procession — the families walking together, the children singing, the animals marked for sacrifice. And someone would always approach. "Where are you going?" a farmer would ask, standing at the edge of his field. "To the house of Hashem in Shiloh," Elkana would answer. "Come with us." "To Shiloh? That's days away. My crops —" "Will be here when you get back. Hashem watches the fields of those who come to His house." Some came. Not all. Not even most, at first. But some. A family here, a widow there, a young man searching for something he couldn't name. Elkana gathered them the way a shepherd gathers strays — not by force, but by walking in the right direction and trusting that the lost would follow.

The Rashi on the opening verse reveals Elkana's lineage: he was a Levi. Not from Ephraim by tribe, but by residence — he lived in the territory of Ephraim, in a town called Ramathaim-Tzofim. The town sat in the hills, looking out over the lowlands — tzofim means "watchers," and the Radak explains that it was called this because the town's elevation gave its residents a commanding view. Elkana watched from his hill. He watched a nation forgetting its God, and he decided to do something about it — not with a sword, not with a prophecy, but with his feet. Every year, a different road. Every road, a different set of villages. Every village, a conversation. "Why don't you come to Shiloh?" he would ask. "No one goes anymore." "I go. My family goes. And everyone who walks with us comes back changed." This was the generation before Shmuel. The Mishkan still stood in Shiloh — it had been there since the conquest under Yehoshua — but fewer and fewer Israelites made the journey. The fire on the altar still burned. The Kohanim still served. But the great gatherings of the nation, the festivals that were supposed to unite twelve tribes under one God, had dwindled to a trickle. The roads to Shiloh were emptying the way the roads in Naftali had emptied under Sisera's chariots, except this time the oppressor was not iron wheels. It was indifference. Elkana would not accept indifference.

He had two wives. This was not unusual in that era, but it was complicated. The first wife was Chanah. The second was Peninnah. Peninnah had children. Many children — sons and daughters who filled the house with noise and life and the thousand small crises of a growing family. Chanah had none. Every year, when the pilgrimage came around and Elkana distributed the portions of the korban to his family, the mathematics of grief displayed itself with terrible clarity. Peninnah received portions — for herself, for each of her sons, for each of her daughters. Chanah received one portion. One. The portion of a woman alone. Elkana tried. The text says he loved Chanah — loved her more than Peninnah, loved her with the fierce, protective love of a man who sees his wife suffering and cannot fix it. He gave her a special portion. The Radak explains the phrase manah achat apayim as a "double portion" — one share worth two, a public declaration that Chanah's value was not measured by the

number of children beside her. "Am I not better to you than ten sons?" he said to her one evening, watching her eyes redden with tears she was trying not to shed. The question hung in the air. It was the wrong question, and Elkana — for all his goodness, for all his faith, for all those different roads to Shiloh — didn't understand why. The Alshich explains what Chanah heard in those words. She heard a husband saying: my love should be enough for you. She heard a man asking her to set aside the deepest desire of her soul because he was standing right there, and wasn't he sufficient? He wasn't sufficient. Not because she didn't love him. Because a woman's desire for a child is not a commentary on her husband. It is a prayer in its own language, spoken from a part of the soul that a husband — even the best husband — cannot reach. Chanah said nothing. She looked away. She couldn't eat.

And Peninnah made it worse. The text uses a word — tzaratah — that means both "her rival" and "her tormentor." Every year, at the festival, when the portions were distributed and the gap between abundance and absence was on full display, Peninnah would provoke her. "Your children," Peninnah would say to a friend, just loudly enough for Chanah to hear, "which tunic did you dress them in for the festival?" Or: "I need to count heads again. With so many little ones running around, I always lose track." Or, to Chanah directly, with a smile that didn't reach her eyes: "You look tired, Chanah. It must be restful, having no one to keep you up at night." The Gemara in Berachot asks the question that anyone with a conscience would ask: how could Peninnah do this? Was she simply cruel? The answer is more complicated than cruelty. The Tanna D'vei Eliyahu and several midrashim suggest that Peninnah's provocations were l'shem shamayim — for the sake of Heaven. She saw that Chanah, despite her pain, had not yet prayed with the total, desperate, world-shattering intensity that could break open the gates of Heaven. Peninnah calculated — perhaps correctly — that Chanah needed to be pushed to the edge before she would find the prayer that was inside her. "You could pray harder," Peninnah said to her once, when they were alone.

Chanah stared at her. "You think I don't pray?" "I think you pray with hope. Hope is not enough. You need to pray with everything — with rage, with desperation, with the willingness to say things to Hashem that a polite woman would never say." Peninnah leaned closer. "Scream at Him, Chanah. Demand. Don't sit there weeping and waiting. Fight." Whether Peninnah's intentions were pure or poisoned — and the sources allow for both readings — the result was the same. Year after year, the pain built. Year after year, the wound deepened. And year after year, Chanah returned to Shiloh with her husband, received her single portion, endured the provocations, wept, couldn't eat, heard Elkana's well-meaning question about ten sons, and felt the prayer inside her growing larger and more dangerous. Like a storm gathering over the hills. It would break soon.

The road wound upward through the hills of Ephraim, and Elkana's caravan climbed with it. Donkeys brayed. Children complained about their feet. The wives walked on opposite sides of the procession — Peninnah near the front, surrounded by her noisy brood, and Chanah near the back, quiet, watching the dust rise from the road ahead. A young woman from a village they had passed through the previous day was walking beside Chanah. She had joined the caravan that morning, timidly, carrying a small lamb for a korban. "Is this your first time to Shiloh?" Chanah asked. "Yes." The woman looked nervous. "My mother used to go, when she was young. She said the Mishkan was beautiful." "It is." "She said you can feel Hashem's presence there. Is that true?" Chanah was quiet for a moment. She thought about the Mishkan — the curtains, the altar, the smoke rising, the faces of the Kohanim in the lamplight. She thought about all the years she had stood there and prayed and waited and prayed and waited and nothing had changed. "Yes," she said. "You can feel His presence. Whether He feels yours — that's the question." The woman looked at her with concern. Chanah shook her head. "Ignore me. I'm in a difficult mood. You'll love Shiloh. The first time is always the most powerful." "Will you show me where to go? I don't know the procedures."

Chanah smiled — a real smile, the kind that surfaces through sadness like a flower through stone. "Of course. Stay with me. I'll show you everything." They walked on. The hills rose around them. Shiloh was close now — perhaps half a day's walk. Chanah could feel it ahead of her the way a person feels a thunderstorm before the first cloud appears. Something was different this year. Something had shifted in the pressure of her soul, and the prayer that had been building for years — through all the empty festivals and silent nights and single portions — was pressing against the walls of her heart with a force she had never felt before. This year would be different. She didn't know how she knew. But she knew. * * *
