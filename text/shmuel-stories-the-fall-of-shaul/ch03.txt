Chapter Three: The Oath and the Honey On the day of the great victory, Shaul made a terrible mistake. He bound the people with an oath: "Cursed be the man who eats any food until evening, until I have avenged myself on my enemies." The Radak identifies the flaw immediately: Shaul said "my enemies." Not Hashem's enemies. Not Israel's enemies. My enemies. The possessive was a warning sign — the king was personalizing a war that belonged to God. No one ate. The army was pursuing the Pelishtim through a long, exhausting day of fighting, and the soldiers — who had been demoralized and half-starved even before the battle — were now forbidden to stop and refuel. The pursuit that should have been decisive was being undermined by the king's own decree. They came to a forest. Honey was dripping from the combs — wild honey, flowing onto the ground. The soldiers looked at it and swallowed hard and kept walking. No one reached out his hand to his mouth, because they feared the oath. Yonatan had not heard the oath. He had been at the top of the cliff, fighting the garrison, when Shaul pronounced it. He didn't know. He dipped the end of his staff into the honeycomb and raised it to his mouth and ate. "His eyes became bright." The phrase is simple and devastating. The honey gave him what the oath had been denying the entire army — strength, clarity, the energy to keep fighting. His eyes brightened. His body recovered. One taste of honey did what hours of cursing and exhaustion could not. A soldier saw him and said: "Your father strictly bound the people with an oath, saying 'Cursed be the man who eats today.'" Yonatan's response was blunt: "My father has troubled the land. See how my eyes have brightened because I tasted a little of this honey. How much more so if the people had eaten freely today of the spoil of their enemies — would not the slaughter of the Pelishtim have been even greater?" He was right. Shaul's oath had cost Israel a more complete victory. The soldiers were too exhausted to pursue the enemy to destruction. When evening finally came and the oath expired, the famished men fell upon the captured livestock and slaughtered animals and ate them with the blood — a sin in itself, because the hunger was so acute that they could not wait for proper preparation.

And then Shaul discovered that Yonatan had eaten. He cast lots. The lot fell on Yonatan. Shaul, bound by his own oath, prepared to execute his own son. "You shall surely die, Yonatan," Shaul said. Yonatan looked at his father. This was the man who had hidden among the baggage. The man who had demonstrated mercy at his coronation. The man who had united the nation against Nachash. And now he was going to kill the hero of Michmash for eating a spoonful of honey. "I tasted a little honey with the tip of the staff that was in my hand," Yonatan said. "Here I am — I will die." The people intervened. "Shall Yonatan die — who has wrought this great salvation in Israel? God forbid! As Hashem lives, not a hair of his head shall fall to the ground, for he has worked with God this day!" The people redeemed Yonatan. They stood between the father's oath and the son's life and said: no. This far and no further. The king's word is the king's word, but a rash oath does not override justice, and the man who scaled the cliff with a "perhaps" does not die for a taste of honey. Shaul backed down. The pursuit was called off. The Pelishtim went home to their territory. The victory was incomplete. And the crack in Shaul's kingship — the one that had opened at Gilgal when he offered the forbidden sacrifice — widened. * * *
