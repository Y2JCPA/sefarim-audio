Chapter Five: The Tearing of the Cloak Hashem spoke to Shmuel that night. "I regret that I made Shaul king. For he has turned back from following Me and has not carried out My commands." The word "regret" — nichamti — is one of the most difficult words in the Torah. Can God regret? Can the infinite, all-knowing Creator of the universe look at a decision and wish He had made a different one? The commentators wrestle with this. The Rambam reads it as anthropomorphism — God speaking in human language to convey the magnitude of His displeasure. The Radak says it means Hashem altered the course He had set for Shaul's dynasty, which from a human perspective looks like regret. The text is not claiming that God made a mistake. It is saying that Shaul's choices had consequences so severe that even the language of divine disappointment cannot fully contain them. Shmuel was distressed. The text says "vayichar l'Shmuel" — it burned in him. And he cried out to Hashem all night. All night. The old prophet — the boy from Shiloh, the voice of Hashem — spent the entire night in prayer. Not for himself. For Shaul. For the kingdom. For the possibility that the sentence could be reversed, the decree annulled, the trajectory altered. It could not.

Shmuel went to find Shaul in the morning. They told him: "Shaul went to Carmel — he set up a monument for himself — and then turned and went down to Gilgal." The monument again. Even the messenger mentioned it. Shmuel arrived at Gilgal. Shaul saw him coming and went out to meet him with the greeting of a man who has no idea that his world is about to end. "Blessed are you to Hashem!" Shaul said. "I have carried out the command of Hashem." Shmuel stopped. He looked at the king. He listened. And then, into the silence between them, came a sound that made Shaul's words ring hollow.

"Then what is this bleating of sheep in my ears?" Shmuel said. "And the lowing of cattle that I hear?" The animals. The best of the sheep and the best of the cattle — the ones Shaul had spared, the ones the command had said to destroy — were bleating and lowing in the background, providing a live soundtrack to the king's lie. Shaul's defense came immediately, and it was the defense of every leader who has ever been caught between what he promised and what he delivered. "They brought them from Amalek," he said. "The people spared the best of the sheep and cattle to sacrifice to Hashem your God. And the rest we destroyed." The people. Not me — the people. They wanted to keep the animals. They planned to sacrifice them. The intention was good. Every word of this excuse made it worse. "The people" — a king who blames his subjects for his own failure to command. "To sacrifice to Hashem your God" — your God, not mine, as though Shaul were distancing himself from the very God who had anointed him. "The rest we destroyed" — as though partial obedience were the same as complete obedience. Shmuel cut him off. "Stop." The single word silenced the king mid-sentence. Shmuel's voice was not loud. It did not need to be. There is a quality in the voice of a true prophet that operates below volume — a frequency that the soul hears even when the ears resist. Shaul stopped. Shmuel looked at him for a long moment. The silence stretched between them — the prophet who had anointed the king, and the king who had failed the prophet. Two men who had started this journey together on a road outside a city where young women drew water, when one of them was looking for donkeys and the other was looking for a king. "Let me tell you," Shmuel said, "what Hashem said to me last night." The word "last night" carried weight. While Shaul was sleeping among the spoils of a victory he had corrupted, Shmuel had been awake — crying out to Hashem, pleading, arguing, doing what prophets do when the word they receive is a word they do not want to deliver. He had spent the night trying to save Shaul. He had failed. Shaul's face changed. He saw something in the prophet's eyes that no excuse could answer. The word in Hebrew — heref — means be still. Enough. No more excuses.

"Let me tell you what Hashem said to me last night." Shaul fell silent. "When you were small in your own eyes," Shmuel said, "were you not made head of the tribes of Israel? Hashem anointed you king over Israel. And Hashem sent you on a mission, and said: 'Go and destroy the sinners, the Amalekites, and fight against them until they are consumed.' Why did you not listen to the voice of Hashem? Why did you pounce upon the spoil and do what was evil in the sight of Hashem?" When you were small in your own eyes. The callback was devastating. The man who had hidden among the baggage. The man who had said "Am I not from the smallest tribe?" The humility that had been beautiful was gone. In its place was a king who built monuments to himself and blamed his people for his own disobedience. Shaul tried again. "But I did listen! I went on the mission. I brought back Agag. I destroyed Amalek. The people took from the spoil —" Shmuel raised his hand. The old prophet's voice filled the space between them like fire filling a furnace. "Does Hashem delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices as much as in obeying the voice of Hashem?" The question was rhetorical. The answer was the hinge of the entire narrative — the single sentence upon which Shaul's kingship turned. "Behold — to obey is better than sacrifice. To heed is better than the fat of rams." Obedience. Not sacrifice. Not korbanot. Not the elaborate machinery of religion that Chofni and Pinchas had corrupted and Shaul was now misusing. Obedience. Listening. Doing what you were told to do, even when your own moral reasoning or political calculation or populist instinct tells you to do something else. "For rebellion is as the sin of divination. And stubbornness is as iniquity and idolatry." Shmuel paused. Let the words settle. "Because you have rejected the word of Hashem — He has rejected you as king."

The word struck Shaul like a physical blow. Something collapsed inside him — the thing that had been cracking since Gilgal, since the forbidden offering, since the rash oath, since the monument — finally broke.

"I have sinned," Shaul said. His voice was different now. Stripped. Bare. "I have transgressed the word of Hashem and your words. Because I feared the people and listened to their voice." I feared the people. There it was — the confession that explained everything. The king who was supposed to lead the people had been led by them. The man appointed to execute divine commands had deferred to popular opinion. The agent had forgotten that he was an agent. "Now please forgive my sin," Shaul said. "Return with me, and I will prostrate myself before Hashem." "I will not return with you," Shmuel said. "For you have rejected the word of Hashem, and Hashem has rejected you from being king over Israel." Shmuel turned to leave. Shaul reached out and grabbed the edge of Shmuel's cloak. The cloak tore. The sound of tearing fabric filled the silence between the prophet and the king. Shmuel looked at the torn piece in Shaul's hand. "Hashem has torn the kingdom of Israel from you today," Shmuel said, "and has given it to your fellow who is better than you." The cloak was a sign. The torn garment was a prophecy enacted in cloth. The kingdom that Shaul had been given — torn. The dynasty that should have lasted forever — torn. By his own hand. In his own desperate grip. "And also the Eternal One of Israel does not lie and does not change His mind," Shmuel said. "For He is not a man that He should change His mind." The verse contradicts the earlier verse — the one where Hashem said nichamti, "I regret." The contradiction is the teaching. God can express disappointment in human terms while remaining unchanging in His essence. The decree against Shaul was both a response to human failure and an expression of eternal purpose. Both were true. Both existed simultaneously. Shaul asked one more time: "I have sinned. But please honor me before the elders of my people and before Israel. Return with me, so that I may prostrate myself before Hashem your God." Shmuel returned with him. It was not a reversal — it was a mercy. One last public moment of dignity for a king who had lost everything. And then Shmuel said: "Bring me Agag, king of Amalek."

They brought him. Agag came delicately — the text uses the word ma'adanot, which means in chains or with dainty steps, depending on the reading. The Amalekite king approached the prophet and said: "Surely the bitterness of death has passed." He thought he was safe. He had survived the battle. He had been spared by the Israelite king. The danger, he believed, was over. Shmuel looked at him. "As your sword has made women childless," the prophet said, "so shall your mother be childless among women." Shmuel looked at Agag. The Amalekite king — tall, proud even in chains, a man who had ordered the slaughter of innocents and expected to survive because a softer king had spared him. "As your sword has made women childless," the prophet said, "so shall your mother be childless among women." The words fell like the blade that would follow them. Shmuel was not a warrior. He was an old man — the boy from Shiloh, the child who had run to Eli's room in the dark thinking his name was being called by human lips. He had spent his life in prayer and judgment and teaching. He had never wielded a sword. But he wielded one now. The text uses the word vayeshaseif — he hewed. Not struck. Not killed. Hewed. The way a butcher hews meat. The way a woodsman hews timber. With precision and force and no hesitation. Shmuel did what the king had been too compassionate — too "overly righteous" — to do. The old prophet completed the mission that the young king had abandoned. And in doing so, he demonstrated the principle his own farewell speech had proclaimed: to obey is better than sacrifice. He hewed Agag in pieces before Hashem at Gilgal. The old prophet. The boy who had worn a linen ephod in Shiloh. The man who had never let a word fall to the ground. He took a sword and did what the king had been too compassionate — too "overly righteous" — to do. Then Shmuel went to Ramah. And Shaul went up to his house in Givat Shaul. And Shmuel did not see Shaul again until the day of his death. But Shmuel mourned for Shaul.

He mourned. The prophet who had delivered the harshest verdict in the history of Israelite kingship went home and wept for the man he had condemned. Because Shmuel had anointed Shaul. He had poured the oil on his head and kissed him and told him that Hashem had chosen him. And the memory of that anointing — the oil, the kiss, the hope — was now a grief that would not heal. And Hashem regretted that He had made Shaul king over Israel. The Gemara in Yoma 22b asks the question that haunts the entire narrative: why was Shaul punished so severely? David would later sin — with Batsheva, with the census — and his dynasty endured. Shaul sinned once, and his dynasty was cut off. Why? The answer the Gemara gives is startling: Shaul's lineage was examined, and it was found to be without blemish. His family was clean — too clean. David, by contrast, descended from Ruth the Moabite, from Yehudah and Tamar, from a lineage that carried the scars of compromise and survival. A man from a perfect lineage is held to a perfect standard. A man from a flawed lineage is given room to fail and recover. The answer is not a justification. It is a description of how divine justice operates — not by human logic, but by a calculus that accounts for the weight a soul carries and the distance it has traveled. The sentence closed like a door. Behind it, in Beit Lechem, a boy named David was tending sheep. His time was coming. * * *
