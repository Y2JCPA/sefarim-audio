Chapter Four: Go and Destroy Amalek The command came like a blade. "Thus says Hashem Tzvaot," Shmuel told Shaul. "I remember what Amalek did to Israel — how he ambushed him on the way when he came up from Mitzrayim. Now go and strike Amalek and destroy everything he has. Do not have pity on him. Kill man and woman, child and infant, ox and sheep, camel and donkey." Total destruction. Cherem. The most extreme command in the Torah's vocabulary — the annihilation of an entire people and everything they possessed. No prisoners. No plunder. No mercy. The Gemara in Yoma records what happened in Shaul's soul when he received this command. Shaul reasoned. He made a kal vachomer — a logical deduction from a lesser case to a greater one. If the Torah requires an eglah arufah — the breaking of a heifer's neck — to atone for a single unresolved murder, how much more so should we grieve over the destruction of an entire nation? And if the adults sinned, what sin did the children commit? And if the humans sinned, what did the animals do? A bat kol — a heavenly voice — answered: "Do not be overly righteous." The phrase comes from Kohelet, and its placement here is shattering. Shaul's moral reasoning was not wrong in the abstract. His compassion was genuine. His logic was sound. But obedience to a divine command does not permit the luxury of moral second-guessing. Hashem had spoken. The command was clear. Shaul's job was not to evaluate the command's morality — it was to execute it. This was the test. Not of courage — Shaul had proven his courage at Yavesh Gilad and at Michmash. Not of faith — Shaul believed in Hashem. The test was of submission. Could the king subordinate his own moral intuition to the explicit word of God? The answer was no.

Shaul stood alone in his tent that night and wrestled with the command. Total destruction. Every man, woman, child, animal. He had been a farmer before he was a king — a man who knew the weight of a newborn calf in his arms, who had watched lambs nurse at their mothers' sides. He had lived among animals his entire life. And now God was commanding him to destroy them all.

The moral reasoning rose inside him unbidden. The Gemara in Yoma records the kal vachomer that played out in his mind, but the Gemara could only capture the logic. The feeling was something else — a revulsion, a doubt, a whisper that said: this cannot be right. The God who creates does not command destruction without limit. There must be room for mercy. There must be a space where compassion is not disobedience. He was wrong. Not because compassion was wrong — compassion is never wrong in itself — but because there are moments when a human being is asked to set aside his own moral compass and follow a compass that points somewhere his heart cannot reach. This was one of those moments. And Shaul's tragedy was not that he felt the revulsion. It was that he acted on it. Shaul gathered the army — two hundred thousand foot soldiers from Israel and ten thousand from Yehudah. He warned the Kenites — Yitro's descendants, the same family that had produced Yael — to separate from Amalek, because they had shown kindness to Israel during the Exodus. The Kenites withdrew. Shaul attacked. He won. Decisively. He struck Amalek from Chavilah to Shur. He destroyed the people with the edge of the sword. But he did not destroy everything. "Shaul and the people spared Agag" — the king of Amalek — "and the best of the sheep and the cattle, the fatlings and the lambs, and everything that was good. They were not willing to destroy them. But everything that was despised and worthless — that they destroyed." The good they kept. The worthless they destroyed. The command had been: destroy everything. The execution was: destroy the things we don't want. The Ralbag identifies this as the precise inversion of divine service. At the korban, the best is offered to Hashem and the rest is kept. Here, the rest was offered to Hashem and the best was kept. It was the sin of Chofni and Pinchas replayed on a national scale — taking before giving, choosing before obeying, treating Hashem as the recipient of leftovers rather than the Lord of firsts. And Shaul — the king who should have known better, who had stood at the Mitzpah assembly and heard Shmuel's warning, who had been anointed with oil and kissed and told that Hashem had chosen him — set up a monument for himself. For himself. The Radak says Shaul erected a yad — a marker, a commemorative pillar — to celebrate his victory over Amalek. As though the victory were his. As though the command to destroy had been his idea. As though the God who had given the command was secondary to the king who

had partially executed it. The monument was the tell. Not the sheep. Not Agag. The monument. Because a man who builds a monument to himself after a divine mandate has confused the agent with the Author. * * *
