Chapter Two: Yonatan at Michmash While Shaul sat with his six hundred men under the pomegranate tree in Migron, his son decided to act. Yonatan did not tell his father. He turned to the young man who carried his armor — his na'ar nosei kelav, his weapons-bearer — and said: "Come, let's cross over to the Pelishti garrison on the other side." The garrison was positioned at the top of a rocky pass — a natural fortress flanked by two sharp crags that the text names Botzetz and Seneh. The pass was narrow. Two men could climb it, but they would be completely exposed. If the Pelishtim saw them coming, they would die on the rocks. Yonatan's plan was insane. "Come," he said to his armor-bearer. "Let's cross over to these uncircumcised. Perhaps Hashem will act for us. For there is no limit to Hashem's ability to save — whether by many or by few." Perhaps. The word was not doubt — it was humility. Perhaps Hashem will act. Not: Hashem will certainly act. Not: I have received a prophecy. Perhaps. If He chooses. If this is the moment. If two men climbing a cliff is how He wants to deliver Israel today. The armor-bearer's response was immediate: "Do everything that is in your heart. Turn to it. I am with you according to your heart." Two men. Against a garrison. With a "perhaps" and a prayer. The armor-bearer looked at Yonatan. He was young — the text never names him, and that anonymity is part of his heroism. He was nobody. A servant. A weapons-carrier. And his master was asking him to climb a cliff toward twenty armed men with no backup, no plan, and a "perhaps." "Do everything that is in your heart," the armor-bearer said. "Turn to it. I am with you according to your heart." According to your heart. Not according to the odds. Not according to military logic. According to the thing burning inside you that says this is the moment and God is in it. They crouched at the base of the pass and studied the cliff above them. Botzetz on one side, Seneh on the other — jagged rock faces, nearly vertical, with handholds that only a climber could see. The Pelishti garrison sat at the top like an eagle's nest, looking down at the valley where Israel's pathetic six hundred huddled under a pomegranate tree.

"Ready?" Yonatan whispered. "Ready." Yonatan devised a sign. They would reveal themselves to the Pelishtim at the base of the cliff. If the soldiers said "wait until we come down to you" — they would hold their position, because that meant the advantage was with the enemy. But if they said "come up to us" — they would climb, because that was the sign that Hashem had delivered them. They showed themselves. The Pelishtim on the cliff looked down and laughed. "Look — the Hebrews are crawling out of the holes they've been hiding in!" Then the garrison commander called down: "Come up to us, and we'll teach you a lesson!" The sign. Yonatan turned to his armor-bearer. "Come up after me. Hashem has delivered them into the hand of Israel." They climbed. Hand over hand, on their hands and feet, scaling the crag that rose above them like a wall. Twenty men were at the top. Twenty armed soldiers against two men pulling themselves up a cliff face. The first twenty fell. Yonatan and his armor-bearer struck them down in a space the text measures carefully — about half a furrow's length of field. A narrow strip. Close quarters. Chaos. And then the ground shook. "There was a trembling in the camp, in the field, and among all the people. The garrison and the raiders trembled. The earth quaked, and it became a trembling from God." The Pelishtim panicked. They turned on each other — the confusion was so total that they could not distinguish friend from foe. Swords swung blindly. Men fell screaming. The army that had assembled to crush Israel was destroying itself. From Migron, Shaul's watchmen saw the Pelishti camp churning. The Malbim explains what happened next: Shaul's six hundred men, who had been too few and too demoralized to fight, now joined the rout. And the Israelites who had been hiding in the hill country — in caves, in cisterns, among the rocks — emerged and joined the pursuit. Hashem saved Israel that day. Not through a king and an army. Through a prince and an armor-bearer and a cliff and a "perhaps." * * *
