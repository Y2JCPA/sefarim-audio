Chapter Five

The Sons of Tzeruyah

The register in Shmuel Bet 23 lists them in a single dense cluster, the way families are listed in a census — Yoav, Avishai, Asa'el, the three brothers, the sons of Tzeruyah, David's sister's boys, the nephews who had been with David since before he had a kingdom.

Asa'el is at the top of the list of the Thirty: "Asa'el the brother of Yoav was one of the thirty." He had been the fastest runner in the camp — kal be-raglav ke-achat ha-tzeva'im ba-sadeh, "swift of foot like one of the gazelles of the field," the Tanach had said of him in Shmuel Bet 2. He had chased Avner after the battle at the pool of Giv'on. Avner had warned him to turn aside — twice, the Tanach emphasizes — and when Asa'el would not turn aside, Avner had struck him with the butt of the spear in the fifth rib and killed him. His blood had been the first real wound in the civil war between the house of David and the house of Shaul. It had been the wound that had made Yoav's subsequent killing of Avner in the gateway of Chevron a personal act of blood-vengeance rather than a purely political maneuver. The fifth rib — chomesh — is a phrase that will appear three times in the book of Shmuel Bet, always in connection with a killing by a son of Tzeruyah or against a son of Tzeruyah. Avner kills Asa'el in the fifth rib. Yoav kills Avner in the fifth rib in revenge. Yoav kills Amasa in the fifth rib — "the place where the gallbladder and the liver are suspended," the Gemara says — many years later at the great stone at Giv'on. The fifth rib is the signature wound of the sons of Tzeruyah. They have killed and been killed by the same anatomical strike across generations.

Yoav is not explicitly listed among the Thirty in Shmuel Bet 23 — the Tanach assumes his status is so far beyond ordinary membership that he does not need to be listed. He is referenced throughout the chapter as the frame of the register. The Thirty are identified as "Yoav's men" or "the men of Yoav." The list is organized around him without him being an item in it. This is deliberate. Yoav is the commander. The Thirty fight for him. He is above the list.

Avishai is in the list and is described with a signature deed: "And Avishai the brother of Yoav was the head of the three. And he lifted up his spear against three hundred slain, and had a name among the three." The phrase had a name (shem) is repeated for several members of the Thirty — it is the Tanach's shorthand for "his individual reputation was such that he was famous in his own right, not just as a member of the collective." Avishai's three hundred are half the eight hundred of Yoshev-baShevet and twice the three hundred of Abishai's less famous counterparts in the list — a middle figure, appropriate for the middle son of Tzeruyah.

The three brothers are a unit. They were born together, they grew up together in Chevron in the household of their mother Tzeruyah (whose husband is never named in the Tanach, a silence that has generated its own body of rabbinic speculation), they came to David at the cave of Adullam together, they fought beside each other for decades, and they died in three different ways — Asa'el killed by Avner in the dust of Giv'on, Yoav killed by Benayahu at the horns of the altar, Avishai dying of old age in quiet retirement some time between the two. The only one of the three whose death is not dramatic is Avishai's. His death is not recorded in the Tanach at all. He disappears from the register of officials after the Sheva ben Bichri rebellion and is never mentioned again. The mefarshim take the silence as a blessing — Avishai, alone of the three, was allowed to grow old and die in his bed.

* * *

This chapter is about the brothers as a unit — about the pattern of their loyalty to David, the pattern of their violence, and the recurring phrase that ran through David's relationship with them for his entire life. The phrase was this:

Mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah?

"What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah?"

David said it, in some form, at least three times in the book of Shmuel Bet. He said it after Yoav killed Avner in the gateway of Chevron: "I and my kingdom are guiltless before Hashem forever of the blood of Avner ben Ner. Let it rest on the head of Yoav, and on all his father's house... These men, the sons of Tzeruyah, are too hard for me." He said it in a milder form after Avishai wanted to kill Shimei at the ford of the Yarden during the flight from Avshalom: "What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah, that you should be an adversary to me today?" He said it implicitly after Yoav killed Avshalom in the forest of Efrayim against a direct royal order. He said it implicitly after Yoav killed Amasa at the great stone at Giv'on. The phrase became the verbal signature of David's lifelong frustration with his violent nephews — the men whose loyalty to him was unwavering but whose methods he could not always stomach.

Mah li ve-lakhem. What have I to do with you. It is the voice of a man who cannot quite get rid of his relatives and cannot quite make peace with them either.

* * *

Tzeruyah was David's sister. Divrei HaYamim Aleph 2:16 preserves her name in the genealogy of the house of Yishai: "And their sisters were Tzeruyah and Avigail. And the sons of Tzeruyah: Avshai, and Yoav, and Asa'el — three." Yishai had eight sons (of whom David was the youngest) and two daughters, Tzeruyah and Avigail. Tzeruyah's three sons were Avishai, Yoav, and Asa'el. All of them came into David's life as dependents of their aunt's side of the family — which is to say, as David's nephews on the maternal side.

The Tanach never names Tzeruyah's husband. The silence is striking. Every other mother in the genealogies has her husband named. Tzeruyah's husband is simply absent. The sons are always described as "the sons of Tzeruyah" — never "the sons of so-and-so." The rabbinic tradition reads this silence in several ways. Some mefarshim (following the reading preserved in Bavli Kiddushin 76b and elsewhere) suggest that Tzeruyah's husband was not a full Israelite — perhaps a convert, perhaps from one of the border tribes, perhaps a Moabite related to Ruth's family — and that the Tanach deliberately suppresses his name to avoid complicating the pedigree of Yoav and Avishai. Other mefarshim read the silence more simply: the husband died young, and the sons were raised by Tzeruyah alone, and the family identity naturally attached to the mother rather than the absent father. Still others read it mystically: the sons of Tzeruyah carried something of their mother in a way that overrode the ordinary patrilineal identification.

Whatever the explanation, the sons were benei Tzeruyah — the sons of Tzeruyah — in the Tanach's own voice. They were identified through their mother. And David, their uncle on the mother's side, was the closest senior male relative in their lives. When they came to the cave of Adullam, they came not as distant kinsmen seeking an opportunity but as immediate family reporting for service with their mother's brother.

* * *

Asa'el's story is the shortest and most tragic. He was the youngest of the three. He was famous for his speed — the Tanach compares him to a gazelle, a compliment that does not appear anywhere else in the Tanach for a human being. He was a scout, a messenger, the fast man in a band of fast men. He had probably been the one who ran ahead with news on a hundred different raids during the cave years. His speed was his identity.

At the pool of Giv'on, in the first battle between the house of David and the house of Shaul after Shaul's death, Asa'el did what his speed told him to do. He chased Avner, the general of the house of Shaul, across the field. Avner was older, heavier, slower on his feet, but a far more experienced warrior. Avner knew he could not outrun Asa'el. Avner also knew that if Asa'el caught him and the two of them were to fight, Avner would certainly win — but the winning would mean killing the younger brother of Yoav, and Yoav was the kind of man who would never forgive such a killing even under the formal rules of war.

Avner called over his shoulder as he ran.

"Sur lekha mei-acharai."

"Turn aside from following me."

Asa'el did not turn aside. He was a hundred paces behind Avner now and closing. He could see the older man's labored breathing in the dust ahead.

"Asa'el — turn aside! Turn to your right or to your left! Take one of the young men and seize his armor for yourself! Do not chase me!"

Asa'el did not answer. He kept running.

Avner called again, more urgently this time.

"Sur lekha mei-acharai. Why should I strike you to the ground? How then should I lift up my face to Yoav your brother?"

Asa'el closed another twenty paces. He was close enough now to see the sweat running down Avner's neck. He could hear his own breath in his ears. He could feel the muscles in his thighs burning. But he was Asa'el. He was the fastest man in David's army. He had been chasing this man for half a mile and he was not going to stop now.

"Asa'el!"

"Stop running, Avner. Stop running and turn around and fight me."

"I will not fight you. I am too old for this and you are too young. Turn aside. There are a hundred other men in this field you can chase. Chase one of them. Not me."

"I am chasing you. You are the general. The general is the prize."

"Asa'el — for the love of your brother — turn aside!"

"I will not."

Avner stopped running. He planted his feet. He turned around. He reversed his spear — held it not point-forward but butt-forward — and as Asa'el closed the distance, Avner drove the butt of the spear into Asa'el's stomach at the fifth rib. The butt end of the spear, rather than the point, because Avner was still hoping to wound rather than kill — hoping to knock Asa'el to the ground without penetrating the body. But Asa'el was moving at full speed, and the impact of his own body against the butt of the spear was enough to drive the wood through the leather of his armor and into the soft place at the base of the ribs where the liver and gallbladder hang. The wood came out the other side. Asa'el fell face-forward into the dust.

He was dead before he hit the ground.

The Tanach closes the scene with a sentence that has been quoted by commentators for two thousand years: "And all who came to the place where Asa'el fell down and died stood still."

Every man in the pursuit. Every man on both sides of the battle. They came to the place where Asa'el had fallen. They stopped. They could not walk past him. The fastest man in the army was lying face-down in the dust with a spear-shaped hole through his body, and the whole world had to stop for a moment to absorb what had happened.

* * *

Yoav absorbed it differently. Yoav did not stop. Yoav filed it away. Yoav made a quiet promise to himself that Avner would not survive the war. And many months later, when Avner had defected from the house of Shaul and had come to Chevron to make peace with David, Yoav was the one who met him at the gate of the city and took him aside and killed him, in the same fifth rib, in the same kind of strike, with no warning at all. The Tanach preserves the scene in Shmuel Bet 3: "And when Avner was returned to Chevron, Yoav took him aside in the gate to speak with him quietly, and smote him there in the fifth rib, that he died, for the blood of Asa'el his brother." The phrase for the blood of Asa'el his brother is the Tanach's way of telling us that Yoav's act was a revenge killing — a go'el ha-dam, a blood-avenger's killing, carried out under the technical permission of the ancient blood-vengeance laws even though Avner was innocent of murder in the formal sense (since he had given Asa'el the double warning and Asa'el had chosen to keep chasing him).

David was furious.

He came out into the courtyard of the palace at Chevron in front of the assembled court. Yoav was there. The body of Avner had been brought back to the city and was being prepared for burial. Word of the killing was already spreading through the streets. The northern tribes who had been waiting to see whether David would deal honestly with the house of Shaul were watching to see what David would say.

David said it loudly enough for everyone to hear.

"I am innocent. I and my kingdom are innocent before Hashem forever of the blood of Avner ben Ner. Let it rest on the head of Yoav and on all his father's house!"

He turned and pointed directly at Yoav.

"You. May the house of Yoav never lack one with a flow, or a leper, or one who holds the distaff like a woman, or one who falls by the sword, or one who lacks bread!"

The courtyard went silent. Yoav stood at the edge of the gathering, his face unreadable. He did not protest. He did not defend himself. He simply stood and listened to the curse.

David lowered his arm.

"Tear your clothes. All of you. Wear sackcloth. Mourn before Avner. He was a great man, and a great man has fallen this day in Israel. I, the king, will walk behind the bier myself."

The court broke into motion. Men began tearing their cloaks. The mourning rituals began. David turned and walked toward the house where Avner's body was being prepared.

Yoav fell into step beside him as he walked.

"My lord."

"Do not speak to me, Yoav."

"My lord — he killed Asa'el."

"He killed Asa'el in war. Under double warning. With the butt of his spear, not the point. He did not want to kill Asa'el. You killed him in peace. In the gateway of Chevron. Without warning. With the point of your sword. There is no comparison."

"He was the general of the house of Shaul. He was the only thing standing between you and the kingdom of all Israel. With him gone, the northern tribes will have to come to you."

"Yoav. The northern tribes were already coming to me. He was bringing them. He had defected. He was here in Chevron to negotiate the transfer of the kingdom. You killed the man who was handing me the kingdom. You did not advance my cause. You poisoned it."

"My lord — "

"Be quiet. Mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah. What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah? You are too hard for me. I am the king and you are my general and I cannot get rid of you, but I am cursing you in front of the whole court today, and the curse will follow your house as long as your name is remembered. Now get out of my sight and put on sackcloth and walk in the funeral procession."

Yoav did not answer. He fell back. He put on sackcloth. He walked in the procession.

And the strange thing — the thing that the mefarshim wrestle with — is that David did not fire Yoav. David did not demote him. David did not even confine him to quarters. David cursed him in public and then kept him as the chief general of the army for another forty years. The curse had been pronounced, but the operational relationship continued. David could not actually get rid of Yoav. Yoav was his nephew. Yoav was the man who had been in the cave of Adullam. Yoav was the man who knew where every skeleton in the royal closet was buried. Yoav was the man David needed, even after he had cursed him.

Mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah. It became the refrain of the whole relationship.

* * *

Avishai was different.

Avishai was the quieter of the two surviving brothers, the more religious of the two, the one whose violence was always in direct service of David's safety rather than in pursuit of his own agenda. The first famous episode of his life is the raid on Shaul's camp at Chakhilah, preserved in Shmuel Aleph 26. David had been hiding in the wilderness and had seen Shaul's camp at night from a nearby hill. He had asked his men: "Who will go down with me to Shaul to the camp?" Achimelech the Chitti had been silent. Avishai had immediately answered: "I will go down with you."

The two of them had crept into Shaul's camp in the middle of the night. They had walked silently through the sleeping formations of Shaul's men until they reached the center of the camp, where Shaul was sleeping with his spear stuck in the ground by his head and a water-jug beside him. Avishai had crouched next to Shaul's sleeping body.

"David. Let me strike him. God has delivered your enemy into your hand this day. Let me strike him with his own spear, down to the ground, in one blow. I will not need to strike him a second time."

David had stopped him.

"Do not destroy him. Who can lift his hand against the anointed of Hashem and be guiltless?"

They had taken only the spear and the water-jug. They had left the camp the way they had entered it, in total silence. They had climbed back up to the hill where David's men were waiting, and David had called across the valley to the camp and had shown Shaul the spear and the water-jug, and Shaul had wept from humiliation and confusion.

The Tanach preserves the moment for two reasons. The first is to show David's mercy — even with Shaul sleeping at his feet, David would not strike the anointed king. But the second reason is to show Avishai's character. Avishai's first impulse was to kill the enemy. Avishai's instinct, in every situation, was strike once, hard, and be done with it. He did not waste motion. He did not hesitate. He did not ask for a second opinion. If the enemy was in front of him and his commander gave the word, Avishai would strike.

And David, many times throughout his life, would have to say to Avishai some version of no, not this time. The relationship between the two of them was a relationship in which Avishai wanted to kill and David wanted to hold back. Avishai at Chakhilah wanted to kill Shaul and David held him back. Avishai at the ford of the Yarden wanted to kill Shimei and David held him back — "What have I to do with you, sons of Tzeruyah, that you should be an adversary to me today? Should any man be put to death in Israel today? Do I not know that today I am the king of Israel?" Avishai throughout the Avshalom rebellion was ready to kill anyone whose loyalty was suspect, and David had to rein him in every time.

The dynamic is telling. Avishai was not cruel. He was simply tactical. He was a man whose whole life had taught him that when an enemy was in front of you, the right answer was to kill the enemy before the enemy could kill you. He had learned this lesson in the cave of Adullam, in a hundred ambushes in the wilderness, in the hand-to-hand chaos of the early campaigns. He could not unlearn it. And David, who had learned the same lessons in the same places, had also learned a second lesson that Avishai had never quite absorbed: sometimes not killing the enemy was the better long-term play. Sometimes the enemy who was spared would become a friend, or a neutral, or at least a man whose death would cost the kingdom more than his life. David had the political intelligence to see this. Avishai did not.

But Avishai had one quality that Yoav did not have: Avishai accepted David's restraint. When David said no, Avishai stopped. He did not go behind David's back. He did not carry out the killing secretly. He obeyed. He grumbled, perhaps, but he obeyed. This is the deepest difference between the two surviving brothers. Yoav, when David said no, would often find a way to do it anyway — killing Avner against David's wishes, killing Avshalom against a direct order, killing Amasa without consulting the king. Avishai, when David said no, put his sword back in his sheath. The brothers shared the same instincts and the same training. They differed in one thing: Avishai's loyalty to David was stronger than his own instincts, and Yoav's instincts were stronger than his loyalty to David.

This is probably why Avishai died old and quietly and Yoav died violently at the horns of the altar.

* * *

Yoav is the most complicated of the three. He is also the most fully developed character in the entire book of Shmuel Bet after David himself. We have been tracking him across all five of the previous volumes — the killing of Avner, the siege of Rabbat Bnei Ammon, the receipt of the sealed letter about Uriah, the intercession for Avshalom's return through the wise woman of Tekoa, the killing of Avshalom in the forest of Efrayim against orders, the rebuke of David in the upper chamber at Machanayim, the killing of Amasa at the great stone at Giv'on. Every major hinge of the second half of David's reign has Yoav somewhere in it.

The pattern that emerges across all these episodes is this: Yoav's loyalty to the kingdom was absolute, but his loyalty to the king was conditional on whether the king was doing what the kingdom needed. When David's grief for Avshalom was undermining the army's morale, Yoav walked into the upper chamber at Machanayim and rebuked the king in public — an action for which any other general would have been executed within the hour, but which David accepted because he knew Yoav was right. When David's sentimental order to "deal gently with the young man" was going to result in the continuation of the rebellion, Yoav threw three darts into Avshalom's heart himself, because he knew the kingdom needed the rebellion ended. When David's decision to appoint Amasa was going to weaken the army's command structure during a live insurgency, Yoav killed Amasa himself at the great stone at Giv'on, because he knew the kingdom needed a real general, and the real general was himself.

In every one of these cases, Yoav was right about the kingdom and wrong about the king. He was right that the rebellion had to end, wrong to disobey David's order. Right that the army needed him as commander, wrong to kill Amasa to achieve it. Right that David's grief was destroying his army, wrong to speak to the king the way he spoke to him. Yoav was the man who knew what needed to be done and who would do it even when the king told him not to. He was, in a certain sense, the most effective servant David ever had. But he was also the servant whose effectiveness came at the cost of the relationship, because Yoav had stopped respecting the king's judgment and had started acting on his own authority.

This is what David was trying to capture when he said mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah. He was not saying you are evil. He was saying I cannot control you. He was saying your judgment has outgrown my authority in a way that I cannot live with but cannot undo. He was saying you were right about the right things but you were wrong about the one thing I cannot ever let you be wrong about, which is whether the king's orders are still the king's orders.

And at the very end of his life, on his deathbed, David made the decision that he had been unable to make for forty years. He told Shlomo: Deal with Yoav according to your wisdom, but let not his hoary head go down to the grave in peace. He knew Shlomo would understand. He was telling his successor: I could not fire Yoav, because he was my nephew and my general and my old friend from the cave. You are not bound by any of those things. You can do what I could not do.

Shlomo did it. And Benayahu — as we saw in the previous chapter — was the one who actually swung the sword.

* * *

The three sons of Tzeruyah. Asa'el, the fast one, killed young in the dust of Giv'on. Yoav, the effective one, killed old at the horns of the altar. Avishai, the obedient one, dying quietly in a bed we never see, with his name preserved in the middle of the register of the Thirty and then — for the last many years of the kingdom — disappearing into the silence of a faithful servant who had outlived his generation.

They were David's family. They were his oldest companions. They were the core of the Gibborim. They were also the men he could not always control and could not always forgive and could not always trust. Mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah. The phrase haunted him his whole life.

And the phrase is the Tanach's way of telling us that family is not always easy, and that the men closest to a king are not always the men the king would have chosen if he had been choosing freely, and that the loyalty of a nephew who is willing to do what the king himself will not do is both the most valuable thing a king can have and the most dangerous thing.

The sons of Tzeruyah held the kingdom together. The sons of Tzeruyah also nearly tore it apart. The Tanach preserves both halves of the story in the same verses, in the same register, in the same family name.

Mah li ve-lakhem.
