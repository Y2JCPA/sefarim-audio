Chapter Six

The Name at the End of the List

We come at last to the end of the register.

The catalogue of the Gibborim in Shmuel Bet 23 runs for thirty-two verses. It begins with the Three. It continues with the narrative of the three men breaking through the Pelishti lines to draw water from the well of Beit Lechem. It gives a special honorific paragraph to Benayahu ben Yehoyada. And then, beginning at verse 24, it becomes a list — a simple catalogue of names, each warrior identified by a patronymic and a place of origin.

The list runs for sixteen verses. It contains, by the Tanach's own count at the end, thirty and seven in all.

Most of the names in the list are unknown to any ordinary reader of the Tanach. They are preserved because the Tanach thought they should be preserved. Each name was once a man — a man with a face, a voice, a fighting style, a village he had come from, a family he had left behind. Each had once been at the cave of Adullam or had joined the band in one of the later waves of recruitment. Each had earned his way into the register by doing things that the Tanach does not bother to describe in individual detail but that were apparently memorable enough, in the oral traditions of the military, for the names to be preserved when the register was finally compiled.

We are going to read the list.

Not all at once. We will pause at a few of the names to note what the mefarshim have said about them. Then we will come to the final name — the thirty-seventh, the one at the very end — and we will stop there for the rest of the chapter, because that name is the reason this volume exists in the shape it exists.

* * *

The list begins (Shmuel Bet 23:24):

Asa'el the brother of Yoav, one of the Thirty.

We have already discussed him. He died in the dust of Giv'on, chased down by Avner's reversed spear. His name is still on the list because his legacy was still inside the brotherhood of the Thirty. The men who had run with him in the wilderness years still counted him as one of theirs.

Elchanan ben Dodo of Beit Lechem.

Elchanan returns in Shmuel Bet 21:19, where he is credited with killing Lachmi the brother of Goliath the Gitti. The parallel version in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 20:5 clarifies that it was Lachmi, the brother of Goliath, not the original Goliath himself — a clarification that resolves the apparent contradiction with David's killing of Goliath in Shmuel Aleph 17.

Shammah the Harodi.

A different Shammah from the Shammah ben Agei who held the field of lentils in Chapter Two. He was from the town of Harod, where Gidon had tested his men by the water in Shoftim 7. Shammah the Harodi came to David from the north, probably in the later waves of recruitment when the band had moved out of Yehuda.

Elika the Harodi.

Another man from Harod. Probably a relative of Shammah — perhaps a cousin or a brother — who came to David along with him. The two of them are listed consecutively, which the mefarshim read as evidence that they were paired in the field, fighting side by side as many of the Gibborim did.

Cheletz the Palti. Ira ben Ikesh the Tekoi. Aviezer the Anethoti. Mevunai the Chushathi. Tzalmon the Achochi. Maharai the Netofati. Cheleiv ben Ba'anah the Netofati.

The names continue. Each is a man. Each man is a story the Tanach has chosen not to tell in detail. The register runs down the page like the honor roll of a small unit that has been preserved by its own internal memory and then written down when the last of the original members began to die off.

About twelve verses in, we encounter a group of names from the eastern side of the Yarden and from the foreign converts who had attached themselves to David — Itai ben Rivai of Giv'at of the children of Binyamin. Benayahu the Pirathoni. Hidai of the brooks of Ga'ash. Avi-alvon the Arvati. The band had been ecumenical from the beginning. It had accepted men from every tribe and from the border regions. The list reflects the mixed origin of the kingdom's best soldiers.

And then, near the end of the list, we reach a specific pair of verses that the mefarshim treat as one of the most poignant moments in the entire register.

Shmuel Bet 23:37:

Tzelek the Ammoni.

An Ammonite. A convert. A man from the nation that the Torah had said (in Devarim 23:4) could not enter the congregation of Hashem forever. And yet here he was, in the register of David's Gibborim, under his own name, identified by his place of origin as Tzelek the Ammoni. The Tanach was not ashamed of him. The Tanach preserved his name alongside the names of the Yehudites and the Binyaminites and the Efrayimites. Tzelek the Ammoni had come to David in the wilderness years, had fought beside him, had earned his place in the Thirty, and had been honored with a name in the honor roll.

The mefarshim treat Tzelek's inclusion as a theological statement. The Torah's prohibition against accepting Ammonite converts was understood by the later tradition (Yevamot 76b) to apply only to male Ammonites, not to female Ammonites. Ammoni ve-lo Ammonit, Moavi ve-lo Moavit. The mefarshim handle Tzelek by assuming either that his mother was an Israelite (which would make him a Jew by matrilineal descent despite his Ammonite father) or that the prohibition was understood in a more limited sense in David's time. Either way, the Tanach's decision to preserve his name with his ethnic origin intact is a decision that says: this man was a Gibbor of David. His origin does not matter. His place in the register does.

And then, in the very next verse — Shmuel Bet 23:39:

Uriah the Chittite.

Thirty and seven in all.

* * *

Stop.

Read that line again.

Uriah the Chittite. Thirty and seven in all.

* * *

We have reached the reason this volume exists in its present shape.

Uriah's name at the end of the register is, by a long margin, the most devastating single line in all of Shmuel Bet's closing material. The reader who has been working through the list — past Tzelek the Ammoni, past Aviezer the Anethoti, past Ira ben Ikesh the Tekoi, past all the obscure names — comes to the last name on the list, and the last name is Uriah the Chittite, and the reader stops, because the reader remembers who Uriah was.

Uriah was one of the Thirty.

Uriah was a Gibbor of David.

Uriah had been in the cave of Adullam, or had joined the band shortly after, and had fought beside David through the wilderness years and the early wars of the kingdom, and had earned his place in the register by the same kind of years-long loyalty that had earned the place of Tzelek the Ammoni and Aviezer the Anethoti and Ira ben Ikesh the Tekoi. He had been one of the elite inner circle. He had been a friend of the king. He had slept in the same camps as David, had eaten from the same fires, had ridden on the same raids, had shared in the same victories.

And then, one spring, when the kings of Israel were supposed to go out to battle but David had stayed in Yerushalayim, David had walked on the roof of the palace in the early evening and had seen Batsheva bathing in the courtyard of her house. And David had asked his servants who she was. And the servants had told him: Is this not Batsheva the daughter of Eliam, the wife of Uriah the Chittite?

The daughter of Eliam. The granddaughter of Achitofel — the counselor who would later defect to Avshalom to avenge the wound we are about to describe. And the wife of Uriah the Chittite. Uriah's wife. One-of-the-Thirty's wife. The wife of a man who at that very moment was on campaign with Yoav at the siege of Rabbat Bnei Ammon, serving faithfully in David's army, fighting the king's wars.

We told the story of what happened next in Volume III of this series. We told it in the chapter called "The Roof in the Spring" and in the chapter called "The Loyal Soldier" and in the chapter called "The Sword and the Letter." We told it at length, with full emphasis on the terrible detail that Uriah — when summoned back from the siege and given every opportunity to go home to his wife and thus obscure the paternity of the child Batsheva was now carrying — had refused to enter his own house. He had slept at the door of the palace among the servants of the king. When David had asked him why, Uriah had given the answer that has become one of the most devastating lines in Shmuel Bet:

"My lord — the Aron, and Yisrael, and Yehuda are dwelling in booths. My lord Yoav and the servants of my lord are encamped in the open field. Shall I then go into my house, to eat and to drink and to lie with my wife? By your life and by the life of your soul, I will not do this thing."

By your life and by the life of your soul, I will not do this thing.

The answer of a Gibbor. The answer of a man who had been in the cave at the beginning and who still thought of himself as a man under discipline forty years later. The answer of a man who would not take a comfort his fellow soldiers could not take. The answer of a man whose loyalty to the brotherhood of the Gibborim was so total that he would not walk two hundred paces across a courtyard to sleep with his own wife because the Aron of Hashem was sitting in a tent on a battlefield instead of in its proper place in the tabernacle.

David had then sent Uriah back to Yoav carrying a sealed letter. The letter had ordered Yoav to place Uriah in the front rank of the next assault and then to withdraw the supporting troops around him so that Uriah would be exposed and killed. Uriah had carried the letter himself, not knowing that it was his own death sentence. Yoav had received the letter. Yoav had understood what was being asked of him. Yoav — who would later kill Avner in the gateway of Chevron and Amasa at the great stone at Giv'on and who already had a long career of morally questionable killings behind him — had nonetheless felt the weight of this particular order. He had rearranged the battle formation to expose Uriah. Uriah had died with his face to the enemy, taking an arrow or a spear in the front rank of the assault on the wall of Rabbat, carrying out his duty in the dusk of his last day.

And now — at the end of the register of the Thirty, at the very last line of the honor roll, after all the obscure names, after Tzelek the Ammoni — the Tanach writes:

Uriah the Chittite.

And then it writes: Thirty and seven in all.

And it closes the book on the Gibborim of David.

* * *

The placement is not accidental.

The mefarshim have been reading the end of Shmuel Bet 23 for two thousand years, and they have been agreed about one thing: Uriah's name at the end of the register is the Tanach's quiet, devastating verdict on David's sin. The Tanach does not need to spell out the verdict. The Tanach does not need to repeat the story. The Tanach only has to put Uriah's name at the end of the list, and the reader — if the reader has been paying attention to the rest of the book — feels the full weight of what David did without needing a single additional word.

Think about what the placement accomplishes.

First, it reminds us that Uriah was not a minor character. Uriah was not a nameless husband of Batsheva whose role in the story was to be an obstacle to be removed. Uriah was one of the Gibborim. He was a member of the most elite military unit in the kingdom. He was in the same register as Yoav and Avishai and Benayahu and the Three. He was one of the thirty-seven most important warriors of David's reign. The man David had killed was not a peripheral figure. He was a member of the innermost circle.

Second, the placement forces us to read the list of the Gibborim with the knowledge of what David had done to one of them. We cannot read the register innocently. We cannot read the stories of the Three and the Water of Beit Lechem and Benayahu and the sons of Tzeruyah without remembering that at the very end of the list we are going to come to the name of the man David killed. The whole register becomes, retrospectively, a context for the crime. The Gibborim were these men. These are the men David led and loved and fought beside and, in one catastrophic case, betrayed. The register is not a separate document. It is the list of victims of whom Uriah was the final and most painful entry.

Third, the placement is the Tanach's way of saying that Uriah's status as a Gibbor survived his death. He was killed unjustly. He was buried in an obscure grave near Rabbat Bnei Ammon with no honors and no eulogy. His name was not spoken, at least not officially, during David's lifetime. And yet — when the final register of the Thirty was compiled, at the end of David's reign, in the closing chapters of the book of Shmuel Bet — the compiler, whoever he was, put Uriah's name at the end of the list. Someone had kept the memory alive. Someone had refused to let Uriah's status as a Gibbor be erased by the circumstances of his death. Someone had said: he was one of us, and he will be in the register, and the list is not complete without him.

The identity of that someone is not known. The mefarshim suggest various possibilities. Some say it was Natan the prophet, who had confronted David about Uriah and Batsheva in the famous "you are the man" scene in Shmuel Bet 12 — Natan would have had every reason to ensure that Uriah's name was preserved in the official records. Some say it was Yoav, who had carried out the killing and who (according to some midrashic readings) felt a residual guilt that he expressed by insisting that Uriah be honored in the list — even though Yoav's own hands had been on the letter that arranged the killing. Some say it was David himself, in his later years, after the long arc of the Avshalom rebellion had worked on him for years, and after he had understood the full weight of what he had done. In this reading, the register of the Gibborim was compiled under David's own supervision, and David — now an old man, closer to his own death than to his early years of conquest — instructed the compiler to include Uriah's name at the end. It was David's own way of confessing. Not a public confession. Not a written apology. Just a name on a list, at the end of a register, where anyone paying attention could see it.

The Etzion commentary treats this possibility as the most likely. David compiled the register. David put Uriah's name at the end. David could not bring Uriah back. David could not undo the sin. But David could, in the quietest and most indirect way possible, make sure that Uriah was remembered as a Gibbor. He could make sure the list was complete. He could make sure the man who had said by your life and by the life of your soul, I will not do this thing was preserved in the honor roll of the men who had been with him from the beginning.

This is why the book of Shmuel Bet ends the register with Uriah's name and then immediately gives the count: thirty and seven in all. The count is not a summary. It is a way of saying: Uriah is the thirty-seventh. He completes the list. Without him the list would be incomplete. The kingdom is founded on thirty-seven men, and the thirty-seventh is the one David killed.

* * *

Imagine the moment of compilation.

It is late in David's reign. He is an old man. He has stopped going out to battle — the Gibborim, after Avishai had to save him from Yishbi-benov in Shmuel Bet 21, had taken his sword from his hand and told him "You shall no longer go out with us to battle, lest you quench the lamp of Israel." He had accepted the instruction. He sits now in the upper chamber of the palace in Yerushalayim, looking out toward the hills, dictating to a scribe who is preserving the records of the reign for the official archives.

The scribe is sitting on a low stool with a wax tablet on his knees. He has been working with David for several days on the register of the Gibborim. They have been going through the names in order. The scribe has been writing each one as David dictates it.

"Asa'el the brother of Yoav."

"Yes, my lord."

"Elchanan ben Dodo of Beit Lechem."

"Yes."

"Shammah the Harodi."

"Yes, my lord."

"Elika the Harodi. Cheletz the Palti. Ira ben Ikesh the Tekoi. Aviezer the Anethoti."

"Slower, my lord — I am still on Cheletz."

"Take the time you need. I am not going anywhere."

The scribe writes. They go on, name after name. Some of the names David remembers easily. Some of them he has to pause for, closing his eyes, trying to recall whether the man was from Anatot or from Anuv, whether he had been the second of the Harodites or the third. The scribe waits patiently. The list grows.

"Mevunai the Chushathi."

"Yes."

"Tzalmon the Achochi. Maharai the Netofati. Cheleiv ben Ba'anah the Netofati."

"My lord — was Cheleiv from Netofa or from Nezofa? The records vary."

"Netofa. The same town as Maharai. They were cousins. Write Netofa."

"Yes, my lord."

After perhaps an hour, they reach the second-to-last entry.

"Tzelek the Ammoni."

The scribe writes it. He looks up.

"My lord, that is thirty-six. There is one more, by my count."

David does not answer immediately.

He is looking out the window. The light is changing. It is late afternoon. The hills outside the city are turning from gold to a deeper amber as the sun moves lower. He has been sitting in this chamber for most of the day.

The scribe waits.

"My lord."

"I heard you."

"There is one more name to add. By my count, the register has thirty-seven men."

"I know there is."

"My lord — shall I write it?"

David is silent for a long time. The scribe does not press him. He has been with David for years. He understands when the king is wrestling with something that the scribe is not entitled to ask about.

Finally, David turns from the window.

"Yes. Write it."

"What is the name, my lord?"

"Uriah the Chittite."

The scribe's stylus hovers above the wax. He looks up at David. He had been expecting this. He had known, the moment he counted thirty-six, that the thirty-seventh would have to be Uriah — there was no one else from the original cave-years whose name belonged in the register but had not yet been recorded. He had been waiting for David to say it. But hearing the name spoken aloud, in the king's own voice, for the official record of the kingdom — it was different from anticipating it.

"My lord — are you sure?"

"I am sure."

"My lord — I do not have to write it. The register can end at thirty-six. No one will know the difference. The list of the Gibborim has had different counts in different versions over the years. I can simply stop here. We can call it the register of the Thirty-Six and no one will ask why there is one fewer than the records suggest there should be."

"Write it."

"My lord — "

"Write it. Uriah the Chittite. And then add the count. Thirty and seven in all. Do not leave him out. He was one of us. He was at the cave. He was at the wars in the Negev. He was at the siege of Yerushalayim. He was at every campaign of my kingdom from the beginning until the year I sent him back to Yoav with the letter. He was a Gibbor. The list is not complete without him. Write his name."

"Yes, my lord."

The scribe writes. He forms the letters carefully. Uriah ha-Chitti. Kol shloshim ve-shiv'ah. The wax holds the letters cleanly. The register is complete.

David is looking out the window again.

"My lord — would you like me to read the full register back to you, to check it?"

"No. I do not need to hear it. The list is correct. Have it copied to the official archive tomorrow morning, and bring me the wax tablet for safekeeping."

"Yes, my lord."

"And — scribe."

"My lord?"

"Do not speak about this conversation to anyone. Not to the other scribes. Not to the chief of the household. Not to Natan if he should ask. The list is the official record. The way the list was made is between you and me."

"I will not speak of it, my lord."

"Thank you."

The scribe stands, bows, and leaves the chamber. The wax tablet — the original on which David has just dictated the entire register of the Gibborim — is left on the low table beside the king's chair. David picks it up after the scribe is gone. He holds it for a long time. He runs his finger down the list of names. He stops at the last entry. Uriah ha-Chitti. He looks at it for a while.

Then he sets the tablet down and turns back to the window, and the light fades, and the chamber goes dark, and the official record of the Gibborim of David — the thirty-seven men who held the kingdom up — is complete.

* * *

The Tanach does not preserve this scene. We have invented it. But the inventing is not idle. It is the only way to feel the weight of what the bare verse is telling us. Uriah the Chittite. Thirty and seven in all. The verse is so compressed that its emotional content has to be unpacked by the reader. The image of David dictating the list — pausing at the second-to-last name, sitting in silence for a long moment, finally speaking the thirty-seventh name into the wax — is the kind of image the Tanach trusts us to construct for ourselves.

And the construction matters because it is the only way to understand why the register exists at all. The register is not a military document. It is the king's confession. It is the king's quiet act of remembering, at the end of his life, the men who had served him — and especially the one man who had served him faithfully and whom he had killed.

## The four giants of Shmuel Bet 21

There is one more piece of the catalogue to deal with before we close this volume.

Shmuel Bet 21 — a chapter we have mostly been saving for Volume VII — contains, in its second half, a brief catalogue of four encounters between David's men and four Pelishti giants who were the descendants of the Rafa'im, the ancient race of giants. The four giants are:

Yishbi-benov, whom Avishai kills in battle after Yishbi-benov has come close to killing the aging David.

Saf, whom Sibbechai the Chushati kills in a battle at Gov.

Lachmi the brother of Goliath the Gitti, whom Elchanan ben Ya'arei Oregim of Beit Lechem kills in a different battle at Gov.

A giant of Gat, a man of great stature who had six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot — twenty-four in all, whom Yonatan the son of Shimea the brother of David kills.

Four giants. Four killings. Each killing handled by a different Gibbor.

The first of the four killings — Avishai's killing of Yishbi-benov — is particularly poignant because it happens in a moment when David himself is in mortal danger. The Tanach preserves the scene with these details:

"And David grew faint, and Yishbi-benov, who was of the sons of the Rafa, and the weight of whose spear was three hundred shekels of brass in weight, being girded with new armor, thought to slay David. But Avishai the son of Tzeruyah came to his help, and struck the Pelishti and killed him."

David had grown faint in the middle of the battle. He was an old man now. His sword was slower than it had been. He had nearly been overpowered by a Pelishti giant whose spear weighed three hundred shekels of brass. And Avishai — quiet, loyal, obedient Avishai, the same Avishai who had crept into Shaul's camp at Chakhilah forty years earlier and had asked David for permission to kill Shaul with a single blow — had intervened at the last possible moment and killed the giant with his own sword and saved David's life.

Imagine the moment.

David is on one knee in the dust of the field. His shield arm is shaking. Yishbi-benov is standing over him with the spear raised. The spearhead is enormous — the size of a man's head. The shaft is the length of a small tree. The giant is grinning. He has been waiting his whole life for this — for the chance to kill the king of Israel in single combat — and the moment has come.

He brings the spear down.

Avishai comes from the left, at full speed, sword already drawn. He reaches the giant in three strides. He does not bother with finesse. He drives the sword into the giant's side under the raised arm — into the soft place below the ribs where the giant's armor does not reach. The giant grunts. His arm sags. Avishai pulls the sword out and strikes again, this time at the back of the neck. The giant falls forward across David. David rolls out from under the body just before it lands.

For a long moment, no one speaks. David is on his back in the dust, breathing hard, looking up at the sky. Avishai is standing over him with the bloody sword still in his hand. The other Gibborim are running across the field toward them.

Avishai speaks first.

"My lord."

"Avishai."

"My lord — you cannot do this anymore."

"Do what?"

"You cannot go out with us to battle. You cannot stand in the front line. You cannot ride at the head of the army. We came too close today. If I had been on the other side of the field, you would be dead in the dirt right now."

"I have been a soldier my whole life, Avishai. I have led every campaign of this kingdom. I cannot suddenly stop."

"You can. You will. The kingdom needs you in Yerushalayim, not in the field. The Gibborim will fight your wars from now on. You will sit in the upper chamber of the palace and you will write your psalms and you will plan the building of the Temple by your son, and we will fight the Pelishtim and the Aramim and whoever else needs fighting, and we will report to you when we return."

David looks around at the other Gibborim who have just arrived. They are all watching him. They are all waiting for his answer.

Yoav steps forward.

"My lord. Avishai is right. We have all just understood the same thing. The lamp of Israel cannot be quenched in a Pelishti skirmish. You are too valuable. We are not. Sit at home. Let us go out without you."

Benayahu steps forward.

"My lord — we are swearing this. All of us. The Kreti and the Pelti as well. We will not allow you to ride out with us anymore. If you try, we will not unsaddle the horses for you. We will refuse to let you mount."

David looks from one face to another. Yoav. Avishai. Benayahu. The faces of the men who have been with him since the cave of Adullam. The men who have buried friends for him. The men who have just saved his life. The men who are now telling him, in the only language he can hear, that the time has come for him to step back from the field.

He nods slowly.

"As you say."

The Tanach preserves the moment in a single sentence. They swore to him. The Gibborim swore an oath. The king accepted it. From that day forward, David did not lead the army into battle. He went home to Yerushalayim. He sat in the upper chamber of the palace. He wrote psalms. He planned the building of the Temple by his son. And the Gibborim went out without him.

It is a beautiful moment. It is also the moment at which the book of Shmuel Bet is quietly telling us that the era of David the warrior-king is coming to an end. From this point forward, David would be the king only — the one who sat on the throne and gave orders and wrote psalms and prepared for the building of the Temple. The fighting would be done by the Gibborim. And the Gibborim would fight his wars until the day he died and then they would bury him and then they would serve his son.

The register of the Thirty at the end of chapter 23 is the Tanach's way of making sure we remember their names when the wars are over.

## The closing of the volume

We began this volume with a question: who were the men who made it all possible?

We have now answered the question as fully as the Tanach allows us to answer it. They were men who had been broken before they came to David — metzok, nosheh, mar nefesh, the distressed and the indebted and the bitter of soul. They were men who gathered in a cave because they had nowhere else to go and who stayed with David because he treated them like the men they were, not the men the world had reduced them to. They were men who trained in the wilderness for a decade under a fugitive prince and who emerged as the most disciplined military unit in the ancient Near East. They were men who conquered Yerushalayim for David and who brought the Aron up from Kiryat Ye'arim and who held Avshalom's rebellion back with their bodies in the forest of Efrayim and who chased Sheva ben Bichri through the tribes to Avel Beit Ma'akhah. They were the Gibborim.

The Three were the peak: Yoshev-baShevet, Elazar ben Dodo, Shammah ben Agei. The three signature deeds of the ballad register.

The Water of Beit Lechem was the moment of the highest expression of their loyalty: the three men breaking through the Pelishti lines to draw water from the well of David's childhood, and the king pouring the water out before Hashem because he would not drink the blood of men who had risked their lives for him.

Benayahu ben Yehoyada was the scholar-warrior whose biography in the rabbinic tradition grew and grew until it became the longest continuous narrative in the Babylonian Talmud, and whose grave was revealed to Rabbi Chaim Vital in the sixteenth century and whose titles became the names of the Ben Ish Chai's entire literary corpus.

The Sons of Tzeruyah were the complicated ones — the nephews whose loyalty held the kingdom together and whose methods nearly tore it apart, the three brothers who died three different deaths and whose story was the substance of David's lifelong refrain mah li ve-lakhem, benei Tzeruyah.

And at the very end of the register, after all of the others, the Tanach preserves the name of the one man whose story does not have a heroic ending. Uriah the Chittite. The thirty-seventh. The Gibbor who was killed not by the Pelishtim, not by the Ammonim, not by the Aramim, but by a letter sealed in Yerushalayim and delivered by his own hand to his own commander. The Gibbor who died at the wall of Rabbat Bnei Ammon carrying out an order he did not know was meant for him.

Uriah the Chittite.

Thirty and seven in all.

The Tanach closes the register with his name. It does not add a eulogy. It does not add a lament. It does not explain itself. It simply writes the name at the end of the list and then writes the count and then moves on, because it trusts the reader — if the reader has been reading the whole book — to understand what the placement means.

And if you have been reading this volume of Sipurei Shmuel Bet, you understand.

You understand that the register of the Gibborim is not a military document. It is a moral document. It is a list of the men who made the kingdom possible, and it is a reminder that the kingdom was not only built on their loyalty but was also, in one terrible case, built on the sacrifice of one of them by the very king they had served.

The Gibborim held the kingdom up. Uriah was one of the Gibborim. Uriah died at David's order. The kingdom continued, but only because the other Gibborim went on serving David even after they understood what had happened to Uriah. Their loyalty to the king outlasted the king's loyalty to at least one of them. That loyalty is what makes the kingdom survive the sin. That loyalty is what makes the register worth preserving. That loyalty is the reason the Tanach takes the time, in the middle of the closing chapters of the book, to stop and write down all thirty-seven names.

They were the ones who made it possible.

Every one of them. Even the one at the end.
