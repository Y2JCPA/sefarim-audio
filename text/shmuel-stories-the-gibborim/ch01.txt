Chapter One

Bitter of Soul — The Cave of Adullam

## Volume VI — The Gibborim ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

To understand the warriors, we have to go back.

All the way back. Past the rebellion of Sheva ben Bichri, past the death of Avshalom in the forest of Efrayim, past the sin on the roof in the spring, past the bringing of the Aron up from Kiryat Ye'arim, past the coronation in Chevron, past the lament over Shaul and Yonatan on Mount Gilboa. Back to the earliest years of David's life — back to the years before he was a king, before he was a commander of armies, before he was even a fugitive prince. Back to the hill country of Yehuda, in a cave with a low entrance and a deep interior, in a village called Adullam, at a moment when David was nothing but a hunted man with a price on his head and nowhere else to go.

Because the men who would eventually be called ha-gibborim — the Mighty Men, the elite warriors whose names would be inscribed in the closing chapters of Shmuel Bet in a list as carefully preserved as any list of high priests or kings — those men did not come to David from the royal court. They did not come from the army of Shaul. They did not come from the great houses of the northern tribes or the wealthy estates of Yehuda. They came from the cave. And they came, every one of them, from lives that had broken before they found him.

The book of Shmuel Aleph preserves the origin story in one of the most famous and most cryptic verses in all of Tanach. "And everyone who was in distress, and everyone who was in debt, and everyone who was bitter of soul, gathered themselves unto him; and he became a captain over them. And there were with him about four hundred men."

Three words describe the men.

Metzok. In distress.

Nosheh. In debt.

Mar nefesh. Bitter of soul.

Read those three words slowly. They are not the words Tanach uses for warriors. They are not the words Tanach uses for heroes. They are the words Tanach uses for the brokenhearted, the humiliated, the failed, the ones the world had decided to throw away. The men who came to David at the cave of Adullam were not volunteers for a glamorous expedition. They were refugees from their own lives.

* * *

David had arrived at the cave a few weeks earlier.

He had been running from Shaul for some time by this point. The final break between Shaul and David had already happened — the moment when Shaul's madness had hardened into a fixed determination to kill the young man who had once played the harp in his court and had once stood up for Israel against Golyat of Gat in the valley of Elah. David had fled from Gibeah. He had gone to the priest Achimelech at Nov and had taken the sword of Golyat from the sanctuary — the same sword he had won from the giant years earlier, now wrapped in a cloth behind the ephod. He had fled to Achish, the Pelishti king of Gat, and had pretended to be insane to avoid being killed. He had fled from Gat with no particular destination in mind.

He had arrived at the cave of Adullam because there was nowhere else left to go.

The cave was in the lowlands of Yehuda, in the Shefelah, about sixteen miles southwest of Yerushalayim. It was a large limestone cave with a narrow entrance that opened into a deep interior chamber. The chamber was big enough to shelter dozens of people. The narrow entrance made it defensible — a single man with a sword could hold the entrance against many attackers. The surrounding hills were honeycombed with smaller caves that could serve as lookouts and supply caches. The site was, in its way, an ideal hideout for a man on the run.

David entered the cave alone on the first day.

He had no one with him. Not a servant, not a companion, not a member of his family, not a single soldier. The future king of Israel, the anointed of Hashem, the man for whom the prophet Shmuel had poured the horn of oil in the courtyard of his father's house in Beit Lechem — was sitting alone in the dark interior of a limestone cave in the Shefelah with the sword of Golyat across his knees and the weight of his own life pressing down on him from all sides.

The tradition preserved in the title of Psalm 142 says that David wrote that psalm in the cave.

Maskil le-David. Be-heyoto ba-me'arah tefilah. "A contemplation of David. When he was in the cave. A prayer."

The psalm is the prayer of a man at the end of his rope. "I cry out with my voice to Hashem. With my voice to Hashem I make my supplication. I pour out my complaint before Him. I declare before Him my trouble. When my spirit was overwhelmed within me, then You knew my path. In the way where I was walking, they have hidden a snare for me. I looked on my right hand, and behold, there was no man who would know me. Refuge had failed me. No man cared for my soul." Every line is the voice of a man who has been deserted by the world. No refuge. No friend. No one who cares for his soul. And then the last verse: "Bring my soul out of prison, that I may praise Your name. The righteous shall surround me, for You shall deal bountifully with me."

That last verse is a prophecy.

It is David prophesying, in the middle of his despair, that he would not always be alone. The righteous shall surround me. The prayer of a man in a cave, predicting that the cave would become the gathering place of an army. The prayer of a man who had no friends, predicting that he would someday be surrounded by tzadikim. The prayer of a man whose only companion was the sword of Golyat, predicting that the sword would someday be the standard of a kingdom.

The prayer was answered within days.

* * *

The first to arrive was David's family.

Shmuel Aleph 22:1 records it in a single line: "His brothers and all his father's house heard, and they went down to him there."

They had been in Beit Lechem, five miles to the east. They had heard the report that David was in the cave at Adullam. They understood, immediately, what the report meant: Shaul was hunting David, and Shaul did not confine his hunting to the man he was targeting. Shaul would also come for the family. Shaul would come for David's father Yishai, who was old and could not run. Shaul would come for David's mother, who was older still. Shaul would come for the seven brothers — some of whom had never approved of David in the first place, but all of whom were now legally connected to the most wanted man in Israel.

The whole family packed up what they could carry and walked south to the cave.

David heard them approaching. He came out of the cave. He saw his father — old, bent, walking with a staff, supported by two of his sons. He saw his mother — even older, wrapped in a traveling cloak. He saw his brothers. Eliav, the oldest, the one who had mocked him when he came to the battlefield in the valley of Elah and asked about Golyat. Abinadav, the second. Shammah, the third. The younger ones whose names are not preserved in the Tanach but who were there that day.

He walked toward them.

"Father."

Yishai lifted his head. His eyes were wet.

"David. My son."

They embraced.

Then David turned to his mother and embraced her.

Then he turned to his brothers — one after another, including Eliav, who had once mocked him in the valley of Elah, and who was now coming to his cave because there was nowhere else safe for him in the land. David embraced Eliav without comment. Whatever the old wound had been between them, it was no longer relevant.

"How many of you are there?"

"All of us. The whole house. Wives. Children. Servants."

David looked past his brothers. Behind them, on the path that led up from the lowland, a long line of people was still arriving. Relatives. Servants. Dependents. Members of the extended clan of Yishai. Small children being carried. Women with bundles on their heads. Old men leaning on the shoulders of younger men.

He closed his eyes for a moment. He opened them.

"Welcome, all of you. Come into the cave. There is room. We will figure out the rest as we go."

* * *

The decision about David's parents was the first real decision he made as a leader of men.

His father and mother were too old to live in a cave. The cave was cold at night. The ground was hard. The food was uncertain. The risk of a Shaulite raid was constant. Yishai and his wife would not survive more than a few weeks in those conditions. David had to find them somewhere safer.

He called his brothers together.

"I am going to take Father and Mother to the king of Moav."

Eliav stared at him.

"Moav? The Moavim are our enemies. Shaul just fought them in a border war last year."

"The Moavim are our political enemies. But our great-grandmother Rut was a Moavite. Our family has blood ties to the royal house of Moav. The king of Moav will honor the claim of descent — he has to, or he insults his own lineage. Father and Mother will be safe there. It will buy us time."

"And if Shaul marches on Moav to get them?"

"Shaul is not going to march on Moav. Shaul is losing in the north. He cannot afford another front in the east. Father and Mother will be safe there for as long as we need."

Eliav nodded slowly.

"All right. When will you go?"

"Tomorrow at dawn. Just me and two of the servants. I will ride ahead with Father and Mother to Mitzpeh of Moav, present them to the king, and return within a week. While I am gone, you are in charge of the cave. Do not let anyone leave. Do not let anyone enter. If men come asking for me, do not tell them I am here. If they are friendly, tell them to wait."

"Understood."

* * *

David made the journey to Mitzpeh of Moav and returned within the week, as he had promised. His father and mother were safely installed in the house of the king of Moav, protected by the ancient blood claim of Rut. They would remain there for the entire period David was in the cave and in the years of wandering that followed. They would not return to the land of Israel in David's lifetime as refugees — only later, after Shaul was dead and David was established in Chevron, would they come back.

But while David was gone, something happened at the cave.

Men began to arrive.

Not just a few men. Not just the family and its dependents. A steady trickle, and then a stream, and then a flood, of men from all over the land of Yehuda and from the lower reaches of the Shefelah, walking up the hill toward the cave of Adullam and asking, at the narrow entrance, whether David was there.

Eliav was at the entrance when the first of them came.

He was a man of perhaps thirty — unshaven, wearing a travel-stained cloak, with calluses on his hands that suggested he had once worked the soil and with a face that suggested he had not been sleeping well for several months.

"I am looking for David ben Yishai."

Eliav looked him over.

"Who are you?"

"My name is Avram. I am from a small village in the hills east of Chevron. I had a small vineyard there. Two bad years in a row ruined the harvest. I borrowed from a man in the next village to buy seed. I could not pay him back. He took the vineyard. He took my house. He said he would take my daughter as a household slave if I did not find the money within a month. I could not find the money. I ran. I have been sleeping in the hills for two weeks. I heard that David the son of Yishai was at this cave. I heard he takes in men who have nowhere else to go. I came."

Eliav studied him.

"Who told you David takes in men?"

"The shepherds in the hills. They say the son of Yishai is in the cave at Adullam and that he is gathering men who are in distress. I am in distress. My daughter is going to be a slave if I do not do something. I have nothing left. I came."

"David is not here right now. He will be back within a few days. Wait outside. There is a spring at the foot of the hill where you can drink. Do not enter the cave until David returns. When he returns, I will tell him you are here."

"Thank you."

The man walked back down the hill toward the spring.

Eliav watched him go. Then he turned and went back into the cave, where his brothers were sitting around a small fire. He told them about the man who had arrived.

Abinadav frowned.

"Where did he hear that David was here? We have not sent messengers. We have been here less than a week."

"I do not know. He said the shepherds are talking about it."

"The shepherds?"

"He said the shepherds in the hills know David is here. I think the news has spread from village to village, man to man, on the shepherd roads. David is the only anointed figure in the land who is not Shaul. He is the only one who has anything that looks like the blessing of the prophet Shmuel. Men who are desperate are always looking for an anointed one who is not the current king. They are coming here because they have nowhere else to go and because somewhere in their hearts they know that David has been anointed to be the next king even if nobody else will say it out loud."

Eliav did not say anything for a while.

"How many more do you think will come?"

"I do not know. Ten? A hundred? A thousand?"

"A thousand?"

"I do not know."

* * *

Over the next several days, more men arrived.

The second man came in the late afternoon of the day after the first. He was an older man — perhaps forty-five — with graying hair and the weathered hands of a man who had worked outdoors for most of his life. He said his name was Shemaryahu and that he had been a soldier in the army of Shaul before the present crisis.

"Why did you leave the army?" Eliav asked.

"I was in one of the units that was supposed to deliver a message to the priests at Nov. I was in the unit just before the one that Do'eg the Edomi joined. I was sent home on leave three days before the massacre. I came back from my leave and found that my whole squadron had been ordered to help kill the priests. I refused. I walked out of the camp. I have been wandering ever since. I heard David was at Adullam. I came."

"You know David was the one at Nov the day before the massacre. You know David took the sword of Golyat from Achimelech."

"I know. That is why I came. If Shaul killed the priests for helping David, then the priests are on David's side, and I am on the side of the priests. If the choice is between Shaul and the man the priests were willing to die for, I choose the man the priests were willing to die for. Where do I go?"

Eliav pointed him toward the spring.

"Wait there. David will be back within a few days."

The third man came the next morning. He was young — perhaps twenty — and he said his name was Yehonadav. He had been living in a border village near the Pelishti territory. He had been drafted into Shaul's border militia and sent to watch the passes into the Shefelah. His unit had been attacked by a Pelishti raiding party and wiped out — he was the only survivor. He had walked home to his village to find that the village had been burned by the same raiders who had wiped out his unit. His parents were dead. His younger brother was dead. His wife, to whom he had been married for three months, was dead. He had nothing left.

"Why did you come here?"

"Because everyone I know is dead. Because I have nowhere to go. Because I heard the son of Yishai was here. Because I heard he was the only man in the land who cared about men like me."

Eliav pointed him toward the spring.

The fourth man came that afternoon. And the fifth. And the sixth. By evening, there were twelve men at the spring at the foot of the hill. By the end of the next day, there were thirty. By the end of the week, there were eighty.

Eliav kept them all at the spring. He did not let them into the cave. He gave them what bread the family could spare. He sent two of his younger brothers to watch them and make sure none of them tried to approach the cave without permission. He waited for David to return.

* * *

David returned on the eighth day.

He came up the hill at sunset, alone except for the two servants who had accompanied him to Moav and back. He was travel-worn. His face was drawn. He had completed the task of delivering his parents to safety, and the task had been emotionally exhausting — leaving his elderly mother in the hands of a foreign king, even a king with an ancestral obligation, was not an easy thing.

He crested the hill and saw the crowd at the spring below.

He stopped.

The crowd was large. Eighty men, maybe more. They were sitting on the rocks around the spring, talking quietly, waiting for something. A few of them had small fires going. A few were sleeping under blankets they had brought with them. None of them was armed with more than the kind of small knife a traveler would carry. They did not look like an army. They looked like refugees.

David turned to the servant walking beside him.

"Who are those men?"

"I do not know, my lord."

"Go and fetch Eliav."

The servant ran the rest of the way up the hill to the cave and returned a few minutes later with Eliav. Eliav came down the hill to where David was standing.

"David."

"Eliav. Who are those men at the spring?"

Eliav took a breath.

"They are men who have come asking for you. They have been arriving since two days after you left for Moav. A few at first. Then more. Now there are eighty or so. I have been keeping them at the spring and not letting them into the cave. I did not know what to do with them."

"Eighty?"

"Eighty. And more arriving every day."

"Who are they? Where are they from?"

"They are from all over Yehuda. Some from the Shefelah, some from the hill country, some from as far away as Gilead across the Yarden. Most of them are men who have lost everything — bankrupted by debt, displaced by war, exiled by Shaul's enforcers, wounded in small ways that have added up to the end of their lives. One of them is a former soldier in Shaul's army who refused to kill the priests at Nov. One of them is a young man whose whole family was killed by a Pelishti raid. One of them is a farmer who lost his vineyard to a creditor. Each of them has a different story. But all of the stories end the same way."

"How do the stories end?"

"Each of them says, I heard the son of Yishai was at this cave. I came because I had nowhere else to go."

David looked down at the men at the spring.

He stood there for a long time. He did not move. He did not speak. The servants behind him did not speak either — they understood that the moment was important and that David needed the silence. The sun was setting behind the hills to the west. The light was turning gold, then orange, then red. The men at the spring had lit a few small fires. The fires flickered in the gathering dusk. There were more men now than David had expected to see at the spring of a cave where he had thought he would be hiding alone.

Finally he spoke.

"Eighty men. And more arriving every day."

"Yes."

"Have any of them said why they are coming to me specifically?"

"They all say the same thing, David. They say they heard you were here. They say the shepherds in the hills are talking about you. They say you are the anointed of Hashem."

"The anointed of Hashem."

"That is what they say."

"Eliav — "

"Yes?"

"Bring me the first ten of them. Right now. One at a time. I want to hear their stories myself. I want to know who they are. I want to know what they have lost. I want to know why they came."

Eliav nodded.

* * *

The first man Eliav brought up the hill was Avram — the farmer who had lost his vineyard. David sat down on a flat rock at the entrance to the cave and motioned for Avram to sit on another rock across from him.

"Your name is Avram."

"Yes, my lord."

"Do not call me my lord. I am not a king. I am a man who is hiding in a cave just like you are. Call me David."

"Yes. David."

"Tell me your story. All of it. Take your time."

Avram told his story. The vineyard. The two bad harvests. The loan from the neighbor. The inability to repay. The loss of the vineyard. The loss of the house. The threat against his daughter. The flight to the hills. The rumor of David at Adullam. The long walk from the hills east of Chevron to the spring at the bottom of this hill. He told the story in halting sentences, the way a man tells a story he has been afraid to tell for a long time. When he was done, he looked down at his hands.

David did not speak for a moment.

"Avram. Listen to me. The vineyard is not coming back. Not today. The house is not coming back. Not today. I cannot pay the debt that was owed. I cannot restore the land. I do not have the resources to do those things. But I can tell you this: your daughter is not going to be taken as a slave. I will not allow it. If the creditor comes for her, he will have to come through me and through every man who sits in this cave. That is the first thing."

Avram looked up.

"The second thing is this. You are welcome here. You will sleep in this cave tonight. You will eat from the same bread we eat. You will be under my protection for as long as you need to be under my protection. I cannot promise you a great future. I cannot promise you anything beyond today. But I can promise you today. Is that enough to begin with?"

"My — my lord. Yes. Yes, it is enough."

"Do not call me my lord. Come. I will introduce you to my brothers. You will sleep inside the cave tonight. Eat first. There is bread in the back chamber."

David stood up. Avram stood up with him. They walked together into the cave.

* * *

David met with each of the men individually that night.

He sat on the flat rock at the entrance to the cave, and one by one, the men came up from the spring and sat across from him and told him their stories. Shemaryahu the former soldier. Yehonadav the young man whose village had been burned. A shepherd from the hills whose flock had been taken by a rival clan. A scribe from a small town whose master had accused him of theft and expelled him without proof. A stonemason who had been wounded in Shaul's last campaign and who could no longer use his right arm and had been thrown out of the army without pension. A priest's servant who had fled Nov on the night before Do'eg's massacre and had been wandering the hills for weeks, afraid to reveal his identity to any traveler. A boy of sixteen whose father had been a tax collector for Shaul and had been lynched by a mob of starving farmers and whose whole family had scattered into the Shefelah in fear.

Each story was different. Each ending was the same. I heard the son of Yishai was at this cave. I came because I had nowhere else to go.

David listened to all of them.

He did not interrupt. He did not promise any of them more than he could deliver. He did not preach to them or try to convert their grief into something glorious. He simply listened, and at the end of each story he said some version of the same thing: You are welcome here. You will eat from the same bread. You will be under my protection. Come into the cave.

By the middle of the night, all eighty men were sleeping inside the cave or in the chambers of the smaller caves nearby. David was still sitting on the flat rock at the entrance. Eliav came out and sat next to him.

"Eighty men."

"Yes."

"What are we going to do with them, David?"

David looked at his brother in the moonlight.

"Eliav. I do not know yet. I know this. Those men came here because they had nowhere else to go. If I send them away, they will die — of hunger, of exposure, of the creditors who are waiting to catch them, of the Pelishti raids, of the Shaulite enforcers who will find them in the hills. They came to me. Me, Eliav — a man who has nothing. A man who is sitting in a cave with the sword of Golyat on his knees. A man who is less powerful, at this moment, than any of the village elders who exiled them. And still they came. Because I was the only option left. Because they heard my name and they heard the word anointed and they decided that even a weak anointed one was better than a strong Shaul."

"So what are you going to do?"

"I am going to make them into something."

"Into what?"

"Into the men they were supposed to be before their lives broke."

Eliav did not answer.

David went on.

"Eliav, listen. Each of those men came here because he was metzok — in distress. Each of them came because he was nosheh — in debt, owing things he could not pay. Each of them came because he was mar nefesh — bitter of soul, so full of pain and grief that he could not see the next morning without wanting to close his eyes. I cannot change what broke them. The vineyard is gone. The village is burned. The brother is dead. The wife is dead. The arm does not work. Those things are not coming back. But I can give them something they do not have right now, which is a purpose. A reason to get up in the morning. A place where they are not humiliated, where they are not alone, where they are part of something that is larger than their own private grief. That is what I am going to do. I am going to make them into an army. Not just any army. My army. The army that Hashem has been building for me while I was hiding from Shaul and did not know it. The righteous shall surround me, I prayed in this very cave eight days ago. That was the prayer. And now the prayer is being answered. The righteous are surrounding me. They are eighty men sleeping in the chambers of this cave because they had nowhere else to go, and I am going to make them into the righteous that I prayed for."

He turned to look at his brother.

"Eliav. I am going to train them. I am going to feed them. I am going to hold them together. I am going to teach them what I know about fighting and what I know about leadership and what I know about staying alive in the hills. Some of them will be with me for a few months and will go home when the situation changes. Some of them will be with me for years. And some of them — I can feel it, Eliav, the way I could feel it in the valley of Elah before I picked up the five smooth stones — some of them are going to be with me all the way to the end. To the throne, if we get there. To the battles that come after. To the kingdom. They came here broken. They are going to leave this cave as warriors."

Eliav looked at his brother for a long moment.

"David — "

"Yes?"

"You sound like you already believe it."

"I already do believe it."

"Eight days ago you were sitting alone in this cave writing a psalm about how nobody cared for your soul. Now you are sitting at the entrance telling me you are going to build an army."

"Eight days ago I did not have eighty men at the spring."

"And you have eighty men at the spring because — "

"Because Hashem sent them to me. I did not know He was sending them. I did not know any of them were coming. I was sitting in the cave writing the psalm and thinking I was alone, and the whole time Hashem was walking them up the shepherd roads of Yehuda toward this hill. That is how Hashem works, Eliav. You pray to Him about being alone and He starts moving the men who will not leave you alone in the direction you cannot see yet. By the time you finish the prayer, the men are already on the road."

Eliav nodded slowly.

"So what do we do first?"

"First we assign sleeping positions inside the cave so that no one is lying in a dangerous spot. Second we work out the food supply — we have enough bread for three more days, and we are going to need more bread than that. Third, in the morning, I want to start talking to the men about what they can do. Some of them were soldiers. Some of them were farmers. Some of them were scribes. I want to know what each of them knows. And fourth — and this is the thing I am going to need your help with most — I want to start training them. Today. Tomorrow morning at first light. I want to teach them how to handle a sword and a spear. The ones who already know will help me teach the ones who do not. By the end of a month, I want every man in this cave to be able to defend himself in a close fight. By the end of three months, I want every man in this cave to be able to fight in a coordinated unit. By the end of six months, I want every man in this cave to be one of the most dangerous fighters in the land of Yehuda."

Eliav was silent.

"Do you think I am being unrealistic?"

"No, David. I think you are being — you. The same David who went out to fight Golyat with five stones and a sling. The same David who killed the lion and the bear when they came for the sheep. The same David. I do not think you are being unrealistic. I think you are going to do exactly what you say you are going to do. I am just a little stunned at the speed of it."

"The speed was Hashem's. Not mine. I just got here eight days ago."

"I know."

* * *

The training began the next morning.

David woke up before dawn. He came out of the cave with the sword of Golyat and stood on the flat rock at the entrance and called the eighty men to him. They came, bleary-eyed, from the various chambers where they had been sleeping. They gathered in a loose circle around the rock.

"Good morning. My name is David ben Yishai. Most of you already know that. I want to say something to you before we start the day."

They listened.

"Each of you came here last night because you had nowhere else to go. Each of you told me a story that ended with loss. I heard those stories. I am not going to forget them. I want you to know that. What happened to you happened. I am not going to pretend it did not happen. I am not going to pretend that you are going to get your vineyards back, or your brothers back, or your villages back. I am not a wizard. I cannot undo the losses. But I am going to tell you what I can do, and I am going to ask you what you are willing to do in return."

The men were very still.

"Here is what I can do. I can give you food. I can give you shelter. I can give you the protection of this cave and of every man in it. I can give you training in how to use a sword and a spear. I can give you companions — men who understand what you have lost because they have lost the same things. I can give you a reason to wake up in the morning that is better than the reason you had yesterday. And — if Hashem wills it — I can give you a future. Not today. Not next week. Not next month. Maybe not this year. But someday, if we are all still alive and if the anointing I received from Shmuel means what I believe it means, I am going to become the king of Israel. And on that day, the men who stood with me in this cave will not be forgotten. They will be the men who ride beside me through the gates of Yerushalayim. They will be the men whose names will be written in the court records. They will be the men who will never have to worry about debts or broken vineyards or burned villages ever again — not because the loss will be undone, but because the future will be large enough to hold both the loss and the restoration together."

The men did not move.

"That is what I can offer. Here is what I am asking from you in return. I am asking for loyalty. Not to me as a person — loyalty to a person is a weak thing. I am asking for loyalty to the cause: the cause of becoming the men you were supposed to be. The men who do not run from their own names. The men who do not hide their pain behind bitterness. The men who wake up every morning and train until their muscles burn because they have decided to stop being victims and start being warriors. That is what I am asking. It is a hard thing. It is harder than what you were doing yesterday, in some ways. But in one other way it is easier — because yesterday you were alone, and today you are not."

He raised the sword of Golyat.

"Every man here who is willing to do this — raise your hand."

Eighty hands went up.

They did not hesitate. They did not look at each other first. They did not wait to see what the man next to them would do. Every single man in the circle raised his hand in the same moment, and every single man was looking at David, and every single face was already changing — in small, almost imperceptible ways — from the face of a broken refugee into the face of a soldier who had just rediscovered the possibility of his own strength.

David lowered the sword.

"Good. Then let us begin."

* * *

The training lasted for months.

The number at the cave grew. Eighty became a hundred. A hundred became two hundred. Two hundred became three hundred. By the end of the third month, the Tanach's count of four hundred men had been reached. By the end of the fourth month, six hundred. The cave and the chambers around it were full. David had organized the men into sub-units — squads of ten, platoons of fifty, companies of a hundred — each with its own appointed leader, each with its own assigned training schedule, each with its own rotating duty for watch and supply and foraging.

A few of the names that would later appear in the closing list of Shmuel Bet 23 were already present in the cave in these early months.

Shammah ben Agei the Harari, who would later stand alone against a Pelishti raid in a lentil field and become one of the Three. He arrived at the cave in the second month, a middle-aged farmer from the highlands whose family had been displaced by a Pelishti incursion. He turned out to have an extraordinary aptitude for the spear — no one in the cave could throw farther or straighter — and within six weeks he was teaching the other men.

Elazar ben Dodo, who would become the second of the Three. He arrived in the third month, a young man with a cold steady gaze and enormous hands. When David first put a sword in his hand, the sword looked small against his grip. Elazar did not smile much and did not talk much, but when he trained he trained harder than anyone else. David started calling him the hand, because his hand seemed to have been made for the hilt.

Yoshev-baShevet (the Tanach's somewhat corrupted form of his name — the name is preserved slightly differently in Chronicles as Yashovam), who would become the captain of the Three. He arrived later — perhaps in the sixth month, after the cave was already at six hundred men. He was the most professional soldier of all the early arrivals; he had served in Shaul's army as a mid-level officer and had deserted specifically to join David when he heard the reports about the cave. He came with his own weapons and his own armor. David made him a captain on the day he arrived.

Asa'el ben Tzeruyah, one of David's own nephews — the son of his sister Tzeruyah — arrived early, with his two older brothers Avishai and Yoav. The three brothers came together from the household of Tzeruyah, which had been the household of David's sister and therefore part of the extended family that had walked south from Beit Lechem after the news of Adullam. Asa'el was already famous, even in his youth, for being extraordinarily fast on his feet. The Tanach would later describe him as "swift-footed like a gazelle of the field." He was perhaps twenty years old when he arrived. Avishai, his brother, was in his mid-twenties. Yoav, the eldest, was twenty-six or twenty-seven. All three of them were trained in the use of weapons by the standards of village sons of a Yehudite clan, but none of them had the formal military training that David was now teaching. They became David's pupils and his family at the same time.

Benayahu ben Yehoyada is not yet in the cave during these early months. He will come later — he is the son of Yehoyada the Kohen, a priestly household from the town of Kabtze'el in the far south of Yehuda, and he will arrive in David's service only after the cave period, in the years when David was wandering the wilderness of Zif and En Gedi. He will become the most legendary of all the warriors, the subject of the Berachot 18a sugya, the captain of the Kreti and the Pelti, and eventually the executioner of Yoav at the horns of the altar. But his name is not on the roster of the cave. His greatness comes later.

* * *

What mattered about the cave was not the individual names.

What mattered was the transformation.

The men who walked into the cave at Adullam were broken. The men who walked out, six months or a year or three years later, were not. The brokenness did not disappear — that is never how it works. Each of them still carried the grief he had brought in. The farmer who had lost his vineyard still dreamed at night about the rows of vines. The father whose daughter had been taken as a slave still wept when he thought of her, and still hoped, in the silent parts of his heart, that he might someday be able to find her again. The soldier who had refused to kill the priests at Nov still carried the memory of his comrades who had obeyed. The boy whose village had been burned still saw the faces of his family in every dream. The grief was not erased.

But the grief was no longer the whole of them.

They had something else now. They had a place where they belonged. They had companions who understood them without needing the stories to be retold. They had a commander who had listened to each of them personally on the night they arrived and who still, six months later, remembered each of their names and each of their histories and each of the specific things that had broken them. They had skills. They had weapons. They had a sense that their lives were not over, that the humiliation they had experienced was not the last chapter, that there was still a story to be written and that they were still going to be characters in it.

This is the deeper meaning of the origin of the gibborim.

The word gibbor in Tanach does not mean strong in the simple physical sense. A gibbor is a man of valor — a man whose strength has been forged through the experience of a particular kind of trial. Avraham was called a gibbor in the midrash not because he was physically powerful but because he had faced the test of the Akedah. Shimshon was called a gibbor not only for his physical strength but for the sacrifice of his eyes at the end. The gibborim of David were called gibborim not because they had been born strong, but because they had been born broken and had been made strong — through the specific alchemy of David's leadership, the cave at Adullam, the shared grief, and the long months of training that turned distressed debtors into professional soldiers.

The Maharal, in his writings on the nature of the hero in Tanach, reads the story of the cave as the paradigm of all such transformations. A gibbor, he writes, is not a man who has never been broken. A gibbor is a man who has been broken and has refused to stay broken. The broken place becomes, over time, the place of strongest reformed bone. The grief becomes, over time, the motivation for the training. The humiliation becomes, over time, the reason not to stop until every enemy of Israel has been defeated. Every gibbor in David's army carried a broken place in his chest that he could not remove. And every gibbor had learned, under David's teaching, that the broken place was the source of the strength, not its opposite. It was the thing that made him a warrior. Without it, he would have been nothing more than a comfortable villager. With it, he was one of the Thirty.

* * *

The book of Shmuel Bet lists the names of the Thirty at the very end, in the last chapter before the census and the plague and the threshing floor of Aravnah. The list is in two parts: the Three, who were the elite within the elite, and the Thirty, who were the broader corps of professional warriors. Shmuel Bet 23:8 opens with the Three: Yoshev-baShevet the Tahkemoni, the captain of the Three; he lifted up his spear against eight hundred men whom he killed at one time. Then Eleazar ben Dodo: He arose, and smote the Pelishtim until his hand was weary, and his hand clave to the sword. Then Shammah ben Agei the Harari: He stood in the midst of the field, and defended it, and slew the Pelishtim. Then the famous detail about the Three descending to the cave of Adullam at harvest time to bring David water from the well of Beit Lechem.

Then the other elite warriors — Avishai, Benayahu. Then the Thirty, each with his hometown and his lineage. Asa'el ben Tzeruyah. Elchanan ben Dodo of Beit Lechem (possibly the same as Eleazar, or possibly a different man). Shammah of Charod. Elika of Charod. Chelets the Palti. Ira ben Ikesh of Tekoa. Aviezer of Anatot. Mebunai the Chushati. Tzalmon the Achochi. Maharai the Netofati. Cheleb ben Ba'anah the Netofati. Itai ben Rivai of Givat of Binyamin. Benayahu the Piratoni. Hidai from the brooks of Ga'ash. Avi-alvon the Arvati. Azmavet the Bachurimi. Elyachba the Sha'alvoni. The sons of Yashen. Yonatan ben Shammah the Harari. Achiam ben Sharar the Harari. Elifelet ben Achasbai the son of a Ma'akhati. Eliam ben Achitofel the Giloni. Chetzrai the Karmeli. Pa'arai the Arbi. Yigal ben Natan of Tzovah. Bani the Gadi. Tzelek the Amoni. Nahari the Be'eroti, armor bearer of Yoav ben Tzeruyah. Ira the Yitri. Gareb the Yitri. And the thirty-seventh name, the last on the list, the one whose name closes the roster of David's mighty men as a kind of terrible punctuation mark on the whole enterprise:

Uriah the Hittite.

Kol sheloshim ve-shivah. "Thirty-seven in all."

We will come back to Uriah in the last chapter of this volume. For now, the point is the list itself, and the fact that the list begins with men who walked into a cave at Adullam one evening in the middle of David's worst year and asked whether a broken man in a dark chamber might take them in.

He took them in. He trained them. He made them into the army that would conquer the Yevusim and take Yerushalayim and defeat the Pelishtim in six major wars and carry the Aron through the gates of the city on the day David danced. The Thirty were not a military unit that David assembled after he became king. The Thirty were the cave. The Thirty were the metzokim, the noshim, the marei nefesh — the ones who had come to him when he was nothing, and whose loyalty had made possible everything he later became.

Now we turn to the specific deeds of the elite among them. To the Three, whose individual battles stand out even among the stories of the Thirty. And then to Benayahu, the crown jewel, whose exploits on the snowy day and in the pit with the lion and in the contest with the Egyptian giant became the subject of some of the most beautiful aggadic readings in the whole Talmud.
