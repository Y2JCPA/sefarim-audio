Chapter Two

The Three

Out of the cave came an army.

Out of the army came the Thirty.

And out of the Thirty came the Three.

The Tanach is very precise about the hierarchy. It uses three distinct terms for the levels of David's elite corps. At the broadest level, there is the general body of warriors — the thousands of men who fought in David's army during the early wilderness years and later during the campaigns of the kingdom. Within that body, there is a named elite: ha-gibborim, the Mighty Men, numbered at thirty-seven in the closing list of Shmuel Bet 23 but usually referred to as ha-Shloshim, the Thirty. And within the Thirty, there is an even more exclusive group: ha-Shelosha, the Three — the three top warriors whose individual battles were so extraordinary that the Tanach preserves each of their signature deeds with a short narrative, like a ballad compressed into a single verse.

The Three were Yoshev-baShevet the Tahkemoni, Elazar ben Dodo the Achochi, and Shammah ben Agei the Harari.

Their names are not famous in the way the names of Moshe and Aharon and Yehoshua are famous. They are not heroes whose stories are taught to children. They are preserved in a dense military register at the back of a book that most people skim on the way to the prophets. But each of them did a thing, on a particular day in a particular place, that the Tanach thought was worth preserving — worth preserving even if it meant interrupting the great theological arc of David's kingdom with a short brutal narrative about one man holding a spear against eight hundred enemies.

This chapter is about those three days.

* * *

## Yoshev-baShevet

The first of the Three was Yoshev ba-Shevet ha-Tachkemoni, rosh ha-shalishi. "Yoshev-baShevet the Tachkemoni, head of the captains."

The name is preserved in a form that the mefarshim agree is textually difficult. The parallel list in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 11:11 gives the name as Yashovam ben Chachmoni. The two forms are almost certainly the same name in slightly different spellings — the Masoretic text of Shmuel has suffered some ancient corruption, and the Divrei HaYamim version is probably closer to the original. The Gemara in Moed Katan 16b goes further and reads the name symbolically: Yoshev ba-shevet can be parsed as "the one who sits in the seat" — meaning the seat of Torah study, the seat of the sage. Rabbi Yochanan in Moed Katan reads the name as meaning that Yoshev-baShevet was the head of the Sanhedrin as well as the head of the military — a man who sat in the seat of judgment when the army was not fighting, and who led the army when the army was fighting.

This is the first of many signals the Tanach sends about the Thirty. They were not merely professional soldiers. They were scholar-warriors. Several of them, according to the aggadic tradition, sat in the Sanhedrin. Several of them taught Torah when they were not fighting. The separation between the life of the sword and the life of the study house, which would feel natural to a later reader, did not exist in David's court. The gibbor was expected to know both how to use a sword and how to expound a verse. Yoshev-baShevet was the captain of the Three precisely because he was the model of both at once.

His signature deed is described in a single, devastating line.

Hu orer et chanito al shemoneh me'ot challal be-fa'am echat.

"He lifted up his spear against eight hundred slain at one time."

The verse is dense and the mefarshim have worked hard to unpack it. Eight hundred killed in one time — be-fa'am echat — suggests a single engagement, not a lifetime total. The Tanach is claiming that in one particular battle, Yoshev-baShevet personally killed eight hundred enemies with his spear. The Rashi on the verse takes this literally. The Ralbag takes it literally as well but adds the note that Yoshev-baShevet was empowered in that moment by a special measure of divine assistance — no ordinary human could kill eight hundred men in a single battle by sheer skill alone. The Metzudat David offers a slightly softer reading: Yoshev-baShevet did not physically kill each of the eight hundred with his own hand, but he broke the formation of the enemy so decisively that eight hundred of them fell in the ensuing rout.

The Ben Ish Chai (Rabbi Yosef Chayim of Baghdad, the nineteenth-century sage whose own titles were drawn from the Benayahu sugya we will come to in a later chapter) reads the verse as pointing to the spiritual dimension of the deed. Yoshev-baShevet's eight hundred is meant to be read as the numerical inverse of Elazar ben Dodo's hand "cleaving" to the sword. Yoshev-baShevet defeated many through the power of motion. Elazar defeated the enemy through the power of stillness. Shammah defeated the enemy through the power of holding ground. The Three represent three modes of military gevurah, each of which is necessary in the life of a nation and each of which expresses a different dimension of divine service. Yoshev-baShevet is the first because motion is the first stage — the decision to engage, the decision to attack, the decision to close the distance with the enemy and to strike.

Let us try to reconstruct the day.

The eight hundred had come up out of a wadi at dawn — a Pelishti raiding column that had crossed the border in the night and was moving fast through the hill country, probably toward one of the unfortified Israelite villages. They were heavily armed. They were experienced. They had done this kind of raid many times before.

Yoshev-baShevet was waiting at the head of a narrow place in the path with a small force of his own — perhaps two dozen men, no more — drawn from the scouts who patrolled this stretch of the border. His scouts had seen the raid coming the night before and had ridden ahead to set the ambush. The plan had been simple. Yoshev-baShevet would hold the choke point with his spear. The other two dozen men would harass the column from both sides as it tried to push through. The geometry of the path meant that only a few of the eight hundred could engage at any given moment — the Pelishtim could not bring their full strength to bear against a single defender at the bottleneck.

The only question was whether one defender could hold long enough.

His second-in-command had asked it directly the night before, when they were laying out the plan.

"You can hold for how long?"

"As long as I need to."

"Yashovam — be serious. Eight hundred men. One spear. How long?"

"As long as I need to. If I fall, you take my place. If you fall, the next man takes your place. The point is the choke point. The man at the choke point matters less than the choke point itself. Whoever is standing there is holding it."

"And if no one is standing there?"

"Then the eight hundred get through, and the village is gone, and we have failed."

"Yashovam — "

"I am not falling. Stop asking. I am the head of the captains for a reason. Get to your positions."

The second-in-command had walked away without answering.

The next morning Yoshev-baShevet had stood at the bottleneck with his spear in his hand from before sunrise. The first of the Pelishtim had reached him about an hour after dawn. The first Pelishti had died on his spear. The second had died on his spear. By mid-morning he had killed perhaps fifty of them. By midday he had killed perhaps two hundred. By the late afternoon, when the sun was finally beginning to slant down toward the western hills, the count had passed five hundred.

He never moved from the spot.

The Pelishti commanders, watching from the back of the column, eventually understood that they were not going to break through. They tried to send men around the choke point through the rocks above. The two dozen scouts harassed those flankers to death from the sides. They tried to overwhelm Yoshev-baShevet with sheer numbers. The bodies piled up in front of him to a height that became its own defensive wall — a wall of dead Pelishtim that the next wave of Pelishtim had to climb over to get to him.

By evening, the count was at eight hundred.

The remaining Pelishtim — perhaps a few dozen — turned and ran back the way they had come.

Yoshev-baShevet sat down on a stone at the bottleneck. He was bleeding from a dozen small wounds. His spear arm was so cramped that he could not open the fingers of his hand. His second-in-command came up from the side of the path and crouched beside him.

"Yashovam."

"I told you."

"Yashovam — eight hundred men."

"I told you I was not falling."

"How — how does a man do this?"

Yoshev-baShevet did not answer for a long moment. He was looking out at the sunset over the western hills, at the place where the light was turning the sky orange and gold. When he finally spoke, his voice was very quiet.

"A man does it because the alternative is that the village is gone. There is no other answer. You stand at the place where you have to stand, and you do not move, and you keep killing them until they stop coming. That is the whole answer."

"And the Sanhedrin?"

"What about the Sanhedrin?"

"You sit on the Sanhedrin. You are Yoshev ba-Shevet, the one who sits in the seat. How does the man who sits in the seat of judgment also stand at the choke point and kill eight hundred men in a single day?"

Yoshev-baShevet smiled, just slightly.

"They are the same answer. In both places, you sit at the point where the work is and you do not move. The Sanhedrin is a different kind of choke point. The principle is the same. Stand at the place. Do the work. Do not let anything past you that should not be allowed to pass."

His second-in-command did not answer. He helped Yoshev-baShevet stand up. The two of them walked back to the camp together as the last of the light faded over the hills of Yehuda.

That is the story of the eight hundred. The Tanach preserves it in eight Hebrew words. The mefarshim preserve the rest in their commentaries. And the principle — stand at the place and do not move — became the principle of the entire register of the Three.

* * *

The place was probably in the wilderness of Yehuda, during one of David's early campaigns against the Pelishtim. The specific location is not given, but the form of the Tanach's language — orer et chanito, "he lifted up his spear" — suggests a moment of decision in the open field rather than an ambush or a siege. Yoshev-baShevet was engaged in a pitched battle, with Israelite forces on one side and a large Pelishti force on the other.

The Tanach's number of eight hundred is probably approximate, but the point of the number is clear: Yoshev-baShevet was outnumbered in a way that no ordinary soldier could survive. Eight hundred men against one man is not a fight. It is a slaughter. The only way the one man survives is if he enters a state of divinely assisted battle fury — what the prophets later called the ruach Hashem that rested on Shimshon and on other warriors of Israel in their moments of transcendent combat.

The man who was there that day was Nahari the Be'eroti, who the Tanach lists at number 34 in the roster of the Thirty as the armor bearer of Yoav ben Tzeruyah. Yoav's armor bearer was a young man in those early years, perhaps twenty-one or twenty-two. He had been present in several of the early campaigns. And he had watched, from the back ranks of the Israelite force, when Yoshev-baShevet had done the thing that would later be summarized in the single verse.

Nahari described the day many years later to a scribe who was collecting stories for the archives of David's court. His account, though not preserved in the Tanach itself, is the kind of account the mefarshim have in mind when they try to flesh out the narrative behind the compression of verse 8. Nahari spoke something like this:

"We were outside a village in the lowlands. The Pelishtim had come up with a force of perhaps two thousand men. We had maybe six hundred. Yoav was the field commander that day. David was not with us — he was in another part of the country on another mission. Yoav ordered the line to form up and we formed up, but the Pelishti line was too wide for ours. Their flanks extended past ours on both sides. Yoav saw that we were going to be enveloped within the first quarter of an hour of fighting and that our flanks would collapse and the center would be cut off and all of us would die on that piece of ground.

"Yoav called for Yoshev-baShevet.

"Yoshev was standing about twenty paces behind Yoav, watching the line. He came forward.

"Yoav said to him: We are going to be enveloped. Their flanks are too wide. We need to break their center before their flanks close on us. Can you do it?

"Yoshev looked at the Pelishti center. There were perhaps eight hundred men in the central formation — the densest knot of the enemy force, the command tent at the back, the senior officers in the middle, the elite Pelishti infantry in a compact square in front. Eight hundred men behind a wall of shields.

"Yoshev said: I can do it. Give me the command.

"Yoav said: Go.

"Yoshev turned around and walked back to the Israelite line. He took his spear — a long Israelite war-spear, perhaps seven feet of sharpened iron on an ash-wood shaft — from the ground where he had set it. He raised the spear above his head. He shouted to the men in the front rank of the Israelite center: Follow me. We are going to break them.

"And then he ran.

"I mean he ran, the way a man runs who is not afraid to die. He ran straight at the Pelishti center at full speed. The men of the Israelite front rank hesitated for a second — any sane soldier would hesitate — and then they ran after him. All of us ran after him. Six hundred Israelites, shouting, running at full speed toward the center of an enemy force of two thousand.

"Yoshev reached the Pelishti line first. He hit the shield wall at a dead run with the point of his spear leading the way, and the spear went through the first Pelishti shield and through the chest of the man holding it and out the back, and Yoshev kept running — kept running — tearing the shield off the shaft of the spear with his momentum and bringing the point of the spear around to hit the second Pelishti soldier in the throat before the second man could even react to the death of the first.

"The second man went down.

"By that time the rest of us were arriving at the shield wall behind him. The Pelishti line broke in a dozen places at once. Yoshev was already four bodies deep into the formation, and his spear was moving in ways I had never seen a spear move — short stabs, long stabs, sideways swipes that broke bone through armor, upward thrusts that caught men under the chin of their helmets.

"I was fighting my own battle by then, on the outside of the penetration — I was Yoav's armor bearer and I was at Yoav's side and Yoav was coming behind Yoshev with his own sword out. But I could see Yoshev ahead of us. He was going deeper into the formation. Other men were going down on both sides of him, and he was not even slowing. The Pelishti officers in the center of the formation tried to form up a new line, and Yoshev broke through the new line before it could set. They tried again. He broke through again. They tried a third time. He broke through a third time. By the time he reached the command tent at the back of the Pelishti center, the eight hundred men of the central formation were broken — scattered, trampled, falling back in every direction. The rout had begun. The Pelishti flanks that had been about to envelop our line now saw their own center collapsing behind them, and they began to pull back to avoid being cut off from their line of retreat. Within half an hour, the whole Pelishti army was running.

"We spent the rest of the afternoon counting the dead. The field was thick with bodies. The tally for the Pelishti dead, when we finally got a count, was about a thousand. Of the thousand, the scribes who did the count estimated that eight hundred had fallen in the central formation that Yoshev had broken. The remaining two hundred had fallen in the chaos of the flanks as the Pelishti line collapsed.

"The scribes credited Yoshev-baShevet with the eight hundred. They recorded it as eight hundred slain at one time — meaning eight hundred killed in a single battle by a single warrior operating in the direct flow of his own spear-work. It is almost certainly an exaggeration to say that Yoshev personally killed eight hundred men with his own hand. But it is not an exaggeration to say that Yoshev broke a formation of eight hundred men by being the first man to hit it and by being unstoppable once he was inside it. That is what the scribes meant.

"Yoav himself said, afterward: I have never seen one man change the outcome of a battle like that. If Yoshev had not gone into the center, all of us would have died on that field. The king's army would have been destroyed. The anointed one would have lost his best fighting men in a single afternoon. Yoshev saved everything.

"David heard the report that night. He came to the field the next day. He walked through the dead. He looked at the bodies Yoshev had cut down. He stood in the center of the Pelishti formation where the command tent had been. He called for Yoshev.

"Yoshev came forward, covered in dried blood from the previous day, his spear still in his hand, his eyes hollow from the lack of sleep.

"David said to him: You are the head of the Three.

"Yoshev said: There are not yet three, my lord.

"David said: There will be. And you will be the head. Take this title now. It is yours from this day forward.

"Yoshev bowed.

"He was the head of the Three from that afternoon until the end of his life."

* * *

## Elazar

The second of the Three was Elazar ben Dodo ha-Achochi.

His battle took place at a specific location the Tanach names: Pas-Dammim. The name is sometimes rendered as Ephes-Dammim, and it refers to a valley in the lowlands of Yehuda, near the place where David had faced Golyat many years earlier in his youth. The valley was a borderland between the hill country of Yehuda and the Pelishti coastal plain, and it was the site of repeated Pelishti-Israelite clashes across several generations. By the time of Elazar's battle, it was a well-known Pelishti staging ground.

The Tanach's description of Elazar's day is worth quoting in full:

"And after him was Elazar ben Dodo the Achochi, one of the three mighty men with David, when they defied the Pelishtim who were there gathered together to battle, and the men of Israel were gone away. He arose and smote the Pelishtim until his hand was weary, and his hand clave to the sword. And Hashem wrought a great victory that day; and the people returned after him only to strip the slain."

Read that slowly.

And the men of Israel were gone away. The Israelite army had fled. The entire Israelite force in that engagement had retreated from the field. Elazar was alone. Not alone in the sense of being the last man standing at the end of a long battle — alone in the sense of being the only Israelite soldier who refused to retreat when the order was given. The rest of the army had pulled back, and Elazar had stayed behind.

Why?

The mefarshim offer different explanations. The Radak reads the verse as saying that Elazar did not choose to stay behind — he was fighting so intensely in the front rank that he did not hear the retreat order, and by the time he realized that the rest of the army had pulled back, he was already cut off and surrounded and his only options were to fight or to die on the spot. The Ralbag takes a different view: Elazar heard the retreat order perfectly well and rejected it. He had decided that the retreat was wrong — that abandoning the field would give the Pelishtim a moral victory that would embolden them for future campaigns — and that even if the rest of the army was unwilling to stand, he personally was going to stand. He stood because he refused to give the Pelishtim the pleasure of seeing an Israelite warrior turn his back.

The Ben Ish Chai reads the scene as a spiritual meditation on the nature of loyalty. The army of Israel, he writes, is subject to the same temptation every army is subject to: the temptation to retreat when the battle is too hard. The Torah permits retreat in many circumstances — the halacha of war in Devarim 20 explicitly allows certain categories of soldiers to return home from the battlefield, and even the rest of the army is not expected to fight to the death in every engagement. But there is a kind of warrior for whom retreat is not an option, not because he refuses to obey orders, but because his entire being has become so fused with the cause that he cannot imagine leaving it. Elazar was that kind of warrior. His refusal to retreat was not disobedience; it was an expression of the unity between his soul and the purpose for which he had been trained. He had come out of the cave at Adullam four years earlier with nothing but his own grief and his own determination to be made new. He had been made new. And the new man that Elazar had become could no more retreat from a Pelishti line than a man could retreat from his own heart.

* * *

Let us try to reconstruct that day as well.

Pas-Dammim was a shallow valley — not a steep-sided ravine but a broad, sloping basin of earth with a small stream running through the middle of it. There was a field — the Tanach uses the word chelkat ha-sadeh — of particular agricultural importance. The field was full of ripening crops. The exact crop is disputed among the sources; the parallel account in Divrei HaYamim Aleph 11:13 identifies it as a field of se'orim, barley, while the Shmuel Bet text leaves the crop unspecified in Elazar's verse but identifies it as adashim, lentils, in the next verse about Shammah. Some mefarshim read the two verses as referring to two different fields on the same day. Others read them as referring to the same field, with the crop preserved differently in the two manuscripts.

Whatever the crop, the field was important. A Pelishti raid on a ripening field at harvest time would not only steal food from the Israelite population — it would burn or trample the crop, leaving the families of the region without their winter stores. A successful Pelishti raid on the field of Pas-Dammim in harvest season was the kind of disaster that would kill hundreds of Israelite villagers in the hungry months that followed. The Israelites who were defending the field were not only defending a piece of ground. They were defending the food supply of an entire region.

The battle began in the morning. The Pelishti force was larger than the Israelite force — perhaps twice the size, perhaps three times. The Israelite line held for the first few engagements. Then, for reasons the Tanach does not specify, the line broke. Va-ya'alu ish Yisrael, the verse says in the Tanach — "the men of Israel went up," which the mefarshim read as "the men of Israel withdrew from the field." They pulled back up the slope of the basin, toward the higher ground, toward the safety of the hills behind them.

Elazar did not pull back.

He was in the front rank. He was facing the Pelishti line at a distance of about ten paces. He heard the retreat order. He turned his head to look at the Israelite line behind him and saw the men of his own squad already moving backward up the slope, their shields angled behind them in the defensive posture of a retreating force. He turned his head forward again. He looked at the Pelishti line. He tightened his grip on the hilt of his sword. He took a single breath. He stepped forward.

One of his squad-mates later gave an account of the moment. His name was Yonatan ben Shammah the Harari — the same man whose father Shammah would become the third of the Three, whose own name is at #25 in the list of the Thirty. Yonatan was fighting near Elazar in the front rank that day. He had been pulling back with the retreat when he saw Elazar step forward instead of back.

"Elazar! Come back! The order is retreat!"

Elazar did not turn around.

"Elazar! Did you hear me? Retreat!"

"I heard you."

"Then come back."

"I am not retreating."

"Elazar, you are going to die. Alone. In front of their whole line. Come back."

"I am not retreating, Yonatan. Go."

Yonatan hesitated. He was a trained soldier; his instinct was to obey the retreat order. But he was also Elazar's squadmate, and the thought of leaving his friend alone in the front of a Pelishti charge was almost impossible to bear. He took a step back toward Elazar.

"If you are not going to retreat, I will stay with you."

"No. You go. Pull back with the others. I will do this alone."

"Elazar, I cannot leave you here."

"Yonatan. Listen to me. I am not going to die today. I know this in my bones. Hashem is with me in this moment the way He was with me at the cave at Adullam when I had nothing left. I am going to stand in this spot and the Pelishtim are going to come at me and I am going to kill them, one after another, until the line breaks or until the rest of our army comes back. But I need you to go. Because if you stay with me, and we both die, then two of David's men are dead in this field instead of zero. And if you go and I live, then our army will still have you. Go. Tell the commander I am still fighting. Tell him to regroup and come back. But go."

Yonatan stared at him.

"Go, Yonatan."

Yonatan went.

He ran up the slope after the rest of the Israelite line. When he reached the top of the slope, he turned around and looked back. Elazar was alone at the bottom of the basin, facing the Pelishti line by himself. The Pelishtim had paused for a moment, uncertain what to make of the single Israelite soldier who had not retreated. Then they began to advance on him.

* * *

What followed was recorded by the scribes of David's court in the form of a short ballad that did not survive in the main text of the Tanach but that is summarized in the single phrase va-tidbak yado el ha-cherev: "and his hand clave to the sword."

The ballad, as it is preserved in later midrashic fragments, described Elazar's fight in stages.

The first stage was the initial clash. The Pelishti line advanced on him in a loose wedge, expecting to overwhelm a single man within a few seconds. Elazar took the first three attackers with a single combination — a low sweep that took the lead man's knees, an upward thrust that caught the second man under the chin, and a backhand return swing that opened the third man's throat. Three bodies on the ground in the first ten seconds of the engagement. The men behind the first three hesitated.

The second stage was the disciplined defense. Elazar fell back into a fighting stance — one foot forward, one foot back, sword up in a guard position — and began to work methodically through the Pelishtim who came at him one and two at a time. The Pelishti line could not come at him as a unit because the ground in front of him was now littered with the bodies of the first three and the bodies were a physical obstacle to the advance of a formed line. So the Pelishtim came at him individually, or in pairs, stepping over the bodies of their fallen. Elazar killed each one. Some with a direct thrust. Some with a slash. Some with a shield-punch followed by a blade. He was working economically now, not wasting any motion, conserving his strength.

The third stage was the wall of the dead. After perhaps twenty minutes of fighting — twenty minutes is a long, long time in direct combat — the bodies of the Pelishti dead had piled up around Elazar's feet to the point where they formed a kind of fortification. He was now standing in the middle of a low wall of corpses, about knee-high in places and waist-high in others. The Pelishtim who wanted to get at him had to climb over the wall of their own dead, and the act of climbing made them vulnerable to the next stroke of Elazar's sword. The bodies became his defense. He was being protected, in a literal physical sense, by the men he had already killed.

The fourth stage was the cramp. Elazar's sword-hand had been gripping the hilt with maximum tension for the whole fight. His fingers had been locked around the leather-wrapped grip for more than half an hour at this point. His forearm muscles had been contracting continuously. By the end of the fourth stage, his hand was no longer capable of opening voluntarily. His fingers had cramped into a fixed position around the hilt. The sword was still moving — he could still swing his arm, still thrust, still block — but he could not have released the sword even if he had wanted to. His hand had become one physical unit with the weapon. This is the moment the Tanach captures in the phrase va-tidbak yado el ha-cherev: his hand clave to the sword. Dvekut, the same Hebrew root that is used for spiritual clinging to Hashem. Elazar's hand had become spiritually and physically fused with the instrument of his service. The blade and the hand were one thing now.

The fifth stage was the quiet. By the time the sun was directly overhead, the attack had slowed. The Pelishti line had exhausted its willingness to send individual fighters against the terrifying Israelite who stood in the wall of dead. A standoff had developed. Elazar was in the center, panting, his hand welded to the sword, his clothes soaked with his own sweat and the blood of the men he had killed. The Pelishtim were in a loose semicircle around him, perhaps forty or fifty of them left, none willing to be the next to try climbing the wall. Behind the Pelishti line, the command officers were shouting orders that the fighters were ignoring. The fighters were looking at Elazar and at the wall of dead and thinking about their wives and children and deciding that this particular Israelite was not the man any of them wanted to be the one to challenge.

Then the men of Israel came back.

* * *

Yonatan had run all the way up to the top of the slope and kept running until he reached the Israelite commander at the regrouping point. He had delivered his message: Elazar is still fighting alone at the bottom of the basin. He will not retreat. Regroup and come back.

The commander had stared at him.

"He is still fighting?"

"He is still fighting. I saw him from the top of the slope before I turned. He was in a stance. The Pelishti line was advancing on him. He had not moved from the spot."

"One man against their whole line?"

"One man against their whole line."

The commander was quiet for a long moment. Then he gave the order that Elazar had, by the force of his own refusal, compelled him to give.

"All units. Reform the line. We are going back."

The Israelite army regrouped on the ridge above the basin. The men formed up in their original positions — the same front rank that had broken an hour earlier, now re-forming with something in their faces that had not been there before. They had been given a second chance. They had been given the opportunity to not-be the men who had abandoned a comrade to die alone. The shame of the first retreat had become, in the intervening hour, the motivation for the second advance.

They came down the slope in formation.

The Pelishtim saw them coming. The Pelishti commanders on the far side of the basin realized that the tactical situation had just reversed — they had been the winners when the Israelite line had retreated, and now they were the losers because the Israelite line had reformed and was returning with the lone warrior from the first fight still alive and fighting in the center of their own formation. The Pelishtim broke.

They did not break in an orderly retreat. They broke in a rout. They turned and ran — away from Elazar, away from the returning Israelite line, away from the field of Pas-Dammim — and they did not stop running until they had reached the safety of their own coastal territory. The rout was so complete that the Tanach says, with a kind of dry irony, that the men of Israel returned after Elazar only to strip the slain. The rest of the army came back not to fight the Pelishtim but to gather the spoil from the dead Pelishtim that Elazar had already killed. The battle had been decided before the Israelite army even arrived on the second approach. It had been decided by one man standing in the center of a wall of dead men with his hand welded to his sword.

Elazar himself had to be pulled out of the fighting stance. When the first Israelite reached him, Elazar was still holding the sword upright in the defensive position, his eyes glazed, his breath coming in short hard gasps. The Israelite — a young soldier who had been in the second rank of the original line — approached carefully, reading Elazar's posture and being careful not to seem like an enemy in case Elazar was too far into the battle rhythm to distinguish friends from enemies.

"Elazar. It is me. Yoram. The Pelishtim are gone. The battle is over."

Elazar did not move.

"Elazar. It is me."

Slowly, Elazar's focus returned. He looked at Yoram. He nodded. He tried to lower his sword. The sword did not move. He tried again. His hand was still locked around the hilt.

"My hand."

"What is wrong with your hand?"

"It will not — the fingers will not open."

Yoram moved closer. He took Elazar's wrist and looked at the hand. The fingers were white where they gripped the hilt. The skin was mottled. The muscles of the forearm were rigid. It was clear, even to a young soldier, that the hand had been locked in that position for too long and that it was not going to release voluntarily.

"We will need warm water. And oil. And time. Come. Let me help you back up the slope."

Yoram put Elazar's free arm over his own shoulder and began to walk him up the slope toward the Israelite lines. Elazar walked slowly. The sword was still in his hand, still gripped in the dead position, the point dragging behind him in the dirt as he moved. Yoram did not try to take the sword. He understood that the hand and the sword had become, for the moment, a single thing that could not be separated without injury.

It took the better part of an hour, after they reached the Israelite camp, to pry Elazar's fingers off the hilt. They used warm water and olive oil. They massaged the forearm muscles. They stretched the fingers one at a time. Elazar gritted his teeth through the process. Eventually the fingers released. The sword fell out of his hand onto the ground. Elazar looked at his own hand for a long time.

"I could not let go of it."

"I know."

"I did not know I could not let go of it until the fight was over. While I was fighting, I thought my hand was holding the sword. Afterward I understood that the sword was holding my hand."

Yoram looked at him.

"Elazar. When David hears about this, he is going to put you in the Three."

Elazar nodded slowly.

"Maybe. If Hashem wills it."

"There is no maybe, Elazar. One man stopping a Pelishti rout? Hashem has already decided. David is just going to announce it."

* * *

## Shammah

The third of the Three was Shammah ben Agei the Harari. His deed is the one that the Tanach preserves with the clearest spatial detail.

Va-yityatzev be-toch ha-chelkah va-yatzileha va-yakh et ha-Pelishtim, va-ya'as Hashem teshu'ah gedolah.

"And he stood in the middle of the field, and delivered it, and smote the Pelishtim, and Hashem wrought a great deliverance."

The field was a chelkat ha-sadeh — an agricultural plot — full of adashim, lentils. The Pelishtim had gathered to raid the field at harvest time. The Israelite militia that was supposed to defend the field had run away. Shammah did not run. He stepped into the middle of the field and stood there. When the Pelishtim came at him, he fought them. When they tried to flank him, he moved with them. When they tried to encircle him, he broke the encirclement. By the end of the day the Pelishtim were retreating and the field was still standing and Shammah was still in the middle of it.

The Divrei HaYamim version of the story — Divrei HaYamim Aleph 11:13 — identifies the crop as barley rather than lentils and places the incident at Pas-Dammim. The two versions are close enough to be two tellings of the same story, or close enough to be two different stories on consecutive days. Both versions end the same way: Shammah alone in the middle of a field, refusing to give up the harvest.

The theological weight of the lentil-or-barley detail is important. A field of lentils is not glamorous. A field of lentils is a peasant's field — a small plot, a modest harvest, a few sacks of beans that will feed a village through the winter. It is not a royal treasure. It is not a city. It is not even a strategic military objective. It is the kind of thing a professional soldier would normally consider beneath his attention.

But Shammah stood in it.

He stood in it because the lentils belonged to the people of Israel. The lentils were the winter food supply of some small village in the lowlands. The villagers who owned the field were not kings or princes or soldiers. They were farmers. They were the kind of people who had once come to David at the cave of Adullam because they had nowhere else to go. And Shammah — who had himself come to David at Adullam after losing his own land in a Pelishti raid years earlier — understood, in his body, what it meant for a farmer to lose his field to an enemy raid. He understood what the winter would be like for the family that owned the field if the lentils were taken. He understood what it meant to be a peasant whose only hope of survival was a few sacks of dried beans stored in the corner of a small house in a small village.

He stood in the field because he could not bear to let another family become what he had once been.

* * *

The battle was shorter than Elazar's. Shammah was fighting a smaller Pelishti raiding party, perhaps seventy or eighty men, not a full army. But the intensity was the same. He stood in the middle of the plowed earth, surrounded by rows of ripening lentil plants, with his sword in his hand and his shield on his arm, and he let the Pelishtim come to him.

The first wave came at him from the west. He killed the first three men who reached him with a single sweeping movement — low, then up, then across. The fourth man he took with a direct thrust. The fifth he took with a shield-punch followed by a throat-cut. The sixth he took with a back-swing. By the time the first wave had thinned out, there were perhaps fifteen Pelishtim dead in the rows of lentils at his feet.

The second wave came at him from the east. He turned to face them. He fought them the same way. He did not move from his central spot — he pivoted on his own heel, turning to face each direction as the attacks came. The lentil plants around him were being trampled by the dead and dying, but he himself did not move.

One of the Pelishti officers, watching the scene from a safe distance, shouted to his men.

"He is only one man! Go around him!"

Several Pelishtim tried to go around him. Shammah broke out of his stance and intercepted them, killing two before they could reach the edge of the field. Then he returned to his central spot. The Pelishti officer shouted again.

"Surround him! All at once!"

The Pelishtim tried to surround him. Shammah turned in a slow, precise circle, engaging each of the men who approached from each direction. He used the lentil plants themselves as a minor tactical advantage — the thick rows made it harder for the Pelishtim to rush him in a coordinated manner, because the plants slowed their footing and broke their formation. Shammah had chosen his spot deliberately. He had chosen a spot in the densest part of the lentil field, where the plants were thickest, because he understood that the plants would help him fight alone against many.

By the time the third wave came, the Pelishti officer had decided the raid was not worth the cost. He called his men back. The remaining Pelishtim retreated from the field. Shammah watched them go, still standing in his central spot, breathing hard, his sword still at the ready.

When the Pelishtim were out of sight, Shammah finally lowered his sword.

He walked slowly out of the field. His feet were muddy with the earth of the lentil rows. His clothes were streaked with blood and dust. The lentil plants around him had been trampled and smashed in the fighting — the field would yield less of a harvest than it had promised, but it would yield some harvest, and the family that owned it would have food for the winter, and the next generation would grow up in a house that had not been emptied by a Pelishti raid.

The farmer who owned the field came out from his house at the edge of the field. He had been hiding inside with his wife and children during the fighting. He approached Shammah carefully.

"My lord."

"I am not a lord. I am a soldier. Are you the owner of this field?"

"I am."

"Your field is still yours. You will have a smaller harvest than you expected — some of the rows were trampled — but most of the field is still standing. You will have food for the winter. Your family will live."

The farmer stared at him.

"My lord — "

"Do not call me that. Who are you?"

"My name is Yehonatan. I am from this village. My wife and my three children live in that house over there. We thought we were going to lose everything today. The militia came through here yesterday and said the Pelishtim were coming and that they would not be able to hold the field. We were preparing to flee. Then we saw you come into the field alone, and we hid in the house."

"How old are your children?"

"Seven, five, and two."

"What are their names?"

"Miryam, Aharon, and Yosef."

"Yehonatan. Listen to me. I am Shammah ben Agei the Harari, a soldier of David ben Yishai. I came out of the cave at Adullam with David four years ago. I lost my own family's land in a Pelishti raid seven years ago. I know what it is like to lose everything to an enemy. I did not come to this field today because I was ordered to. I came because I could not bear the thought of another family losing what my family lost. Your children are going to have food this winter. Your lentils are going to be harvested. I am going to tell David what happened here, and David is going to send men to help you bring in the harvest and to repair what was damaged. You are not going to be alone. Do you understand?"

Yehonatan could not speak. He was crying.

Shammah put his hand on the farmer's shoulder for a moment. Then he turned and walked out of the field, back toward the Israelite lines, where his squad was waiting for him on the ridge above the basin.

* * *

David heard the report of Shammah's deed the same night he heard the report of Elazar's.

The two deeds had happened on the same day, in two separate engagements, in two separate fields, against two separate Pelishti forces. The coincidence was so striking that David's scribes recorded both stories in a single section of the court archive, and the Tanach preserves them in the same sequence — Elazar in verses 9–10, Shammah in verses 11–12. The two men became, in David's mind, the second and third members of the elite triumvirate that Yoshev-baShevet had already joined as captain.

David made the announcement a few days later. He gathered the Thirty — the full corps of his elite warriors — at a forward camp in the Shefelah. He stood at the front of the assembly with the three men facing him.

"Yoshev-baShevet ben Chachmon. Elazar ben Dodo ha-Achochi. Shammah ben Agei ha-Harari. Step forward."

The three men stepped forward.

"By the authority of the anointed of Hashem, I hereby name you the Three. You will be the elite within my elite. Your deeds — the eight hundred at one time, the hand cleaving to the sword, the middle of the lentil field — will be preserved in the court archive for all the generations that come after us. You will be remembered as long as there is an Israel to remember you. And in this life, for as long as you live, you will be the first to be called into any engagement that cannot be entrusted to ordinary soldiers. You will be the ones who do the impossible things. That is the blessing of being the Three. And it is also the burden."

The three men bowed.

Yoshev-baShevet spoke for them.

"My lord. We accept the burden."

David nodded.

"Go. The rest of the evening is yours. Tomorrow we march."

* * *

A week later, the three of them would come down to the cave of Adullam where David was hiding from a Pelishti campaign, and they would hear David sigh and say, Who will give me water to drink from the well of Beit Lechem at the gate? And the three of them would look at each other, and without a word they would stand up and leave the cave and walk out into the night toward the Pelishti-occupied town of Beit Lechem to answer the question David had asked.

That is the story of the next chapter.
