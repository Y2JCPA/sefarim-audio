Chapter Three: Dagon Falls The Pelishtim brought the Aron to Ashdod. They carried it in triumph — the war trophy of a nation that believed it had defeated not only an army but a God. This was how ancient warfare worked. You conquered a people, and you conquered their gods. You took the symbols of their divine power and placed them in your own temple, where your gods would stand over them in perpetual dominance. They brought the Aron into the temple of Dagon. Dagon was the grain god of the Pelishtim — a deity whose image was part man and part fish, according to the Gemara in Avodah Zarah, standing upright on its pedestal with the authority of a god in its own house. The Pelishtim set the Aron beside Dagon. Not above — beside. A captured trophy, humiliated, subordinate. The message was clear: our god is greater than yours. Your Aron is furniture in our temple now. They locked the doors. They went to sleep.

The next morning, the priests of Dagon opened the temple doors and walked inside. Dagon was on the floor. The statue — heavy stone or wood or whatever material it was fashioned from — had fallen forward onto its face, before the Aron. Not sideways. Not backward. Forward. In the posture of prostration. As though the fish-god of the Pelishtim had gotten down off his pedestal in the night and bowed to the God of Israel. The priests stared. They looked at each other. They looked at the idol on the floor and the Aron standing upright and undisturbed behind it. No one said anything about the obvious interpretation — not yet. They picked Dagon up. They set him back on his pedestal. They brushed off the dust and straightened the ornaments and restored the temple to its proper order. One night. A fluke. A tremor in the earth. The wind. They locked the doors and left.

The next morning, they opened the doors again.

Dagon was on the floor again. But this time, it was worse. The head of Dagon was severed. Both palms of his hands were cut off. They lay on the threshold of the temple — separated from the body, arranged with what almost seemed like deliberate precision on the doorsill, as though someone had placed them there to be seen. Only the torso remained on the floor — the dagon, the fish-body, the part that was not human, the part that could not think or grasp or decide. The Radak explains the symbolism: the head represents wisdom. The hands represent action. By severing the head and hands, the message was absolute — your god cannot think and your god cannot act. What remains is a fish. A dead thing. Incapable of anything. The Rashi adds that this is the origin of the custom mentioned in the text: "Therefore the priests of Dagon, and all who enter the house of Dagon, do not tread upon the threshold of Dagon in Ashdod, to this day." The threshold where the god's severed parts had lain became permanently sacred in the most ironic way — the spot where Dagon was humiliated became a site of reverence. The Pelishtim of Ashdod began to whisper. Something was wrong. Something was very wrong. And then the plague began. * * *
