Chapter Four: The Plague The hand of Hashem was heavy on them. The text uses that word again — kaved. Heavy. The same root as kavod — glory. The glory that had departed from Israel now manifested as weight upon the Pelishtim. A different kind of presence. Not the sheltering presence of the Shechinah in the Mishkan, but the crushing presence of a God demonstrating that He had not been captured — that He had, in fact, allowed Himself to be taken, and was now showing His captors exactly what they had brought into their house. The people of Ashdod were struck with tumors — the text says ofalim, swellings in the hidden parts of the body. The Radak and other commentators understand these as hemorrhoids or boils of an agonizing and humiliating nature. Additionally, their land was overrun with mice that devoured their crops and entered their stores and made life unbearable. "The Aron of the God of Israel cannot stay with us," the men of Ashdod said. "His hand is harsh upon us and upon Dagon our god." They sent the Aron to Gat. The plague followed. The men of Gat — tall, strong, the city that would later produce Goliath — were struck with the same tumors. The hand of Hashem was upon the city with a "very great panic." Men young and old were afflicted. The city convulsed with pain and fear. They sent the Aron to Ekron. This time, the people of Ekron saw it coming. "They have brought the Aron of the God of Israel to us," the Ekronites cried, "to kill us and our people!" The Aron had become the most terrifying object in the ancient world. Every city that hosted it was destroyed. Every people that touched it was punished. The Pelishtim — the conquering army, the iron-weaponed professionals who had shattered Israel at Even HaEzer — were now screaming in terror at the prize they had won. The Radak notes the extraordinary reversal. The Pelishtim had mocked the God of Israel. They had carried His Aron into Dagon's temple. They had treated it as a trophy. And now they were begging to get rid of it. The cry of Ekron rose to Heaven.

The seranim — the five lords — convened. Something had to be done. The Aron had been with them for seven months, and every day brought more suffering. The tumors spread. The mice multiplied. The crops rotted. The cities that had celebrated their victory over Israel were now desperate to undo it. They called for their priests and diviners. "What shall we do with the Aron of the God of Israel? Tell us — how do we send it back?" The diviners answered: "If you send back the Aron, do not send it empty. You must return it with a guilt offering. Then you will be healed, and you will know why His hand has not turned away from you." "What guilt offering?" "Five golden tumors and five golden mice — according to the number of the lords of the Pelishtim. For one plague was upon all of you and upon your lords. Make images of your tumors and images of the mice that are ravaging the land, and give glory to the God of Israel." Give glory — havu kavod. There it was again. The kavod that had departed from Israel when the Aron was taken was now being demanded back by the very people who had taken it. The circle was closing. And then the diviners said something remarkable. "Why do you harden your hearts as Mitzrayim and Pharaoh hardened their hearts? After He dealt harshly with them — did they not send the people away, and they departed?" The Pelishtim's own priests were citing the Exodus. They were warning their lords not to repeat Pharaoh's mistake — not to cling stubbornly to something that clearly belonged to the God of Israel. The echo was deliberate and devastating. The same God. The same pattern. The same outcome for those who resist. * * *
