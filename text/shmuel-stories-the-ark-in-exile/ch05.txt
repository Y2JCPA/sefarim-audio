Chapter Five: The Road Home The diviners devised a test. "Make a new cart," they said. "Take two milk cows that have never worn a yoke. Hitch them to the cart. Take their calves away from them and shut the calves at home. Place the Aron on the cart. Put the gold objects in a box beside it. And then — let the cows go." The test was elegant in its simplicity. Milk cows that have never been yoked do not pull carts. They have no training, no instinct for the road, no reason to walk in any particular direction. And milk cows whose calves have just been taken from them have one overwhelming drive — to turn around and go back to their young. Every instinct in the animal would pull them away from the road and back to the pen where their calves were crying. "If the cows go up the road to Beit Shemesh, toward the territory of Israel," the diviners said, "then we will know that it was His hand that struck us. But if they do not — then we will know it was not His hand. It was just chance." The lords agreed. They built the cart. They found the cows. They separated the calves. They placed the Aron on the cart with the golden tumors and the golden mice. They let the cows go.

The cows walked straight. They took the road to Beit Shemesh and did not turn to the right or to the left. They walked on the highway — vayisharnah — the word means they went straight, directly, without hesitation or deviation. Two untrained animals pulling a heavy cart up a road they had never traveled, walking away from their crying calves, heading toward Israelite territory as though an invisible hand were guiding them. The text adds a detail that makes the miracle vivid: "They went along, lowing as they went." They were crying. The cows were mooing — calling for their calves, their instincts screaming at them to turn around — but they kept walking. Their bodies were pulled forward by something stronger than instinct. They wept and they walked. The Rashi notes that the word vayisharnah also carries the connotation of song — they went along singing. The Gemara in Avodah Zarah says the cows were singing shirah — a song

of praise to Hashem. Animals, doing what the Pelishtim would not do voluntarily: giving glory to the God of Israel. The five lords of the Pelishtim followed the cart to the border. They watched it cross into Israelite territory. They stood at the edge of their own land and watched the Aron go home.

The cart came to Beit Shemesh. It was wheat harvest. The people of Beit Shemesh were in the fields, cutting grain, bent over their work in the summer heat. Someone looked up. Someone saw the cart on the road — the unfamiliar cart, pulled by unfamiliar cows, with something on it that caught the light. "Look!" They stopped cutting. They straightened up. They shielded their eyes against the sun and stared at the road. And they saw the Aron. The text says: "They lifted their eyes and saw the Aron, and they rejoiced to see it." Seven months. The Aron had been gone for seven months — taken in battle, humiliated in a pagan temple, carried from city to plague-ridden city. And now it was coming home. Rolling up the road on a cart pulled by crying cows, under the open sky, in the light of a summer afternoon. The cart came to the field of a man named Yehoshua of Beit Shemesh and stopped beside a large stone. The Levi'im lifted the Aron from the cart. They split the wood of the cart for fuel. They offered the cows as an olah — a burnt offering — to Hashem. It was done. The Aron was back. But the story did not end there. The men of Beit Shemesh looked at the Aron. They looked inside it. The text uses the word vayiru — they looked, they gazed, they peered — with the casual curiosity of men who had forgotten what the Aron was. They treated it the way the elders at Even HaEzer had treated it — as an object. A thing to be examined. A curiosity to be explored rather than a presence to be revered. Hashem struck them. The number recorded in the text — fifty thousand and seventy — has been debated extensively by commentators. The Radak and others note that such a large number from a single town seems impossible, and various readings have been proposed. But whatever the precise count, the message was unmistakable. The Aron was not a museum piece. It was not a trophy. It was the dwelling place of the God who had just humiliated Dagon and broken five

cities with plagues. The people of Beit Shemesh understood. Too late for the dead. But they understood. "Who is able to stand before Hashem, this holy God?" they cried. "And to whom shall He go up from us?" They sent messengers to Kiryat Yearim. "The Pelishtim have returned the Aron. Come down and take it up to you." The men of Kiryat Yearim came. They brought the Aron to the house of Avinadav on the hill. They consecrated his son Elazar to guard it. And there it stayed. For twenty years. * * *
