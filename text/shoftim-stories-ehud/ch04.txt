Chapter 4: The Tribute The morning of the tribute, Ehud rose before dawn and dressed carefully. The cloak was important. It had to be loose enough to conceal the bulge of the dagger against his right thigh but not so loose that it looked unusual. He adjusted it three times, turning before his mother's copper mirror, checking the drape from every angle. "You look fine," his mother said from the doorway. She was holding a clay cup of water and a piece of flatbread. "Eat something." "I'm not hungry." "Eat anyway. You have a long walk." He took the bread and broke it but couldn't bring himself to swallow more than a bite. His mother watched him with the careful attention of a woman who knows something is wrong but has decided not to ask. "Come home safe," she said. "I will." "Promise me." He looked at her. She was standing very straight, the way she always stood when she was trying not to cry. He crossed the room and kissed her forehead. "I promise." The delegation assembled at the edge of the village — a dozen men from Binyamin and Efraim, each carrying a portion of the annual tribute. Sacks of grain. Jars of oil. A small chest of silver. The tangible evidence of their subjugation, carried on their own backs to the palace of the man who had imposed it. They walked in silence for most of the journey. There was nothing to say. They had all made this walk before — some of them many times — and the humiliation didn't lessen with repetition. If anything, it deepened, like a wound that reopens every time it begins to heal. The road descended from the hill country into the Jordan Valley, and the air grew thick and hot. Date palms lined the approaches to Yericho, their fronds motionless in the windless morning. The

city appeared in the distance — low mud-brick buildings, the flat roof of Eglon's palace rising above them, the Moabite pennants hanging limp in the heat. The gates were open. Soldiers flanked the entrance. The delegation approached, and the lead man — an elder from Efraim named Tzuri — announced their purpose. "Tribute for King Eglon, from the people of Israel." The captain of the guard looked them over with undisguised boredom. "Weapons?" "We carry no weapons." "We'll see about that." The guards came forward and began the search. Hands moving over shoulders, down the torso, across the left hip, down the left thigh. One by one, the delegates were patted, cleared, and waved through. Ehud was fourth in line. The guard who searched him was young — younger than Ehud — with the soft hands of a man who had never farmed a day in his life. He ran his hands down Ehud's left side with practiced efficiency: shoulder, ribs, hip, thigh. He checked the small of the back. He even checked the front of the waist, where a small knife might be tucked into a belt. He did not check the right thigh. "Clear," the guard said, and waved him through. Ehud walked into the palace with his heart hammering so hard he was certain the man next to him could hear it. But his face showed nothing. His hands were steady. His stride was even. The dagger pressed against his right leg like a second pulse. The hall was large and cool, built of stone with high windows that caught whatever breeze moved through the valley. Eglon sat at the far end on a wooden throne reinforced with bronze fittings — reinforced, the delegates noted grimly, because an ordinary chair could not support his weight. Because Eglon was enormous. The Tanach records it without delicacy: v'Eglon ish bari me'od — Eglon was a very fat man. This was not merely a physical description. It was a portrait of a king grown massive on the wealth he had extracted from others, a man whose body had become the visible manifestation of his greed. He sat on his throne like a mountain of flesh, his small eyes surveying the Israelite delegation with the lazy satisfaction of a man who has been collecting tribute for eighteen years and expects to collect it for eighteen more.

"Approach," said a courtier. Tzuri stepped forward and bowed. The others followed. One by one, the gifts were laid before the throne — grain, oil, silver — each offering announced by the courtier in a formal voice that made the transaction feel like a ritual, which it was. A ritual of submission. A ritual that said: You are the master and we are the servants, and this is the price of our continued existence. Eglon accepted it all without comment. A nod here, a wave of the hand there. When the last offering was placed, he spoke for the first time. "You may go." The delegation bowed again and turned to leave. "Wait," Eglon said. The delegates froze. "The silver. Is this all of it?" Tzuri turned back, his jaw tight. "It is the full amount, my lord. As agreed." "Agreed." Eglon tasted the word like a man testing an unfamiliar food. "I don't recall agreeing. I recall telling." He leaned forward, and the throne groaned under the shift of his weight. "Next year, add ten percent. Your people seem to be recovering nicely from the famine. I'd hate for prosperity to make you... restless." "Yes, my lord." "And Tzuri?" "My lord?" "Smile when you bring it. I like to see gratitude." The delegates walked out of the hall in silence. No one spoke until they were past the gate and out of earshot of the guards. "Ten percent," one of the younger men muttered. "Where are we supposed to find ten percent more? My village can barely —" "Quiet," Tzuri said. "Not here." "Then where? When? We've been quiet for eighteen years and all it's gotten us is —" "I said quiet." Tzuri's voice was granite. "We'll talk when we're home. Not on this road. Not where they can hear." They walked in silence for most of the journey. There was nothing to say that hadn't been said a hundred times before, in a hundred darkened houses, in a hundred whispered conversations that all ended the same way — with clenched fists and no plan.

But one man in the delegation was not clenching his fists. One man was watching the road with the patient, measuring gaze of someone who was counting something — steps, perhaps, or seconds, or the distance between the palace gate and the first bend in the road where a man could no longer be seen from the walls. Ehud walked with the others, and said nothing, and noticed everything. The road descended from the hill country into the Jordan Valley, and the delegation made its way toward Yericho in the early morning heat.
