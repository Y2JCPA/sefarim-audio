Chapter 5: The Return At Pesilim, Ehud stopped. The landmark stood along the road like a boundary marker between two worlds — some said it was the carved stones Yehoshua had set up when Israel first crossed the Jordan; others said it was a cluster of Moabite idols, the foreign gods that had no business standing on Israelite soil. Either way, it was a recognizable point, and Ehud had chosen it as the place where one story would end and another would begin. "Tzuri," he said quietly. The old man from Efraim turned. "What is it?" "Take the men home." Tzuri's brow furrowed. "What do you mean?" "I need to go back." "Go back? Go back where?" "To the palace." The silence that followed lasted only a moment, but it was the kind of moment that contains an entire conversation. Tzuri looked at Ehud's face. He looked at the road behind them — the road that led back to Yericho, back to the palace, back to the throne of the most powerful man in the region. He looked at Ehud's right side, where the cloak hung slightly differently than a cloak should hang. He opened his mouth, then closed it. "I don't want to know," he said. "No." "Whatever you're about to do —" "Don't," Ehud said. "Don't say anything. Don't think about it. Take the men home. Walk normally. Don't rush, don't look back, don't change your route. Go home and wait." "Wait for what?"

"You'll know." Tzuri stared at him for a long moment. Then the old man did something that surprised them both. He reached out and gripped Ehud's arm — the left arm, the strong arm — and held it. "Hashem be with you," he said. "He already is," Ehud said. "That's the only reason I'm going back." Tzuri released his arm, turned to the waiting men, and led them north toward the hill country. Ehud watched until they disappeared around a bend. Then he was alone. The road back to Yericho was perhaps four miles. In the heat of the Jordan Valley, with the sun directly overhead and the air shimmering above the packed earth, four miles felt like forty. Every step carried him farther from safety and deeper into danger. Every step was a step he could not take back. He passed groves of date palms that gave spotty shade. He passed a dry wadi where a lizard watched him from a rock with unblinking eyes. He passed a Moabite patrol heading in the other direction — two soldiers on a routine circuit — and nodded to them without breaking stride, just another Israelite on the road, too insignificant to question. He arrived at the palace gate. "Back again?" the guard said. He recognized Ehud from the tribute delegation — the same young guard who had searched him less than two hours ago. "I forgot something," Ehud said. And then, lowering his voice: "I have a message for the king. A private message." The guard's expression shifted. Private messages from Israelite collaborators were not unusual. Eglon maintained a network of informants among the subject population — men who traded information for protection, who reported on their neighbors in exchange for lighter tribute. A man returning alone from a tribute delegation with a private message for the king was not a threat. He was an asset. "Wait here." The message was relayed. The word came back: the king would see him. Ehud walked back into the palace, up the stairs, and into the audience hall where courtiers and officials clustered around Eglon's throne like planets around a bloated sun. "I have a secret message for you, O king," Ehud said. Devar seter li eilecha, hamelech.

A hidden word. A concealed thing. The irony was exquisite — because Ehud did indeed have something hidden for the king. Just not the kind of secret Eglon was expecting. The king's eyes narrowed with interest. He raised one thick hand. "Has," he said. Silence. The courtiers filed out. The guards withdrew. The doors of the upper chamber closed. Two men remained. The king of Moav, seated on his throne. And the left-handed Binyaminite, standing before him with a dagger strapped to the wrong leg. Alone.
