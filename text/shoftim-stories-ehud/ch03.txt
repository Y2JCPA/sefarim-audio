Chapter 3: The Dagger He made it in secret. At night, in the back of his father's workshop, by the light of a single lamp whose wick he kept trimmed low so that the glow wouldn't be visible from the road. The blade was iron — good iron, traded from a Kenite smith who didn't ask questions. Ehud heated it, folded it, hammered it, heated it again. Night after night, while his family slept, the soft ring of metal on metal echoed in the dark workshop like a heartbeat. "What are you making?" Ehud's hand froze over the anvil. His younger brother Avinoam stood in the doorway, rubbing sleep from his eyes. "Go back to bed." "Is that a sword?" "It's a tool. Go back to bed, Avinoam." "It has two edges. Tools don't have two —" "Avinoam." Ehud set down the hammer and turned to face his brother. "Listen to me. You didn't see anything. You didn't come into this room. You went back to sleep and in the morning you won't remember any of this. Do you understand?" The boy looked at the blade — half-finished, glowing faintly in the lamplight, already sharp enough to draw blood — and then at his brother's face. Whatever he saw there made him take a step backward. "I understand," he said. He went back to bed. He didn't ask again. The next night, Ehud was back at the anvil. And the night after that. "How much longer?" Shimi asked. He had come to the workshop uninvited, ducking through the low doorway with the silence of a man who knew how to move without being heard. "Two more nights. Maybe three."

Shimi examined the blade in the lamplight. He ran a thumb along one edge — carefully — and drew it back with a thin line of red on the pad of his finger. "Sharp." "It needs to be sharper." "This would cut through leather armor." "It's not going through armor." Ehud took the blade back and held it against his forearm, measuring. "It's going through flesh. A lot of flesh." Shimi understood. He didn't say the name, but he understood. "The handle is wrong," he said after a moment. "What's wrong with it?" "It's too long. If the blade goes deep enough, the handle will stick out. Someone will see it." Ehud looked at the handle. Shimi was right. He took up the file and began working it down, shortening the grip, tapering the pommel until it was barely wider than the blade itself. "Better," Shimi said. "Now it can go all the way in." Neither man commented on what "all the way in" implied about the intended target. Some things didn't need to be said. The finished weapon was exactly one gomed in length — roughly the distance from Ehud's elbow to the tips of his fingers. Short enough to strap against his thigh and remain invisible under a cloak. Long enough to reach the vital organs of an unusually large man. The double edge was critical. A single-edged blade might glance off bone or catch in flesh. A double-edged dagger would cut in any direction, with any movement, regardless of the angle. Ehud was not designing a weapon for a fair fight. He was designing a weapon for a single, decisive thrust, delivered at close range, with no margin for error. He strapped it to his right thigh. This was the heart of everything. A right-handed man carries his weapon on his left side and draws it across his body with his right hand. Every guard in every palace in the ancient world knew this. When they searched a man for concealed weapons, they checked the left side — the left hip, the left thigh, the left ribcage. Nobody checked the right. Unless they knew the man was left-handed. But who would know? Ehud was not a famous warrior. He was not a general. He was one more anonymous Israelite in a crowd of anonymous Israelites,

and that anonymity was his greatest weapon — greater even than the blade pressing cold and secret against his right leg. He practiced the draw. Night after night, standing alone in the workshop, he reached across with his left hand, found the handle under his cloak, pulled the blade free, and thrust forward — all in one smooth, continuous motion. He practiced until the movement was as natural as breathing. Until he could do it in the dark. Until he could do it without looking, without thinking, without the slightest hesitation. One evening, after practice, he walked to the edge of the village and stood looking south toward the Jordan Valley. The sun was setting behind the hills, painting the sky in shades of red and gold. Somewhere down there, in the heat haze, Yericho sat in its ring of palms, and Eglon sat in his palace, and the machinery of oppression ground on like a millstone that never stops turning. "Are you afraid?" Shimi had come up behind him, silent as always. Ehud considered the question. "I'm afraid of failing," he said. "I'm not afraid of dying." "There's a difference?" "A big one. If I die and the plan works, Israel is free. If I fail and survive, Eglon will make an example of me that will keep the people terrified for another generation. Dying is easy. Failing is the thing I can't afford." Shimi was quiet for a while. "You know I can't come with you." "I know." "If you don't come back —" "If I don't come back, forget my name. Forget this conversation. Forget the workshop. Tell anyone who asks that I went to visit relatives in Efraim and you haven't seen me since." "And if you do come back?" Ehud turned to face him. In the dying light, his face looked older than his years — carved, determined, already belonging to a man who had committed himself to something that could not be uncommitted. "If I come back," he said, "have the shofar ready." Then he went to bed and lay awake until dawn, staring at the ceiling, running through the plan in his mind like a man walking a road he has memorized but never traveled.
