Chapter 2: The Left-Handed Man "You're putting too much weight on your right foot." Ehud looked up. His older cousin Shimi was leaning against the wall of the practice yard, arms crossed, watching with the critical eye of a man who had been training fighters since before Ehud could hold a stone. "My right foot is fine," Ehud said. "Your right foot is compensating for your right hand. You're leaning into the sling instead of letting your arm do the work." Shimi pushed off the wall and walked over. "Show me again." Ehud fitted a stone into the leather pouch, wound up, and released. The stone sailed wide, cracking against the trunk of a tamarisk tree twenty paces to the left of the target. "See?" Shimi said. "You're muscling it. Stop trying to throw right-handed." "I'm not —" "You are. Every time. Your body remembers what your teachers told you — lead with the right, anchor with the left. But your right hand doesn't work like theirs. So stop pretending it does." Shimi picked up a stone and placed it in Ehud's left palm. "Again. Left hand. Your natural hand." Ehud shifted his stance. He let the weight settle onto his left leg, wound the sling with his left arm — the arm that had always been stronger, faster, more precise — and released. The stone hit the center of the target with a crack that sent splinters flying. Shimi smiled. "That's the hand Hashem gave you. Use it." To understand what this meant, you have to understand the tribe of Binyamin. Binyamin was the warrior tribe. The smallest of the twelve, occupying a narrow strip of mountainous territory between Yehuda and Efraim, but fierce beyond their numbers. The book of Shoftim itself would later record that Binyamin could field seven hundred left-handed slingers, every one capable of hitting a hair's breadth without missing. The tribe bred fighters the way other tribes bred shepherds. But Ehud was not simply left-handed. The expression itter yad yemino — literally "restricted in his right hand" — suggests something more specific. The Targum understood it as ambidexterity, a man

who could use both hands equally. Others took it more literally: his right hand was weak, perhaps from birth, and he had trained his left to do everything — eat, write, fight — that most men did with their right. In either case, the result was the same. In a world built for right-handed people — where swords hung on the left hip, where guards checked the left side of the body for concealed weapons, where every assumption about combat and movement was built around right-handed men — Ehud's left-handedness was more than a physical trait. It was a tactical advantage that no one else could see. "You think about the palace a lot," Shimi said one evening, as the two men sat cleaning stones after practice. It wasn't a question. Ehud didn't answer immediately. He turned a smooth river stone over in his left hand, feeling its weight, testing its balance. "I think about a lot of things." "You think about the guards. Where they stand. How they search the delegates." Ehud looked at his cousin. "How do you know what I think about?" "Because I watch you the way you watch them." Shimi set down his pouch. "I was in the delegation two years ago. I stood in that hall and handed barley to a fat Moabite king and I wanted to put my fist through the wall. But wanting isn't enough, Ehud." "I know." "And courage isn't enough either. Courage without a plan is just a faster way to die." "I know that too." Shimi studied him for a long moment. "You already have a plan, don't you?" Ehud said nothing. "You don't have to tell me what it is," Shimi said quietly. "I don't want to know. If they catch you and question you, you'll talk eventually — everyone talks. And if they question me, I need to be able to say I didn't know." He paused. "But whatever you're planning — do it right. Because you won't get a second chance." Ehud nodded. "And Ehud?" "Yes?"

"Use your left hand."
