Chapter 1: Eighteen Years The boy's name was Gershon, and he was carrying barley when the soldiers came. He heard them before he saw them — the crunch of boots on the road, the clatter of bronze, the low murmur of Moabite soldiers speaking a language he understood just well enough to fear. He stepped off the path and pressed himself against the trunk of a date palm, holding the sack of grain against his chest like a shield. There were six of them. They wore the short swords and leather armor of Eglon's garrison, and they walked with the particular swagger of men who know that no one will challenge them. The tallest one — a captain, by the marks on his shoulder — saw the boy immediately. "You," the captain said in accented Hebrew. "What's in the sack?" Gershon's mouth went dry. "Barley, sir." "For where?" "For my mother. My father sent it from the —" "Give it here." "But my father —" The captain didn't repeat himself. He simply extended his hand. Behind him, two soldiers shifted their weight, and the gesture was enough. Gershon handed over the sack. The captain looked inside, grunted, and slung it over his own shoulder. "Tell your father the king thanks him for his generosity." The soldiers laughed. Gershon stood against the palm tree and watched them continue down the road, his family's barley disappearing around a bend with the sound of bronze and laughter. He walked home empty-handed. His mother saw him coming through the doorway and knew immediately. She didn't ask. She just closed her eyes for a moment and then turned back to the grinding stone where she was working the last of yesterday's flour.

"Sit down," she said. "We'll manage." "How?" Gershon asked. "That was supposed to last us the week." "Hashem will provide." "Hashem has been providing for Eglon for eighteen years," Gershon said. The bitterness in his voice surprised even him. He was twelve years old. He had never known a world without Moabite soldiers on the roads. His mother stopped grinding. She looked at him with an expression he couldn't quite read — anger, maybe, or grief, or something older than either. "Don't say that." "Why not? It's true. Abba says it himself when he thinks I'm not listening." "Your father says many things when he's tired. What he doesn't say — because he keeps it for his prayers — is that Hashem delivered us from Pharaoh when we had been slaves for two hundred and ten years. Eighteen years is nothing. Eighteen years is a blink." "It doesn't feel like a blink." "It never does." She resumed grinding. "Not from the inside." This was life in the territory of Binyamin, in the eighteenth year of Eglon's occupation. The king of Moav had arrived eighteen years earlier with an army that shouldn't have been able to cross the Jordan, let alone conquer the heartland of Israel. But Hashem had strengthened Eglon's hand — the Tanach says this explicitly, vayechazek Hashem et Eglon — because Israel had done evil in His sight, and the comfortable complacency that had settled over the land in the forty years since Otniel's death had finally reached its consequence. Eglon had been shrewd about it. He didn't come alone. He forged an alliance with Ammon from the north and Amalek — Israel's oldest enemy — from the south. Three nations united against a people who could barely unite among themselves. The coalition swept across the Jordan, overran the tribes of Reuven and Gad, and drove straight into Binyamin's territory to seize the one prize that mattered most: Yericho. The City of Palms. The gateway to the land. From a palace he built in Yericho — a grand structure with an upper chamber cooled by windows, designed for the brutal heat of the Jordan Valley — Eglon ruled over a broken Israel. He demanded tribute. He stationed soldiers. He made sure that every Israelite who walked the roads near Yericho could see the Moabite flag flying over a city that was supposed to be theirs.

And every year, the tribute delegation came. Chosen men from the affected tribes, marching to Yericho with their hands full of gifts, bowing before the man who was crushing them. It was at one of these tributes — perhaps the fifteenth, perhaps the sixteenth — that a young man from the clan of Gera first entered Eglon's palace. His name was Ehud. He was quiet. Watchful. Unremarkable. And he was itter yad yemino — restricted in his right hand.
