Chapter 6: A Message from God The upper chamber was quiet. The windows let in slats of light and the faint sound of palm fronds rustling outside, but otherwise the room was sealed — sealed and private, exactly as Eglon wanted it when he received confidential intelligence from his informants. Eglon leaned forward on his throne. Despite the heat, he seemed energized. A secret message from an Israelite who had just brought tribute — this was exactly the kind of thing a king needed to hear. Perhaps news of a planned rebellion. Perhaps the name of a disloyal elder. Perhaps something he could use to tighten his grip. "Well?" Eglon said. "What is this secret of yours?" Ehud took a step closer. His heart was a war drum, but his voice came out steady — steady and calm, as if what he was about to say were the most ordinary thing in the world. "I have a message from God for you." The words hung in the air. Devar Elokim li eilecha. Not devar seter — a political secret — but devar Elokim — a word from God. Ehud had shifted registers. He was no longer speaking the language of espionage. He was speaking the language of prophecy. And something extraordinary happened. Eglon stood up. The Tanach records it with devastating simplicity: Vayakom me'al hakisei. He rose from his seat. It was not easy. Eglon was bari me'od — massively, staggeringly fat. Rising from a seated position required effort that was almost painful to watch. His thick hands gripped the arms of the throne. His body heaved forward. His enormous belly led the way as he labored upright, his face reddening with exertion, his breath coming in short gasps. But he stood. A pagan king. A descendant of Lot through Moav. An idolater. An oppressor who had crushed Israel for eighteen years. He heard the name of the God of Israel — and he rose from his throne.

Why? The Gemara in Sanhedrin offers an answer that echoes across the generations. Hashem said: "You honored My name by rising from your seat. As a reward, I will establish from you a descendant who will sit upon My throne." That descendant was Ruth. Ruth — Eglon's own granddaughter, according to Ruth Rabbah — who would one day leave everything she knew, abandon Moav, follow Naomi to Beit Lechem, glean in the fields of Boaz, and become the great-grandmother of David, king of Israel. From David would come Shlomo. From Shlomo's line would come the Mashiach himself. All because of a single act. The act of rising. The effort of pushing himself from his throne to a standing position — the distance between sitting and standing, between indifference and honor. And for that one gesture, performed by the most unlikely man in the most unlikely moment, eternity shifted. But Ehud, standing three paces from the rising king, knew none of this. What Ehud saw was something far more immediate: A very large man. Both hands gripping the throne. His belly exposed and unprotected. Ehud's left hand moved.
