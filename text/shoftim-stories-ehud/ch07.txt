Chapter 7: The Blade and the Door The dagger came out of the darkness of his cloak like a tongue of fire. Left hand. Right thigh. The motion he had practiced a thousand times in the back of his father's workshop — smooth, seamless, a single flowing arc from concealment to strike. The blade entered Eglon's belly and kept going. The Tanach describes what followed with a precision that is almost clinical: "The fat closed over the blade, and the hilt went in after the blade — for he did not pull the dagger out of his belly — and the parshedonah came out." The entire weapon — blade, handle, everything — disappeared into the king's body. Swallowed by the sheer mass of his flesh. The wound sealed itself behind the blade, Eglon's own fat closing over the hilt like water closing over a stone dropped into a well. And from the wound came what the Tanach euphemistically calls parshedonah — the contents of the bowels, released by the fatal puncture. Eglon made no sound. Or if he did, it was lost in the collapse of his enormous body — a slow, terrible folding, like a great tree falling in a forest where no one is watching. The king of Moav was dead. Ehud did not pause. He did not check for breath or pulse. He did not search the room or take anything from the king's possessions. Every second in this chamber was a second closer to discovery, and discovery meant death — not just for him, but for the plan, and not just for the plan, but for Israel. He stepped over the body. He crossed the room to the doors of the upper chamber. He stepped out into the misderonah — the vestibule that connected the king's private room to the rest of the palace. And then he did the thing that made the escape possible. He closed the doors. He locked them. A locked door from the outside would look, to anyone approaching, like a door locked from the inside. And a king who locks his own door is a king who does not wish to be disturbed. Ehud pocketed the key, straightened his cloak, and walked down the hallway.

Not ran. Walked. A man running through the halls of a king's palace attracts guards the way blood attracts wolves. But a man walking — a man who looks like he has just concluded a private audience and is returning to his business — is invisible. He is expected. He fits the pattern. He raises no alarm. Ehud walked past a servant carrying a tray of figs. He nodded. The servant nodded back. Ehud walked past two courtiers engaged in a murmured conversation near the staircase. They glanced up. He was just the Israelite who had come back with a message. They looked away. Ehud walked down the stairs, through the entrance hall, past the guards at the gate — the same young guard who had searched him, who had checked the left side and missed the right — and out into the blazing light of the Jordan Valley. He kept walking. Past the first grove of palms. Past the dry wadi. Past the spot where the patrol had been. His pace was steady, measured, the pace of a man with nowhere urgent to be. Inside his chest, his heart was trying to break through his ribs. But his feet moved like they belonged to someone else, someone calm, someone who had not just driven a blade into the belly of a king. He did not look back. Behind him, in the cool upper chamber, the locked door held its secret. The courtiers returned. They had been dismissed by the king himself — has, silence — and they had obeyed. Now, perhaps half an hour later, they approached the upper chamber to resume their duties. The door was locked. "He's probably relieving himself," said one courtier to another, with the casual tone of men who have worked in a palace long enough to know their king's habits. "Should we knock?" "Absolutely not. You want to be the one who interrupts the king on the toilet?" They waited. The smell that seeped from under the door only confirmed their assumption. The parshedonah — the bowel contents that had spilled from Eglon's wound — smelled exactly like what they expected to smell. They waited longer.

Vayachilu ad bosh — they waited until they were embarrassed. The word bosh carries the weight of social shame. These were professional courtiers, trained in the elaborate etiquette of a royal court. You did not knock on the door of the king's private chamber. You certainly did not open it. The protocol was absolute, and the more time passed, the more awkward it became to break it. Every minute of their embarrassment was another minute of Ehud's escape. "This is ridiculous," one courtier finally said. "He's been in there for —" "You want to open that door? Be my guest." "Something could be wrong." "Or he could be fine, and you could be explaining to the king of Moav why you interrupted his privacy. You've seen what he does to people who displease him." A third courtier joined them. "Still locked?" "Still locked." "Has anyone heard anything?" "No." "Not even —" "Nothing. And frankly, after the amount he ate at the morning meal, I'd expect him to be in there for a while." Nervous laughter. Then silence. Then more waiting. A servant passed carrying a tray and glanced at the three courtiers standing outside the locked door. "Everything all right?" "Fine," said the first courtier, too quickly. "The king is... occupied." The servant nodded knowingly and continued on his way. More silence. More minutes ticking away while Ehud climbed higher into the hill country, putting distance and altitude between himself and the discovery that was coming. Finally — the Tanach doesn't say how long, but the reader feels every second of it — concern overcame protocol. They found the key. They opened the door. V'hinei adoneihem nofel artzah met. And behold — their master, fallen on the ground, dead.
