Chapter 8: The Shofar Ehud ran. He passed Pesilim — the same landmark where he had said goodbye to Tzuri and the delegation, the same place where he had turned his face back toward Yericho. He passed it going the other direction now, and this time he did not stop. He climbed west into the hill country of Efraim, his legs burning from the ascent, the sweat pouring from his forehead, the Jordan Valley falling away behind him like a world he was leaving forever. He made it to Se'irah — a place somewhere in the mountains, its exact location unknown to us — and there, standing on a ridge with the wind in his face and the hills of Israel spreading out before him in every direction, he did something that no one had done in eighteen years. He lifted a ram's horn to his lips. And he blew. In a village near Shiloh, a man setting stones for a wall straightened his back and turned toward the sound. "Did you hear that?" His wife came to the doorway. "Hear what?" "Listen." The sound came again — long, mournful, unmistakable. The tekiah. The blast of the shofar. The ancient call that had echoed from Sinai, that had brought down the walls of Yericho, that meant only one thing: Come. Now. Bring whatever you have and come. "That's a shofar," the man said. "I can hear that. But who —" "I don't know." He was already reaching for his sandals. "But I'm going." "Going where?" "Toward the sound."

In a field south of Beit-El, a farmer dropped his hoe and stood motionless, his face lifted toward the hills. "You hear it too?" his neighbor called from the next terrace. "I hear it." "What do we do?" The farmer looked at his hoe. He looked at the hills. He looked at his neighbor. "We go," he said. They came from every direction. From the villages and the farms and the shepherd's camps, from the terraced hills and the hidden valleys, they came streaming down the mountain paths like water finding its way to the sea. They carried whatever weapons they could find — swords where they had them, clubs and sickles and sharpened staves where they didn't. They were not soldiers. They were not trained. They were farmers and herders and craftsmen who had spent eighteen years swallowing their fury. The fury was no longer being swallowed. Ehud stood on the ridge and watched them come, and when enough had gathered — hundreds, then thousands — he raised his voice. "Follow me! For Hashem has delivered your enemies, the Moabites, into your hand!" Not will deliver. Has delivered. Past tense. It was done. The king was dead. The head had been cut from the snake. All that remained was to finish what had been started. "Seize the fords of the Jordan!" Ehud commanded. "Let no Moabite cross. Cut them off from their homeland. Cut them off from retreat." It was a military order of stunning clarity. In that single command, Ehud revealed himself as something far more than an assassin — he was a strategist. By seizing the river crossings, he trapped every Moabite soldier and official west of the Jordan in a noose that could not be loosened. No reinforcements could come from Moav. No survivors could escape back to Moav. The occupation force was isolated, leaderless, and surrounded by a population that had just learned its chains were broken. The Israelites swept down from the hills and took the fords. They turned on the stranded Moabites with eighteen years of accumulated fury. "They struck down about ten thousand men of Moav," the Tanach records — "all of them strong, all of them men of valor. Not one escaped."

All strong. All brave. Not cowards. Eglon's best soldiers, and they died to the last man. Not because Israel was stronger. Because the tide had turned, and when Hashem turns the tide, no strength of arm can hold it back.
