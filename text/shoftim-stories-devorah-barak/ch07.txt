Chapter Seven: The Song They gathered on the hillside above the Kishon valley, and Devorah sang. Not immediately. Not that day. The battle had to end first, the pursuit of the scattered remnants toward Charoshet HaGoyim, the slow realization that Sisera was dead, the gradual comprehension that the army which had terrorized them for twenty years no longer existed. That took time. The shock of victory can be as disorienting as the shock of defeat, and for a people who had spent two decades under the iron heel, the sudden absence of the boot was almost harder to process than its weight. But when the dead had been counted and the chariots had been dragged from the mud and the rain had stopped and the Kishon had returned to its normal shallow self, as if nothing had happened, as if it hadn't just swallowed an army, the people gathered. They came from the battlefield and from the villages and from the hiding places where they had waited for news. They came because something had happened that needed to be marked, named, lifted up and held against the light so everyone could see it. Devorah stood before them. Barak stood beside her. And she sang.

The song began the way all great songs begin: with an acknowledgment that the story does not start here. "Hashem, when You went out from Seir, when You marched from the field of Edom, the earth trembled, the heavens dropped, the clouds poured water." This was Sinai. Devorah was reaching back across the centuries, past the Judges and the conquest and the wandering in the desert, all the way back to the mountain where it all began. Rashi explains why: the Torah is difficult to abandon and glorious to hold. Israel was delivered to its enemies because it let go of the Torah. Israel was saved because it reached for the Torah again. The entire cycle, sin, oppression, cry, redemption, was encoded in those opening lines.

"In the days of Shamgar ben Anas, in the days of Yael, the highways were unoccupied. The travelers walked through byways." A man in the crowd, one of the soldiers from Zevulun, still wearing the mud of the Kishon, nodded. He knew those byways. He had walked them his entire life because the main roads were death. "They chose new gods. Then was war in the gates." An old woman listening near the back of the crowd lowered her head. She remembered. She remembered the carved figures in the marketplace, the red threads, the high places on warm evenings. She remembered how it had started, so gradually, so innocently, and where it had led. "Was there a shield or spear seen among forty thousand in Israel?" A young man, Yoel, the one with the mattock, the one who had left his daughter, looked down at the farming tool still in his hand. The iron head was stained. He would never use it in a field again without remembering what else it had been used for. No shield. No spear. Forty thousand men, and not a weapon among them. And they had won.

"Until I, Devorah, arose. I arose a mother in Israel." The crowd roared. They cheered. They stomped their feet and clapped their hands and shouted her name. This was their moment, the moment when twenty years of suffering crystallized into a single sentence of triumph. But something happened when Devorah said those words. The Gemara in Pesachim records it. The self-reference, the "I", caused the prophetic spirit to withdraw. Not permanently. Not completely. But enough. The flame that had burned so brightly throughout the campaign flickered. The vessel had drawn attention to itself instead of to the light it carried, and for a moment, just a moment, the light dimmed. The song itself records the consequence. The very next line: "Awake, awake, Devorah! Awake, awake, utter a song!" Rashi reads this on two levels. On the surface, it was a call to self-encouragement, sing louder, sing stronger, don't hold back. But the Sages read it as a correction. Wake up, Devorah. You lost the prophecy. You said "I arose" when you should have said "Hashem raised me up." The "I" is the enemy of prophecy. The moment the vessel says "look at me," the light says "I'm leaving." It's a remarkable thing, a rebuke embedded in the song itself. Unerasable. Permanent. A warning encoded in the text by the very woman it warns against.

Devorah caught herself. She found the prophetic thread again. The song continued. "Awake, awake. Utter a song. Arise, Barak. Lead your captivity captive, son of Avinoam." She turned to Barak and sang his name into the story. The Tanna D'vei Eliyahu had said that Barak merited sharing in the song because he trusted the prophecy. Here was the proof. His name, woven into the song beside hers. Not above. Not below. Beside.

Then came the roll call. And the roll call was devastating. "From Ephraim came those whose root was in Amalek. After you, Binyamin, among your peoples." Ephraim came. Binyamin came. Good. "From Machir came down commanders. And from Zevulun, those who carry the staff of the scribe." Machir came. Zevulun came, and Devorah praised them especially. Rashi explains that the "staff of the scribe" means the scholars of Zevulun came to war carrying not just weapons but Torah. They fought with swords in their hands and the law of Hashem in their hearts. "And the princes of Yissachar were with Devorah." Rashi identifies these princes as the members of the Sanhedrin, the scholars who "knew the understanding of the times," who had been studying Torah alongside Devorah under the palm tree and now stood with her in the hour of truth. But then the song turned. And its warmth became ice. "Among the divisions of Reuven, there were great searchings of heart." A silence fell over the crowd. Everyone knew what this meant. Reuven hadn't come. "Why did you sit among the sheepfolds, listening to the piping of the flocks?" Rashi's interpretation cuts like a blade: Reuven positioned himself between the two sides of the conflict. Waiting. Watching. Listening to which way the wind blew before choosing a side. The "great searchings of heart" weren't the searchings of conscience. They were the calculations of self-interest. "Gilad dwelt beyond the Yarden." Stayed home. "Dan, why did he linger by the ships?"

Loaded his wealth onto boats. Ready to flee if things went badly. "Asher sat at the shore of the sea and dwelt by his harbors." Watched from the coastline. Uninvolved. Untouched. Safe. And then, the contrast. Absolute. Searing. "Zevulun is a people that risked their lives unto death. And Naftali, on the high places of the field." They risked their lives unto death. Cheref nafsho lamut. They went to the mountaintop, the exposed high ground, no cover, no retreat, knowing they might not come back. They went because they were called. And when you are called, you go. Yoel, standing in the crowd with his stained mattock, felt tears on his face. He was from Zevulun. He had gone. He had risked his life unto death on the high places of the field. And the prophetess was singing his tribe's name.

"Curse Meroz," said the angel of Hashem. "Curse bitterly its inhabitants, because they came not to the help of Hashem." Who was Meroz? The Gemara in Moed Katan debates it. A town near the battlefield that refused to help. A star that refused to fight when the other stars descended. A powerful individual who was asked and declined. Nobody knows for certain. But the curse is certain. It's the only place in all of Tanach where an angel pronounces a curse on those who merely failed to show up. Not those who fought against Hashem. Those who simply didn't come. The curse of the bystander. It would echo through Jewish history with a resonance its original audience couldn't have foreseen.

"Blessed above women shall Yael be. Wife of Chever the Kenite. Above women in the tent she shall be blessed." A murmur went through the crowd. They had heard what happened in the tent by now. The story had spread the way stories spread in a small community, fast, with details added and subtracted at every telling. But Devorah was fixing it now. Putting it into song. Making it permanent.

"He asked water. She gave him milk. She brought forth cream in a lordly dish." The hospitality. The milk in the fine bowl. The careful, deliberate performance of kindness that concealed a different purpose. "She put her hand to the tent peg. Her right hand to the workman's hammer. She struck Sisera. She crushed his head. She pierced and struck through his temple." The crowd was silent now. Completely silent. The poetic version was more violent than the whispered accounts. Devorah didn't soften it. She didn't look away. The hammer struck. The head was crushed. The temple was pierced. This was iron through bone, and it was holy. "At her feet he sank. He fell. He lay. At her feet he sank. He fell. Where he sank, there he fell down dead." Seven verbs of falling. Seven collapses. The fall of his body. The fall of his army. The fall of his kingdom. The fall of every chariot that had ever rolled across a road in Israel. Seven falls, and then the final word: shadud. Destroyed. Finished. Done.

And then the song reached its strangest passage. Its most human passage. The passage that no one expected. "The mother of Sisera looked out through the window. And she cried through the lattice: Why is his chariot so long in coming? Why tarry the wheels of his chariots?" The crowd shifted. This was not what they expected to hear in a victory song. This was the enemy's mother. The mother of the man who had oppressed them for twenty years. Why was Devorah singing about her? "Her wise ladies answered her. Yes, she returned answer to herself." Yoel lowered his mattock. He was thinking of his own mother. He was thinking of every mother in Israel who had waited for a son to come home from the fields, wondering if today was the day the chariots would take him. He was thinking that somewhere, right now, in a palace in Charoshet HaGoyim, a woman was looking out a window and asking a question that would never be answered. "Surely they are dividing the spoil. A maiden, two maidens for every man. For Sisera, a spoil of dyed garments, embroidered cloth..." The ladies imagined the victory they needed to imagine. The triumph that would explain the delay. The plunder that would justify the wait. They did not know.

She did not know. The crowd knew. And the knowing was what made the passage unbearable. An old man in the crowd, a Levi, one of the scholars who had been with Devorah under the palm tree, leaned to the young man beside him and whispered: "Remember this. The shofar on Rosh Hashanah, the sound of the terua, comes from this woman's cry." "From the enemy's mother?" "From a mother. The Gemara in Rosh Hashanah teaches that the word vateyabev, 'she wailed', is the word that defines the terua blast. Whether it's a groan or a wail, the Sages debate. But both sounds are blown on Rosh Hashanah. One hundred blasts. For the one hundred cries of Sisera's mother before she understood that her son was never coming home." The young man stared at him. "The shofar sound comes from her?" "The shofar sound comes from grief. From the cry of a mother. Any mother. The Torah doesn't say: imitate the righteous. It says: imitate the mother. Because grief is grief, and a mother's cry reaches Heaven regardless of whose mother she is." The young man said nothing. He looked at the crowd. He looked at Devorah, still singing, her voice carrying across the hillside like fire carried by wind. He thought about the shofar, that piercing, broken sound that would one day, every year, on the day of judgment, shatter the defenses of the heart and open it to teshuvah. And that sound, the most sacred sound in Jewish liturgy, would be modeled on the weeping of a pagan woman mourning a wicked son. Because even the enemy is human. Even the enemy has a mother. And that knowledge, held inside the same song that celebrates the enemy's destruction, is what makes this song greater than any song a man had ever composed.

"So let all Your enemies perish, O Hashem." Devorah's voice rose. "But let those who love Him be as the sun when it goes forth in its might." The crowd erupted. The sound was like nothing the valley had heard in twenty years, the full-throated roar of a people set free. Men embraced. Women wept. Children who had never heard their parents laugh with abandon stared wide-eyed at a world that had suddenly, impossibly, changed. "And the land had rest forty years."

Forty years. A generation of peace. Children would be born and grow and marry without hearing the sound of iron wheels on the roads. The palm tree where Devorah had judged would become a landmark, then a legend. Barak would return to Kedesh. Yael would return to her tent, to her husband, to the ordinary life from which she had been briefly and terrifyingly pulled. The Kishon would subside. The valley would dry. Farmers would plant barley where the chariots had burned. The Ruth Rabbah records that even this peace would not last. The famine in the Book of Ruth, "In the days when the judges judged, there was a famine in the land", occurred because the people refused to heed Devorah and Barak. Even the generation that witnessed the miracle at the Kishon would eventually drift. The cycle would turn again. But that was the pattern of Sefer Shoftim. Not the pattern of this story. This story ends with the sun going forth in its might. With the voice of a prophetess rising over the hills of Ephraim. With a song that would be chanted on Shabbat Shirah for three thousand years. This story ends with Devorah.
