Chapter One: The Thornbush Gideon had seventy sons. This is what happens when a man who defeats the Midianites with three hundred torches and a shofar blast is offered the crown of Israel and refuses it but lives like a king anyway. Gideon — Yeruba'al, the man who tore down his father's altar to Ba'al, the man who laid out the fleece and watched God wring water from wool — had many wives and many sons and a household so large that when he finally died, the web of alliances and rivalries he left behind was more tangled than anything the Midianites had ever produced. But there was one son who was not like the others. Avimelech was born to a pilegesh — a concubine — from the city of Shechem. He was Gideon's son by blood but Shechem's son by upbringing. He grew up in his mother's city, among his mother's clan, walking the same streets as the Shechemite merchants and priests and landowners who had watched Gideon's rise from a distance and had never fully accepted that an Israelite judge — even a victorious one — had authority over their ancient city. Shechem was old. Older than the conquest. Older than Yehoshua. It had been a Canaanite city before Avraham passed through it, and the Shechemites carried that history in their bones. They had a temple — the house of Ba'al Berith, the "Lord of the Covenant" — and they had money, and they had the particular arrogance of people who believe that age confers superiority. Avimelech understood these people. He was half of them. After Gideon died, the seventy sons inherited their father's authority — or tried to. Seventy men sharing power is not power. It's a committee. And committees don't inspire loyalty. They inspire frustration. Avimelech went to his mother's brothers. He stood in their courtyard — a young man, handsome, with his father's confidence and his mother's connections — and he made his pitch. "Speak in the ears of all the lords of Shechem," he said. "Which is better for you — that seventy men rule over you, all the sons of Yeruba'al, or that one man rule over you? And

remember that I am your bone and your flesh." It was a masterpiece of political manipulation. Three moves in three sentences. First: frame the current arrangement as absurd. Seventy rulers? Chaos. Second: offer the alternative. One man. Clear. Simple. Strong. Third: remind them of the blood tie. I am yours. Not theirs. Not the Israelite sons of legitimate wives. Yours. His mother's brothers carried the message through Shechem. And the people listened, because the argument was seductive, and because people who are tired of complexity will always be drawn to a man who offers simplicity, even when the simplicity is a lie. "Their hearts inclined toward Avimelech," the text says. "For they said: he is our brother." They gave him seventy pieces of silver from the temple of Ba'al Berith — one piece for each brother he was about to murder. Whether the symmetry was intentional or coincidental, the text doesn't say. But the silver came from a pagan temple, and it was used to hire killers, and the equation was as neat as a closing argument. Avimelech took the silver and hired anashim reikam u'fochazim — reckless and worthless men. Empty men, like the ones who had gathered around Yiftach in Tov. But Yiftach's empty men became soldiers. Avimelech's became assassins. He went to Ophrah, to his father's house, and he killed his brothers. Seventy men. On a single stone. The text gives this detail — "on one stone" — and the detail is horrifying in its specificity. Not in battle. Not in separate incidents. On one stone. One by one, in sequence, as though the stone were an altar and the brothers were offerings and the god being served was Avimelech's ambition. Rashi notes that Yotam — the youngest brother — survived because he hid. One son out of seventy. One seed left in the ground when the field has been burned. The lords of Shechem crowned Avimelech king. They gathered at the oak by the pillar in Shechem — the same Shechem where Yaakov had buried the foreign gods, the same Shechem where Yehoshua had renewed the covenant — and they made the murderer of seventy brothers the ruler of their city. It was the most cynical political act in the entire book of Shoftim. And it would be repaid, measure for measure, with fire.
