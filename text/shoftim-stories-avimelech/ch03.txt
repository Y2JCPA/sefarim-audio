Chapter Three: The Evil Spirit Avimelech ruled for three years. Three years. Not the forty of Devorah or the twenty of Shimshon. Three years — enough time to collect taxes and make enemies and demonstrate to everyone who had cheered his coronation that the thornbush's promise of shade was exactly as empty as the parable had predicted. And then God moved. "God sent a ruach ra'ah — an evil spirit — between Avimelech and the lords of Shechem." The Radak explains: not a demon. Not a supernatural creature. A spirit of discord. Distrust. The slow poison of suspicion that turns allies into enemies. God didn't send an army against Avimelech. He didn't send a plague or a famine or a prophet with a rebuke. He sent something far more efficient: He let the people who had chosen the tyrant discover what tyrants are actually like. The lords of Shechem began to resist. They set ambushes on the hilltops. They robbed travelers on the roads — not Avimelech's enemies but everyone, disrupting the commerce that funded his rule, destabilizing the economy that kept his mercenaries paid. Then a man named Gaal ben Eved arrived. He was a Shechemite with ambition and wine and a loud mouth — a dangerous combination. Gaal came to Shechem during the grape harvest. The people were in the vineyards, picking and pressing and drinking, and the wine loosened their tongues, and Gaal's tongue was the loosest of all. "Who is Avimelech?" Gaal said, his voice carrying across the celebration. "And who is Shechem, that we should serve him? Is he not the son of Yeruba'al? And Zevul his officer? Serve the men of Chamor, the father of Shechem! Why should we serve this man?" The crowd cheered. Wine and resentment made a potent fuel, and Gaal was holding the match.

"If only this people were in my hand!" Gaal continued, warming to his audience. "I would remove Avimelech. I would say to him: gather your army and come out!" Zevul — Avimelech's governor in Shechem — heard every word. He sent a message to Avimelech in the night: come now, set an ambush, strike at dawn. Avimelech came. He divided his men into four companies. He waited in the fields outside the city. At dawn, Gaal walked out of the city gate. He saw movement on the hills. "Look," he said to Zevul, who was standing beside him. "People are coming down from the hilltops." Zevul — the double agent, the governor who served both masters — looked at the hills and smiled. "You're seeing the shadows of the mountains. They look like men." "No — look! People are coming from the center of the land. And one company is coming from the direction of the Diviners' Oak." Zevul dropped the pretense. "Where is your big mouth now?" he said. "Isn't this the people you despised? Go out and fight them." Gaal went out. Gaal fought. Gaal lost. Avimelech chased him to the city gate, and Zevul drove Gaal and his brothers out of Shechem. But Avimelech was not finished. The next day, the people of Shechem went out to the fields — farmers going to work, as though the rebellion had been settled and life could resume. Avimelech had divided his men into three companies. He attacked. He fought the people all day. He captured the city. He killed the people in it. He tore the city down. He sowed it with salt. Rashi notes the salt: a symbol of permanent destruction. Nothing would grow there again. The city that had crowned him was now a wasteland. The people who had paid seventy silver pieces to make him king had been repaid with annihilation. A thousand people — men and women — fled to the stronghold of the temple of El Berith. They climbed to the roof. They thought the temple would protect them.

Avimelech went to Har Tzalmon. He cut a branch from a tree with his axe. He put it on his shoulder. "What you have seen me do — hurry and do the same," he told his men. They cut branches. They piled them against the stronghold. They set fire to them. A thousand people burned alive in the temple that had funded Avimelech's rise. The silver that had paid for the murder of seventy brothers came from that temple. The fire that consumed it was the fire Yotam had prophesied from the top of Gerizim. Let fire come out of Avimelech and consume the lords of Shechem. The first half of the curse was fulfilled. ∗ ∗ ∗
