Chapter Two: The Parable Yotam heard about the coronation. He was hiding — somewhere in the hill country, somewhere in the network of caves and gorges that had sheltered fugitives since the days of the Patriarchs. He was the youngest of seventy brothers, and he was the only one alive, and the stone where his brothers had died was still stained with their blood, and the man who had killed them was being crowned king in the city below. He came to Har Gerizim. The Radak notes the choice of location: Gerizim was the mountain of blessing, the place where six tribes had stood during the covenant ceremony under Yehoshua to pronounce blessings over Israel. Yotam climbed the mountain of blessing to deliver a curse. The irony was deliberate. Every element of this scene was calculated. He stood on the summit. Below him, Shechem spread across the valley — the houses, the temple, the marketplace, the oak where they had just crowned his brother's murderer. He could see the people going about their business, carrying on as though nothing had happened, as though the blood of seventy men was a reasonable price for political simplicity. Yotam lifted his voice and shouted. "Listen to me, lords of Shechem, so that God may listen to you!" The sound carried across the valley. People stopped. They looked up at the mountain. A lone figure standing on the ridge, silhouetted against the sky. "The trees went out to anoint a king over themselves." He was telling a mashal — a parable. And it was the greatest parable in all of Sefer Shoftim. "They said to the olive tree: 'Reign over us!'" The olive tree. Rashi identifies this as Otniel ben Kenaz — the first judge, the quiet scholar, the man who conquered through Torah and through war. The productive tree. The

tree whose oil lights the Menorah and anoints kings and heals wounds. "And the olive tree said to them: 'Should I leave my oil, by which they honor God and men, and go wave over the trees?'" Should I abandon what I do well — what is useful, what serves God and humanity — to go hold a title? The olive tree understood something fundamental: leadership is not a reward. It's a sacrifice. And a tree that is already bearing fruit has better things to do than stand above other trees pretending to be important. "They said to the fig tree: 'Come, reign over us!'" The fig tree. Rashi connects this to Devorah — the judge who sat under a tree and made her wicks thick because the light should be bright. Sweet, productive, nourishing. "And the fig tree said: 'Should I leave my sweetness and my good fruit, and go wave over the trees?'" The same answer. The same refusal. I have something real to give the world. Kingship would take me away from it. "They said to the vine: 'Come, reign over us!'" Gideon himself. The man who was offered the crown and said no. The vine whose wine "cheers God and men." "And the vine said: 'Should I leave my wine, which cheers God and men, and go wave over the trees?'" Three refusals. Three productive trees. Each one asked to rule. Each one recognized that ruling would mean abandoning the work that actually mattered. The olive would stop producing oil. The fig would stop bearing fruit. The vine would stop making wine. Leadership, in the parable's economy, was not an addition. It was a subtraction. "Then all the trees said to the thornbush: 'Come, reign over us!'" The thornbush. The bramble. The plant that produces nothing edible, provides no shade worth mentioning, and whose only notable quality is its thorns. The plant you burn to clear a field. The plant that exists, as far as agriculture is concerned, to get in the way. "And the thornbush said to the trees: 'If in truth you anoint me king over you, come, take shelter in my shade.'"

Yotam paused. Let the absurdity land. The shade of a thornbush. The thornbush has no shade. It barely reaches a man's knee. The offer of shelter from a plant that provides none was the thornbush's first lie — and the first sign that this king's promises would be as empty as his branches. "'But if not — let fire come out of the thornbush and consume the cedars of Lebanon!'" The crowd below was silent now. Every person in Shechem who could hear was listening, because the parable was no longer abstract. The thornbush was Avimelech. The trees were the men of Shechem. And the fire — the fire was a prophecy. Yotam dropped the mashal and spoke plainly. "If you have acted in truth and integrity in making Avimelech king — if you have dealt well with Yeruba'al and his house — if you have done to him according to the work of his hands — my father fought for you. He risked his life for you. He delivered you from Midian. And you have risen against my father's house today and killed his sons, seventy men, on one stone. And you have made Avimelech, the son of his maidservant, king over the lords of Shechem, because he is your brother." The accusation was surgical. You owe your freedom to my father. He saved you from Midian. And you repaid him by slaughtering his sons and crowning his concubine's child because it was politically convenient. "If you have acted in truth and integrity — then rejoice in Avimelech, and let him rejoice in you." A pause. The conditional hung in the air like a raised blade. "But if not — let fire come out of Avimelech and consume the lords of Shechem and Beth Millo. And let fire come out of the lords of Shechem and Beth Millo and consume Avimelech." Fire from the king consuming the people. Fire from the people consuming the king. Mutual destruction. The curse was symmetrical, and it would be fulfilled with a precision that would make the prophets weep. Yotam ran. He fled to Be'er, and he hid there, and he was not heard from again. The youngest son of Gideon vanished into the landscape, leaving behind nothing but a parable and a curse. Both would come true.
