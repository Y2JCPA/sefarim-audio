Chapter Four: The Millstone Avimelech marched to Teveitz. He was drunk on destruction now — the burning of Shechem behind him, the salt still on his hands, the smell of the temple fire still in his clothes. He had annihilated the city that made him king, and instead of pausing to consider what that meant — instead of hearing, in the roar of the flames, the echo of Yotam's curse — he marched to the next city and began again. He captured Teveitz. The people fled to a strong tower in the middle of the city. They went up to the roof — men, women, children — and barred the door below. Avimelech approached the tower. He had done this before, at Shechem. The same tactic: branches, fire, burn them out. He walked up to the door of the tower, close enough to touch it, ready to set it ablaze. A woman was watching from the roof. The text doesn't give her name. It doesn't describe her. It doesn't explain how she came to be holding an upper millstone — a pelach rechev, the heavy stone that sits on top of the grinding apparatus, the stone a woman lifts and turns every morning when she grinds the family's grain. She looked down. She saw Avimelech. She saw the man who had killed seventy brothers on a single stone and burned a thousand people in a temple and sowed an entire city with salt. She saw him walking toward the door of the tower where her children were hiding. She dropped the millstone. It struck him on the head. The text says vataritz et gulgalto — she crushed his skull. The heavy grinding stone, falling from the height of the tower, hit the man who had ground an entire region into dust. Avimelech didn't die instantly. He was conscious. He could feel the fracture. He could feel the blood. And he could feel something worse than the pain — the knowledge that he had been brought down by a woman with a kitchen tool.

He called to his armor-bearer. The young man ran to him, his face white with shock. "Draw your sword," Avimelech said. His voice was thick, broken, the voice of a man whose skull was cracked and whose pride was the last thing functioning. "Kill me. So that they will not say of me: a woman killed him." The Radak notes the extraordinary vanity of this request. Avimelech was dying. His kingdom was ash. His city was salted earth. His allies were burned corpses. And his final thought — the last flicker of consciousness before the darkness — was about his reputation. Not repentance. Not God. Not the seventy brothers whose blood had funded all of this. His reputation. Lest they say a woman killed him. But a woman did kill him. The armor-bearer's sword was a formality. The millstone was the truth. And the truth — which the Tanach always tells, regardless of what the dying man wants people to say — was that Avimelech, the man who murdered his way to a throne, was brought down by an unnamed woman dropping a stone from a roof. The same God who had used Yael's tent peg to end Sisera and would use a sling stone to end Golyat used a millstone to end Avimelech. The weapons were domestic. The hands were ordinary. The message was the same: no amount of military power protects you from a God who has decided you're finished. The armor-bearer drew his sword. He killed Avimelech. And the men who had followed Avimelech — the reckless and worthless men, the mercenaries bought with temple silver — saw their commander dead and went home. Every man to his place. The army dissolved like smoke. Three years of tyranny, and there was nothing left — no dynasty, no succession, no legacy. Just a dead man, a salted city, a burned temple, and a millstone lying in the dust beside a cracked skull. ∗ ∗ ∗
