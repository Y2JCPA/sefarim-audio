Chapter Five: Thus God Repaid The text delivers the verdict in two verses. They are among the most satisfying in all of Tanach. "Thus God repaid the wickedness of Avimelech, which he had done to his father by killing his seventy brothers." One verse for the king. His wickedness — not his ambition, not his strategy, not his political skill, but his wickedness — was repaid. The seventy brothers, killed on a single stone, were avenged by a single stone dropped from a tower roof. The symmetry was divine. The stone answered the stone. "And all the wickedness of the men of Shechem God returned upon their heads, and upon them came the curse of Yotam the son of Yeruba'al." One verse for the people. Because the men of Shechem were not innocent. They had funded the murder. They had cheered the coronation. They had chosen the thornbush when the olive and the fig and the vine had all tried to warn them. The fire that consumed them was the fire they had paid for — seventy pieces of silver from the temple of a god who could not save them from the God of Israel. Yotam's curse, spoken from the mountain of blessing, fulfilled in the valley of ashes. Let fire come out of Avimelech and consume the lords of Shechem. The temple burning. Let fire come out of the lords of Shechem and consume Avimelech. The millstone falling. The Ralbag draws the final lesson: the story of Avimelech is the story of what happens when leadership is seized rather than given. Every judge before Avimelech — Otniel, Ehud, Devorah, Gideon, Yiftach — was called. Chosen. Appointed by God or summoned by the people in their desperation. None of them sought the position. The olive tree didn't want to reign. The fig tree didn't want to reign. The vine didn't want to reign. Only the thornbush wanted the crown. And the thornbush burned everything it touched.

This was the anti-story of Sefer Shoftim. Not a story of deliverance but a story of self-destruction. Not a story of a flawed hero rising to meet a divine call but a story of a man who manufactured his own call with silver and blood and paid for it with everything. The people of Shechem chose badly. They chose the man who told them what they wanted to hear instead of the truth they needed. They chose simplicity over complexity, one ruler over seventy, the brother who flattered them over the father who saved them. And the thornbush rewarded their choice with fire. Three thousand years later, the parable still burns. ∗ ∗ ∗
