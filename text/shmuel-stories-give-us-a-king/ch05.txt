Chapter Five: The Farewell Shmuel stood before the nation one last time. He was old. His hair was white. His sons stood beside him — the sons who had not walked in his ways, the sons whose failure had precipitated this revolution. He looked at the people he had served since he was a child, the people he had judged on his circuit from Ramah to Beit-El to Gilgal to Mitzpah, and he spoke. "Here I am. Testify against me before Hashem and before His anointed." He challenged them. Not with anger — with vulnerability. If I have wronged you, say so. If I have taken what was not mine, say so. If I have oppressed or cheated or accepted a bribe to look the other way — say so now, in front of God and the king, and I will make it right. "Whose ox have I taken?" he asked. "Whose donkey have I taken? Whom have I defrauded? Whom have I oppressed? From whose hand have I taken a ransom to blind my eyes?" The crowd was silent. Then they answered: "You have not defrauded us. You have not oppressed us. You have not taken anything from any man's hand." The Radak notes that this exchange was not a formality. Shmuel needed the people to say it — publicly, before Hashem, on the record. Because the era of the judges was ending and the era of the kings was beginning, and before the transition was complete, it had to be established that the outgoing system had been clean. That the problem was not with the prophetic judiciary. That the failure was not Shmuel's. That the people had chosen a king not because the old way was broken, but because they wanted something different. "Hashem is witness against you," Shmuel said, "and His anointed is witness this day, that you have not found anything in my hand." "He is witness," the people said.

Then Shmuel recounted the history. From Mitzrayim to the Judges, from Yerubaal to Yiftach to Shmuel himself — the long arc of deliverance, the pattern of sin and salvation, the God who had never abandoned them even when they abandoned Him. "And when you saw that Nachash king of Ammon came against you, you said to me: 'No — a king shall reign over us.' Yet Hashem your God was your King."

The rebuke was gentle but absolute. You had a King. The best King. The King who splits seas and sends thunder and makes the stars fight in their courses. And you traded Him for a man. "Now, here is the king you have chosen, whom you have asked for. Hashem has set a king over you." He didn't say "the king Hashem chose." He said "the king you have chosen, whom you have asked for." The emphasis was deliberate. This was their decision. Their responsibility. "If you will fear Hashem and serve Him and listen to His voice, and not rebel against the commandment of Hashem — then both you and the king who reigns over you will follow Hashem your God." If. The conditional was everything. The monarchy could work. Human kingship could coexist with divine kingship. But only if — only if — the king and the people remained subordinate to Hashem. The moment they forgot who the real King was, everything would collapse. "But if you will not listen to the voice of Hashem, and you rebel against His commandment — the hand of Hashem will be against you, as it was against your fathers."

And then Shmuel did something extraordinary. "Stand now, and see this great thing which Hashem will do before your eyes." It was wheat harvest — late spring or early summer. The sky was clear. The fields were golden. Rain at this time of year was almost unheard of. The land was dry, the way it should be at harvest. "Is it not wheat harvest today?" Shmuel said. "I will call to Hashem, and He will send thunder and rain. And you shall know and see that the evil you have done in the sight of Hashem is great, in asking for yourselves a king." Shmuel called to Hashem. And Hashem sent thunder and rain. The sky that had been blue and cloudless split open. Thunder cracked across the valley — the same kind of thunder that had routed the Pelishtim at Mitzpah, the unmistakable voice of a God demonstrating His power. And rain fell — torrential, impossible, out-of-season rain that drenched the wheat and soaked the fields and left the people standing in a downpour, terrified. "All the people greatly feared Hashem and Shmuel."

They turned to the old prophet, soaked and shaking, and they said: "Pray for your servants to Hashem your God, that we may not die. For we have added to all our sins the evil of asking for ourselves a king." They understood. Too late to take it back — the king was anointed, the kingdom was established, the lots had been cast and the oxen had been sacrificed at Gilgal. But they understood what they had done. The rain was the proof. The God who could send thunder in wheat harvest was the God who did not need a human intermediary to rule His people. Shmuel looked at them. Soaked. Terrified. Repentant. The people he had judged and guided and served his entire life, from the moment his mother left him at the doorpost in Shiloh. "Do not be afraid," he said. His voice was steady. The anger was gone. The rebuke was over. What remained was the thing that had always been at the center of Shmuel — not judgment, but love. "You have done all this evil. Yet do not turn aside from following Hashem. Serve Hashem with all your heart. Do not turn aside after empty things that cannot profit or deliver, for they are empty." His voice rose. "For Hashem will not abandon His people, for the sake of His great name. Because Hashem has resolved to make you His people." This was the heart of the farewell. Not the rebuke. Not the thunder. This. Hashem will not abandon you. Even when you reject Him. Even when you ask for a king instead of trusting in His kingship. Even when you repeat the same mistakes your ancestors made at Sinai and at Baal Peor and at every crossroads where the choice was between the hard truth and the comfortable lie — He will not abandon you. Because He chose you. And His choice does not expire. "As for me," Shmuel concluded, "far be it from me that I should sin against Hashem by ceasing to pray for you. And I will teach you the good and right way." He would not stop praying. He would not stop teaching. He was no longer the judge — the king held that role now. But Shmuel would remain what he had always been: the voice. The man who heard Hashem in the night and spoke His words in the morning. "Only fear Hashem and serve Him in truth with all your heart. For consider what great things He has done for you." He paused. "But if you persist in doing evil — both you and your king will be swept away."

The rain stopped. The sky cleared. The wheat fields steamed in the sudden sun. The era of the judges was over. The era of the kings had begun. And Shmuel — the child of Chanah, the boy from Shiloh, the prophet who had never let a single word fall to the ground — went home to Ramah. He would be back. There was an Amalek to destroy. A king to anoint. A boy named David, tending sheep in Beit Lechem, who didn't know yet that history was waiting for him. But that is another story. * * *
