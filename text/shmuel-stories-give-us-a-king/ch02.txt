Chapter Two: They Have Not Rejected You Shmuel prayed. He did what he always did when the world delivered a blow — he turned to Hashem. The man who had heard God's voice as a child in a dark chamber, who had stood at Mitzpah and cried out on behalf of a terrified nation, now prayed again. Not for victory this time. Not for deliverance. For understanding. The elders wanted a king. They wanted to replace the system that Shmuel had given his life to — the prophetic judiciary, the circuit of justice, the direct governance of Hashem through His chosen servants. They wanted a man with a crown and a sword instead of a prophet with a staff and a prayer. Hashem answered. "Listen to the voice of the people in all that they say to you." Listen to them. Give them what they want. Then the words that carried the weight of a millstone: "For it is not you they have rejected. It is Me they have rejected from being King over them." Not you, Shmuel. Me. The Radak explains what Hashem was saying: the people's demand was not a personal insult to Shmuel. It was a theological crisis. From the day Hashem brought Israel out of Mitzrayim, He had been their King. He had fought their battles. He had governed through prophets and judges. He had been present — in the pillar of fire, in the thundercloud at Sinai, in the Aron, in the voice that spoke at Shiloh. And the people said: we want someone else. "Like everything they have done from the day I brought them up out of Mitzrayim to this day," Hashem continued. "They have forsaken Me and served other gods. So they are doing to you also." There was sadness in these words. The commentators hear it — a quality that transcends the text and enters the space between the words. God telling His prophet: this is who they are. This is what they do. They wanted golden calves at Sinai. They wanted Baal in the valleys. Now they want a king. The rejection is not new. It is ancient. It is the oldest pattern in the story of this people.

But Hashem did not refuse. "Listen to their voice," He said. "But warn them. Tell them the manner of the king who will reign over them."

Shmuel stood before the elders and delivered the warning. He spoke it like a prophecy — measured, precise, each sentence landing like a stone. "This will be the manner of the king who will reign over you: He will take your sons and appoint them to his chariots and his horsemen, and they will run before his chariots." Your sons. Not someone else's sons. Yours. The boys who play in your courtyards and help with your harvests — they will belong to the king. "He will appoint for himself commanders of thousands and commanders of fifties. He will set them to plow his ground and reap his harvest and make his instruments of war." Your labor. Your time. Your hands — they will serve his purposes. "He will take your daughters to be perfumers and cooks and bakers." Your daughters. "He will take your fields and your vineyards and your olive orchards — the best ones — and give them to his servants." Your land. The inheritance of your fathers. Gone. "He will take a tenth of your grain and your vintage and give it to his officers." A tax. Above the tithe you already owe to Hashem. "He will take your male servants and female servants and your best young men and your donkeys, and put them to his work. He will take a tenth of your flocks, and you yourselves will become his servants." You will be slaves again. Not to Pharaoh. Not to the Pelishtim. To your own king. "And you will cry out on that day because of your king whom you have chosen for yourselves — and Hashem will not answer you on that day." The last line was the cruelest. The people who were rejecting Hashem as their King in order to choose a human one would discover, too late, that human kings do not hear cries the way Hashem hears them. And when they cried out — as they had cried out in Mitzrayim, as they had cried out under Sisera, as they had cried out at Mitzpah — the silence would be deafening.

The Gemara in Sanhedrin debates whether this passage describes the legitimate authority of a king — mishpat hamelech, the law of the king, the powers he is entitled to exercise — or whether Shmuel was warning about the abuses that a king would commit regardless of his authority. Rabbi Yosi says the king is permitted to do all these things. Rabbi Yehudah says Shmuel was only frightening them. Both readings carry truth. The king would have authority. The king would also abuse it. The distinction between legitimate power and its abuse is thinner than anyone standing in that courtyard in Ramah could have imagined. The people listened to every word. And then they said: "No. We will have a king over us. That we also may be like all the nations, and that our king may judge us and go out before us and fight our battles." Like all the nations. They said it again. The words that had stung Shmuel the first time now cut deeper — because now they were saying it after the warning. After they had been told exactly what they would lose. After Hashem Himself had said they were rejecting Him. They chose the king anyway. And Hashem said to Shmuel: "Listen to their voice, and appoint for them a king." * * *
