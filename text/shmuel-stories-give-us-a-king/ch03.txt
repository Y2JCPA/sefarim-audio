Chapter Three: The Lost Donkeys There was a man from Binyamin. His name was Kish — a wealthy man, a man of substance, from one of the prominent families of the tribe. He had a son named Shaul. The text describes Shaul with two words that carry enormous weight: bachur vatov — young and good. And then adds: "There was no man among the children of Israel more handsome than he. From his shoulders and upward he was taller than any of the people." Taller. More handsome. Good. He looked like a king before anyone knew he was going to be one. And Hashem had already spoken to Shmuel: "Tomorrow at this time I will send you a man from the land of Binyamin, and you shall anoint him as leader over My people Israel. He will save My people from the hand of the Pelishtim, for I have seen My people's suffering, because their cry has come to Me." The language echoed Mitzrayim. "I have seen... their cry has come to Me." The same God who had sent Moshe to deliver Israel from Pharaoh was now sending Shmuel to anoint a king. The mechanism was different. The compassion was the same. But the king did not arrive on a horse. He arrived looking for donkeys.

Kish's donkeys had wandered off. This was an expensive loss — donkeys were working animals, essential to a farming household. Kish sent his son Shaul with a servant to find them. They searched. Through the hill country of Ephraim. Through the land of Shalishah. Through the land of Shaalim. Through the territory of Binyamin. Days of walking, days of searching, and nothing. "Come," Shaul said to his servant. "Let's go back. My father will stop worrying about the donkeys and start worrying about us." The servant had an idea. "There is a man of God in this city. He is an honored man — everything he says comes true. Perhaps he can tell us which way to go." The man of God was Shmuel. Shaul hesitated. "If we go to him, what can we bring him? The bread in our bags is gone. We have no gift for the man of God."

The servant produced a quarter-shekel of silver. "I have this. I'll give it to the man of God, and he'll tell us our way." They climbed the hill toward the city. They met young women going out to draw water and asked: "Is the seer here?" "He is. Hurry — he has just come to the city today, because there is a sacrifice for the people today on the high place. Go up. You'll find him before he goes up to eat." They went up. And at the gate of the city, they met Shmuel.

Shmuel saw Shaul, and Hashem spoke. "This is the man I told you about. This one will rule over My people." One moment. One glance. The intersection of a lost donkey search and a divine appointment. Shaul was looking for animals. Hashem was looking for a king. And the two searches converged at a city gate on an afternoon when a sacrifice was being prepared. Shaul approached. "Tell me, please — where is the seer's house?" Shmuel looked at him. He looked at the height, the shoulders, the face of the young man from Binyamin. He looked at the man Hashem had chosen. "I am the seer," Shmuel said. "Go up before me to the high place, and you shall eat with me today. In the morning I will let you go, and I will tell you everything that is in your heart." Then he added, with a gentleness that must have bewildered this young man searching for donkeys: "As for the donkeys that were lost three days ago — do not worry about them. They have been found." Shaul stared at him. "And to whom belongs everything desirable in Israel?" Shmuel continued. "Is it not to you and to all your father's house?" Shaul's response was immediate — and it would prove prophetic in ways he could not know. "Am I not a Benjaminite, from the smallest of the tribes of Israel? And my family the least of all the families of the tribe of Binyamin? Why do you speak to me this way?" Humility. Genuine humility. The man who was about to become king could not imagine why a prophet would treat him with honor. He saw himself as small — small tribe, small family, small man on a small errand looking for lost donkeys.

The Radak notes that this humility was one of the reasons Hashem chose him. A man who sees himself as unworthy of authority is precisely the man who should hold it. The tragedy of Shaul would be that this humility — so beautiful at the beginning — would curdle into something else. Into paralysis. Into the inability to act decisively when the moment demanded it. The same quality that made him worthy of the crown would eventually make him unworthy of it. But that was the future. For now, a humble man sat down to eat with a prophet, and the prophet gave him the seat of honor and the choicest portion, and Shaul ate — confused, overwhelmed, not yet understanding that the donkeys were the last ordinary thing he would ever lose. In the morning, Shmuel took a flask of oil and poured it over Shaul's head. "Has not Hashem anointed you as leader over His inheritance?" He kissed him. And the spirit of God rushed upon Shaul, and he prophesied. * * *
