Chapter Four: Hidden Among the Baggage The private anointing was complete. Now came the public. Shmuel gathered all Israel at Mitzpah — the same Mitzpah where the thunder had routed the Pelishtim, the same place where Israel had fasted and confessed and poured out water before Hashem. The place of national teshuvah was now the place of national transformation. But before the selection, Shmuel delivered a rebuke. "Thus says Hashem, the God of Israel: 'I brought Israel up from Mitzrayim. I delivered you from the hand of Mitzrayim and from the hand of all the kingdoms that oppressed you.' But you have today rejected your God, who saves you from all your calamities and distresses, and you have said to Him: 'Set a king over us.'" Even now — even as he was about to comply with their demand — Shmuel would not let them forget what they were doing. The rebuke was not cruelty. It was love. The kind of love that insists on truth even when truth is unwelcome. "Now present yourselves before Hashem by your tribes and by your clans." The selection began. Tribe by tribe, the lots fell. The lot fell on Binyamin. Clan by clan within Binyamin — the lot fell on the family of Matri. Man by man within the family — the lot fell on Shaul ben Kish. They looked for him. He was gone. "Is there yet a man to come here?" they asked Hashem. "He is hidden among the baggage." The Metzudat David explains: Shaul had hidden himself among the vessels and luggage piled at the edge of the gathering. He knew the lot would fall on him — Shmuel had already told him, had already anointed him in private, had already poured the oil and given the signs. But when the moment came — the public moment, the irreversible moment, the moment when the lost-donkey-searcher would become the king of Israel — Shaul hid. The Ralbag reads this as humility carried to its extreme. A man so convinced of his unworthiness that he could not bring himself to stand before the nation and accept the crown. The same "Am I not a Benjaminite?" that had charmed Shmuel at the gate now manifested as paralysis at the assembly.

They found him. They pulled him out from among the baggage. They brought him before the people. And when he stood — tall, broad, his head and shoulders rising above every man in the crowd — the nation saw what Hashem had seen. "See him whom Hashem has chosen!" Shmuel cried. "There is none like him among all the people!" "Yechi hamelech!" the people shouted. "Long live the king!" Not all of them. The text records that "worthless men" — bnei beliyaal, the same phrase used for Eli's sons — muttered: "How can this man save us?" They despised Shaul and brought him no gifts. And Shaul held his peace. He heard the mockery and said nothing. The Radak says this was another sign of his fitness — a man who could absorb contempt without retaliation was a man who could rule without tyranny. Shmuel wrote the manner of the kingdom in a scroll and laid it before Hashem. Then he sent all the people away, each man to his house. And Shaul went home to Givah. With him went men of valor, whose hearts God had touched. The king had been chosen. The kingdom had begun. But the kingdom had not yet been proven.

The proof came from Yavesh Gilad. Nachash the Ammonite besieged the city. The men of Yavesh sent messengers begging for terms. Nachash offered them terms that were designed not as a treaty but as a humiliation: "I will make a covenant with you on one condition — that I gouge out every right eye among you, and I will set it as a disgrace upon all Israel." The elders of Yavesh asked for seven days to send messengers throughout Israel. If no one came to save them, they would submit. The messengers reached Givah. The people heard and wept. Shaul was coming in from the field, behind his oxen. He was still farming. The king of Israel was plowing a field when the crisis arrived. He heard the weeping and asked what was wrong. They told him.

And the spirit of God rushed upon Shaul, and his anger burned. He took a pair of oxen and cut them in pieces and sent the pieces throughout the borders of Israel by the hand of messengers, saying: "Whoever does not come out after Shaul and after Shmuel — so shall it be done to his oxen!" The fear of Hashem fell on the people, and they came out as one man. Three hundred thousand from Israel. Thirty thousand from Yehudah. Shaul divided them into three companies. They attacked the Ammonite camp in the morning watch and struck Nachash until the heat of the day. The survivors were scattered — not two of them remained together. Yavesh Gilad was saved. The king had proven himself. The people turned on the bnei beliyaal who had mocked Shaul. "Bring those men," they demanded, "and we will put them to death." "No man shall be put to death this day," Shaul said. "For today Hashem has wrought salvation in Israel." The first act of the new king was mercy. Not vengeance. Mercy. The man who had hidden among the baggage now stood before his nation and demonstrated that power does not require cruelty, and that victory does not require blood beyond what the battle demands. Shmuel said: "Come, let us go to Gilgal and renew the kingdom there." They went. They made Shaul king before Hashem in Gilgal. They offered peace offerings. And Shaul and all the men of Israel rejoiced greatly. The kingdom was established. And it was time for Shmuel to say goodbye. * * *
