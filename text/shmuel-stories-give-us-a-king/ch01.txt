Chapter One: The Sons Who Failed Shmuel grew old. He had judged Israel for decades — walking his circuit from Ramah to Beit-El to Gilgal to Mitzpah, settling disputes, teaching Torah, holding the nation together by the sheer force of his righteousness. He had rebuilt what Eli's sons had destroyed. He had stood at Mitzpah and called down thunder from Heaven. He had restored the roads that Elkana his father had walked. And now his body was telling him what his spirit would not accept: the work was too much for one man, and the man was running out of time. He appointed his sons as judges. Yoel and Aviyah. He stationed them in Be'er Sheva — the southern edge of the land, far from Ramah, in the territory that needed judicial oversight the most. They would extend his reach. They would carry his legacy. They would be what Chofni and Pinchas should have been to Eli. They were not. "His sons did not walk in his ways," the text says. The simplicity of the sentence is its own kind of violence. Five words that collapsed a father's hopes. The Gemara in Shabbat offers a defense — the same instinct that had defended Eli's sons now rose to defend Shmuel's. "Whoever says the sons of Shmuel sinned is in error." Their offense was not bribery in the conventional sense. They were Levi'im entitled to certain tithes, and they demanded those tithes aggressively, traveling from town to town to collect what was owed to them rather than waiting for the people to bring it. The appearance of corruption, even without the substance. But the Radak takes a harder line. The text says they "turned after gain, took bribes, and perverted justice." The Radak reads this at face value — they were corrupt. Not as dramatically as Chofni and Pinchas, not with the brazen public desecration that had hollowed out Shiloh, but corrupt enough. Quietly corrupt. Bureaucratically corrupt. The kind of corruption that makes people lose faith not in God but in the institutions that are supposed to represent Him. The pattern was devastating in its repetition. Eli's sons had destroyed the priesthood. Now Shmuel's sons were destroying the judiciary. The greatest leaders of Israel could not transmit their greatness to their children. The thing that made them extraordinary — the combination of faith and discipline and selfless service — was not genetic. It could not be inherited. It had to be chosen, and Yoel and Aviyah chose otherwise.

Shmuel must have known. He must have heard the reports — the way Eli had heard reports, the way fathers always hear reports about their children, too late and too softly. Whether he confronted them, whether he removed them, the text doesn't say. What the text says is what happened next. The elders came to Ramah.

They came in a delegation — the elders of Israel, the tribal leaders, the men who spoke for the nation. They came to the old prophet's home, and they stood before him, and they said the words that changed Jewish history. "You have grown old, and your sons do not walk in your ways. Now appoint for us a king to judge us, like all the nations." Like all the nations. Those three words were the problem. Not the request itself — the Torah had anticipated a king. The passage in Devarim said plainly: "When you come to the land... and you say, 'I will set a king over me like all the nations around me,' you shall surely set a king over yourself." The Gemara in Sanhedrin records the machloket: Rabbi Yehudah says appointing a king is a mitzvah, one of three commandments Israel received upon entering the land. Rabbi Nehorai says it is not a mitzvah but a concession — the Torah foresaw that Israel would want a king and regulated the desire rather than commanding it. The elders' request fell on the wrong side of this debate. They did not say: "We want to fulfill the mitzvah of appointing a king." They said: "Like all the nations." They wanted what the Pelishtim had. What Moav had. What Ammon had. A permanent military commander. A visible, human authority. A man on a throne. The word that stung was "like." Like the nations. Israel was not supposed to be like the nations. Israel was supposed to be different — governed by prophets and judges and the direct word of God, answering to a King who had no palace and no army and no crown but whose authority was absolute. The elders were not just asking for a political restructuring. They were asking to be ordinary. Shmuel heard the words, and the text says: "The thing was evil in Shmuel's eyes." * * *
