Chapter Two

The King Who Danced

The second procession began at dawn.

The sun was still below the eastern hills when David and his entourage rode out of the gates of Yerushalayim and started north on the road toward Kiryat Ye'arim. Behind them came the Kehati Levi'im — a hundred and twenty of them, chosen by name, each man descended from the family that had carried the sacred vessels through the wilderness in the days of Moshe. Behind the Kehatim came the elders of the tribes. Behind the elders came the musicians. Behind the musicians came the people — thousands of them, men and women and children, walking on foot in the cool of the morning to witness what no Israelite of any living generation had ever witnessed before.

The Aron of Hashem was coming home.

At the house of Oved-Edom, the procession stopped. Oved-Edom came out to meet David, and behind him came his wife and his eight daughters-in-law, and behind them — in the arms of nursemaids and relatives — came the fifty-four babies. The chapter does not describe the scene, but the Midrash asks us to imagine it. Nine women, each holding six infants. Fifty-four children born in a single family in three months. David looked at them, and his eyes filled with tears.

"Oved-Edom."

"My lord."

"Is it true?"

"What, my lord?"

"That your wife and your sons' wives all gave birth to six each, in the months that the Aron has been in your house?"

Oved-Edom looked down at his own feet. He was an embarrassed man by nature — the kind of quiet Levi who spent his life singing in the back row of the chorus and preferred not to be noticed.

"It is true, my lord."

"And there has been no illness in your house?"

"None, my lord."

"No misfortune? No death? No strange occurrence of any kind?"

"Only the blessings, my lord. The fields produced twice what they produced last year. The olives were heavy on the branches. The chickens laid double. One of my grandchildren fell from a roof and landed in a pile of hay and did not break so much as a finger."

David turned to Tzadok the kohen.

"The Aron has been teaching Oved-Edom the same lesson it taught me at Peretz Uzza," David said, "except that Oved-Edom was given the pleasant version of the lesson, and I was given the terrible version."

"What is the lesson, my lord?"

"The same lesson both ways. Treat the Aron with reverence, and everything will flourish. Treat it with carelessness, and everything will die. I learned it by having a man die in front of me. Oved-Edom learned it by having fifty-four babies appear in his house. Two methods. One lesson."

He turned back to Oved-Edom.

"Bring it out."

* * *

The Kehati Levi'im entered the house.

They went into the inner chamber where the Aron had been resting on its cedar table for three months. Kenanyahu had assigned each man his position by name.

"Yisachar ben Ovadyah — front right pole, forward position."

"Here, my lord."

"Shemaryahu ben Gershom — front left pole, forward position."

"Here."

"Ma'asai ben Elkana — rear right, forward. Mattanyah — rear left, forward. The rest of you, take your positions two paces back in case one of the first four must be relieved. Remember what I told you last night: keep your shoulders level. If one of you stumbles, the others do not reach. You hold your position and you let the man beside you recover. The Aron will not fall. Is that understood?"

"Understood, my lord."

"Say it again."

"If a man stumbles, the others do not reach."

"Good."

They did not rush. They had been trained and retrained over the past three months. Each man knew exactly where he was supposed to stand, exactly which pole he was supposed to carry, exactly how to position his shoulder beneath the carved wood.

They lifted the Aron.

They carried it out of the chamber, through the house, through the courtyard, out into the road — slowly, carefully, without a single stumble or a single jolt. Oved-Edom watched them go with an expression that was hard to read. Three months ago, the Aron had been a terrifying presence in his house, one that he had been half-afraid to sleep near. Now it was leaving, and he was feeling the way a man feels when a beloved guest leaves after a long visit — grateful, honored, and already missing the presence.

David had planned for this moment too.

He stepped forward.

"Stop."

The Kehatim stopped.

"Before we take another step, we offer sacrifices."

Six paces from the house, on a small altar that had been built overnight, David offered an ox and a fatted calf as a sacrifice to Hashem. The smoke rose straight up into the morning sky without being bent by any wind — which is a detail that the Ralbag mentions as a sign of divine favor, and which David took as exactly that. The smoke was rising cleanly. The wind was still. The Aron had not killed anyone. The second procession was going to work.

David looked up at the sky with his face wet with tears, and he spoke quietly.

"Thank You."

Then he turned to the musicians.

"Play."

* * *

The music began.

It was not the same music as the first procession. David had rewritten the program. The first procession had included the full range of instruments — harps, lyres, cymbals, drums, timbrels, flutes — all playing together in a riot of festive sound. The first procession had been a celebration in the body. It had been joyful and loud and human.

This procession was different.

David had dialed down the instrumentation. The timbrels and the drums were kept to a minimum. The primary instruments now were the shofar and the silver trumpets — the instruments of the Mishkan, the instruments of divine service, the instruments that carried the people not into celebration but into awe. The Abarbanel, writing centuries later, noticed the difference. He contrasted the verb mesachakim used in the first procession (a word suggesting frivolity, sport, even a kind of dancing-around) with the word simchah used in the second (a word suggesting joy of the soul, the deep and ordered joy of a heart in the presence of something sacred). The first procession had been schok. The second was simchah. Same word-family, entirely different meaning.

And David began to dance.

* * *

The Tanach's description is carefully worded: "And David danced with all his might before Hashem. And David was girded with a linen ephod."

With all his might. The Hebrew phrase is b'chol oz. All his strength, all his power, everything he had. David was not dancing decorously. He was not doing a polite king's shuffle with his robes carefully arranged so as not to disturb his dignity. He was leaping. He was spinning. He was throwing his arms up to the sky and shouting praise to the God who had brought him out of the wilderness and onto the throne and now, finally, was allowing him to bring the Aron home.

Girded with a linen ephod. This is the detail that the mefarshim dwell on. A linen ephod was the uniform of the kohanim — a short white linen garment, worn over a simple tunic, reaching to the hips. It was the garment of a servant of the Mishkan, not the garment of a king. The king normally wore royal robes of purple and blue, heavy with embroidery and ornamentation. The ephod was the opposite. It was light. It was plain. It was white. It was the uniform of a man who had put aside his crown to serve Hashem.

The Metzudat David notes something interesting about the verb used later in the chapter when Michal sees David dancing. The verb is mefazez u-mekharker — "leaping and dancing." Mefazez has connotations of hasty, wild, unrestrained motion. It is from a root that also gives us the Hebrew word for the fine, shimmering movement of gold dust in sunlight. David was moving so fast, so wildly, so unrestrainedly, that he looked to an observer like a blur of motion — like the sparkle of gold catching the light.

This is who David was.

This was not the dignified David of the throne room at Chevron. This was not the grieving David of the lament for Shaul and Yehonatan. This was not the cold David who had executed Rechav and Ba'anah. This was the David who, as a boy, had sat on a hillside above Beit Lechem with his harp and his sheep and sung psalms to the sky because nobody was listening and he could be as loud and as joyful as he wanted to be. The shepherd was still inside the king. The shepherd was dancing in the streets of Yerushalayim because the God of the shepherd had come home.

And the Aron came up the road behind him.

* * *

At every sixth step, the procession stopped.

David had instituted this at the advice of Tzadok and Evyatar — a sacrifice at every six-step interval, as a sign of consecration and gratitude. At the sixth step, they offered an ox. At the twelfth step, a fatted calf. At the eighteenth step, another ox. The procession would stop, the sacrifice would be offered, the smoke would rise, David would dance again, and then they would advance another six steps.

At this rate, it was going to take them all day to reach Yerushalayim.

That was the point.

David did not want to rush. Three months ago, he had rushed, and a man had died. This time, he was going to go slowly, and the going-slowly was itself a form of teshuvah — a physical, embodied confession that his first attempt had been wrong, that he was now willing to spend as long as it took to do this the right way. Every time the procession stopped, it was David telling Hashem: I am in no hurry. I am not in control here. You are. I will move at Your pace.

The musicians played on. The Levi'im walked under the weight of the Aron on their shoulders. David danced. The people sang. The sun rose higher in the sky.

* * *

By mid-morning, they had reached the outer walls of Yerushalayim.

Word had gone ahead of them. The entire city was waiting. Men and women and children lined the streets from the northern gate all the way to the tent that David had prepared for the Aron inside the walls of the metzudah. They were cheering. They were throwing flower petals. They were holding out their children to see.

At one of the sacrifice stops, just outside the gate, David paused near a knot of servant girls who had crowded up to the edge of the procession route to watch. They were young — fourteen, fifteen, sixteen years old — the daughters of the households of his officers and the wives of his tribal captains. They were staring at him with wide eyes.

One of them, a girl with braided hair and a simple brown dress, said softly: "My lord."

David turned.

"Yes, child."

"Is it true that the Aron has killed men before?"

David looked at her for a moment.

"It is true."

"Are you afraid?"

"Today?"

"Today."

David thought about it. He thought about the threshing floor of Nachon, and the body of Uzza lying on the cart, and the three months of study, and the sacrifices at every six steps, and the fact that the procession had now walked a full day without a single stumble.

"Today," he said, "I am not afraid. Today I know that we are doing it the way the Torah commands. And today the Aron is blessing us, not killing us. Do you see the Levi'im? Their shoulders are tired, but they are alive. Do you see the oxen? They are walking steady. Do you hear the music? It has not stopped all morning. Today is not a day of fear. Today is a day of joy."

The girl smiled.

"Then I will dance too, my lord."

"Dance, child. Dance as hard as you can. There is no one watching you that matters, except Hashem, and Hashem loves a dancing child more than a standing king."

She laughed, and she ran back to her friends, and a moment later a small cluster of servant girls was leaping and spinning at the edge of the procession.

David watched them for a second, and then he danced past the walls of Yerushalayim and into the city.

* * *

David danced through the gate.

He danced down the main street. He danced past the marketplace, past the cisterns, past the palace courtyard, past his own house — not stopping to enter, not even glancing sideways at the windows.

And in one of those windows, his wife was watching.

* * *

Michal had not gone out with the procession.

She had been invited. She had been invited several times, by several different messengers. She had refused. She had said she was unwell. She had said she did not feel like walking that far. She had said the crowds would tire her. She had given a different excuse each time, and David had eventually stopped asking, because he did not want to press her.

But Michal had wanted to see the procession all the same — or at least she had wanted to see the moment when her husband came home with the Aron. Morbid curiosity, perhaps. Or wounded pride. Or something deeper and sadder that we will come to in the next chapter. Whatever the reason, she had positioned herself at the upper window of their private quarters in the palace, the window that looked out over the main processional route, and she had watched.

She watched the Kehati Levi'im carry the Aron past the window.

She watched the musicians.

She watched the elders of the tribes.

And then she watched her husband.

David was directly below her window when she saw him. He was leaping — barefoot, in the linen ephod of a common Levite, his hair wild and loose on his shoulders, his beard tangled from the morning's exertion, his arms thrown up to the sky, his face transfigured with a joy so unguarded and so public that it seemed almost shameful to watch.

Michal's face, according to the Scripture, went through several changes in the next few seconds.

The first change was confusion. That is not how a king behaves.

The second change was recognition. That is the boy I fell in love with fifteen years ago — the same boy who played the harp in my father's court and stood shyly in front of the throne with his rough shepherd's hands folded. That is still him.

The third change — and this was the change that the Scripture records, the change that would define the rest of her life — was contempt.

"And Michal the daughter of Shaul looked out of the window, and she saw the king David leaping and dancing before Hashem, and she despised him in her heart."

* * *

The word translated as despised in Shmuel Bet 6:16 is the Hebrew word vatibez, from the root bazah. It is the same root used in the Torah when Esav despises his birthright — trades it away for a bowl of lentils because he cannot see the value in it, because he is hungry and tired and prefers the bird in his hand to the bird in the bush of his distant future. Bazah is not simple anger. It is not the hot flash of rage that passes in a moment. It is a settled, cold contempt — a final judgment, a writing-off, a decision that what you are looking at is not worth your respect.

Michal, looking down at her husband dancing in the streets in the ephod of a common Levite, wrote him off.

She did not write him off as a man. She had loved him once. Scripture tells us in Shmuel Aleph that she loved him — she was the one who put the teraphim in his bed when Shaul's soldiers came for him, who let him down through the window in the darkness, who bought him the hours he needed to escape. Some part of her still remembered that David. Some part of her, perhaps, still loved him.

But she wrote him off as a king.

She was a king's daughter. She had grown up in the household of Shaul — Shaul the tall, Shaul the warrior, Shaul who stood head and shoulders above every other man in Israel, Shaul who would never in a thousand years have danced down a public street in a short white ephod like a common Levite. Shaul would have ridden a horse through that gate. Shaul would have worn the purple. Shaul would have stood still in the center of the procession and let the people bow to him, because that was what kings did. Shaul had been a king in the way that Michal had been raised to understand the word.

David was not that kind of king.

David was a shepherd wearing a borrowed crown. And for the first time, watching him from the window, Michal saw it clearly. He had no royal dignity. He had no royal distance. He was the kind of man who would dance in the street with servant girls and throw his arms up in the air and shout praise to Hashem in the middle of the marketplace. He was everything Michal had been raised not to marry.

And so she wrote him off.

The Radak notes the tragedy buried in this moment. Michal, daughter of Shaul. Not Michal, wife of David. Scripture is careful with its identifications. When Michal loved David, in the earlier books, she is sometimes called "the wife of David" or simply "Michal." Now, at the window, she is called by her father's name. The text is telling us that in this moment, Michal is not seeing her husband. She is seeing the man who is not her father. She is judging him by her father's standards, and finding him lacking.

A wife who looks at her husband and sees a comparison with her father has already lost the husband. It does not matter what the husband does next. The loss is already complete.

* * *

David did not see her at the window.

He danced past the palace without looking up. His eyes were on the Aron behind him and the tent ahead of him. His mind was full of psalms he was composing on the spot and prayers he was trying to remember and the sheer physical exertion of leaping for hours on end. He did not feel Michal's eyes on him.

But Hashem did.

The chapter closes the scene with a devastating sentence — a sentence that will not be fully explained until after David returns home at the end of the day. For now, the sentence is this: "And Michal the daughter of Shaul had no child until the day of her death."

The sentence is placed at the end of Chapter 6 of Shmuel Bet, many verses after the window scene, as if Scripture is pausing to deliver the punchline in advance. Michal looks down at David dancing. Michal despises him in her heart. Thirty verses later, the narrator tells us flatly that she never bore a child.

The connection is unstated but unmistakable.

The Talmud will discuss the meaning of this silence — whether it is a divine judgment, a human consequence, a natural outcome of a marriage in which contempt has taken root. Different sages will offer different readings. But all of them will agree on one thing: from the moment Michal looked down from the window and despised her husband for dancing, her womb closed, and it never opened again.

* * *

The procession reached the tent.

David had built a tent for the Aron near the center of the city — not the Beit HaMikdash, not the permanent structure he would dream of for the rest of his life, but a beautiful tent of blue and purple fabric stretched over cedar poles, modeled on the Mishkan of the wilderness. Inside the tent, a place had been prepared for the Aron. The Kehatim carried it to the place. They lowered it onto its stone pedestal. They backed out of the tent with their heads bowed.

David entered the tent alone.

Inside, in the dim light filtering through the blue fabric, he stood before the Aron of Hashem and wept. Not for Uzza, this time. Not for the mistake he had made. He wept because the Aron was home. For the first time since the days of Yehoshua — for the first time in four hundred years — the sacred presence of Hashem was resting in a place that belonged to a king of Israel. The city was His. The land was His. The throne was His. And David was His servant.

He raised his voice and he sang — a psalm the Chronicler preserves in 1 Chronicles 16, a psalm that would be sung in the Beit HaMikdash for generations and in synagogues around the world to this day:

"Give thanks to Hashem, call upon His name, make known His deeds among the peoples. Sing to Him, sing praises to Him, speak of all His wonders. Glory in His holy name; let the heart of those who seek Hashem rejoice. Seek Hashem and His strength; seek His face continually."

When he came out of the tent, the people were still gathered in the square. He blessed them in the name of Hashem. He distributed gifts — to every man and every woman in the crowd, a loaf of bread, a portion of meat, a cake of raisins. This was not a king being generous. This was a man celebrating a wedding that had been seven hundred years in the making. The Aron of Hashem had come home. Every Israelite in Yerushalayim deserved a piece of the feast.

Then David turned toward his own house to bless his family.

He did not know that the hardest conversation of his day was still waiting for him there.
