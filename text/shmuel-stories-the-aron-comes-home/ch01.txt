Chapter One

The Hand of Uzza

## Volume II — The Aron Comes Home ### A Dramatic Retelling Based on Talmudic, Midrashic, and Classical Sources

* * *

The Aron had been waiting for twenty years.

It had come home from the land of the Pelishtim on the back of a cart drawn by two nursing cows who had lowed all the way from Ekron to Beit Shemesh without turning their heads, because Hashem had been pulling them by a lead rope that no human eye could see. It had stopped in the field of Yehoshua the Beit Shimshi, and the men of Beit Shemesh had rejoiced — and then some of them had done a thing that the chapter in Shmuel Aleph refuses to describe in detail, but it had involved looking into the Aron in a way that no man should ever look, and seventy of them had dropped dead where they stood. The survivors had sent a frightened message to the nearest large town: Come and take the Aron away from us. We are not holy enough to keep it.

And the men of Kiryat Ye'arim had come, and they had carried the Aron up the hill to the house of a man named Avinadav, and they had sanctified his son El'azar to guard it.

That was the beginning of the twenty years.

For two decades, the Aron of Hashem sat in a small stone room in the house of Avinadav on the hill at Kiryat Ye'arim. El'azar lit oil lamps in the room every morning and every evening. The curtains were kept clean. The door was kept closed. On Shabbat, the people of the nearby villages would come to stand in the courtyard and sing — but they did not enter the room. Only El'azar entered the room, and even he entered with his eyes lowered.

The Aron was there when Shaul fought the Pelishtim at Michmas. It was there when David killed Golyat in the valley of Elah. It was there during all the years of David's flight through the wilderness, through Ein Gedi and Ma'on and Tziklag. It was there during the battle of Gilboa, when Shaul fell on his own sword. It was there during the seven and a half years of civil war. It was there during the coronation at Chevron. It was there when David's men climbed through the water shaft of the Gichon and took the metzudah of Tzion.

For twenty years, the Aron of Hashem had been sitting in a private house on a hill, and the kings of Israel had been too busy — or too afraid, or too unworthy, or too scattered — to bring it home.

David was going to change that.

* * *

The council met in the new palace at Yerushalayim.

David had been living in his new city for less than a year. The walls of the metzudah were still being reinforced by Tyrian stonemasons that Chiram, the king of Tzor, had sent north as a goodwill gesture along with cedar beams and carved ivory panels. The streets were still being widened. The marketplace was still being organized. But the most urgent project in David's mind was not construction. It was restoration.

"I have been thinking," David said, "about the Aron."

The men around the table looked up. Yoav was there. Avishai. Benayahu ben Yehoyada. Tzadok and Evyatar, the two kohanim. A scribe named Sheraiah, who was taking notes. And a young Levi named Kenanyahu, who had been trained in the music of the Mishkan and who had come to Yerushalayim as the chief musician of the new court.

"What about it, my lord?" Tzadok asked.

"It has been in Kiryat Ye'arim for twenty years. Before that, seven months in the land of the Pelishtim. Before that, in the Mishkan at Shilo, which was destroyed. Before that — " David waved a hand. "Before that, the Mishkan at Gilgal. Before that, the desert. For the entire lifetime of Shaul, no king of Israel inquired about the Aron. Not once. It is written in the book of Shmuel Aleph: 'And the Aron of Hashem was in Kiryat Ye'arim for a very long time, and the children of Israel yearned for Hashem.' The children of Israel yearned — but they did not go up the hill."

"Shaul was busy with war," Yoav said.

"Shaul was busy with many things that were not war," David said. "And the Aron sat on its hill, waiting."

Evyatar leaned forward. "You intend to bring it to Yerushalayim."

"I intend to bring it to Yerushalayim."

"When?"

"Now. This spring. Before the planting is finished and the roads become clogged with carts. I want thirty thousand men. I want every elder of every tribe present. I want musicians. I want the Levi'im. I want" — and here David's voice rose, and his eyes went a little bit wild — "I want a procession the likes of which this land has not seen since Yehoshua crossed the Yarden."

There was a silence around the table.

Yoav cleared his throat.

"My lord, a question."

"Ask."

"Who will carry it?"

David blinked. "What do you mean?"

"The Aron. How will we transport it from Kiryat Ye'arim to Yerushalayim? It is a good day's journey on foot. The Aron is heavy."

"It has poles. The Torah commands that it be carried on the shoulders of the Levi'im. There are — " David glanced at Kenanyahu. "There are specific Levi'im assigned to this work, are there not?"

"The sons of Kehat," Kenanyahu said. "The Kehati clan of Levi. Their ancestral task since the days of Moshe is to carry the sacred vessels of the Mishkan on their shoulders, with poles, on foot."

"There, then," David said. "The Kehatim will carry it."

Evyatar was quiet. He had gone very still, the way Evyatar always went still when something was about to go wrong and he did not yet know exactly what. But he did not speak.

"The problem, my lord," Yoav said carefully, "is the distance. Kiryat Ye'arim is perhaps fifteen kilometers. On foot, on shoulders, with the weight of the Aron and the procession behind them — it will take two days, perhaps three. And the Pelishtim, when they returned the Aron seventy years ago, did not carry it on shoulders. They put it on a cart drawn by oxen."

"The Pelishtim were idolaters," David said. "They did not know the Torah."

"They also did not die."

David looked at Yoav sharply.

"The cart worked for them," Yoav continued, his voice level. "They placed the Aron on a new cart drawn by two cows that had never been yoked, and the cows pulled the cart all the way to Beit Shemesh without a stumble. The text of Shmuel Aleph makes a point of describing it. If the cart method worked for the idolaters, it will certainly work for us. And it will be faster, and safer, and less exhausting for the Kehatim."

David looked at Evyatar. Evyatar did not meet his eye.

"Evyatar. What do you say?"

"I say…" Evyatar paused. "I say that the Pelishtim had no choice. They did not have Levi'im. They had no one authorized to carry the Aron at all. A cart was the only option available to them. It was a pragmatic arrangement that Hashem permitted in the absence of any alternative."

"But Hashem did permit it."

"In that case."

"Then He will permit it in this case as well."

Evyatar looked down at his hands.

"My lord — I am uneasy."

"Why?"

"I am not sure. There is something about the cart that troubles me. But I cannot point to a specific verse. The Torah commands that the sacred vessels be carried on shoulders. But it does not explicitly forbid a cart when the distance is great and the Kehatim are weary. I do not know the halacha here with certainty."

David leaned back. "Yoav's plan is practical. It is fast. It will allow the procession to reach Yerushalayim in a single day, with enough strength left in the Levi'im to play their instruments and sing the psalms I have written for this occasion. Unless you can give me a specific verse that forbids it, I am going to do it."

Evyatar did not give him a verse.

The meeting ended.

The cart was built.

* * *

Two weeks later, thirty thousand men gathered on the plain below Kiryat Ye'arim.

It was a spring morning. The sky was a brilliant blue, and the terebinths were in full leaf, and the hills of Yehuda were green with the last rains of the season. David had kept his promise — every elder of every tribe was present, and the musicians were present, and the Levi'im were present in their robes, and the new cart — a thing of polished cedar wood with iron reinforcements at the corners — stood in the center of the plain with two strong oxen yoked to it.

The Aron was carried down from the house of Avinadav on the shoulders of El'azar and his two brothers, Uzza and Achyo, who had grown up in the shadow of the sacred box and knew its weight and its presence the way a child knows the weight and presence of a father. They lowered it onto the cart with extraordinary care. El'azar tied it down with linen cords. Uzza climbed up onto the cart beside it, to guide the oxen and to steady the Aron if it should shift on the ride. Achyo walked ahead, leading the oxen by their rope.

David stood in front of the cart. He was wearing a white linen robe. His hair was loose on his shoulders. He had been fasting since the night before.

"Let us begin," he said.

Kenanyahu raised a silver trumpet to his lips and blew a long, ringing note that echoed off the hills.

The procession began to move.

* * *

They sang as they walked.

David had composed the songs for this day. Some of them would later be preserved in the Book of Tehillim — Psalms 24, 68, 132. They sang of the God of Israel going up with a shout, of the gates of Tzion lifting up their heads, of the King of Glory entering. They played on harps and lyres and flutes and timbrels and cymbals. The sound of the procession was the sound of a whole nation rejoicing. It carried across the valleys and up onto the ridges and down the far side into the fields of Binyamin and Yehuda beyond.

David danced at the head of the procession.

He was already dancing — even before the scene with Michal, even before the second procession — because he could not walk in front of the Aron without moving. His body kept time with the music. He was joyful. He was triumphant. He was bringing the Aron home after twenty years.

And then they came to the threshing floor of Nachon.

* * *

The threshing floor was a flat area of packed dirt where farmers beat the grain out of the wheat stalks in the summer. It was at the bottom of a slight dip in the road, where the ground was uneven and the cart wheels had to negotiate a shallow depression before climbing the next rise.

The oxen stumbled.

It was not a dramatic stumble. It was not a fall. It was the kind of stumble an ox makes a dozen times a day without anyone noticing. The cart lurched. The Aron slid a hand's breadth to one side. The linen cords — which had been tied carefully, but had loosened on the bumpy road — held it from falling, but barely.

Uzza, standing beside it on the cart, saw the Aron shift.

His reaction was instinctive.

He reached out his hand to steady it.

His fingers closed around the edge of the gold overlay.

And Uzza ben Avinadav, who had been raised in the same house as the Aron for most of his life, who had seen it every day since he was a boy, who had touched it before on the day his father had lowered it onto the cart — Uzza was struck dead where he stood.

The silence that fell on the procession was instantaneous. The music stopped. The singing stopped. Thirty thousand men went silent at once. The only sound was the breathing of the oxen and the soft flap of a banner in the spring wind.

Uzza's body lay beside the Aron on the cart.

* * *

David ran forward.

He pushed through the front of the procession and he climbed onto the cart and he bent over Uzza, and he saw that Uzza was dead, and he saw that the Aron was untouched and intact, and he understood — in the terrible clarity of a single moment — that he had made a mistake.

He was furious.

The Scripture records his fury with a word that is difficult to translate. Vayichar le-David. "And it burned in David." The Hebrew is ambiguous about who the fury was directed at. Was David angry at Uzza, for the presumption of touching the Aron? Was David angry at Hashem, for the seeming injustice of killing a man who had only tried to help? Was David angry at himself, for listening to Yoav and building the cart in the first place?

The Gemara in Sotah reads the verb vayichar in a surprising way. Some of the sages take it not as anger but as a kind of burning illness. Rabbi El'azar, the Gemara says, understood the verse to mean that David's face changed color — his features darkened, and he became physically stricken, and for a moment he could not speak.

Because he understood that the death was on his hands.

The Aron could take care of itself. That was the first lesson. The cart had not been necessary. The Kehatim could have carried the Aron on their shoulders, as the Torah commanded, and they would have arrived a day later and no one would have died.

Uzza had reached out his hand because the cart was unstable. But the cart was unstable because David had built it. And David had built it because David had failed to insist on the Torah's method. And so the blood of Uzza was not, in the deepest sense, on Uzza's hands. It was on David's.

He climbed down from the cart slowly.

He walked a few paces away. He turned and looked back at the Aron and the body lying beside it. The thirty thousand men watched him in silence. Kenanyahu held his silver trumpet at his side and did not raise it.

"Call this place," David said finally, "Peretz Uzza. The Breach of Uzza."

Then he turned to Tzadok and Evyatar.

"We are not bringing the Aron to Yerushalayim today."

"My lord — "

"We are not bringing it to Yerushalayim today. I do not know enough. I made a mistake that cost a man his life. I will not compound the mistake by continuing the journey before I understand what I did wrong."

"Where will we put it, my lord?"

David looked around. The threshing floor was at the edge of a small village. He pointed at the nearest house — a modest stone building with a low roof and a courtyard visible through an open gate.

"Whose house is that?"

A man from the village stepped forward nervously. "My lord, that is the house of Oved-Edom the Giti."

"A Giti? A Pelishti?"

"No, my lord. Oved-Edom is a Levi — one of the Kehatim, in fact. He is called 'the Giti' because his family once lived in the Pelishti town of Gat-Rimon, which was given to the Levi'im in the days of Yehoshua. But he is a full Levi and a man of impeccable lineage. He has served in the Mishkan at Nov and at Giv'on. He is a singer and a gatekeeper."

David stared at the man. Then he looked at the house.

"A Kehati."

"Yes, my lord."

David almost laughed — a short, bitter laugh.

"Of course. Of course he is a Kehati. Hashem is teaching me a lesson, and I am finally starting to hear it. Carry the Aron on the shoulders of the Levi'im — short distance, on foot, by hand. The proper way. Take it off the cart. Carry it to the house of Oved-Edom the Giti. Place it in his most honored room. Sanctify the room. And then leave the Aron there, in his care, until I understand what I need to understand."

"How long, my lord?"

"I don't know. Until I am ready to do this the right way. Until Hashem tells me the lesson has been learned."

* * *

The Aron stayed in the house of Oved-Edom for three months.

During those three months, two things happened. The first thing was that David studied. He pulled every scroll of the Torah that he could find. He called in Tzadok and Evyatar and the elders of the Levi'im and the scholars of the tribe of Yehuda, and he made them read to him from Bamidbar and from Devarim and from every passage in the Torah that mentioned the Aron. He wanted to know, in exact detail, every halacha governing its transport. He wanted to know who could touch it, who could carry it, how it had to be covered, what songs had to be sung when it moved, what sacrifices had to be offered, what the distance between bearers had to be. He wanted to know everything. And by the end of the three months, he knew everything.

The Chronicler preserves a speech that David gave to the Levi'im at the beginning of the second procession. It is worth quoting, because it shows what David had learned:

"Because you did not carry it at the first — Hashem our God broke out against us, because we did not seek Him according to the proper custom."

Because we did not seek Him according to the proper custom.

David said those words in front of thirty thousand men, standing beside a new group of Kehati Levi'im who had just been instructed in their duties. He was admitting, publicly, that the death of Uzza had been his fault. Not Uzza's. Not Yoav's. His.

It is rare in Tanach to see a king admit a mistake in public. David would do it more than once.

The second thing that happened during those three months was that Oved-Edom the Giti became the most blessed man in Israel.

* * *

The Midrash tells the story in extraordinary detail. When the Aron arrived at Oved-Edom's house, he and his wife prepared the finest room in the house — the inner chamber, where Oved-Edom normally stored his family's grain — and they cleaned it and they scrubbed the walls and they laid down fresh mats on the floor and they placed the Aron in the center of the room on a low cedar table. They hung curtains. They lit oil lamps. They did everything they could think of to honor the sacred presence that had suddenly entered their home.

Oved-Edom had a wife. He had eight daughters-in-law — his sons' wives — living in the household. The family was large by ordinary standards but no larger than many Levi families in the towns of Yehuda.

In the first month after the Aron arrived, Oved-Edom's wife became pregnant. So did every one of his eight daughters-in-law. All nine women in the household were suddenly carrying children.

In the second month, they could feel the children moving inside them.

In the third month — which was unusually early by any normal reckoning — all nine women went into labor. And when they gave birth, each one of them delivered six children at once.

Fifty-four babies. In a single household. In three months.

The Midrash preserves this tradition as a description of the blessing that the Aron of Hashem brings when it is treated with proper reverence. It is a fantastical story in the literal sense — no one thinks it happened as a precise medical event — but as a midrashic image it captures something real. The presence of Hashem is a presence of life, of fertility, of abundance. When the Aron was treated badly, it killed a man. When it was treated well, it made flowers bloom out of every crack in the house. It was the same Aron. It was the same Hashem. The difference was in the reverence of the people around it.

Word of the blessing on Oved-Edom's household spread quickly. Within weeks, every family in Yerushalayim knew about the babies. Within a month, the elders of the tribes were sending messengers to David.

"The Aron is blessing the house of Oved-Edom the Giti. Why is it not blessing your house, my lord?"

"Is it time, my lord, to try again?"

David was ready.

* * *

On the last day of the third month, David summoned Tzadok and Evyatar and Kenanyahu and the elders of the Kehati Levi'im. He did not summon Yoav. Yoav would come to the procession, but Yoav would not be consulted this time.

"We are ready," David said. "No cart. No oxen. Shoulders and poles, the way the Torah commands. We will offer a sacrifice after the first six steps — an ox and a fatted calf — to test whether Hashem accepts our approach. If He strikes us down, we will stop. If He does not strike us down, we will continue. And we will sing every step of the way."

"And you, my lord?" Kenanyahu asked. "What will you wear?"

David smiled.

"I will wear a linen ephod," he said. "A simple linen ephod, like the one a kohen wears in the Mishkan. Not a royal robe. Not a crown. Not the purple of a king. When the Aron of Hashem enters Yerushalayim, there is only one King in that city, and His name is not David ben Yishai. I will enter as a servant, not as a lord."

The elders nodded.

And as they walked out of the council chamber, preparing for the second procession — the one that would go right, the one that would not kill anyone, the one in which David would dance with all his might before the Aron of Hashem — Evyatar the kohen, who had been silent for most of the past three months, finally spoke a single quiet sentence to Tzadok.

"I told him at the first meeting that I was uneasy about the cart."

"I know," Tzadok said.

"I wish I had been able to give him a verse."

"I know," Tzadok said again. "But he listened the second time. That is a rare thing in a king. He listened."
